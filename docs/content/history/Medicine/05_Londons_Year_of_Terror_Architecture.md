### Runtime note

Episode 5 owns the Great Plague comparison and the wider Renaissance prevention/public-health strand. It should show that ordinary explanations, remedies and much prevention remained rooted in medieval ideas, while local government became more organised in recording, cleaning, isolation and enforcement.

---

## Chapter narrative

The Great Plague of 1665 exposed a striking contradiction: Renaissance investigators had begun changing how medical knowledge was created, but ordinary explanations and remedies still looked much like those used during the Black Death. The clearest progress was not a cure or a correct theory of disease, but a more organised government response built around recording, cleaning, quarantine, searchers and watchmen.

---

## Navigation spine (6 parts)

Every Medicine module must be built as six clear navigation parts. These titles should appear in the module journey/progress navigation so the student always knows where they are in the story.

1. **Plague returns to the capital** — Black Death/Renaissance recall, hook and learner roadmap.
2. **London counts the dead** — context, spread, fear and Bills of Mortality.
3. **Old explanations, new city** — God, astrology, miasma and familiar remedies.
4. **Shut the door and clean the streets** — prevention, quarantine, pest houses and government orders.
5. **More organised, still not modern** — direct comparison with 1348 and final judgement.
6. **Exam prep: Compare two plagues** — examiner teaching, comparison language and assessed application.

## 2. Architecture checklist (tailored)

### Section 1 — Intro, Recall & Roadmap

- **Purpose:** Recall the Black Death and the Renaissance knowledge story, then frame 1665 as a test of whether medicine had really changed.
- **Required content:**
  - PriorKnowledgeRecall: Black Death explanations, treatments and prevention; Vesalius/Harvey knowledge-before-treatment judgement.
  - Hook: More than 300 years after the Black Death, plague returns to London — would people respond differently now?
  - You will learn: what people believed; how they treated and prevented plague; what government did; how 1665 compared with 1348.
- **Recommended components:**
  - `CinematicRevealMoment` or chapter hook — London street with shut-up houses, red crosses and watchmen; restrained and non-graphic.
  - `PriorKnowledgeRecall`.
  - `WhatYouWillLearn` / governed chapter-outcomes pattern.

### Section 2 — London Counts the Dead

- **Purpose:** Establish the scale, urban setting and growing administrative visibility of the crisis.
- **Required content:**
  - 1665 London context: density, movement, rubbish and fear.
  - Symptoms and helplessness similar to earlier plague outbreaks.
  - Bills of Mortality recording deaths by parish.
  - Bills as evidence of organisation and communication, not germ theory.
- **Recommended components:**
  - `VisualNarrativeScreen` — outbreak spreads through the city while weekly death totals rise.
  - `InteractiveHotspotImage` or evidence-exploration pattern — London street, mortality bill, red-cross house and public notices.
  - `QuickRecallScreen`.

### Section 3 — Old Explanations, New City

- **Purpose:** Make continuity in medical understanding unavoidable.
- **Required content:**
  - God and sin.
  - Planetary alignment in 1664.
  - Miasma as the dominant explanation.
  - No germ theory despite recent advances in anatomy and circulation.
  - Treatments: sweating, herbs, transference, diet advice and quack remedies.
  - Individual prevention: prayer, pomanders, fires, herbs, plague-doctor clothing and flight.
- **Recommended components:**
  - `TheoryCompare` — 1348 versus 1665 explanations and responses.
  - `MatchingTask` — belief → treatment/prevention response → why it seemed logical at the time.
  - `MisconceptionCheck` — plague-doctor clothing was not evidence of germ understanding.
  - `QuickRecallScreen`.

### Section 4 — Shut the Door and Clean the Streets

- **Purpose:** Teach the wider prevention/public-health strand and distinguish government organisation from correct medical understanding.
- **Required content:**
  - Wider continuity: Regimen Sanitatis lifestyle advice and reduced enthusiasm for bathing.
  - Miasma-based prevention: herbs, bonfires, rubbish removal and street cleaning.
  - Growing government role: cleaning rules, fines, use of criminals to clear rubbish and closure of bathhouses.
  - Pest houses as isolation for plague or pox.
  - 1665 measures: shut-up houses, red crosses, watchmen, searchers, examiners, crowd restrictions, animal killing and mortality records.
  - Limits and cruelty of enforced quarantine.
- **Teaching rule:** Separate **what the measure did** from **why people thought it worked**. Some actions could reduce spread or improve conditions even when based on the wrong theory.
- **Recommended components:**
  - `GuidedChoiceCarousel` or scenario interaction — act as a city official choosing responses, then reveal intended logic and actual limitation.
  - `ColSortBlock` — individual / government; or likely helpful / based mainly on wrong belief / mixed effect.
  - `QuickRecallScreen`.

### Section 5 — More Organised, Still Not Modern

- **Purpose:** Complete teaching through an explicit Black Death comparison and balanced judgement.
- **Required content:**
  - Similarities: God, miasma, astrology, prayer, sweet smells, fire, herbs, ineffective remedies and incomplete isolation.
  - Differences: watchmen, searchers, red crosses, Bills of Mortality, crowd restrictions, pest houses and more systematic enforcement.
  - Judgement: medical understanding changed little; government administration changed more.
  - Agents of change: government increasingly active; religion still influential; science not yet transformative for epidemic response.
- **Recommended components:**
  - `ColSortBlock` or comparison matrix — same since 1348 / more organised in 1665.
  - `ExplainReveal` — why better organisation did not equal modern medical understanding.
  - `MisconceptionCheck` — challenge all-change, no-change and "government understood the cause" claims.
  - Retrieval close on the final judgement.

### Section 6 — Summary & Examiner

- **Purpose:** Teach and assess Edexcel comparison without introducing new content.
- **Required content:**
  - Comparison structure: identify a similarity or difference, support both sides with precise evidence, explain the comparison, then make an overall judgement.
  - Comparative language: similarly, whereas, however, more organised, still limited.
  - Common traps: listing 1665 facts without comparing; claiming the response was modern; confusing public-health action with cure or correct causation.
  - Assessed question: **"How similar were people's reactions to the Black Death (1348) and the Great Plague (1665)?"**
- **Recommended components:**
  - `ExaminerExplainsScreen`.
  - `GuidedExamResponse` or `FaceTheExaminer` as assessed exam-technique application.
  - `ChapterCompleteScreen`.

### Module Completion Test

- [ ] Section 1 includes retrieval (`PriorKnowledgeRecall`).
- [ ] A concise learner-facing "You will learn" roadmap is present.
- [ ] Black Death comparison is revisited throughout, not saved only for the final screen.

- [ ] Treatments and prevention are clearly distinguished.

## 3. Scope boundaries and gap analysis

### Owned here

- Great Plague context, beliefs, treatments and prevention.
- Black Death comparison.
- Wider Renaissance prevention and public-health change.
- Pest houses, street cleaning, bathhouse changes, quarantine, searchers, watchmen and mortality recording.
- Government organisation versus medical continuity.

### Explicitly owned by Episode 3

- Renaissance background, Vesalius, anatomy theatres, printing and the first challenge to Galen's anatomy.

### Explicitly owned by Episode 4

- Harvey, Paré, Fracastoro, Royal Society, Malpighi, Leeuwenhoek, Sydenham and wider changes in treatment, hospitals and medical roles.

### Learning-design risk

The chapter can easily become a list of strange plague remedies. Every remedy or prevention measure must serve one of two questions: **What did people think caused disease?** and **What had actually changed since 1348?** Atmosphere should support, not replace, comparison and judgement.

---

## 4. Build recommendations

1. **Make comparison unavoidable:** repeatedly connect 1665 evidence back to 1348.
2. **Separate treatment, prevention and government action:** learners should not experience them as one undifferentiated list.
3. **Teach intended logic and actual effect:** wrong theory can sometimes produce partly useful action.
4. **Use government as the main change story:** searchers, watchmen, mortality records and enforcement are the clearest developments.
5. **Keep medical continuity visible:** miasma, religion, astrology and ineffective remedies remain central.
6. **End with the precise judgement:** responses were medically similar but administratively more organised.
