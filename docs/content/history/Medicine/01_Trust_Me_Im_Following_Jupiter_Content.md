## 2. Storyline

### Core takeaway
DRAFT (for user confirmation): Medieval medicine was not random superstition — it was a coherent system built on the best available authorities. The Church, Galen, and the theory of the Four Humours formed an interlocking framework that was logically self-consistent for its time. Progress was slow not because medieval people were ignorant, but because every institution — the Church, universities, professional guilds — reinforced the same inherited model and actively blocked alternatives. Understanding *why* the system held so firmly is what unlocks the "explain why" exam questions.

*Note: derived from the recurring pattern across all three agents of change (Religion, Individuals, Science & technology) that each chain traces back to "the Church controlled education and gatekept knowledge."*

### Evidence for the takeaway
- Church controlled all books and education → Galen's texts were the only ones widely taught → no exposure to alternative theories
- Dissection largely illegal (Church: body must remain whole for the soul) → no hands-on testing of Galen's claims → errors persisted for 1300+ years
- Physicians' economic incentive: patients wanted Galen treatments, not experiments → market pressure reinforced orthodoxy
- The printing press (invented 1440) existed by the late medieval period but had not yet disrupted the Church's control of knowledge
- When dissections of criminals were occasionally allowed, disagreements with Galen could be "explained away" because the criminal body was imperfect

### Series throughline
- **Agent: Religion (⛪)** — The Church's dominance is the defining constraint on medieval medicine; every other agent (Individuals, Science & technology) operates under its shadow. This releases slowly in Episodes 3–5 (Renaissance, Harvey, Great Plague) as the Reformation weakens Church authority.
- **Agent: Individuals (👤)** — Hippocrates and Galen are the dominant individuals, but their influence is mediated through the Church. Future individual breakthroughs (Vesalius, Harvey) become possible only as Church authority declines.
- **Continuity link → Episodes 3, 4, 5:** Most ordinary people still believed in Four Humours and miasma even through the Renaissance — this is the key "continuity" argument for "how far do you agree" questions.

### Exam framing
- "Explain why Galen had such a big influence on medieval medicine" — the Three Reasons model (Church promotion, book learning culture, lack of alternatives/no dissection) is the complete answer.
- "How far do you agree that religion was the main reason medicine changed so little in the medieval period?" — the takeaway provides the spine: religion didn't just block change, it made the existing system *feel correct*.

## 3. Specification requirements

- **Background context — medieval Britain:**
  - Church dominance over everyday life (everyone attended church, paid tithes)
  - Dark Ages knowledge loss (c410–1066): much Roman/Greek learning disappeared in Western Europe
  - Population mostly agricultural; few literate
  - Classical thinkers Hippocrates and Galen provided the dominant intellectual framework
  - Printing press invented 1440 — late medieval, not yet transformative

- **Ideas about the causes of disease:**
  - **Religious explanations:** disease = punishment from God for sin, or test of faith from the Devil; Church taught this because God was believed to control all aspects of the world
  - **Astrology:** alignment of stars/planets influenced disease; used in diagnosis (star charts, birth date, date of illness); Church initially disliked astrology but accepted it more after the Black Death; link to God (God controls planets → astrology is God's will)
  - **Miasma:** bad air filled with harmful fumes; came from swamps, corpses, rotting matter; Hippocrates and Galen had written about miasmata; linked to dirt and sin
  - **Four Humours theory:**
    - Created by Hippocrates (Ancient Greece), developed by Galen (Ancient Rome)
    - Four humours: blood, phlegm, black bile, yellow bile — must be balanced in the body
    - Illness = humours out of balance
    - Humours linked to seasons (winter = wet/cold = too much phlegm), personality traits (hot-tempered = too much yellow bile), star signs
    - Galen added Theory of Opposites: treat excess of one humour with its opposite (e.g. too much phlegm [cold] → eat something hot like pepper; too much blood [hot/wet] → eat cucumber)

- **Why Galen and Hippocrates had such influence — three reasons:**
  - **Influence of the Church:** Galen believed in the soul → fitted Christian beliefs → Church promoted his texts → only texts widely taught
  - **Importance of book learning:** Good physician = widely read, not experienced; physicians who hadn't read Galen couldn't find work
  - **Lack of alternatives:** Dissection mostly illegal → couldn't test theories; exceptions (criminals) could be explained away; printing press not yet disruptive

- **Approaches to treatment:**
  - **Religious treatments:** prayer, fasting, paying for a special Mass, pilgrimages — to remove sin and show faith
  - **Supernatural treatments:** charms, amulets, incantations (spells); astrology used in timing of operations
  - **Humoural treatments:**
    - Blood-letting (phlebotomy): cupping, leeches, cutting a vein
    - Purging: emetics (to vomit) or laxatives; apothecaries sometimes gave poisons
    - Theory of Opposites applied to treat specific excess humour
    - Urine examination: colour, smell, even taste checked for humour balance
  - **Herbal remedies:** drunk, sniffed or bathed in; common ingredients: aloe vera, mint, saffron; theriaca (spice-based mixture with many ingredients, used for many illnesses); bathing with herbs added
  - **Hospitals:** 
    - Run by Church in monasteries, or funded by endowments (wealthy person's will)
    - Over 500 hospitals by 1400 but focus on hospitality/care not cure
    - Clean, good for rest and recovery, but no physicians or surgeons
    - Infectious/terminal patients often rejected
  - **Home care:** majority treated at home by women; herbal remedies; comfort and feeding
  - **Three types of medic:**
    - Physicians: university-trained (Galen's works), diagnosed illness (did not treat themselves), very expensive
    - Barber surgeons: small operations (bloodletting), knowledge from experience not books, cheaper
    - Apothecaries: mixed herbal remedies, disliked by physicians (cheaper, sometimes gave poison)

- **Approaches to prevention:**
  - **Religious approach:** lead a sin-free life; prayer, confessions, tithes
  - **Lifestyle — Regimen Sanitatis:** hygiene and diet advice from physicians; keep clean (linked to miasma), bathing (stewes = public baths, free); moderation in diet, regular purging
  - **Purifying the air:** carrying sweet herbs (lavender) or posy of flowers; local government tried to keep towns clean (no rotting animals left out)

## 4. Content reference pack

### Dates & timeline
- c460–370 BC — Hippocrates (Ancient Greece): created Four Humours theory
- c129–216 AD — Galen (Ancient Rome): built on Hippocrates; Theory of Opposites; dissected animals (not humans)
- c410–1066 — Dark Ages: much classical knowledge lost in Western Europe
- c1250 — Edexcel spec start date for medieval medicine
- 1440 — Printing press invented (late medieval; Church still controls knowledge at this point)
- 1348 — Black Death reaches England (EXCLUDED — Episode 2 scope)

### Key people
- **Hippocrates (Ancient Greece, c460–370 BC):** Created the Four Humours theory; encouraged observation and note-taking; the Hippocratic Oath originates here
- **Galen (Ancient Rome, c129–216 AD):** Built on Hippocrates; added Theory of Opposites; believed in the soul (made him compatible with Church doctrine); dissected animals → around 300 errors in his descriptions of human anatomy that persisted until Vesalius; his ideas dominated medieval medicine entirely
- **The Church (institutional "individual"):** Preserved and promoted Galen's ideas through monasteries and universities; controlled all books and education; made dissection largely illegal; ran hospitals

### Key terms & definitions
- **Four Humours:** blood, phlegm, black bile, yellow bile — must be balanced in the body for health
- **Theory of Opposites (Galen):** treat an excess humour by applying its opposite quality
- **Miasma (miasmata):** bad air from swamps, corpses, rotting matter; believed to cause disease
- **Astrology:** influence of stars and planets on health and disease
- **Blood-letting (phlebotomy):** removal of blood to rebalance humours; methods: cupping, leeches, cutting a vein
- **Purging:** causing vomiting or bowel emptying to remove excess humour; emetics or laxatives
- **Theriaca:** spice-based herbal remedy containing many ingredients; used for many illnesses
- **Regimen Sanitatis:** physician's advice document on hygiene, diet and lifestyle
- **Stewes:** public baths (free, in medieval towns)
- **Incantations:** spells or charms used as supernatural treatment
- **Endowment:** legacy/donation from wealthy person funding a hospital
- **Physician:** university-trained medic who diagnosed but did not treat
- **Barber surgeon:** performed minor operations (bloodletting); knowledge from experience, not books
- **Apothecary:** mixed and sold herbal remedies; cheaper than physicians

### Case studies / named examples
- **Humour imbalance diagnosis by urine:** physicians examined colour, smell and taste of urine — standard diagnostic process
- **Theriaca in action:** spice-based multi-ingredient remedy prescribed by apothecaries for multiple conditions — illustrates the speculative, recipe-book approach to treatment
- **The Regimen Sanitatis in action:** physicians advised bathing in public stewes, regular purging, diet moderation — all tied to the humour system
- **Charles II (1685) still receiving humoural treatment:** EXCLUDED — Renaissance period, Episode 3 scope — but note it as continuity evidence that can be cited in an Episode 1 "change and continuity" answer

### Causes & effects
- Church controls education and books → Galen's texts are the only ones widely taught → alternative ideas cannot reach most physicians
- Church declares dissection largely illegal → Galen's errors in human anatomy cannot be detected → Four Humours theory remains unchallenged
- Humours theory logically links to astrology and miasma → all three "causes" reinforce each other → the system appears coherent and correct
- Physicians paid by patients who want Galen treatments → economic pressure prevents experimentation → orthodoxy persists even if individual physicians have doubts
- Monasteries run hospitals AND control books → the Church's institutional grip spans treatment AND knowledge simultaneously

### Exam angles
- **"Explain why Galen had such a big influence on medieval medicine" (12 marks):** Model answer uses Three Reasons (Church, book learning, no alternatives) — each should be developed with specific evidence, not just named. The mark scheme rewards demonstrating *why* each reason meant no challenge was possible, not just stating it.
- **"How far do you agree that religion was the main factor preventing medical progress in medieval England?" (16 marks + SPaG):** Religion is a strong answer, but the best Level 4 responses will also consider the Individuals factor (Galen/Hippocrates' intellectual authority independent of religion) and the Technology factor (no printing press, no microscope). A balanced conclusion that explains why religion was *necessary but not sufficient* on its own reaches the top band.
- **Common exam misconceptions (suitable for `MisconceptionCheck`):**
  - "Medieval people were stupid to believe in the Four Humours" — FALSE: the theory was internally coherent and supported by all authoritative institutions
  - "The Church prevented all medical progress" — PARTLY FALSE: the Church ran hospitals and cared for the sick; what it prevented was *theoretical challenge* to Galen, not all practical medicine
  - "Miasma was a completely wrong belief with no useful effects" — PARTLY FALSE: miasma-avoidance behaviours (avoid rotting matter, keep towns clean) had *some* real disease-prevention effects even if the theory was wrong
  - "Physicians treated patients in medieval times" — FALSE: physicians diagnosed, barber surgeons and apothecaries performed treatments
