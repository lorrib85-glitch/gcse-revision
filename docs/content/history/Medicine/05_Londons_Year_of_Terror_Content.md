## 2. Storyline

### Core takeaway
DRAFT (for user confirmation): The Great Plague of 1665 proves that three hundred years of Renaissance thinking had changed almost nothing about how ordinary people understood epidemic disease. Despite Vesalius and Harvey, people still blamed miasma, planetary alignment, and God's punishment — and responded with the same prayers, bonfires and herbs as their ancestors in 1348. The beliefs were almost identical. The government response, however, was more organised and more actively enforced than in 1348: watchmen guarded quarantined houses, searchers inspected the sick, crowds were banned, and streets were cleaned. The difference is not in medical understanding but in administration.

*Derived from: source material shows 1665 responses mirror 1348 responses with slightly more systematic government organisation.*

### Evidence for the takeaway
- Beliefs: people blamed God, unusual planetary alignments (October/November 1664), and miasma from rubbish and sewage
- Treatments: physicians suggested wrapping up in woollen clothes/sitting by fire (sweating out disease), transference (strapping live chicken to buboes), herbal remedies, quack remedies
- Prevention: carrying pomanders (sweet-smelling herbs), lighting fires, praying, repenting sins
- Government orders: quarantine laws, large crowds banned, stray animals killed, searchers appointed, streets cleaned
- Plague doctors: wore special beaked masks; cloak coated in wax against pus/blood
- The government organised more systematically than in 1348 — but beliefs were essentially identical to those used 300 years earlier

### Series throughline
- **Agent: Government (🏛️):** The 1665 government response is more organised than 1348 — this is the one meaningful change. Shows government gradually taking a more active role in public health (connects forward powerfully to Episode 8, the Great Stink and the 1875 Public Health Act).
- **Agent: Religion (⛪):** Still present and still dominant in explaining epidemic disease — even 300 years after the Black Death.
- **Interleaving from Episodes 1–2:** Every response to the Great Plague mirrors a response to the Black Death. The key exam skill is identifying continuity *and* limited change.
- **Forward connection to Episode 8:** Government action on public health — quarantine in 1348, quarantine + bonfires + searchers in 1665, Bazalgette's sewer 1858, Public Health Acts — a long chain of government becoming progressively more effective.

### Exam framing
- **"How similar were reactions to the Black Death (1348) and the Great Plague (1665)?" — source-comparison question:** The Core takeaway directly provides the exam answer — overwhelmingly similar (same beliefs) with slightly more organised government response as the key difference.
- This is the highest-frequency source-comparison question in the Medicine paper; learners who can articulate this similarity confidently will score very highly.

## 3. Specification requirements

- **Context:**
  - Great Plague broke out across England in 1665
  - Disease spread by fleas on rats — same as Black Death (though not understood at the time)
  - People as helpless to stop it as during the Black Death
  - London particularly badly affected

- **Beliefs about causes (1665):**
  - Religious/supernatural: God sent the plague to punish mankind; sinners were being judged
  - Astrological: unusual planetary alignments in October and November 1664 blamed
  - Miasma: most popular explanation — bad air from rubbish and sewage in cities; foul fumes trapped in soil released during warmer weather (plague worse in summer)
  - Some people correctly identified person-to-person spread but couldn't explain the mechanism

- **Treatments attempted:**
  - Physicians suggested wrapping up in thick woollen clothes and sitting by a fire to sweat out disease
  - Transference: strapping live chicken to the buboes
  - Herbal remedies continued to be popular
  - Quack doctors: people with no qualifications who exploited public panic for money

- **Prevention attempts:**
  - Religious/supernatural: people advised to pray and repent their sins
  - Purifying the air: carrying a pomander (ball of sweet-smelling herbs); bonfires lit
  - Plague doctors: wore special beaked masks (herbs stuffed in beak to filter bad air); wax-coated cloaks
  - Diet advice: fasting and specific foods (garlic-heavy diet) suggested
  - Running away: people simply fled — spread the plague geographically

- **Government orders (more systematic than 1348):**
  - Quarantine laws enforced more systematically
  - Large crowds banned
  - Stray animals killed (animals blamed as carriers of miasma)
  - Searchers appointed: people (often women) employed to inspect houses for plague victims
  - Streets cleaned
  - Red crosses painted on the doors of infected houses; the words "Lord have mercy upon us" written alongside
  - **Watchmen:** placed outside infected houses to stop families breaking quarantine; guarded houses marked with the plague cross; this shows government action was more organised than during the Black Death

- **Comparison with Black Death (1348) — key exam content:**
  - **Similarities:** Both blamed on God and miasma; both used prayer and religious responses; both used purifying the air; both involved quarantine (albeit better enforced in 1665); both were caused by the same actual mechanism (fleas on rats) but this was unknown
  - **Differences:** 1665 government response more systematic (searchers, stray animals killed, large crowds banned, more organised quarantine); 1665 shows transference and quack doctors more prominently; 1665 plague doctors more organised (beaked masks, wax coats)

## 4. Content reference pack

### Dates & timeline
- October/November 1664 — unusual planetary alignments blamed as cause of forthcoming plague
- 1665 — Great Plague of London; spread across England; government issues detailed public health orders

### Key people
- **No named individual medical heroes:** Same as the Black Death — the Great Plague is a story of institutional response, not individual breakthroughs
- **Plague doctors (unnamed):** A recognised professional category; beaked masks, wax-coated cloaks; appear as a visual icon of the episode
- **Quack doctors:** Exploited public panic with useless remedies; no qualifications
- **Searchers:** Women employed by local authorities to inspect houses for plague victims

### Key terms & definitions
- **Pomander:** a ball of sweet-smelling herbs; carried or worn to ward off miasma; also stuffed into the beak of a plague doctor's mask
- **Searchers:** women employed to inspect houses for plague victims during the 1665 Great Plague
- **Quarantine:** isolation of plague victims; laws existed in both 1348 and 1665; more systematically enforced in 1665
- **Quack doctors:** people without medical qualifications who sold useless remedies during public health crises
- **Red cross:** mark painted on the door of a house containing plague victims; accompanied by the words "Lord have mercy upon us"; guarded by watchmen to enforce quarantine
- **Watchmen:** guards placed outside quarantined houses during the Great Plague; prevented infected families from leaving; a more active enforcement mechanism than anything used during the Black Death

### Case studies / named examples
- **Great Plague of 1665 vs. Black Death of 1348:** The primary case study of this episode is the *comparison* of the two epidemics — beliefs, treatments, prevention, government response. This is the most-tested exam scenario for this period.
- **Plague doctors:** Visual icon of the 1665 plague; their beaked masks are a direct consequence of miasma belief — herbs in the beak to filter bad air. The costume is based on a wrong belief.
- **Government orders 1665:** The list of government actions (watchmen outside quarantined houses, searchers, crowd bans, street cleaning, stray animals killed) shows more sophisticated public health administration than in 1348 — but still based on wrong understanding of cause.

### Causes & effects
- Miasma belief → bonfires and pomanders as prevention → no effect on actual transmission (fleas/rats)
- Miasma belief → people fled cities to escape bad air → geographically spread the plague
- Government quarantine orders → more systematic enforcement than 1348 (watchmen outside houses, searchers, crowd bans, animal killing, street cleaning) → some marginal reduction in spread but plague still devastating
- Quack doctors exploit panic → sell useless remedies → public harm compounded by false hope

### Exam angles
- **"How similar were people's reactions to the Black Death (1348) and the Great Plague (1665)?" — source-comparison question:** This is the signature exam question for Episodes 2 and 5. Pattern: state similarity (same belief: miasma/God/astrology) + name specific evidence from both dates + then state difference (1665 government more organised) + judgement ("Overall very similar because beliefs were unchanged").
- **"Describe two features of people's response to the Great Plague" (2 × 2 marks):** Feature + supporting detail. E.g. "People used pomanders (1) — balls of sweet-smelling herbs carried to ward off miasma (1)."
- **Misconceptions for `MisconceptionCheck`:**
  - "The Great Plague of 1665 and the Black Death of 1348 had very different responses" — FALSE: responses were overwhelmingly similar; same beliefs, same treatments
  - "Plague doctors wore beaked masks because they understood infection control" — FALSE: they wore them to filter miasma (bad air); it was based on a wrong belief
  - "The government did nothing about the Great Plague" — FALSE: the government issued detailed orders (quarantine, crowd bans, searchers) — but based on wrong understanding of the cause
