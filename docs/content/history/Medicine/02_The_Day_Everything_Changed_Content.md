## 2. Storyline

### Core takeaway
DRAFT (for user confirmation): The Black Death was the ultimate test of medieval medicine — and medieval medicine failed completely. People applied the same beliefs about God, miasma, and the Four Humours that they always had. They were wrong about every single cause, and every treatment they tried was useless. But their failure wasn't ignorance: it was the inevitable result of a belief system that had no mechanism for self-correction. The Black Death didn't change medieval medicine — and that's the real story.

*Derived from: every cause belief and treatment in source material is shown to have failed; medicine remained unchanged after the Black Death until Renaissance period.*

### Evidence for the takeaway
- One-third of England's population died — greatest catastrophe in medieval history — yet medical explanations and treatments remained unchanged
- Religious explanation → prayer and confession → did not prevent or cure the plague
- Miasma explanation → carrying sweet herbs, burning bonfires → no effect (real cause was flea-borne)
- Astrological explanation (planetary alignment 1345) → no actionable treatment possible
- Humoural treatments → purging and bleeding → did not work
- Government quarantine laws → Church ran as normal, rich moved freely → quarantine failed
- Running away from cities (to escape bad air) → actually spread the plague to rural areas

### Series throughline
- **Agent: Religion (⛪) — dominant and failing:** People prayed harder and flagellants whipped themselves; plague swept on regardless. First major crack in credibility of the religious framework — but people didn't abandon religion.
- **Agent: Government (🏛️) — present but impotent:** First appearance of government attempting public health action; connects forward to Episode 8 (Great Stink/Public Health) where government action becomes effective.
- **Interleaving from Episode 1:** Miasma, Four Humours, astrology, religious causation all established in Ep 1 and tested here — learners apply Ep 1 knowledge to crisis scenario.
- **Forward connection Episodes 3–5:** Black Death did *not* immediately change medieval medicine. Change comes later via Reformation (declining Church power) and printing press — the key counterintuitive point.

## 3. Specification requirements

- **Arrival and scale:**
  - Reached England 1348; spread by fleas on rats (real cause)
  - Main symptom: large, painful buboes (swollen lymph nodes)
  - Most victims died within days
  - Approximately one-third of England's population died

- **Beliefs about causes (medieval framework applied):**
  - Religious/supernatural: God's punishment for sin; unusual planetary positioning in 1345
  - Miasma: bad air from rubbish and sewage; foul fumes from soil released in summer heat; spread by breathing impure air
  - Some people correctly identified person-to-person spread but couldn't explain the mechanism
  - Standard humoral framework applied alongside these

- **Treatments attempted:**
  - Religious/supernatural: prayer, confession, flagellation (self-whipping), purification from priest
  - Humoural: purging and bleeding (ineffective); occasionally lancing buboes
  - Purifying the air: bonfires, sweet-smelling herbs
  - Herbal remedies: used but ineffective
  - Transference: strapping live chicken to the buboes
  - Quack remedies appeared to exploit public panic

- **Prevention attempts:**
  - Religious: prayer, pilgrimages, flagellation (to show repentance)
  - Purifying the air: carrying sweet herbs/posies; running away from cities (paradoxically spread plague)
  - Government quarantine laws: isolation of plague victims ordered; poorly enforced — rich people moved freely, Church ran as normal, local authorities had little power

- **Real cause (what we now know):**
  - Bubonic plague caused by Yersinia pestis bacterium
  - Carried by fleas living on black rats; flea bites transmitted bacteria to humans
  - Symptoms: buboes (armpits/groin/neck), fever, vomiting, bleeding under skin, death within days for most
  - Not identified until 1894 (Kitasato Shibasaburō and Alexandre Yersin independently)

## 4. Content reference pack

### Dates & timeline
- 1345 — Unusual planetary alignment; blamed as omen causing the Black Death
- 1348 — Black Death reaches England; approximately one-third of England's population dies
- 1349 — Peak/continuing impact; some communities completely wiped out
- 1894 — Real cause identified: Yersinia pestis, flea/rat transmission (Kitasato and Yersin)

### Key people
- **No named key individuals dominate this episode** — this is exam-significant: no individual could make a difference because the cause was unknown. Story is one of collective failure.
- **The Church (institutional):** organised religious response; continued running normally during quarantine; authority unchallenged even as medical framework failed
- **Government (institutional):** issued quarantine laws; unable to enforce them

### Key terms & definitions
- **Buboes:** large, painful swellings of the lymph nodes; defining symptom of bubonic plague; appear in armpits, groin, neck
- **Flagellants:** people who whipped themselves publicly to atone for sin and seek God's mercy during the Black Death
- **Quarantine:** isolation of plague victims to prevent spread; laws existed but poorly enforced
- **Transference:** belief that disease could be transferred to another living thing (e.g. strapping live chicken to bubo)
- **Yersinia pestis:** bacterium responsible for bubonic plague; not identified until 1894
- **Bubonic plague:** plague caused by Yersinia pestis, spread by flea bites from infected rats

### Case studies / named examples
- **Scale — one-third of England:** Most important fact for dramatic stakes; historians estimate 30–50% of England's population died 1348–49
- **Flagellation movement:** Groups of flagellants walked between towns publicly whipping themselves; shows doubling-down on religious causation when plague defied all medical understanding
- **Government quarantine failure:** Quarantine laws passed; rich ignored them; Church ran normally; shows limits of 14th-century government on public health — stark contrast with Episode 8 action
- **Lancing buboes:** Surgeons occasionally pierced buboes; occasional survivors — coincidental but noted as "treatment that might work"

### Causes & effects
- Flea bites from infected rats → Yersinia pestis enters bloodstream → buboes develop → fever, vomiting, bleeding → death in days
- Belief: plague = God's punishment → response: prayer and flagellation → no effect on plague transmission
- Belief: plague = miasma → response: sweet herbs, bonfires, running away → running away actually spread plague geographically
- Government issues quarantine laws → Church runs normally + rich ignore → enforcement fails → plague spreads unchecked
- One-third of England dies → labour shortages → eventual social/economic changes (outside episode scope)

Key contrast: 1665 shows more government enforcement (pest houses, searchers, large crowds banned, streets cleaned). Core similarity: beliefs essentially unchanged.
- **Misconceptions for `MisconceptionCheck`:**

  - "The Black Death changed medieval medicine immediately" — FALSE: medicine remained essentially unchanged; changes came 150+ years later
  - "Quarantine was only used in 1665" — FALSE: quarantine laws existed in 1348 but were poorly enforced
  - "Flagellants were considered extremists in medieval society" — UNCERTAIN: flagellation was within the mainstream of religious response; it reflected normal beliefs taken to an extreme
