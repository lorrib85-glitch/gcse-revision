## 2. Storyline

### Core takeaway
DRAFT (for user confirmation): Germ theory was the single most important discovery in the history of medicine — it provided the missing explanation for why disease exists at all, and unlocked every subsequent breakthrough from antiseptics to antibiotics to vaccines. But it didn't change medicine overnight. Pasteur published in 1861 and most doctors took little notice. It took Koch's more practical work — identifying the specific bacteria causing specific diseases — to turn a theory into a tool that medicine could actually use. Together, Pasteur and Koch gave medicine its first map of the invisible world.

### Evidence for the takeaway
- 1861: Pasteur published germ theory — proved spontaneous generation wrong; showed something in the air causes decay/disease
- Initial limited impact: Pasteur was not a doctor; couldn't identify specific disease-causing germs; spontaneous generation still promoted by influential doctors; bacteria found in healthy people too (seemed illogical that they caused disease)
- Koch: identified specific bacteria causing anthrax (1876), tuberculosis (1882), cholera (1883)
- Koch's method (growing bacteria in jelly, colouring with dye, photographing under microscope) enabled other scientists to study bacteria
- Koch had government funding (German government) — a key enabling factor
- Together Pasteur + Koch = the foundation for antiseptics (Lister, Ep 9), magic bullets (Ep 11), antibiotics (Ep 11), and vaccines (Pasteur extended Jenner)

## 3. Specification requirements

- **Spontaneous generation (alternative theory to germ theory):**
  - Developed early 1700s: said microbes were created by decaying matter (e.g. rotting animals/food)
  - Logical to people who could see microbes through microscopes
  - In fact microbes cause decay, not the other way round
  - Still promoted by influential doctors even after Pasteur's 1861 publication

- **Louis Pasteur (1822–1895) and germ theory:**
  - French scientist (not a doctor); his work mainly looked at decay and spoiled food
  - Published germ theory 1861: proved spontaneous generation was wrong; proved something in the air causes decay; if germs cause decay, they might also cause disease
  - Initial limited impact on medicine:
    - Spontaneous generation still promoted by influential doctors
    - Pasteur not a doctor; work focused on food decay not medical disease
    - Bacteria found all over the body, even in healthy people → seemed illogical they caused disease
    - Pasteur hadn't identified specific germs causing specific diseases → no practical use in treatment
  - Later work: developed vaccines for chicken cholera, anthrax, rabies (1870s); published germ theory of infection (1878) — built on Jenner
  - Inspired Emil von Behring to develop vaccines for tetanus and diphtheria (1890s)
  - Inspired Lister to develop antiseptic surgery (Episode 9)

- **Robert Koch (1843–1910):**
  - German scientist; first to identify different microbes that caused specific diseases
  - Discovered bacteria causing:
    - Anthrax (1876)
    - Tuberculosis (1882)
    - Cholera (1883)
  - Koch's method: growing bacteria in solid jelly (culture medium), colouring with purple dye, photographing under microscope → enabled other scientists to identify bacteria systematically
  - Key advantage: received funding from the German government → resources and credibility
  - His method was used by other scientists worldwide to identify further disease-causing bacteria

- **Miasma's decline:**
  - Despite germ theory, many still believed miasma until late 1800s
  - London's sewage in Thames → Great Stink 1858 → seemed to fit miasma theory (hot summer = miasma released)
  - Miasma belief persisted alongside germ theory among the general public

- **Key distinction between Pasteur and Koch:**
  - Pasteur: proved germs cause disease (in general); French; worked on food/fermentation; also developed vaccines
  - Koch: identified specific bacteria for specific diseases; German; worked on bacteria classification; government-funded; created the scientific method for bacteriology
  - Both contributed; Koch's practical method = more immediately useful for medicine

## 4. Content reference pack

### Dates & timeline
- Early 1700s — Spontaneous generation theory developed
- 1858 — Great Stink of London (miasma still believed despite early germ theory)
- 1861 — Pasteur publishes germ theory (germs cause decay → might cause disease)
- 1865 — Lister uses carbolic acid as antiseptic (directly inspired by germ theory — Episode 9 scope)
- 1876 — Koch identifies bacteria causing anthrax
- 1878 — Pasteur publishes germ theory of infection
- 1882 — Koch identifies tuberculosis bacterium
- 1883 — Koch identifies cholera bacterium
- 1890s — Emil von Behring develops vaccines for tetanus and diphtheria

### Key people
- **Louis Pasteur (1822–1895):** French scientist; published germ theory 1861; proved spontaneous generation wrong; developed vaccines for chicken cholera, anthrax, rabies; published germ theory of infection 1878; inspired Lister, Behring
- **Robert Koch (1843–1910):** German scientist; identified specific disease-causing bacteria (anthrax 1876, TB 1882, cholera 1883); created bacteriological method (jelly culture, dye, photography); government-funded; method used worldwide
- **Emil von Behring (1854–1917):** Inspired by Pasteur; developed vaccines for tetanus and diphtheria using germ theory framework
- **Antony von Leeuwenhoek (1632–1723):** Episode 3 scope; first to observe "animalcules" (bacteria) — foundational to what Pasteur later built on

### Key terms & definitions
- **Germ theory:** the theory that microorganisms (germs/bacteria) cause disease; published by Pasteur 1861
- **Spontaneous generation:** incorrect theory that microbes are created by decaying matter; disproved by Pasteur
- **Bacteria:** microscopic single-celled organisms; identified by Koch as causing specific diseases
- **Culture medium:** solid jelly used by Koch to grow bacteria in laboratory conditions
- **Bacteriology:** the study of bacteria; Koch's method established it as a scientific discipline
- **Miasma:** belief that bad air caused disease; persisted alongside germ theory even into the 1880s
- **Anthrax:** bacterial disease caused by *Bacillus anthracis*; first disease Koch identified the bacterium for (1876)
- **Tuberculosis (TB):** bacterial disease caused by *Mycobacterium tuberculosis*; identified by Koch (1882)

### Causes & effects
- Pasteur notices germs cause decay → hypothesises they cause disease → publishes germ theory 1861
- Germ theory published 1861 → spontaneous generation still promoted by influential doctors → limited immediate impact
- Koch receives German government funding → resources to identify specific bacteria → anthrax (1876), TB (1882), cholera (1883)
- Koch develops jelly/dye/photography method → other scientists can use same approach → bacteriology becomes a global scientific method
- Germ theory provides explanation for why Jenner's vaccine worked (Ep 6) → Pasteur develops new vaccines → Behring develops tetanus/diphtheria vaccines
- Germ theory provides mechanism for Lister's antiseptics (Ep 9): if germs cause infection, kill germs during surgery
