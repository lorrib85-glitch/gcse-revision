## 2. Storyline

### Core takeaway
DRAFT (for user confirmation): The Supreme Court's ruling in *Brown v Board of Education* (1954) proved that the law alone could not end segregation — what followed at Little Rock was a lesson in how fiercely white Southerners would resist integration, and how the federal government could be forced to act when the eyes of the world were watching.

*Drawn from recurring pattern: every episode in KT1 follows a structure of protest/legal challenge → Southern resistance → federal intervention (however reluctant); Little Rock is the clearest early example of this cycle.*

### Evidence for the takeaway
- *Brown v Board* (1954): unanimous Supreme Court ruling that segregated schools were unconstitutional, overturning *Plessy v Ferguson*; Thurgood Marshall argued the case for the NAACP
- *Brown II* (1955): desegregation should happen "with all deliberate speed" — vague wording that allowed Southern states to delay
- By 1957, 723 school districts had desegregated, especially in border states and outside the Deep South — showing some progress, but not enough to break Deep South resistance
- Despite the ruling, Southern states used massive resistance — White Citizens' Councils, the Southern Manifesto, school closures, harassment and threats against Black families
- At Little Rock, around 75 Black students applied, 25 were accepted, but intimidation meant only 9 remained willing to attend
- Arkansas Governor Orval Faubus deployed 250 state National Guard troops to prevent the Little Rock Nine entering Central High School on 4 September 1957
- Elizabeth Eckford faced the mob alone — she had not received word to arrive with the group; photographs of her being jeered became internationally published
- The situation was an international embarrassment during the Cold War (the USSR broadcast photographs as anti-American propaganda)
- Eisenhower (reluctant on civil rights personally) sent 1,000 federal troops from the 101st Airborne Division on 24 September 1957; the Nine finally entered the school
- Faubus's response: closed all four Little Rock high schools for the entire 1958–59 academic year rather than integrate; they re-opened under court order
- Long-term impact: Central High School was not fully integrated until the 1970s; Southern resistance to *Brown* continued for years

### Series through-line
This episode continues the through-line of "one nation fighting two battles at once." The international humiliation at Little Rock (Cold War context) is the first clear moment where the US government was forced to act on civil rights partly for foreign policy reasons — the same tension that will recur when Black Americans question why they should fight in Vietnam. Episode 1 established the legal system; Episode 2 shows the first major crack in it — and the fierce resistance to that crack.

### Exam framing
- Q2 "Explain why the federal government became involved in the Little Rock crisis" — three causes: international embarrassment/Cold War, court orders requiring compliance, Faubus's open defiance of federal law
- Q2 "Explain why *Brown v Board* was significant" — the Supreme Court ruling, the end of Plessy's legal cover, the progress in some districts, and the trigger it gave for Southern resistance
- Q3(d) context: interpretations on whether Little Rock shows progress (federal enforcement, 723 districts desegregated by 1957, international attention) or limits (schools re-closed 1958, intimidation, integration took decades)
- Misconceptions worth a MisconceptionCheck:
  - "Brown v Board immediately desegregated American schools" — Southern states resisted for years; integration was slow and contested
  - "Eisenhower was a strong supporter of civil rights" — he personally opposed mixing the races and only intervened because Faubus defied federal authority
  - "Little Rock ended school segregation in the South" — most Southern schools remained segregated well into the 1960s and 1970s
  - "Brown only had positive effects" — it was a major legal breakthrough, but it also triggered violent backlash, school closures, white flight and job losses for some Black teachers

## 3. Specification requirements

### Key Topic 1 — Brown v Board and Little Rock

- **Brown v Board of Education (1954):** Unanimous Supreme Court ruling that separate educational facilities were inherently unequal and unconstitutional; overturned *Plessy v Ferguson* (1896); argued by Thurgood Marshall (NAACP); in practice Southern states defied the ruling
- **Brown II (1955):** Supreme Court ruled desegregation should proceed "with all deliberate speed". This sounded like progress but gave Southern states room to delay because no clear deadline was set
- **Significance and effects of Brown:**
  - Reversed the legal precedent of *Plessy v Ferguson* and made segregated education unconstitutional
  - Gave the NAACP and civil rights lawyers a legal platform for further desegregation cases
  - Increased awareness of civil rights issues across the USA
  - By 1957, 723 school districts had desegregated, mostly outside the Deep South
  - Triggered white backlash: White Citizens' Councils, KKK growth, Southern Manifesto, intimidation of families, school boards claiming they were preparing integration while doing nothing
  - Some good Black schools were closed; some Black teachers lost jobs; Black children entering white schools faced threats, isolation and violence
  - Some white families moved away from areas where Black families lived to avoid integration, creating new patterns of segregation
- **Massive resistance:** Southern states' strategy of defying *Brown* — Southern Manifesto, White Citizens' Councils, legal delays, school closures, economic pressure and violence to prevent integration
- **The Little Rock Nine, 1957:**
  - Around 75 Black students initially applied to attend Central High School, Little Rock; 25 were accepted by the school board; intimidation and threats meant only 9 remained prepared to attend
  - Governor Orval Faubus sent 250 Arkansas National Guard troops to block their entry on 4 September 1957 — claimed he was preventing violence but was defying the federal court order
  - Elizabeth Eckford arrived alone (had not received message to join the group); was jeered and spat on by a white mob; photographs published internationally
  - Federal district court ordered the National Guard withdrawn; Faubus complied but a mob prevented entry on 23 September
  - President Eisenhower federalised the Arkansas National Guard and sent 1,000 troops from the 101st Airborne Division; the Nine entered school on 24 September 1957 under military escort
  - The Nine faced daily harassment and isolation inside the school
  - Ernest Green became the first Black student to graduate from Central High School (1958); white students refused to sit with him at the graduation ceremony, showing how limited social acceptance remained
  - Faubus closed all four Little Rock high schools August 1958–May 1959 to prevent integration ("The Lost Year")
  - The Supreme Court ruled school closures unconstitutional; schools re-opened but slowly integrated
- **Significance of Little Rock:**
  - Demonstrated that federal force could be used to enforce civil rights — a precedent for future interventions
  - International Cold War significance — USSR used images for propaganda; Eisenhower acted partly to preserve US image abroad
  - Showed limits of the Supreme Court ruling without enforcement mechanisms
  - Increased urgency of civil rights organisations to pursue legislation alongside legal challenges
  - Showed that legal victory could produce backlash as well as progress
- **Civil Rights Act 1957 — check placement:** This early federal law belongs in the wider KT1 sequence if not covered elsewhere. It was the first federal civil rights law since Reconstruction; it created the Civil Rights Commission and allowed investigation of voting-rights abuses, but Southern opposition weakened it and its practical impact was limited.

## 4. Content reference pack

### Dates & timeline
- **1896:** *Plessy v Ferguson* — "separate but equal" established
- **17 May 1954:** *Brown v Board of Education* — Supreme Court ruled unanimously that segregated schools unconstitutional
- **May 1955:** *Brown II* — Supreme Court ordered desegregation to proceed "with all deliberate speed" (vague, left to local courts)
- **1956:** Southern Manifesto — 101 Southern Congressmen signed declaration opposing *Brown*; massive resistance organised
- **1957:** 723 school districts had desegregated, mainly outside the Deep South
- **September 1957:** Little Rock crisis — Faubus deploys National Guard; Elizabeth Eckford alone on 4 September; federal court orders withdrawal; Eisenhower sends 101st Airborne; Nine enter school 24 September
- **May 1958:** Ernest Green graduates — first Black student to complete Central High School; white students refused to sit with him at the ceremony
- **August 1958:** Faubus closes all four Little Rock high schools
- **1959:** Schools re-opened under court order

### Key people
- **Thurgood Marshall:** NAACP chief counsel; argued *Brown v Board* before the Supreme Court; later became first Black Supreme Court Justice (1967)
- **Earl Warren:** Chief Justice of the Supreme Court who delivered the unanimous *Brown* ruling
- **Orval Faubus:** Governor of Arkansas; deployed state National Guard to block integration; central figure of Southern resistance; re-elected six times on segregationist platform
- **Elizabeth Eckford:** One of the Little Rock Nine; arrived alone on 4 September 1957; photographs of her walking through the mob became iconic images of the civil rights struggle
- **Ernest Green:** First of the Little Rock Nine to graduate from Central High (1958); white students refused to sit with him at the graduation ceremony; one of the Nine's most prominent later careers (Assistant Secretary of Labor under Carter)
- **Daisy Bates:** President of Arkansas NAACP; mentored and coordinated the Little Rock Nine; key organiser throughout the crisis
- **President Dwight D. Eisenhower:** Reluctant civil rights supporter; acted at Little Rock primarily to uphold federal law and protect US international image rather than from personal commitment to integration

### Key terms & definitions
- **Brown v Board of Education (1954):** Supreme Court ruling that overturned *Plessy v Ferguson* and declared segregated schools unconstitutional
- **Brown II (1955):** Follow-up ruling saying schools should desegregate "with all deliberate speed"; vague wording allowed delay
- **Massive resistance:** Southern strategy of organised defiance of *Brown*, using legal delays, school closures, economic pressure and violence to prevent integration
- **White Citizens' Councils:** White segregationist organisations formed to resist desegregation; used intimidation, economic pressure and threats as well as political lobbying
- **The Little Rock Nine:** Nine Black students selected by the NAACP to integrate Central High School, Little Rock, Arkansas, in September 1957
- **Federalise:** When the federal government takes control of state National Guard units — used by Eisenhower to override Faubus and enforce the court order
- **101st Airborne Division:** Elite US Army paratroop division sent to Little Rock by Eisenhower; their presence allowed the Nine to enter Central High School
- **"The Lost Year" (1958–59):** Period when Faubus closed all Little Rock high schools rather than integrate; approximately 3,600 students had to find alternatives
- **Southern Manifesto (1956):** Declaration signed by 101 Southern members of Congress opposing *Brown* and pledging to resist integration — showed how mainstream massive resistance was
- **Cold War context:** US–Soviet rivalry; the USSR broadcast images of Little Rock internationally as evidence that American democracy was hypocritical; this partly motivated Eisenhower to act

### Case studies / named examples
- **Elizabeth Eckford at Little Rock (4 September 1957):** Most photographed and reported moment of the crisis. Arrived alone because she had no phone and could not be reached the night before; walked through a jeering mob; tried to board a city bus; was protected by a white journalist, Benjamin Fine. The photograph of her — composed and dignified while surrounded by hatred — became one of the defining images of the civil rights movement.
- **Ernest Green's graduation (1958):** Martin Luther King attended the graduation ceremony; Green was the sole Black student graduating that year. The event was completed under heavy security, and white students refused to sit with him, showing that formal progress did not mean genuine social acceptance.
- **"The Lost Year" (1958–59):** Faubus closed all four Little Rock high schools. White students attended private segregationist academies (funded partly by public money). Black students had almost no options. The Supreme Court ruled this unconstitutional in *Cooper v Aaron* (1958) — a landmark ruling asserting federal supremacy over state defiance.

### Causes & effects
- *Plessy v Ferguson* (1896) established "separate but equal" → legally entrenched segregated schools across the South for 58 years
- NAACP legal strategy → *Brown v Board* (1954) → unanimous ruling that segregated schools unconstitutional
- *Brown II* used "all deliberate speed" → no fixed deadline → Southern states delayed integration
- *Brown* ruling announced → Southern states organised massive resistance → 101 Congressmen signed Southern Manifesto 1956 → segregation continued despite the ruling
- *Brown* created progress in some areas → 723 school districts desegregated by 1957 → but Deep South resistance showed the ruling's limits
- White backlash after *Brown* → KKK/White Citizens' Councils grew, Black families intimidated, some Black schools closed, Black teachers lost jobs → progress came with costs
- Faubus deployed National Guard (September 1957) → international outrage and Soviet propaganda use → Cold War embarrassment → Eisenhower forced to act despite personal views
- Eisenhower sent 101st Airborne → Little Rock Nine entered Central High → precedent set that federal force would enforce court orders
- Ernest Green graduated in 1958 → symbolic progress → white students refusing to sit with him showed continuing hostility
- Faubus closed schools 1958 → Supreme Court ruled closures unconstitutional (*Cooper v Aaron*) → federal judicial authority confirmed → further precedent for enforcement

### Exam angles
- **Q2 (Explain why, 12 marks):** "Explain why President Eisenhower sent federal troops to Little Rock in 1957." Three causal pillars: Faubus defied federal court orders (constitutional duty); Cold War / international reputation (pragmatic); media coverage made inaction impossible (domestic political pressure). Own knowledge: 101st Airborne, Elizabeth Eckford, international media coverage.
- **Q2 variant:** "Explain why *Brown v Board* was important for the civil rights movement." NAACP's legal victory; overturned *Plessy*; showed Supreme Court could be used; created some measurable progress (723 districts desegregated by 1957); forced Southern states to take a position → triggered massive resistance which radicalised Black Americans.
- **Q3(d) context:** Interpretations on Little Rock — progress (first federal enforcement, international spotlight, 723 districts desegregated, Ernest Green's graduation) vs. limits (Faubus closed schools, Black teachers lost jobs, Black children faced violence, integration took decades, Eisenhower didn't personally believe in it).
- **Misconceptions worth a MisconceptionCheck:**
  - "The Supreme Court ruling immediately changed schools in the South" — *Brown II*'s "all deliberate speed" gave states cover to delay; most Southern schools remained segregated for years
  - "Eisenhower was a champion of civil rights" — he privately opposed mixing and only acted because federal authority was being openly defied and the world was watching
  - "Little Rock settled the school integration question" — Faubus's "Lost Year" immediately followed; full integration of Southern schools took until the 1970s in many places
  - "Brown had only positive consequences" — it was a crucial breakthrough, but also triggered violent backlash, closures, job losses and avoidance tactics
