## 2. Storyline

### Core takeaway
DRAFT (for user confirmation): By 1975, Black Americans had won legal equality — in law. But the distance between law and lived reality remained vast. Economic inequality, housing discrimination, police violence and political underrepresentation persisted. The movement had transformed America's legal framework without yet transforming its economic and social structures.

*Drawn from recurring pattern: every mark scheme in the series uses the "progress vs limits" framework for Q3(d) questions; this episode is the content base for those evaluation answers; the 2021 paper Q3(d) used interpretations directly assessing the position of Black Americans in this era.*

### Evidence for the takeaway

**Progress:**
- 1965 Voting Rights Act: abolished poll taxes, literacy tests; federal registrars sent to Southern states; Black voter registration in Mississippi rose from 6.7% (1964) to 59.8% (1967); by 1975 over 700 Black elected officials in the South (up from virtually none in 1964)
- 1968 Civil Rights Act: outlawed housing discrimination
- Affirmative action: Executive Order 11246 (1965, LBJ) required federal contractors to take affirmative action to employ minorities; opened university places and professional careers
- Economic progress: Black Americans entering middle class in larger numbers; some professionals and entrepreneurs; Maynard Jackson elected first Black mayor of Atlanta (1973)
- Cultural: Black pride, Black studies programmes, increased visibility in media and culture

**Limits:**
- Economic inequality persisted: median Black family income was 61% of white family income in 1975 (down slightly from 63% in 1968); poverty rate among Black Americans roughly three times that of white Americans
- Housing: despite 1968 Act, residential segregation continued through informal means (redlining, refusal of mortgages, white flight from integrating neighbourhoods)
- Education: school busing to achieve integration met fierce white resistance in Northern cities (Boston busing crisis 1974–75); many Northern schools re-segregated as white families moved to suburbs
- Political: 700 Black officials was progress but in a country of 20+ million Black Americans; Congress remained overwhelmingly white; Nixon's "Southern Strategy" courted white conservative voters at expense of civil rights enforcement
- Violence: MLK assassinated April 1968; Fred Hampton (Black Panthers) killed by police and FBI agents December 1969; COINTELPRO continued targeting Black organisations
- Vietnam: Black Americans disproportionately drafted and killed in Vietnam; Martin Luther King's 1967 speech ("Beyond Vietnam") linked the war to the civil rights struggle; Episode 11 develops this

### Series through-line
This episode is the pivot between KT2 and KT3/KT4. By 1975, the legal framework was in place but inequality persisted. The series through-line — one nation fighting two battles — is directly visible here: while Black Americans fought for equality at home, they were also disproportionately dying in Vietnam. Episode 11 (The War Comes Home) connects anti-war and civil rights protest explicitly. Episode 12 (The Long Way Out) will show that the nation that could not win in Vietnam also could not fully deliver on its promises at home.

### Exam framing
- Q3(d) 2021 paper: "How far do you agree with Interpretation A about the position of Black Americans in the 1950s?" — but the framework (progress vs limits) also applies to 1975 assessments
- Q2 "Explain why progress for Black Americans was limited by 1975" — three pillars: economic inequality (median income, poverty rates), housing discrimination (1968 Act not enforced effectively), Nixon's Southern Strategy (political retreat from civil rights)
- Misconceptions worth a MisconceptionCheck:
  - "The 1965 Voting Rights Act solved the voting rights problem" — it removed legal barriers but economic coercion, violence and political gerrymandering continued; enforcement was uneven
  - "By 1975, Black Americans had achieved equality" — legal equality yes; economic and social equality no; median family income still 61% of white income
  - "Affirmative action solved economic inequality" — it opened opportunities for some but didn't address structural poverty, housing segregation or educational inequality for the majority

## 3. Specification requirements

### Key Topic 2 — Progress by 1975

- **1965 Voting Rights Act:**
  - Followed Selma to Montgomery marches (Bloody Sunday, 7 March 1965; third march 21–24 March; 25,000 reached Montgomery)
  - Abolished literacy tests and poll taxes; federal registrars sent to counties where Black voter registration was below 50%
  - Black voter registration in Mississippi: 6.7% (1964) → 59.8% (1967); Alabama: 19.3% (1965) → 51.6% (1966)
  - By 1975: over 700 Black elected officials in the South; Black voter turnout rose substantially
  - Extensions: Voting Rights Act renewed and extended 1970, 1975

- **Selma to Montgomery marches (1965):**
  - Background: SCLC and SNCC voter registration campaign in Selma, Alabama; Sheriff Jim Clark's brutal response attracted attention
  - Bloody Sunday (7 March 1965): 600 marchers crossed Edmund Pettus Bridge; attacked by state troopers with clubs, tear gas and horses; national TV broadcast; public outrage
  - Second march (9 March): MLK led but turned back; court injunction
  - Third march (21–24 March): federal court authorised; 25,000 marchers reached Montgomery under National Guard protection; MLK's "How Long, Not Long" speech
  - LBJ addressed Congress ("We Shall Overcome") and proposed the Voting Rights Act; signed 6 August 1965

- **Civil Rights Act 1968 (Fair Housing Act):**
  - Passed in the week after MLK's assassination (4 April 1968) — his death provided the political momentum
  - Outlawed discrimination in the sale, rental and financing of housing
  - Weak enforcement mechanisms; housing segregation continued through informal means; limited practical impact in many cities

- **Economic progress and continuing inequality (by 1975):**
  - Affirmative action (Executive Order 11246, 1965): federal contractors required to take positive steps to employ minorities; opened opportunities in education and professional employment
  - Maynard Jackson elected first Black mayor of Atlanta, 1973 — an important symbolic and practical milestone
  - Middle-class growth: Black Americans entering professions, education and business in larger numbers
  - BUT: median Black family income remained 61% of white median in 1975; poverty rate among Black Americans approximately 30% vs 10% for white Americans; unemployment twice as high
  - Northern ghettos: continued poverty, overcrowding, high unemployment; the Kerner Report's recommendations (significant federal investment) were never implemented

- **Education after 1965:**
  - School busing: courts ordered busing to achieve racial integration in Northern cities (where residential segregation meant neighbourhood schools were effectively segregated)
  - White resistance to busing: Boston busing crisis (1974–75) — white parents violently opposed busing; schools became sites of racial tension; white flight to suburbs accelerated
  - Result: many Northern schools re-segregated as white families moved; the structural problem of residential segregation remained unsolved

- **Political backlash — Nixon's "Southern Strategy":**
  - Nixon (elected 1968, re-elected 1972) deliberately appealed to white Southern voters alienated by civil rights legislation — the "Southern Strategy"
  - Slowed enforcement of civil rights legislation; weakened EEOC; appealed to "law and order" (coded attack on Black Power and urban unrest)
  - Appointed more conservative federal judges; sought to limit the reach of the Voting Rights Act

- **Violence against Black leaders and organisations:**
  - MLK assassinated Memphis, 4 April 1968 — riots in 120 cities; 1968 Civil Rights Act passed within days
  - Fred Hampton (Black Panther Party Illinois chair) killed in pre-dawn police raid, December 1969 — FBI/COINTELPRO involvement confirmed
  - COINTELPRO dismantled after Senate Church Committee investigation (1975–76) but damage to Black organisations already done

## 4. Content reference pack

### Dates & timeline
- **7 March 1965:** Bloody Sunday — Selma marchers attacked on Edmund Pettus Bridge
- **21–24 March 1965:** Third Selma march; 25,000 reach Montgomery
- **6 August 1965:** Voting Rights Act signed by LBJ
- **August 1965:** Watts riots (see Episode 6)
- **1965:** Executive Order 11246 — affirmative action requirement for federal contractors
- **4 April 1968:** MLK assassinated in Memphis
- **11 April 1968:** Civil Rights Act (Fair Housing Act) signed — one week after MLK's assassination
- **1969:** Nixon's "Southern Strategy" begins reshaping federal approach to civil rights enforcement
- **December 1969:** Fred Hampton killed in FBI/police raid
- **1973:** Maynard Jackson elected first Black mayor of Atlanta
- **1974–75:** Boston busing crisis — white resistance to school integration in the North
- **By 1975:** 700+ Black elected officials in the South; Black voter registration transformed; but economic inequality persists

### Key people
- **Selma figures — Jim Clark:** Selma Sheriff; his brutal response to voter registration marchers attracted the national media attention that made Selma the trigger for the Voting Rights Act
- **LBJ:** Addressed Congress with "We Shall Overcome" (15 March 1965); signed the Voting Rights Act; "Great Society" programmes; but also escalated Vietnam, linking civil rights and the war in Black communities' minds
- **John Lewis (again):** Led the Bloody Sunday march; beaten by state troopers; his courage on 7 March 1965 was instrumental in generating public outrage; later became a US Congressman
- **Maynard Jackson (1936–2003):** First Black mayor of Atlanta (1973); used his position to increase Black employment in city contracts; a concrete example of what the Voting Rights Act made possible
- **Richard Nixon (1969–74):** "Southern Strategy" — deliberately appealed to white conservative voters; weakened civil rights enforcement; represents the political backlash against the movement's gains
- **Fred Hampton (1948–1969):** Chairman of the Illinois Black Panther Party; his community organising and coalition-building across racial lines made him a particular target; killed in a pre-dawn FBI/police raid at age 21

### Key terms & definitions
- **Selma to Montgomery marches (1965):** Three marches from Selma to Alabama's state capital; "Bloody Sunday" on 7 March was the catalyst for the Voting Rights Act
- **Voting Rights Act 1965:** Federal law abolishing literacy tests and poll taxes; provided federal oversight of elections in states with history of discrimination; transformed Black voter registration
- **Bloody Sunday:** 7 March 1965; 600 civil rights marchers attacked by state troopers with clubs and tear gas on the Edmund Pettus Bridge; broadcast nationally; became the catalyst for the Voting Rights Act
- **Affirmative action:** Policy requiring positive steps to increase the representation of minorities in education and employment; opened opportunities but contested and unevenly applied
- **Southern Strategy:** Nixon's political strategy of appealing to white Southern voters opposed to civil rights; represented a major political retreat from federal enforcement of civil rights
- **Fair Housing Act (Civil Rights Act 1968):** Outlawed discrimination in housing sales and rentals; weak enforcement meant housing segregation continued through informal means
- **"How Long, Not Long":** MLK's speech at the conclusion of the Selma to Montgomery march (25 March 1965); one of his most powerful speeches; expressed confidence in the inevitability of justice

### Case studies / named examples
- **Bloody Sunday (7 March 1965):** Cameras were present as 600 marchers crossed the Edmund Pettus Bridge. State troopers attacked with clubs, tear gas and horses. The footage was broadcast on national television, interrupting ABC's broadcast of *Judgment at Nuremberg*. The juxtaposition was not lost on viewers. LBJ addressed Congress eight days later.
- **Maynard Jackson, Atlanta (1973):** Jackson used the city's contracting power to ensure Black businesses received a proportion of city contracts. He increased Black employment in city government. Atlanta's economic transformation under Jackson showed what the Voting Rights Act's political gains could deliver — but also how limited those gains were outside cities with Black majorities.
- **Boston busing crisis (1974–75):** Court-ordered busing to integrate Boston's schools met violent white resistance — bottles, bricks and racial slurs directed at Black children on school buses. The crisis showed that Northern whites were as resistant to integration as Southern whites had been in the 1950s and 1960s — and that the legal framework could not overcome residential segregation without political will.

### Causes & effects
- Bloody Sunday (7 March 1965) → national outrage → LBJ's "We Shall Overcome" address → Voting Rights Act signed (6 August 1965) → Black voter registration transformed across the South
- 1965 Voting Rights Act → 700+ Black elected officials in the South by 1975; Maynard Jackson elected Atlanta mayor 1973
- MLK assassination (4 April 1968) → riots in 120 cities → Congress passed 1968 Fair Housing Act within a week as political memorial
- Nixon's "Southern Strategy" (1968 onwards) → weakened civil rights enforcement → EEOC underfunded → affirmative action implemented unevenly
- School busing ordered by courts → white flight to suburbs → residential segregation worsened → schools effectively re-segregated in many Northern cities
- Median Black family income (61% of white) + poverty rate (≈3×) → economic inequality continued despite legislative gains → the gap between law and lived reality remained wide by 1975

### Exam angles
- **Q3(d) (How far do you agree, 16 marks):** The 2021 paper Q3(d) asked students to evaluate interpretations about the position of Black Americans in the 1950s — but the same framework applies to a 1975 assessment question. Students need: "evidence in support" (Voting Rights Act, 700 Black officials, Jackson, affirmative action) + "evidence against" (economic inequality, housing segregation, Northern busing resistance, Nixon's Southern Strategy) + overall judgement.
- **Q2 (Explain why, 12 marks):** "Explain why progress for Black Americans was limited despite the legislation passed in the 1960s." Three pillars: economic inequality (median income, poverty rates unchanged), housing segregation (1968 Act unenforced), Northern resistance (busing crisis shows limits of legislation without social change). Own knowledge: Nixon's Southern Strategy, Kerner Report ignored, median income 61% of white, Boston busing crisis.
- **Misconceptions worth a MisconceptionCheck:**
  - "By 1975, Black Americans had achieved equality" — legal barriers removed but economic, housing and educational inequality persisted
  - "The Voting Rights Act fixed voting" — registration transformed but gerrymandering and other barriers emerged; enforcement weakened under Nixon
  - "Affirmative action benefited all Black Americans equally" — it opened professional opportunities for some but didn't address structural poverty for the majority
