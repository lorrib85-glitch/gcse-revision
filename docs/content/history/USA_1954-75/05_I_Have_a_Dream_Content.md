# Episode 5: I Have a Dream — Content

## 1. Identity

- **Episode number:** 5
- **Title:** I Have a Dream
- **Subtitle / GCSE topic:** The Birmingham campaign and the March on Washington, 1963 (Key Topic 2)
- **Era:** 1963–1964, with bridge to 1965 voting-rights legislation
- **Key Topic reference (KT2):** Protest, progress and radicalism, 1963–75: the March on Washington, the Civil Rights Act.

---

## 2. Storyline

### Core takeaway
DRAFT (for user confirmation): Birmingham 1963 and the March on Washington are the twin peaks of the non-violent civil rights movement. Bull Connor's fire hoses and police dogs — broadcast on television into every American home — finally forced the federal government to act legislatively. The march proved the movement's power and breadth. Together, they made the 1964 Civil Rights Act inevitable — but the Act did not solve voting rights, so the movement still had to fight on at Selma.

*Drawn from recurring pattern: the Birmingham campaign is the most frequently cited cause of the 1964 Civil Rights Act in mark schemes; the March on Washington is the movement's most iconic moment and cited as proof of its scale and national reach. The 1964 Act is a major breakthrough, but it must be taught as part of a legislative sequence: weak 1957 Act → stronger 1964 Act → voting-rights breakthrough in 1965.*

### Evidence for the takeaway
- Birmingham 1963: MLK and SCLC chose Birmingham deliberately — Bull Connor was reliably brutal and the cameras were rolling; Project C (Confrontation) designed to provoke a reaction
- Children's Crusade (May 1963): schoolchildren marched; Connor used fire hoses and police dogs; images broadcast nationwide and internationally; Kennedy shocked
- Kennedy addressed the nation on civil rights 11 June 1963 — the first time a president framed civil rights as a moral issue (not just a legal one)
- March on Washington (28 August 1963): 250,000 people; 40,000 white; largest civil rights demonstration in US history; MLK's "I Have a Dream" speech
- Medgar Evers (NAACP, Mississippi) assassinated 12 June 1963 — the day after Kennedy's speech
- Four girls killed in Birmingham church bombing (15 September 1963) — the Sixteenth Street Baptist Church
- Kennedy assassinated 22 November 1963 — Lyndon Johnson pledged to pass Kennedy's civil rights bill as a memorial
- Civil Rights Act signed 2 July 1964 — outlawed discrimination in public places and employment; gave federal government power to withhold funding from discriminating institutions
- The 1964 Act was much stronger than the 1957 Act, but it did not solve voting rights — that gap leads directly to Selma and the Voting Rights Act 1965 in Episode 7

### Series through-line
This episode is the moral and legislative climax of KT2's first phase. Birmingham and the March together created the political conditions for the 1964 Civil Rights Act. The episode also introduces the question of what the Act didn't do — it didn't touch voting rights, and the violence (church bombing, Medgar Evers, Kennedy assassination) shows that the movement was winning laws faster than it was changing hearts. Episode 6 (Malcolm X and Black Power) directly responds to this — the question of whether non-violence and legislation were enough. Episode 7 then picks up the unresolved voting-rights gap through Selma and the 1965 Voting Rights Act.

### Exam framing
- Q2 "Explain why the Civil Rights Act (1964) was passed" — three pillars: Birmingham campaign and television images (public opinion shift), March on Washington (demonstrated scale and unity), Kennedy assassination + LBJ's commitment (political opportunity)
- Q2 "Explain why the March on Washington was significant" — 250,000 people, 40,000 white (breadth), "I Have a Dream" speech (national moral statement), political pressure on Congress
- Q3/progress angle: the 1964 Act is strong evidence of progress but should be balanced against its limits — it did not address voting rights, housing, or wider economic inequality
- Misconceptions worth a MisconceptionCheck:
  - "The Civil Rights Act ended racism in America" — it outlawed discrimination in public places but did not address voting rights, housing, or economic inequality
  - "The 1964 Act gave Black Americans full voting rights" — voting rights required the 1965 Voting Rights Act after Selma
  - "MLK's speech was planned word for word" — the "I Have a Dream" section was improvised when gospel singer Mahalia Jackson called out "Tell them about the dream, Martin!"
  - "The March on Washington was only a Black protest" — 40,000 of the 250,000 were white; it was explicitly multi-racial

## 3. Specification requirements

### Key Topic 2 — Birmingham and the March on Washington

- **Birmingham, Alabama, 1963:**
  - Chosen deliberately by MLK and Fred Shuttlesworth (ACMHR) because Bull Connor was reliably brutal and because Birmingham was one of the most segregated cities in the US ("Bombingham" — 50+ Black churches and homes bombed 1945–65)
  - "Project C" (Confrontation): SCLC strategy of sustained marches, sit-ins and boycotts designed to fill the jails and force a violent response
  - Connor's response: fire hoses powerful enough to strip bark from trees; German shepherd police dogs set on protesters; images broadcast on television and newspapers nationally and internationally
  - Children's Crusade (2–5 May 1963): schoolchildren brought in when adult volunteers ran low; Connor used full force; Kennedy reportedly told an aide the images "made him sick"
  - Kennedy sends Burke Marshall to negotiate; Connor arrested over 3,000 people in 5 days
  - Agreement reached mid-May 1963: lunch counters desegregated; Black workers promoted in some businesses; but white businessmen slow to comply; Connor's response: further bombings (including of the Gaston Motel where MLK stayed)
  - 11 June 1963: Kennedy addresses the nation on television — frames civil rights as a moral issue for the first time; announces plans for a civil rights bill
  - 12 June 1963: Medgar Evers (NAACP field secretary, Mississippi) shot dead in his own driveway — less than an hour after Kennedy's speech

- **March on Washington (28 August 1963):**
  - Organised by A. Philip Randolph (had planned a march in 1941; cancelled after FDR signed executive order on employment discrimination) and Bayard Rustin (chief organiser)
  - 250,000 people gathered at the Lincoln Memorial — the largest civil rights demonstration in US history; estimates of up to 40,000 white participants
  - Speakers included all major civil rights leaders: MLK, John Lewis (SNCC — his speech toned down by organisers as too radical), A. Philip Randolph, Roy Wilkins (NAACP)
  - MLK's "I Have a Dream" speech: largely scripted but "I have a dream" passage improvised when Mahalia Jackson called out to him; became the defining statement of the non-violent movement's vision
  - Political impact: Kennedy met march leaders at the White House; pressure on Congress increased; Kennedy's civil rights bill advanced
  - 15 September 1963: Four girls killed in bombing of Sixteenth Street Baptist Church, Birmingham — Addie Mae Collins, Cynthia Wesley, Carole Robertson, Carol Denise McNair; KKK bomb; exposed continued violence despite legislation
  - 22 November 1963: Kennedy assassinated; Lyndon Johnson pledged to pass Kennedy's civil rights bill as a memorial

- **Civil Rights Act 1964:**
  - Signed by LBJ on 2 July 1964
  - Outlawed discrimination based on race, colour, religion, sex or national origin in employment and public places
  - Ended segregation in hotels, restaurants, theatres, cinemas — the public accommodations targeted since the sit-ins
  - Created Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) to investigate complaints
  - Gave federal government power to withhold funds from discriminating institutions
  - Was much stronger than the Civil Rights Act 1957, which had mainly investigated voting-rights abuses and had weak enforcement
  - Did NOT address voting rights (addressed separately in 1965 Voting Rights Act) or housing discrimination (1968 Civil Rights Act)
  - Bridge to Episode 7: the 1964 Act was a major breakthrough on public segregation and employment, but the unresolved voting-rights issue led directly to Selma and the 1965 Voting Rights Act

## 4. Content reference pack

### Dates & timeline
- **1957:** Civil Rights Act 1957 — first federal civil rights law since Reconstruction, but weak and limited; useful contrast with the 1964 Act
- **Spring 1963:** "Project C" begins in Birmingham; SCLC and ACMHR sustained campaign
- **2–5 May 1963:** Children's Crusade; fire hoses and police dogs; Kennedy shocked
- **11 June 1963:** Kennedy addresses the nation on civil rights as a moral issue
- **12 June 1963:** Medgar Evers assassinated
- **28 August 1963:** March on Washington — 250,000 people, Lincoln Memorial, MLK's speech
- **15 September 1963:** Sixteenth Street Baptist Church bombing — four girls killed
- **22 November 1963:** Kennedy assassinated; LBJ pledges to pass civil rights bill
- **2 July 1964:** Civil Rights Act signed by President Lyndon B. Johnson
- **1965:** Voting Rights Act follows Selma — solves the voting-rights gap left by the 1964 Act

### Key people
- **Martin Luther King Jr:** Led the Birmingham campaign and delivered "I Have a Dream" at the march; by 1963 the undisputed face of the civil rights movement nationally
- **Fred Shuttlesworth:** ACMHR leader in Birmingham; invited MLK and SCLC; had been fighting Birmingham's segregation for years; survived multiple bombings
- **Bull Connor:** Birmingham Public Safety Commissioner; used fire hoses and police dogs against protesters and children; his brutality made the civil rights case for the movement
- **A. Philip Randolph:** Veteran organiser; had planned a march in 1941; organised the 1963 march with Bayard Rustin
- **Bayard Rustin:** Chief logistical organiser of the March on Washington; kept in the background because of his openly gay identity (civil rights leaders feared it would be used against the movement)
- **John Lewis:** SNCC chair; his March on Washington speech (toned down before delivery) called for more radical action; it was a signal of the tensions that would produce Black Power
- **Mahalia Jackson:** Gospel singer who called out "Tell them about the dream, Martin!" during MLK's speech — prompting the improvised "I Have a Dream" passage
- **Medgar Evers (1925–1963):** NAACP field secretary in Mississippi; first to file a desegregation lawsuit against University of Mississippi; assassinated 12 June 1963; killer (Byron De La Beckwith) not convicted until 1994
- **Lyndon B. Johnson (LBJ):** Vice-President who became President after Kennedy's assassination; used Kennedy's death as political leverage to pass the Civil Rights Act 1964

### Key terms & definitions
- **Project C (Confrontation):** SCLC's strategy for the Birmingham campaign — systematic, sustained direct action designed to provoke a violent response that would generate media coverage
- **Children's Crusade:** Campaign in Birmingham where schoolchildren joined the marches in May 1963; Connor's response to children brought maximum media outrage
- **Civil Rights Act 1964:** Federal law outlawing discrimination in public places and employment; signed by LBJ; the legislative peak of the non-violent phase
- **EEOC (Equal Employment Opportunity Commission):** Body created by the 1964 Act to investigate discrimination complaints; a federal enforcement mechanism
- **Voting Rights Act 1965:** Later federal law that addressed the voting-rights gap left by the 1964 Act; taught fully in Episode 7
- **"I Have a Dream":** MLK's speech at the March on Washington (28 August 1963); largely scripted, but the defining "dream" passage was improvised; the most famous speech of the civil rights movement

### Case studies / named examples
- **Children's Crusade, Birmingham (2–5 May 1963):** When adult volunteers dried up, SCLC recruited schoolchildren — some as young as 6. Connor used fire hoses powerful enough to roll people along the pavement and pin them against walls. He used police dogs. The images were on front pages of every major newspaper and broadcast on TV. Kennedy, watching, was reportedly appalled. The event is cited in virtually every mark scheme for "explain why Kennedy proposed civil rights legislation."
- **Sixteenth Street Baptist Church bombing (15 September 1963):** Four girls — Addie Mae Collins (14), Cynthia Wesley (14), Carole Robertson (14), Carol Denise McNair (11) — were killed when KKK members bombed the church, a base for civil rights activity. The bombing came less than three weeks after the march. It galvanised national anger and increased pressure on Congress. The bomber (Robert Chambliss) was not convicted until 1977.
- **"I Have a Dream" — improvised passage:** Mahalia Jackson was on the platform and called out "Tell them about the dream, Martin!" MLK put down his prepared notes and improvised the "I have a dream" section. It was not planned. The fact that the movement's defining statement was improvised — and has endured — is itself a remarkable historical footnote.
- **Legislative sequence:** The 1957 Act was weak and focused on investigation. The 1964 Act had much greater scope and attacked public segregation and employment discrimination. The 1965 Voting Rights Act then tackled the voting-rights gap that remained.

### Causes & effects
- Project C strategy → Bull Connor's violent response → Children's Crusade images (fire hoses, dogs) → national and international outrage → Kennedy addressed nation on civil rights 11 June 1963
- Kennedy assassination (22 November 1963) → LBJ committed to passing civil rights bill as memorial → political momentum in Congress
- March on Washington (28 August 1963) → 250,000 people, MLK's speech → demonstrated movement's national scale and moral authority → increased congressional pressure
- Birmingham church bombing (15 September 1963) → four girls killed → national anger → further momentum for legislation
- All of the above → Civil Rights Act signed 2 July 1964 → discrimination in public places and employment outlawed
- Civil Rights Act 1964 did not address voting rights → Selma campaign continued the pressure → Voting Rights Act 1965 tackled the voting-rights gap

### Exam angles
- **Q2 (Explain why, 12 marks):** "Explain why the Civil Rights Act (1964) was passed." Three pillars: Birmingham campaign (TV images of fire hoses and children forced public opinion shift); March on Washington (demonstrated scale, "I Have a Dream" gave moral framework); Kennedy assassination + LBJ's commitment (political leverage). Own knowledge: Bull Connor, Children's Crusade, 250,000 at march, Sixteenth Street Baptist Church bombing.
- **Q2 variant:** "Explain why the March on Washington was significant." Scale (250,000 including 40,000 white — multi-racial breadth); "I Have a Dream" speech (defined the movement's moral vision nationally); political impact (Kennedy met leaders; pressure on Congress increased).
- **Progress/limits angle:** 1964 Act is strong evidence of federal progress because it outlawed public discrimination and employment discrimination; but it left voting rights, housing and economic inequality unresolved. Use this as a bridge to Selma/1965.
- **Misconceptions worth a MisconceptionCheck:**
  - "The 1964 Civil Rights Act solved the civil rights problem" — it outlawed public discrimination but left voting rights (addressed 1965), housing (1968) and economic inequality untouched
  - "The March on Washington was an all-Black protest" — 40,000 of 250,000 were white; it was explicitly multi-racial
  - "MLK's 'I Have a Dream' was scripted from start to finish" — the defining passage was improvised after Mahalia Jackson called out to him
  - "All civil rights laws did the same thing" — 1957, 1964 and 1965 had different strengths, targets and levels of impact
