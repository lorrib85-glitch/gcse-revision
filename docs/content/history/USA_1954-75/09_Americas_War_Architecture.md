### Section 2 — Learning Chunk 1
- **Purpose:** Operation Rolling Thunder — the air war and why it failed.
- **Proposed content for this episode:**
  - Rolling Thunder: aims, scale (864,000 tonnes), duration (Feb 1965 – Nov 1968)
  - Why it failed: North Vietnam's largely agricultural economy; civilian casualties increased resolve; Soviet/Chinese replacement supplies; Trail repaired continuously
  - The trail: the bicycle porters, the 24-hour repair teams, the scale of the network
  - Ground troops: Marines landing at Da Nang (8 March 1965); escalation to 536,000 by 1968
  - ARVN weakness: why US troops increasingly carried the fighting themselves; bridge to later Vietnamisation difficulty
- **Suggested component(s):**
  - `ExplainReveal` — cause-and-effect chain: Gulf of Tonkin Resolution → Rolling Thunder begins → aims stated (destroy infrastructure, disrupt Trail, break morale) → each aim fails (agricultural economy, Soviet supplies, Trail repaired, resolve increased) → ARVN weakness means US troops carry the fight → ground troops deployed as Rolling Thunder proves insufficient; reveals the strategic failure progressively
  - `QuickRecallScreen` — retrieval of Gulf of Tonkin Resolution and ARVN weakness (Episode 8) interleaved with Rolling Thunder facts; connects the legal mechanism to the military execution

### Section 3 — Learning Chunk 2
- **Purpose:** Vietcong tactics — why conventional US power couldn't win against guerrilla fighters.
- **Proposed content for this episode:**
  - No uniforms: mixing with civilians; the impossibility of distinguishing combatants
  - Ambushes and booby traps: punji stakes, tripwires; psychological effect
  - Cu Chi tunnels: scale, use (hospitals, kitchens, command centres); tunnel rats; why they couldn't be destroyed
  - Hit-and-run: "strike and fade"; fighting on their own terms, not the US's
  - Political support: why many South Vietnamese peasants supported or tolerated the VC (Diem's land policies, US bombing of villages)
  - Interleaving: connect to Diem's land policies (Episode 8) — the same peasant alienation that undermined Diem built the VC's support base
- **Suggested component(s):**
  - `InteractiveCollectionExplorer` — explore "Vietcong tactics" (no uniforms / ambushes and booby traps / Cu Chi tunnels / hit-and-run / political support among peasants); colour-coded by type; makes each tactic visceral and memorable rather than just listed
  - `QuickRecallScreen` — retrieval of Diem's failures (Episode 8) interleaved with VC political support facts; connects peasant alienation under Diem to VC recruitment

### Section 4 — Learning Chunk 3
- **Purpose:** US Search and Destroy — what the strategy was, why it failed, and why it lost Hearts and Minds.
- **Proposed content for this episode:**
  - Search and Destroy: the logic (find the VC and kill them), the body count metric, the incentive to inflate numbers
  - Hearts and Minds: the USA needed civilian support, but its tactics often pushed civilians towards the Vietcong
  - Free fire zones: everyone considered hostile; civilian deaths counted as VC kills; villages burned
  - Operation Cedar Falls (January 1967): the largest Search and Destroy operation; 30,000 troops; 500 homes destroyed, 6,000 civilians relocated; VC returned within weeks
  - The alienation effect: South Vietnamese peasants whose villages were burned became VC supporters; Search and Destroy created the enemy it was meant to destroy
  - Napalm and Agent Orange: the weapons and their consequences; distinguish napalm = immediate burning from Agent Orange = defoliant/herbicide with long-term damage
  - Living room war: television images made military methods politically damaging at home
- **Suggested component(s):**
  - `VisualLearning` — cinematic sequence: Search and Destroy patrol → body count metric → free fire zone declared → village burned → civilians displaced → hearts and minds lost → VC recruitment from displaced civilians → TV images reach American homes → same cycle repeats; makes the self-defeating logic of the strategy visible
  - `MatchingTask` — match US method to consequence: (Operation Rolling Thunder → bombing failed to destroy agricultural economy), (Search and Destroy → civilian casualties increased VC support), (Agent Orange → defoliated 17% of forests, long-term health effects), (napalm → immediate burning/civilian imagery), (body count metric → inflated numbers misled Washington); active processing of why each method backfired

## 4. Build recommendations

1. **The self-defeating loop of Search and Destroy as the episode's core insight:** The `VisualLearning` in Section 4 should make this loop vivid: burning villages creates refugees who join the VC who attack US troops who burn more villages. Add Hearts and Minds explicitly so students can name the idea as well as explain it. Students who can describe this causal loop in Q2 answers earn the highest marks.

2. **Hearts and Minds as the key GCSE phrase:** The content already explained the idea, but the build should name it repeatedly. It is the simplest phrase for students to remember why US tactics failed politically.

3. **Cu Chi tunnels as a memorable teaching moment:** The tunnel rats detail — small soldiers sent alone into 250 km of booby-trapped darkness armed with a pistol — is visceral, specific and memorable. The `InteractiveCollectionExplorer` in Section 3 should give it a dedicated card. Students who include this in Q2 answers stand out from the crowd.

4. **ARVN weakness as a bridge to Vietnamisation:** Section 2 and Section 5 should explicitly flag that weak ARVN forced US troops to carry the war and later made withdrawal/Vietnamisation difficult. This will make Episode 12 easier to understand.

5. **The draft inequality as a bridge to Episode 11:** Section 5 should explicitly flag that 30 million were drafted, deferments favoured college students (mostly white, middle-class), and Black Americans were disproportionately in combat roles. This seeds Episode 11's anti-war movement content and the MLK / civil rights connection to Vietnam.

6. **Living room war as anti-war bridge:** Section 4 should introduce the phrase briefly. Keep it as a bridge, not a full Episode 11 lesson: images of military methods in American homes turned battlefield choices into domestic politics.

7. **Westmoreland's optimism as a setup for Tet:** Section 5 should end with Westmoreland's "crossover point" theory and his public statements that the war was being won. This sets up Episode 10's Tet Offensive — which destroyed his credibility overnight — as a reversal the learner is primed to appreciate.

8. **Claude build detail — exact implementation requirements:**
   - The Section 4 `VisualLearning` must show the loop, not just list methods: Search and Destroy → village damaged/burned → civilians displaced/angered → Hearts and Minds lost → more tolerance/support for VC → more US patrols.
   - Use "Hearts and Minds" as an explicit label on screen at least twice: once in Section 4 teaching, once in Section 6 exam explanation.
   - Add a quick misconception check that forces the napalm/Agent Orange distinction: napalm = immediate incendiary burning; Agent Orange = defoliant/herbicide with long-term health/environmental effects.
   - ARVN weakness must be explained as a bridge, not a throwaway: weak ARVN → US carries the fighting → withdrawal becomes harder → Vietnamisation later depends on the same weak army.
   - Living Room War should be a short bridge only: one line showing how TV made military methods politically damaging at home. Do not turn it into Episode 11's full anti-war lesson.
   - In the Examiner model answer, include one paragraph on guerrilla tactics and one paragraph on US methods backfiring. The second paragraph must use Hearts and Minds language.
   - Avoid graphic/gory visual treatment. Keep imagery cinematic and tense: smoke, empty burned village, abandoned supplies, damaged landscape, anxious civilians, not explicit wounds or bodies.
