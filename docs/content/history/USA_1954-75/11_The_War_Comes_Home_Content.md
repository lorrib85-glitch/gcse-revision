## 2. Storyline

### Core takeaway
DRAFT (for user confirmation): The Vietnam War was the first war fought on television — and the first America lost at home as well as abroad. By 1970, the anti-war movement had reached the university campuses, the churches, the sports arenas and the steps of Congress itself. When the National Guard shot and killed four student protesters at Kent State, the nation finally understood that the war in Vietnam and the war at home were the same war.

*Drawn from recurring pattern: the anti-war movement is examinable in its own right; the 2022 paper Q2 tested "explain why opposition to Vietnam grew"; the mark schemes cite TV coverage, the draft, Kent State, and the My Lai revelation as the key causes of opposition growth. The module must also avoid implying that all Americans opposed the war: Nixon's "silent majority" shows that the USA was divided, not united against Vietnam.*

### Evidence for the takeaway
- Television: the first war brought live into American homes; body bags, burning villages, napalm victims — images that were impossible to suppress and impossible to reconcile with official "winning" narratives
- The draft: 30 million called up; college deferments; disproportionate burden on Black and poor working-class Americans; lottery system introduced 1969
- MLK's "Beyond Vietnam" speech (4 April 1967, Riverside Church): linked the civil rights struggle to opposition to the war — Black Americans were being asked to fight for democracy abroad while being denied it at home; the speech cost MLK support from LBJ and some civil rights allies but galvanised the connection between the two movements
- Anti-war movement: Students for a Democratic Society (SDS); Moratorium to End the War in Vietnam (October–November 1969) — millions marched; 250,000 in Washington DC; campuses shut down
- Nixon's "silent majority" speech (3 November 1969): Nixon claimed most Americans still supported his policy and were not represented by vocal protesters. This mattered because it showed America was divided: many conservative, working-class and patriotic Americans saw protesters as ungrateful or un-American.
- Cambodia invasion (30 April 1970): Nixon announced US and South Vietnamese attacks into Cambodia to destroy communist bases and supply routes. Many Americans saw this as the war expanding into another country after Nixon had promised "peace with honour" and withdrawal.
- Kent State (4 May 1970): Nixon's Cambodia invasion triggered protests; Ohio National Guard shot and killed four students (Allison Krause, Jeffrey Miller, Sandra Scheuer, William Schroeder) and wounded nine at Kent State University; 450 campuses closed or went on strike; Congress repealed the Gulf of Tonkin Resolution (June 1970)
- Jackson State (14 May 1970): police opened fire on Black student protesters at Jackson State College, Mississippi; two students were killed. It received less national media attention than Kent State, revealing the continuing racial imbalance in whose deaths were treated as national tragedy.
- My Lai revelation (November 1969, Episode 10 thread): confirmed what many already suspected — moral corruption of the war effort; important chronology point: the massacre happened in March 1968 but the public outrage followed its revelation in 1969
- The Pentagon Papers (1971): leaked by Daniel Ellsberg; classified history of US involvement in Vietnam; confirmed that successive administrations had lied to the public about the war's progress and prospects; Nixon tried to suppress publication; Supreme Court ruled for publication

### Series through-line
This episode is where the two threads of the series — civil rights at home and war abroad — converge explicitly. MLK's "Beyond Vietnam" speech made the connection that had always been implicit. Black Americans were disproportionately drafted and disproportionately dying in a war for democracy they were denied at home. The same federal government that sent troops to Little Rock (Episode 2) and passed the Civil Rights Act (Episode 5) was sending Black men to die in Vietnam in disproportionate numbers. The through-line becomes a question: what does "one nation" mean when that nation is fighting two battles simultaneously?

### Exam framing
- Q2 2022 paper: "Explain why opposition to the Vietnam War grew in the USA 1964–73" — three pillars: television coverage (images of body bags, napalm, My Lai); the draft's inequality (deferments, Black and poor overrepresented); Kent State (showed government willing to kill US students)
- Q2 stronger answer: link the causes. Television made the war visible and widened the credibility gap; the draft made the war personal and unfair; Cambodia made it look like Nixon was widening rather than ending the war; Kent State, My Lai and the Pentagon Papers made opposition more mainstream by making the war look morally and politically indefensible.
- Q2 "Explain why the anti-war movement was significant" — three pillars: scale (millions in Moratorium marches), political impact (Gulf of Tonkin Resolution repealed, LBJ didn't run for re-election), cultural impact (shifted the "silent majority" debate toward opposition)
- Balance point: not all Americans opposed the war. Nixon's "silent majority" mattered because it showed many Americans supported law-and-order politics and viewed protesters as unpatriotic. However, opposition still grew enough to force major political consequences.
- Misconceptions worth a MisconceptionCheck:
  - "The anti-war movement was only students and hippies" — MLK, religious leaders, veterans (Vietnam Veterans Against the War), senators, athletes and ordinary Americans joined; it was a mainstream movement by 1969
  - "By 1970, everyone in America opposed the war" — false; Nixon appealed to a "silent majority" who disliked protesters and supported law-and-order politics
  - "Television simply reported the war neutrally" — the selection of images (body bags, napalm girl, My Lai photographs) shaped public perception; editors chose what to show; the "living room war" was not neutral
  - "Kent State ended the anti-war movement" — it accelerated it; 450 campuses closed; Congress repealed the Gulf of Tonkin Resolution within two months

## 3. Specification requirements

### Key Topic 4 — Reaction in the USA

- **Television and media coverage:**
  - Vietnam was the first war brought live into American homes via television; by 1968, 90% of US homes had televisions
  - Images: body bags being loaded onto planes at Tan Son Nhut Airport; Vietnamese civilians fleeing burning villages; napalm burning children; execution of VC prisoner by South Vietnamese General Loan (Eddie Adams photograph, 1 February 1968); "Napalm Girl" (Nick Ut photograph, June 1972)
  - "Credibility gap": official reports of progress vs what viewers could see on the evening news; Walter Cronkite (Episode 10); media increasingly sceptical after Tet
  - My Lai chronology: My Lai happened in March 1968, but it was not revealed publicly until November 1969; this strengthened the credibility gap because the army had covered it up for more than a year
  - Newspapers: the Pentagon Papers (New York Times and Washington Post, 1971) confirmed government deception

- **The draft:**
  - Selective Service System: all 18-year-old men required to register; local draft boards decided who was called up
  - Deferments: available for college students, essential workers, those with physical disabilities; disproportionately used by middle-class and wealthy (Muhammad Ali, who refused induction as a conscientious objector, was stripped of his boxing title)
  - Race and class inequality: Black Americans were overrepresented in combat roles; in the early years of the war, Black Americans made up 16% of combat deaths despite being 11% of the military; poor and working-class Americans with no access to deferments bore a disproportionate burden
  - Draft lottery (1969): Nixon replaced the system with a birthday lottery to make it appear fairer; 366 dates drawn randomly; young men with low numbers were called up first
  - Draft resistance: draft card burning; fleeing to Canada (50,000–100,000 draft evaders fled to Canada); Muhammad Ali's refusal (1967); conscientious objector applications

- **MLK and the civil rights–Vietnam connection:**
  - MLK's "Beyond Vietnam" speech (4 April 1967, Riverside Church, New York):
    - Called the US government "the greatest purveyor of violence in the world today"
    - Linked the anti-war movement to the civil rights struggle: money spent on Vietnam denied to Great Society programmes (poverty, education, housing) at home
    - Black Americans were being sent to die for rights they were denied at home: "We were taking the Black young men who had been crippled by our society and sending them eight
