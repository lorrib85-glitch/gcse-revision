## 2. Storyline

### Core takeaway
DRAFT (for user confirmation): The Vietnam War was the first war fought on television — and the first America lost at home as well as abroad. By 1970, the anti-war movement had reached university campuses, churches, sports arenas and Congress itself. When the National Guard shot and killed four student protesters at Kent State, the nation finally understood that the war in Vietnam and the war at home were connected.

*Drawn from recurring pattern: the anti-war movement is examinable in its own right; the 2022 paper Q2 tested why opposition to Vietnam grew. Mark schemes cite TV coverage, the draft, Kent State and the My Lai revelation as key causes. The module must also avoid implying that all Americans opposed the war: Nixon's "silent majority" shows that the USA was divided, not united against Vietnam.*

### Evidence for the takeaway
- Television: the first war brought live into American homes; body bags, burning villages, napalm victims and street fighting contradicted official claims that America was winning
- Credibility gap: official optimism from the government and military no longer matched what Americans saw on TV or read in newspapers
- My Lai revelation (November 1969): confirmed what many already suspected — moral corruption of the war effort. Important chronology: the massacre happened in March 1968 but public outrage followed its revelation in 1969
- The draft: 30 million called up; college deferments protected many white middle-class men; Black, poor and working-class Americans were more exposed to combat
- MLK's "Beyond Vietnam" speech (4 April 1967): linked civil rights and Vietnam — Black Americans were asked to fight for democracy abroad while denied equality at home
- Anti-war movement: SDS, teach-ins, Vietnam Veterans Against the War, Moratorium marches, religious leaders, civil rights figures, senators, ordinary families and athletes such as Muhammad Ali
- Nixon's "silent majority" speech (3 November 1969): Nixon claimed most Americans still supported his policy and were not represented by vocal protesters. This matters because it shows America was divided: many conservative, working-class and patriotic Americans saw protesters as ungrateful or un-American
- Cambodia invasion (30 April 1970): Nixon announced US and South Vietnamese attacks into Cambodia to destroy communist bases and supply routes. Many Americans saw this as the war expanding into another country after Nixon had promised "peace with honour" and withdrawal
- Kent State (4 May 1970): protests against Cambodia; Ohio National Guard shot and killed four students — Allison Krause, Jeffrey Miller, Sandra Scheuer and William Schroeder — and wounded nine; 450 campuses closed or went on strike; Congress repealed the Gulf of Tonkin Resolution in June 1970
- Jackson State (14 May 1970): police killed two Black students at Jackson State College, Mississippi; received less national media coverage than Kent State, revealing continuing racial imbalance in whose deaths were treated as national tragedy
- Pentagon Papers (June 1971): leaked by Daniel Ellsberg; confirmed that successive administrations had lied about the war's progress and prospects

### Series through-line
This episode is where the two threads of the series — civil rights at home and war abroad — converge explicitly. MLK's "Beyond Vietnam" speech made the connection that had always been implicit. Black Americans were disproportionately drafted and disproportionately dying in a war for democracy they were denied at home. The same federal government that sent troops to Little Rock and passed civil rights laws was sending young men to die in Vietnam. The through-line becomes a question: what does "one nation" mean when that nation is fighting two battles at once?

### Exam framing
- Q2 2022 paper: "Explain why opposition to the Vietnam War grew in the USA 1964–73" — three pillars: television/media coverage, draft inequality, and Kent State/My Lai
- Strong Q2 chain: television made the war visible and widened the credibility gap; the draft made the war personal and unfair; Cambodia made it look like Nixon was widening rather than ending the war; Kent State, My Lai and the Pentagon Papers made opposition more mainstream by making the war look morally and politically indefensible
- Q2 "Explain why the anti-war movement was significant" — scale (millions in Moratorium marches), political impact (Gulf of Tonkin Resolution repealed), cultural impact (shifted the "silent majority" debate)
- Balance point: not all Americans opposed the war. Nixon's "silent majority" mattered because it showed many Americans supported law-and-order politics and viewed protesters as unpatriotic. However, opposition still grew enough to force political consequences
- Misconceptions worth a MisconceptionCheck:
  - "The anti-war movement was only students and hippies" — false; MLK, religious leaders, veterans, senators, athletes and ordinary Americans joined
  - "By 1970, everyone in America opposed the war" — false; Nixon appealed to a "silent majority" who disliked protesters and supported law-and-order politics
  - "My Lai caused public outrage immediately in March 1968" — false; it happened in March 1968 but was covered up until November 1969
  - "Kent State ended protest" — false; it accelerated it; 450 campuses closed and Congress repealed the Gulf of Tonkin Resolution

## 3. Specification requirements

### Key Topic 4 — Reaction in the USA

- **Television and media coverage:**
  - Vietnam was the first war brought live into American homes via television; by 1968, about 90% of US homes had televisions
  - Images included body bags, burning villages, civilians fleeing fighting, the Eddie Adams photograph of General Loan executing a VC prisoner (1 February 1968), and later the "Napalm Girl" photograph (June 1972)
  - Walter Cronkite's post-Tet "stalemate" broadcast made scepticism respectable and weakened Johnson's ability to claim progress
  - My Lai chronology: My Lai happened in March 1968 but was revealed in November 1969; the cover-up deepened mistrust
  - Pentagon Papers (1971): confirmed government deception and strengthened the credibility gap

- **The draft:**
  - Selective Service System: all 18-year-old men had to register; local draft boards decided who was called up
  - Deferments: college students, essential workers and those with medical exemptions were often protected; this favoured middle-class and wealthy Americans
  - Race and class inequality: Black Americans and poor/working-class Americans were more likely to serve in combat roles; in the early years Black Americans made up about 16% of combat deaths despite being about 11% of the military
  - Draft lottery (1969): introduced to make the system appear fairer; birthdays were drawn randomly and low numbers were called first
  - Draft resistance: draft card burning, conscientious objection, flight to Canada, Muhammad Ali's refusal in 1967

- **MLK and the civil rights–Vietnam connection:**
  - MLK's "Beyond Vietnam" speech (4 April 1967, Riverside Church, New York):
    - Called the US government "the greatest purveyor of violence in the world today"
    - Argued that Vietnam spending damaged Great Society anti-poverty programmes at home
    - Argued that Black Americans were being sent to fight for freedoms in Vietnam that they did not fully have in the USA
    - Angered LBJ and some civil rights allies, but made the civil rights/Vietnam link explicit
  - Vietnam Veterans Against the War (VVAW): returning veterans opposed the war; 1971 Operation Dewey Canyon III saw veterans throw medals over the Capitol fence; John Kerry's 1971 testimony became a major anti-war moment

- **Anti-war movement and protests:**
  - Students for a Democratic Society (SDS): campus-based radical organisation; organised teach-ins and protests from 1965
  - Moratorium to End the War in Vietnam (15 October 1969): millions participated nationally; 250,000 marched in Washington DC
  - Nixon's "silent majority" speech (3 November 1969): appealed to Americans who supported his policy or disliked protesters; short-term political success
  - Second Moratorium (15 November 1969): over 500,000 in Washington DC; showed opposition remained large despite Nixon's appeal
  - Silent majority / pro-war backlash: some Americans saw anti-war protest as unpatriotic. This matters for balance: opposition grew, but America remained divided

- **Cambodia, Kent State and Jackson State:**
  - Cambodia invasion (30 April 1970): Nixon announced that US and South Vietnamese forces had entered Cambodia to attack communist bases and supply routes
  - Why Cambodia caused anger: Nixon had promised to wind down the war, so invading Cambodia looked like widening the war into another country. It reinforced the credibility gap and fears of presidential overreach
  - Kent State (4 May 1970): Ohio National Guard fired 67 shots in 13 seconds; killed four unarmed students — Allison Krause, Jeffrey Miller, Sandra Scheuer and William Schroeder — and wounded nine
  - After Kent State: 450 universities and colleges closed or went on strike; protests spread; Congress repealed the Gulf of Tonkin Resolution in June 1970
  - Jackson State (14 May 1970): police fired on Black student protesters at Jackson State College, Mississippi; two students killed; less coverage than Kent State

- **Pentagon Papers (June 1971):**
  - Leaked by Daniel Ellsberg, a former Defense Department analyst
  - 47-volume classified history of US involvement in Vietnam
  - Published by the New York Times and Washington Post after Nixon tried to block publication
  - Confirmed that successive administrations had misled the public about Vietnam
  - Nixon's attempts to discredit Ellsberg fed into the wider pattern of illegal behaviour that led towards Watergate

## 4. Content reference pack

### Dates & timeline
- **1965:** SDS teach-ins and early campus protests
- **1967:** Muhammad Ali refuses induction; MLK gives "Beyond Vietnam" speech
- **January 1968:** Tet Offensive shifts public opinion
- **March 1968:** My Lai Massacre happens, but is covered up
- **November 1969:** My Lai revealed; Moratorium marches; Nixon's "silent majority" speech
- **30 April 1970:** Nixon announces Cambodia invasion
- **4 May 1970:** Kent State shootings
- **14 May 1970:** Jackson State shootings
- **June 1970:** Congress repeals Gulf of Tonkin Resolution
- **June 1971:** Pentagon Papers published

### Key people
- **MLK:** Linked civil rights and anti-war movements through "Beyond Vietnam"; assassinated exactly one year later, 4 April 1968
- **Muhammad Ali:** Refused induction in 1967; stripped of boxing title; became a global anti-war figure and draft-resistance symbol
- **Daniel Ellsberg:** Leaked the Pentagon Papers; exposed systematic government deception
- **John Kerry:** VVAW leader; gave 1971 Senate testimony questioning how to ask a man to be the last to die for a mistake
- **Nixon:** Used the "silent majority" to frame protesters as unrepresentative; invaded Cambodia; tried to suppress Pentagon Papers
- **Kent State students:** Allison Krause, Jeffrey Miller, Sandra Scheuer and William Schroeder; all unarmed; Scheuer was walking to class

### Key terms & definitions
- **Living room war:** Term for Vietnam because television brought the war into ordinary American homes
- **Credibility gap:** Gap between official government claims and what Americans saw/read about the war
- **Draft resistance:** Refusal or resistance to military conscription; included draft card burning, conscientious objection and flight to Canada
- **Moratorium to End the War in Vietnam:** Large anti-war demonstrations in October/November 1969
- **Silent majority:** Nixon's phrase for Americans who were not protesting and whom he claimed still supported his policy
- **Kent State:** National Guard killed four unarmed student protesters, 4 May 1970
- **Pentagon Papers:** Leaked classified history confirming that governments had misled the public about Vietnam
- **Vietnam Veterans Against the War:** Anti-war organisation of returning veterans

### Case studies / named examples
- **MLK's "Beyond Vietnam" speech:** MLK knew opposing Vietnam would cost him allies. He gave the speech anyway, linking war spending to poverty at home and arguing that Black Americans were being sent to die for liberties they were denied in the USA.
- **Muhammad Ali's draft refusal:** Ali refused induction in 1967, citing his Muslim faith and opposition to the war. He was stripped of his title, convicted of draft evasion and banned from boxing for three years. His conviction was later overturned.
- **Cambodia to Kent State chain:** Nixon invades Cambodia → students see expansion and betrayal → protests grow → National Guard opens fire at Kent State → 450 campuses close → Congress repeals Gulf of Tonkin Resolution.
- **Kent State and Jackson State contrast:** Kent State became a national symbol of state violence against students. Jackson State, where two Black students were killed ten days later, received much less attention, showing that race still shaped national sympathy and media focus.

### Causes & effects
- Television coverage + Tet + Cronkite → credibility gap widens → trust in official claims falls
- Draft inequality → Black, poor and working-class Americans bear more burden → draft resistance and anger grow
- MLK's "Beyond Vietnam" → civil rights and anti-war movements explicitly linked
- My Lai revelation → moral authority of US war effort damaged → anti-war argument strengthened
- Nixon's Cambodia invasion → student protests → Kent State → campus strikes → Gulf of Tonkin Resolution repealed
- Pentagon Papers → government deception confirmed → trust in government damaged → Watergate context
- Silent majority speech → Nixon gains short-term support from pro-war/law-and-order Americans → shows the country remains divided even as opposition grows
- All of the above → pressure for Nixon's Vietnamization and withdrawal strategy (Episode 12)

### Exam angles
- **Q2 2022 paper:** "Explain why opposition to the Vietnam War grew in the USA between 1964 and 1973." Three pillars: television coverage, draft inequality, and Kent State/My Lai/Pentagon Papers.
- **Q2 model structure:** Paragraph 1 media/credibility gap; paragraph 2 draft inequality and MLK/Ali; paragraph 3 Cambodia/Kent State/My Lai/Pentagon Papers as events that made opposition mainstream.
- **Q2 balance:** Silent majority is useful to show the USA was divided, but it does not disprove the growth of opposition.
- **Misconceptions worth a MisconceptionCheck:**
  - "The anti-war movement was just students and hippies" — false
  - "By 1970 everyone opposed the war" — false
  - "Kent State ended protest" — false; it accelerated protest
  - "Television was neutral" — false; the selected images shaped public perception
