## 2. Storyline

### Core takeaway
DRAFT (for user confirmation): The Vietnam War was the first war fought on television — and the first America lost at home as well as abroad. By 1970, the anti-war movement had reached the university campuses, the churches, the sports arenas and the steps of Congress itself. When the National Guard shot and killed four student protesters at Kent State, the nation finally understood that the war in Vietnam and the war at home were the same war.

*Drawn from recurring pattern: the anti-war movement is examinable in its own right; the 2022 paper Q2 tested "explain why opposition to Vietnam grew"; the mark schemes cite TV coverage, the draft, Kent State, and the My Lai revelation as the key causes of opposition growth.*

### Evidence for the takeaway
- Television: the first war brought live into American homes; body bags, burning villages, napalm victims — images that were impossible to suppress and impossible to reconcile with official "winning" narratives
- The draft: 30 million called up; college deferments; disproportionate burden on Black and poor working-class Americans; lottery system introduced 1969
- MLK's "Beyond Vietnam" speech (4 April 1967, Riverside Church): linked the civil rights struggle to opposition to the war — Black Americans were being asked to fight for democracy abroad while being denied it at home; the speech cost MLK support from LBJ and some civil rights allies but galvanised the connection between the two movements
- Anti-war movement: Students for a Democratic Society (SDS); Moratorium to End the War in Vietnam (October–November 1969) — millions marched; 250,000 in Washington DC; campuses shut down
- Kent State (4 May 1970): Nixon announced the Cambodia invasion (30 April 1970); protests erupted; Ohio National Guard shot and killed four students (Allison Krause, Jeffrey Miller, Sandra Scheuer, William Schroeder) and wounded nine at Kent State University; 450 campuses closed or went on strike; Congress repealed the Gulf of Tonkin Resolution (June 1970)
- My Lai revelation (November 1969, Episode 10 thread): confirmed what many already suspected — moral corruption of the war effort
- The Pentagon Papers (1971): leaked by Daniel Ellsberg; classified history of US involvement in Vietnam; confirmed that successive administrations had lied to the public about the war's progress and prospects; Nixon tried to suppress publication; Supreme Court ruled for publication

### Series through-line
This episode is where the two threads of the series — civil rights at home and war abroad — converge explicitly. MLK's "Beyond Vietnam" speech made the connection that had always been implicit. Black Americans were disproportionately drafted and disproportionately dying in a war for democracy they were denied at home. The same federal government that sent troops to Little Rock (Episode 2) and passed the Civil Rights Act (Episode 5) was sending Black men to die in Vietnam in disproportionate numbers. The through-line becomes a question: what does "one nation" mean when that nation is fighting two battles simultaneously?

### Exam framing
- Q2 2022 paper: "Explain why opposition to the Vietnam War grew in the USA 1964–73" — three pillars: television coverage (images of body bags, napalm, My Lai); the draft's inequality (deferments, Black and poor overrepresented); Kent State (showed government willing to kill US students)
- Q2 "Explain why the anti-war movement was significant" — three pillars: scale (millions in Moratorium marches), political impact (Gulf of Tonkin Resolution repealed, LBJ didn't run for re-election), cultural impact (shifted the "silent majority" toward opposition)
- Misconceptions worth a MisconceptionCheck:
  - "The anti-war movement was only students and hippies" — MLK, religious leaders, veterans (Vietnam Veterans Against the War), senators, and ordinary Americans joined; it was a mainstream movement by 1969
  - "Television simply reported the war neutrally" — the selection of images (body bags, napalm girl, My Lai photographs) shaped public perception; editors chose what to show; the "living room war" was not neutral
  - "Kent State ended the anti-war movement" — it accelerated it; 450 campuses closed; Congress repealed the Gulf of Tonkin Resolution within two months

## 3. Specification requirements

### Key Topic 4 — Reaction in the USA

- **Television and media coverage:**
  - Vietnam was the first war brought live into American homes via television; by 1968, 90% of US homes had televisions
  - Images: body bags being loaded onto planes at Tan Son Nhut Airport; Vietnamese civilians fleeing burning villages; napalm burning children; execution of VC prisoner by South Vietnamese General Loan (Eddie Adams photograph, 1 February 1968); "Napalm Girl" (Nick Ut photograph, June 1972)
  - "Credibility gap": official reports of progress vs what viewers could see on the evening news; Walter Cronkite (Episode 10); media increasingly sceptical after Tet
  - Newspapers: the Pentagon Papers (New York Times and Washington Post, 1971) confirmed government deception

- **The draft:**
  - Selective Service System: all 18-year-old men required to register; local draft boards decided who was called up
  - Deferments: available for college students, essential workers, those with physical disabilities; disproportionately used by middle-class and wealthy (Muhammad Ali, who refused induction as a conscientious objector, was stripped of his boxing title)
  - Race and class inequality: Black Americans were overrepresented in combat roles; in the early years of the war, Black Americans made up 16% of combat deaths despite being 11% of the military; poor and working-class Americans with no access to deferments bore a disproportionate burden
  - Draft lottery (1969): Nixon replaced the system with a birthday lottery to make it appear fairer; 366 dates drawn randomly; young men with low numbers were called up first
  - Draft resistance: draft card burning; fleeing to Canada (50,000–100,000 draft evaders fled to Canada); Muhammad Ali's refusal (1967); conscientious objector applications

- **MLK and the civil rights–Vietnam connection:**
  - MLK's "Beyond Vietnam" speech (4 April 1967, Riverside Church, New York):
    - Called the US government "the greatest purveyor of violence in the world today"
    - Linked the anti-war movement to the civil rights struggle: money spent on Vietnam denied to Great Society programmes (poverty, education, housing) at home
    - Black Americans were being sent to die for rights they were denied at home: "We were taking the Black young men who had been crippled by our society and sending them eight thousand miles away to guarantee liberties in Southeast Asia which they had not found in southwest Georgia and East Harlem."
    - LBJ was furious; FBI surveillance of King intensified; some civil rights allies (including Roy Wilkins/NAACP) distanced themselves; but the speech resonated with Black Americans and the anti-war movement
  - Vietnam Veterans Against the War (VVAW): organisation of returning veterans who opposed the war; 1971 "Operation Dewey Canyon III" — veterans threw their medals over the fence at the Capitol; John Kerry's 1971 Congressional testimony

- **Anti-war movement and protests:**
  - Students for a Democratic Society (SDS): campus-based radical organisation; organised protests from 1964; grew rapidly after escalation; "teach-ins" at universities from 1965
  - Moratorium to End the War in Vietnam (15 October 1969): millions across the country stopped work and school; 250,000 in Washington DC; the largest single day of political demonstrations in US history to that point
  - Nixon's response: "silent majority" speech (3 November 1969) — claimed most Americans still supported the war; appealed to the "great silent majority" who were not protesting; a significant short-term political success
  - Second Moratorium (15 November 1969): another 500,000+ in Washington DC

- **Kent State (4 May 1970):**
  - Background: Nixon announced on 30 April 1970 that US forces were invading Cambodia — a dramatic expansion of the war that he had promised to wind down; protests erupted across campuses
  - 4 May 1970: Ohio National Guard opened fire on student demonstrators at Kent State University; killed four students (Allison Krause, Jeffrey Miller, Sandra Scheuer, William Schroeder — all unarmed); wounded nine; guardsmen fired 67 shots in 13 seconds
  - Aftermath: 450 universities and colleges closed or went on strike; hundreds of thousands of students demonstrated
  - Jackson State (14 May 1970): police opened fire on Black student protesters at Jackson State College, Mississippi; two killed; received less national media coverage than Kent State
  - Congressional response: Congress repealed the Gulf of Tonkin Resolution (June 1970) — removed LBJ's/Nixon's legal basis for the war

- **Pentagon Papers (June 1971):**
  - Leaked by Daniel Ellsberg (former Defense Department analyst); classified 47-volume history of US involvement in Vietnam
  - Published by New York Times and Washington Post after Nixon's legal attempts to suppress publication were overturned by Supreme Court
  - Confirmed that successive administrations (Truman, Eisenhower, Kennedy, LBJ) had systematically lied to the public about the war's prospects and their own policies
  - Nixon's attempts to suppress publication were part of the pattern of illegal behaviour that led to Watergate

## 4. Content reference pack

### Dates & timeline
- **1965:** SDS and campus anti-war movement begin; teach-ins; first draft card burnings
- **4 April 1967:** MLK's "Beyond Vietnam" speech, Riverside Church
- **January 1968:** Tet Offensive — turning point in public opinion (Episode 10)
- **November 1969:** My Lai revealed (Episode 10); Moratorium to End the War in Vietnam
- **3 November 1969:** Nixon's "silent majority" speech
- **15 November 1969:** Second Moratorium — 500,000 in Washington DC
- **30 April 1970:** Nixon announces Cambodia invasion
- **4 May 1970:** Kent State shootings — four students killed
- **14 May 1970:** Jackson State — two students killed
- **June 1970:** Congress repeals Gulf of Tonkin Resolution
- **June 1971:** Pentagon Papers published by NYT and Washington Post

### Key people
- **MLK:** "Beyond Vietnam" speech (1967) linked civil rights and anti-war movements; assassinated one year later on the same date (4 April 1968)
- **Muhammad Ali:** Refused military induction as a conscientious objector (1967); stripped of boxing title; convicted (later overturned); became an icon of draft resistance
- **Daniel Ellsberg:** Pentagon Papers whistleblower; risked prison to expose the systematic deception; Nixon's "plumbers" unit broke into his psychiatrist's office to discredit him — leading toward Watergate
- **John Kerry:** VVAW leader; his 1971 Senate testimony ("How do you ask a man to be the last man to die for a mistake?") became one of the most powerful anti-war statements; later Secretary of State
- **Nixon:** "Silent majority" speech attempted to frame protesters as fringe; Cambodia invasion (April 1970) that triggered Kent State; attempted to suppress Pentagon Papers
- **The four Kent State students — Allison Krause (19), Jeffrey Miller (20), Sandra Scheuer (20), William Schroeder (19):** All unarmed; none of the four were among the leaders of the protest; Scheuer was simply walking to class; their names should be known

### Key terms & definitions
- **"Beyond Vietnam" (1967):** MLK's speech linking the civil rights struggle and opposition to the Vietnam War; made the connection between military service in Vietnam and the denial of civil rights at home explicit
- **Draft resistance:** Deliberate refusal to comply with military conscription; draft card burning; flight to Canada; conscientious objector applications
- **Moratorium to End the War in Vietnam (1969):** The largest single-day anti-war demonstration in US history; 15 October 1969; millions participated nationally, 250,000 in Washington DC
- **Kent State massacre (4 May 1970):** National Guard killed four unarmed students protesting Nixon's Cambodia invasion; triggered 450 campus closures; led to the Gulf of Tonkin Resolution's repeal
- **"Silent majority":** Nixon's political framing; claimed most Americans still supported the war and were not represented by the vocal protesters; a successful short-term political move
- **Pentagon Papers:** 47-volume classified history of US involvement in Vietnam; leaked by Daniel Ellsberg to the New York Times; confirmed that successive administrations had deceived the public and Congress
- **Vietnam Veterans Against the War (VVAW):** Organisation of veterans who opposed the war; "Operation Dewey Canyon III" (1971) — veterans threw their medals over the Capitol fence; John Kerry's Congressional testimony

### Case studies / named examples
- **MLK's "Beyond Vietnam" speech (4 April 1967):** MLK's advisers warned him the speech would alienate LBJ and cost the civil rights movement federal support. MLK gave it anyway. He called the US "the greatest purveyor of violence in the world today." He linked the money spent in Vietnam to the poverty programmes being cut at home. He said Black Americans were dying in Vietnam for rights denied to them at home. The speech was immediately denounced in the mainstream press. One year to the day later, MLK was assassinated in Memphis.
- **Muhammad Ali's draft refusal (1967):** Ali was classified 1-A (fit for service) and ordered to report for induction. He refused, citing his Muslim faith and his opposition to the war: "I ain't got no quarrel with them Viet Cong." He was stripped of his boxing title, convicted of draft evasion, and banned from boxing for three years — his prime years. The Supreme Court overturned his conviction in 1971. He became one of the most recognised anti-war figures in the world.
- **Kent State — the four students:** Jeffrey Miller's body lying in the road, photographed by John Filo, won the Pulitzer Prize. The photograph became one of the defining images of the era. Miller was 20 years old and had been protesting Nixon's Cambodia invasion. The guardsmen who shot him were never charged. A federal grand jury indicted eight guardsmen; the charges were dismissed. No one was ever held accountable.

### Causes & effects
- Television coverage (body bags, napalm, Eddie Adams photograph, My Lai) → credibility gap became undeniable → public opinion shifted against the war
- Draft inequality (college deferments, disproportionate Black casualties) → working-class and Black Americans felt the burden was unjust → draft resistance, VVAW, Ali's refusal
- MLK's "Beyond Vietnam" speech (April 1967) → connected civil rights and anti-war movements → Black Americans increasingly linked their domestic and foreign struggles
- My Lai revelation (November 1969) → moral authority of US war effort destroyed → anti-war movement's case strengthened
- Nixon's Cambodia invasion (April 1970) → Kent State (4 May 1970) → 450 campuses closed → Congress repealed Gulf of Tonkin Resolution (June 1970)
- Pentagon Papers (June 1971) → confirmed systematic government deception → public trust in government severely damaged → Watergate context
- All of the above → Nixon's Vietnamization and withdrawal strategy (Episode 12) → Paris Peace Accords (1973)
