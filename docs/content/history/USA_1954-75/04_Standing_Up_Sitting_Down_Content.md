## 2. Storyline

### Core takeaway
DRAFT (for user confirmation): The sit-ins, Freedom Rides and James Meredith crisis showed that ordinary young people — students willing to be beaten, burned and arrested without retaliating — could break the system of segregation through sheer, non-violent courage. They also showed the federal government that ignoring the South's violence was no longer an option.

*Drawn from recurring pattern: the sit-ins, Freedom Rides and Meredith crisis rely on the same mechanism — direct challenge to segregation, violent Southern resistance, national embarrassment, and reluctant federal intervention. This pattern is repeated in Birmingham 1963 and Selma 1965.*

### Evidence for the takeaway
- Greensboro sit-in: 1 February 1960 — four Black students at Woolworth's lunch counter; refused service but stayed; returned next day with more; spread to 55 cities in 13 states within weeks
- SNCC formed 15 April 1960 at Shaw University, Raleigh — student organisation that would drive sit-ins and later Freedom Rides; led by Ella Baker and later John Lewis, Stokely Carmichael
- By end of 1960: over 70,000 people participated in sit-ins; over 3,000 arrested; Woolworth's and other chains desegregated lunch counters
- Freedom Riders: 4 May 1961 — 13 riders (7 Black, 6 white) left Washington DC on Greyhound and Trailways buses; challenging *Morgan v Virginia* (1946) and *Boynton v Virginia* (1960), which had ruled interstate bus/terminal segregation illegal but were ignored in the South
- Anniston, Alabama: Greyhound bus firebombed; riders beaten as they fled; photographs published nationally
- Birmingham: riders beaten with baseball bats and iron pipes by KKK (police stood by — Public Safety Commissioner Bull Connor had arranged it)
- Montgomery: riders beaten again; Attorney General Robert Kennedy negotiated with Governor Patterson
- Federal government compelled to act: Interstate Commerce Commission ordered desegregation of interstate travel facilities (September 1961)
- Over 60 Freedom Rides involving more than 400 people followed the first, through the summer of 1961
- James Meredith, 1962: Black air force veteran and NAACP-backed applicant won a court case to study at the all-white University of Mississippi; Governor Ross Barnett resisted; Kennedy sent federal marshals and around 23,000 troops after riots broke out

### Series through-line
This episode is the third proof-of-concept in the direct action series (after the boycott). It introduces SNCC — a new generation of more radical youth activists who will resurface in Episode 6 (Black Power: Stokely Carmichael was SNCC chair). The Freedom Rides also introduce the Kennedy administration's reluctant engagement with civil rights — a relationship that develops through Episodes 5 and 7. The violence at Anniston and Birmingham is a preview of Birmingham 1963 (Episode 5) and Bull Connor's reappearance. James Meredith extends this pattern from buses to universities: federal power could enforce integration, but usually only after Southern resistance created a crisis.

### Exam framing
- Q2 "Explain why the Freedom Rides were significant" — three pillars: federal intervention (ICC ruling), national media coverage of violence, established that non-violent provocation of Southern violence worked as a strategy
- Q2 "Explain why the sit-ins spread so rapidly" — organised through SNCC, the tactic was simple and replicable, moral clarity attracted participants, economic pressure on segregated businesses
- Q2 "Explain why there was progress in civil rights in 1961–65" — James Meredith is a key own-knowledge example: Kennedy sent federal troops to enforce integration at the University of Mississippi
- Misconceptions worth a MisconceptionCheck:
  - "The sit-ins were spontaneous acts of protest" — they were organised, trained, and deliberately non-violent; participants were coached not to retaliate
  - "The Freedom Rides were a failure because of the violence" — the violence was the point; it forced federal action and national attention
  - "Kennedy enthusiastically supported the Freedom Riders" — he and Robert Kennedy found them an embarrassment and tried to persuade them to stop; they only acted under pressure
  - "James Meredith was simply allowed into university after winning his case" — he only enrolled after violent resistance and major federal intervention

## 3. Specification requirements

### Key Topic 1 — Sit-ins, Freedom Rides and James Meredith

- **Greensboro sit-in (1 February 1960):**
  - Four students from North Carolina A&T — Ezell Blair Jr, Franklin McCain, Joseph McNeil, David Richmond — sat at a Woolworth's whites-only lunch counter; ordered coffee; refused service but stayed until closing
  - Returned next day with 27; next day with 300; within two weeks the tactic spread to cities across the South
  - Sit-in participants were trained in non-violent response: told to sit quietly, ignore abuse (verbal and physical — food poured on them, cigarettes put out on them, seats occupied next to them), not to retaliate
  - By August 1961: over 70,000 people had participated; 3,600 arrested; Woolworth's desegregated its lunch counters nationally
  - Economic impact: sit-ins cost segregated businesses income; some chains desegregated rather than lose trade

- **SNCC (Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee):**
  - Founded 15 April 1960, Shaw University, Raleigh, North Carolina; organised by Ella Baker (SCLC)
  - Student-led; more radical and impatient than SCLC; willing to take more risks; operated with less hierarchy
  - Key figures: John Lewis (early chair), later Stokely Carmichael (whose shift to Black Power is covered in Episode 6)
  - SNCC co-ordinated sit-ins; trained participants in non-violence; provided organisational infrastructure

- **Freedom Rides (May–September 1961):**
  - Background: *Morgan v Virginia* (1946) and *Boynton v Virginia* (1960) ruled interstate bus and terminal segregation unconstitutional; Southern states ignored both rulings
  - CORE organised the first ride: 13 riders (7 Black, 6 white) left Washington DC 4 May 1961 on Greyhound and Trailways buses heading to New Orleans
  - Anniston, Alabama (14 May 1961): Greyhound bus surrounded and firebombed by KKK; riders fled and were beaten; photographs printed nationally and internationally
  - Birmingham (14 May 1961): Trailways bus riders beaten with baseball bats and iron pipes; Bull Connor's police did not intervene (had arranged with KKK to give them 15 minutes without interference)
  - Nashville SNCC students joined to continue the ride after original riders hospitalised
  - Montgomery (20 May 1961): riders beaten at bus station; Attorney General Robert Kennedy sent federal marshals
  - Jackson, Mississippi: riders arrested on arrival for entering "white" waiting rooms; many served 60 days in Parchman Farm (notorious Mississippi state prison)
  - Summer of 1961: over 60 Freedom Rides, 400+ participants; riders kept going despite arrests
  - Federal response: Robert Kennedy petitioned the Interstate Commerce Commission; ICC issued ruling (September 1961) desegregating all interstate travel facilities; enforced from November 1961

- **James Meredith and the University of Mississippi (1962):**
  - James Meredith was a Black US air force veteran who applied to the all-white University of Mississippi ("Ole Miss")
  - Supported by the NAACP, he won a federal court case allowing him to enrol
  - Mississippi Governor Ross Barnett tried to block him, defying federal authority and appealing to segregationist voters
  - Riots broke out when Meredith arrived; two people were killed and hundreds injured
  - President Kennedy sent federal marshals and around 23,000 troops to enforce the court order
  - Meredith enrolled, showing that federal power could enforce integration — but only after violent Southern resistance

- **Significance of sit-ins, Freedom Rides and Meredith:**
  - Demonstrated that non-violent direct action worked against economic segregation (businesses), interstate travel segregation and educational segregation
  - SNCC established as a youth-led civil rights organisation — crucial for later campaigns (Freedom Summer, Selma)
  - Federal government proved it would act when violence was publicised nationally — the Kennedys were embarrassed into intervention
  - International attention mounted (Kennedy about to meet Khrushchev; the timing was acutely embarrassing)
  - Showed that ordinary Americans — not just established leaders — could be the movement's driving force
  - Showed a repeated pattern: court victory or legal right → Southern resistance → media attention/violence → reluctant federal enforcement

## 4. Content reference pack

### Dates & timeline
- **1946:** *Morgan v Virginia* — Supreme Court rules interstate bus segregation unconstitutional (ignored by Southern states)
- **1960:** *Boynton v Virginia* — Supreme Court rules bus terminal segregation unconstitutional (also ignored)
- **1 February 1960:** Greensboro sit-in begins at Woolworth's lunch counter
- **15 April 1960:** SNCC founded at Shaw University
- **4 May 1961:** First Freedom Ride leaves Washington DC
- **14 May 1961:** Anniston bus firebombing; Birmingham beatings
- **20 May 1961:** Montgomery beatings; federal marshals sent
- **24–25 May 1961:** Riders arrested in Jackson, Mississippi; sent to Parchman Farm
- **Summer 1961:** 60+ rides, 400+ participants
- **September 1961:** ICC issues desegregation order for all interstate travel facilities
- **November 1961:** ICC order takes effect
- **September–October 1962:** James Meredith crisis at University of Mississippi; Kennedy sends federal marshals and around 23,000 troops; Meredith enrols

### Key people
- **Ezell Blair Jr, Franklin McCain, Joseph McNeil, David Richmond:** The Greensboro Four — four North Carolina A&T students who began the sit-in movement on 1 February 1960
- **Ella Baker:** SCLC administrator who organised the founding conference of SNCC; "godmother" of SNCC; believed in youth-led, decentralised organising rather than charismatic leadership
- **John Lewis:** SNCC chair; rode the first Freedom Ride; beaten multiple times; later US Congressman; one of the movement's most committed figures
- **James Farmer:** CORE director; organised the Freedom Rides; arrested in Jackson, Mississippi
- **Robert Kennedy (Attorney General):** Tried to dissuade the riders; sent federal marshals to Montgomery when violence escalated; petitioned the ICC; ultimately achieved the desegregation order but under duress
- **President John F. Kennedy:** Reluctant civil rights supporter; sent federal marshals and troops in the James Meredith crisis to enforce federal court orders
- **James Meredith:** Black air force veteran who integrated the University of Mississippi in 1962 after federal court action and major federal intervention
- **Ross Barnett:** Governor of Mississippi; tried to block Meredith's enrolment and became a symbol of state-level resistance to federal desegregation orders
- **Bull Connor:** Birmingham Public Safety Commissioner; arranged with the KKK to give riders 15 minutes of beatings before police arrived; became the face of Southern violent resistance; reappears in Episode 5 (Birmingham campaign 1963)
- **Stokely Carmichael:** SNCC member during the Freedom Rides; later became SNCC chair and coined "Black Power" (Episode 6)

### Key terms & definitions
- **Sit-in:** Protest tactic where participants occupy a segregated facility and refuse to leave; effective because it disrupts business and forces confrontation
- **SNCC (Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee):** Student civil rights organisation founded April 1960; more radical and youth-led than SCLC; organised sit-ins, Freedom Rides, Freedom Summer
- **Freedom Ride:** Journey by mixed-race groups on interstate buses through the South to challenge the failure to enforce Supreme Court desegregation rulings
- **CORE (Congress of Racial Equality):** Organisation that organised the Freedom Rides; founded 1942; used non-violent direct action; more active in the North
- **Morgan v Virginia (1946) / Boynton v Virginia (1960):** Supreme Court rulings that declared interstate bus segregation unconstitutional; Southern states ignored both; Freedom Rides forced enforcement
- **Parchman Farm:** Mississippi State Penitentiary; Freedom Riders sent there were subjected to harsh conditions in an attempt to break the movement; it failed — participants continued to volunteer
- **Federal marshals:** Federal law-enforcement officers sent to enforce court orders where state authorities refused to comply; used in the Freedom Rides/Montgomery and James Meredith crisis

### Case studies / named examples
- **Greensboro Woolworth's (1 February 1960):** The Greensboro Four sat at the whites-only counter and ordered coffee. When refused, they stayed until closing. The next day 27 came; then 300. The tactic was non-retaliatory and training-based: participants were coached to absorb abuse silently. The economic logic was simple: segregated lunch counters needed Black customers for their other business. By August 1961, Woolworth's had desegregated nationally.
- **Anniston bus firebombing (14 May 1961):** The Greyhound bus was surrounded by KKK members who slashed the tyres, followed it out of town and firebombed it when it stopped. Riders fled the burning bus and were beaten. A state investigator on board identified himself and the mob dispersed. Photographs of the burning bus were printed on front pages across the world — including in countries across Africa and Asia that the US was courting in the Cold War.
- **Parchman Farm:** Riders arrested in Jackson were sent to Parchman rather than short-term holding — an attempt to break the movement. It backfired: word spread, more students volunteered to ride, and the moral authority of those who endured Parchman strengthened the movement's case nationally.
- **James Meredith at Ole Miss (1962):** Meredith won the legal right to attend the University of Mississippi, but Governor Ross Barnett tried to stop him. Riots broke out when Meredith arrived. Kennedy sent federal marshals and around 23,000 troops. Meredith enrolled, making this a key example of federal enforcement — and of how much violence Southern states would use to resist integration.

### Causes & effects
- *Morgan v Virginia* (1946) and *Boynton v Virginia* (1960) ruled interstate bus segregation unconstitutional → Southern states ignored both rulings → Freedom Rides organised to force enforcement
- Greensboro sit-in (1 Feb 1960) → simple, replicable tactic → spread to 55 cities in weeks → SNCC founded to co-ordinate → 70,000 participants → Woolworth's desegregated nationally
- Freedom Ride firebombed at Anniston (14 May 1961) → photographs published internationally → Kennedy about to meet Khrushchev → acute Cold War embarrassment → Robert Kennedy sent federal marshals
- Riders arrested at Jackson → sent to Parchman Farm → more students volunteered to ride → movement grew rather than collapsed under pressure
- Robert Kennedy petitioned ICC → September 1961 ICC order → interstate travel facilities desegregated from November 1961 → the strategy had worked
- James Meredith wins court case → Ross Barnett resists → riots at Ole Miss → Kennedy sends federal marshals and troops → Meredith enrols → federal enforcement of integration becomes a key example of progress in 1961–65

### Exam angles
- **Q2 (Explain why, 12 marks):** "Explain why the Freedom Rides were significant for the civil rights movement." Three pillars: federal intervention (ICC ruling — direct result of the rides); national media/Cold War pressure (Anniston photographs embarrassed Kennedy); SNCC established as a new force (youth-led, radical, willing to take higher risks than SCLC). Own knowledge: Anniston, Birmingham beatings, Bull Connor, Parchman Farm, 400+ riders.
- **Q2 variant:** "Explain why the sit-ins spread so rapidly across the South in 1960." SNCC organisation, simple replicable tactic, non-violent training, economic pressure on businesses, moral clarity attracted student volunteers. Own knowledge: Greensboro Four, 70,000 participants, 55 cities.
- **Q2 variant:** "Explain why there was progress in the civil rights movement in the years 1961–65." James Meredith is an important example of Kennedy's federal intervention: around 23,000 troops sent to enforce his right to study at the University of Mississippi.
- **Misconceptions worth a MisconceptionCheck:**
  - "The sit-ins were unplanned" — participants were trained in non-violence; SNCC co-ordinated the spread; the tactic was deliberate
  - "The Freedom Rides failed because of the violence" — the violence was the mechanism; it forced federal action and ICC desegregation order
  - "Kennedy was an enthusiastic civil rights supporter" — he tried to persuade riders to stop; acted reluctantly under media, Cold War and federal-authority pressure
  - "Meredith shows Southern universities accepted integration" — he enrolled only after a court order, riots and huge federal intervention
