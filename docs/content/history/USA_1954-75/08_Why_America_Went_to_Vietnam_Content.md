## 2. Storyline

### Core takeaway
DRAFT (for user confirmation): America did not stumble into Vietnam — it stepped in deliberately, driven by Cold War fear of communism spreading across Southeast Asia. But the country it chose to defend was weak, corrupt, and unable to win the loyalty of its own people. By 1964, the US had already committed 16,000 military advisers and was fighting a war it couldn't quite admit it was in.

*Drawn from recurring pattern: every mark scheme for KT3 cites the domino theory, the failure of the South Vietnamese government, and the Gulf of Tonkin Resolution as the three causal pillars; Kennedy's escalation (without public commitment to combat) is cited as a key step.*

### Evidence for the takeaway
- French Indochina and Geneva Accords (1954): Vietnam divided at the 17th parallel; elections promised by 1956; Ho Chi Minh's communist North; Diem's non-communist South
- Domino theory: Eisenhower's belief that if one country fell to communism, neighbouring countries would follow like dominoes; drove US policy across Southeast Asia
- SEATO (1954): Southeast Asia Treaty Organisation — US commitment to collective defence of the region
- Diem's failures: rigged 1955 election; cancelled 1956 reunification elections (because Ho Chi Minh would have won); persecuted Buddhists; oppressive land policies; lost rural support; relied on US money rather than popular legitimacy
- Vietcong (NLF) formed 1960: organised guerrilla resistance to Diem's government; backed by North Vietnam; grew from rural discontent
- Kennedy (1961–63): increased US military advisers from 900 to 16,000; Operation Ranch Hand (Agent Orange defoliation); Strategic Hamlet Programme (failed — alienated peasants further); Battle of Ap Bac (January 1963 — ARVN humiliated despite US advisers); Buddhist crisis (self-immolations, June 1963 — Diem's sister called them "barbecues"); Diem overthrown and killed in coup, 1 November 1963 — with US knowledge
- Gulf of Tonkin (August 1964): USS Maddox attacked (2 August); second attack (4 August) disputed; Gulf of Tonkin Resolution gave LBJ blank cheque to escalate without declaring war

### Series through-line
This episode begins the Vietnam half of the series and the second "battle" in the through-line. The connection to KT1/KT2 is immediate: Black Americans were disproportionately drafted into the war while fighting for equality at home. MLK's 1967 "Beyond Vietnam" speech (Episode 11) will make this connection explicit. The episode's core tension — defending democracy abroad while denying it at home — is the through-line made concrete.

- Misconceptions worth a MisconceptionCheck:
  - "The USA was defending Vietnamese democracy" — Diem's government was not democratic; his 1955 'election' was rigged; he cancelled the 1956 reunification elections
  - "The Gulf of Tonkin incident was a clear, unprovoked attack" — the second incident (4 August) is now known to have been fabricated or misidentified; the Resolution used it as a pretext

## 3. Specification requirements

### Key Topic 3 — Reasons for US involvement

- **Background: French Indochina and the Geneva Accords:**
  - Vietnam, Cambodia and Laos had been French colonies; Ho Chi Minh's communist Vietminh had fought and defeated the French at Dien Bien Phu (May 1954)
  - Geneva Accords (July 1954): temporary division of Vietnam at the 17th parallel; North under Ho Chi Minh (communist); South under Diem (non-communist); reunification elections promised by July 1956; France and USA (Eisenhower) refused to sign the elections clause
  - US policy: contain communism; Diem seen as a viable anti-communist leader for the South despite lack of popular support

- **Domino theory and Cold War ideology:**
  - Eisenhower's domino theory (1954): if South Vietnam fell to communism, neighbouring countries (Laos, Cambodia, Thailand, Burma) would fall in sequence, like dominoes
  - NSC-68 (1950): US national security document committing the US to contain communism globally; Vietnam seen through this lens
  - SEATO (1954): US, UK, France, Australia, New Zealand, Philippines, Pakistan and Thailand; collective defence treaty; gave the US a treaty obligation in Southeast Asia

- **The government of South Vietnam — Diem's failures:**
  - Ngo Dinh Diem: US-backed; Catholic in a Buddhist-majority country; had spent time in the USA before becoming premier
  - 1955 referendum on monarchy vs republic: rigged — Diem claimed 98.2% of the vote; even in Saigon he claimed more votes than voters
  - 1956: cancelled the reunification elections (because US and Diem knew Ho Chi Minh would win); violated the Geneva Accords
  - Land policy: forced peasants off land given to them by the Vietminh; pushed rural population toward the communists
  - Buddhist persecution: Diem's Catholic-dominated government banned Buddhist flags; police fired on Buddhist protest crowds; Buddhist monks self-immolated in protest; Diem's sister called them "barbecues"
  - ARVN (Army of the Republic of Vietnam): poorly led, US-equipped but not motivated; corruption rife among officers

- **The Vietcong (National Liberation Front, NLF):**
  - Formed 1960 as the political/military arm of communist resistance to Diem in the South; backed by Ho Chi Minh's North Vietnam
  - Grew from rural discontent — Diem's land policies, persecution of Buddhists and former Vietminh created recruits
  - Guerrilla tactics: ambushes, booby traps, hiding among civilian population; difficult to distinguish from civilians; very different from conventional warfare

- **Eisenhower's involvement:**
  - Sent economic aid ($1.6bn by 1960) and military advisers to South Vietnam; refused to send combat troops
  - Inherited a commitment he couldn't easily abandon without appearing to "lose" a country to communism

- **Kennedy's escalation (1961–63):**
  - Inherited Eisenhower's commitment; advisers (900 in 1961) expanded to 16,000 by November 1963
  - Operation Ranch Hand (from 1962): defoliation with Agent Orange to destroy jungle cover and Vietcong food supplies; long-term environmental and human health consequences
  - Strategic Hamlet Programme: 8 million peasants moved into fortified villages to separate them from the Vietcong; failed — peasants resented forced relocation; hamlets attacked from inside by Vietcong already present
  - Battle of Ap Bac (January 1963): 350 Vietcong held off 2,000 ARVN troops supported by US advisers, helicopters and armoured vehicles; exposed ARVN's fundamental weaknesses
  - Diem coup (1 November 1963): South Vietnamese generals overthrew and murdered Diem with US knowledge; created political instability in Saigon; a series of coups followed

- **Gulf of Tonkin (August 1964):**
  - USS Maddox on intelligence mission in North Vietnamese waters; attacked by North Vietnamese torpedo boats (2 August 1964) — a real, if provoked, attack
  - A second reported attack on USS Maddox and C. Turner Joy (4 August) — probably did not happen; was misread sonar and panicked reporting; LBJ used it anyway
  - Gulf of Tonkin Resolution (7 August 1964): Congress gave LBJ power to "take all necessary measures" to prevent further attacks and to "prevent further aggression" — an open-ended authorisation for military action without a formal declaration of war

## 4. Content reference pack

### Dates & timeline
- **May 1954:** French defeated at Dien Bien Phu
- **July 1954:** Geneva Accords — Vietnam divided; elections promised for 1956
- **1954:** SEATO founded; Eisenhower articulates domino theory
- **1955:** Diem's rigged referendum; South Vietnam declared a republic
- **1956:** Diem cancels reunification elections
- **1960:** NLF/Vietcong formed in South Vietnam
- **1961–63:** Kennedy expands advisers from 900 to 16,000; Strategic Hamlets; Ranch Hand
- **January 1963:** Battle of Ap Bac — ARVN humiliated
- **1 November 1963:** Diem overthrown and killed in coup
- **22 November 1963:** Kennedy assassinated; LBJ inherits Vietnam
- **2 August 1964:** USS Maddox attacked in Gulf of Tonkin
- **4 August 1964:** Second alleged attack (disputed)
- **7 August 1964:** Gulf of Tonkin Resolution passed by Congress

### Key people
- **Ho Chi Minh (1890–1969):** North Vietnamese communist leader; had fought the Japanese and then the French; led the Vietminh to victory at Dien Bien Phu; president of North Vietnam; the embodiment of Vietnamese nationalism as well as communist ideology
- **Ngo Dinh Diem (1901–1963):** South Vietnamese premier; US-backed; Catholic; authoritarian; failed to build popular support; overthrown and killed in 1963 coup
- **Dwight D. Eisenhower:** President 1953–61; articulated the domino theory; sent economic aid and advisers to South Vietnam; committed the US to defending Diem's government
- **John F. Kennedy:** President 1961–63; expanded advisers to 16,000; authorised covert operations; aware of Diem's weaknesses; approved the coup that removed him; killed before he could decide whether to escalate further
- **Lyndon B. Johnson (LBJ):** Inherited Vietnam from Kennedy; used the Gulf of Tonkin incident to secure the Resolution; escalated to combat troops from 1965 (Episode 9)
- **Robert McNamara:** Secretary of Defense under Kennedy and LBJ; key architect of escalation; later expressed deep regret about the decisions made

### Key terms & definitions
- **Domino theory:** Belief that if one country in a region fell to communism, neighbouring countries would follow; used by Eisenhower and successive presidents to justify intervention in Vietnam
- **Geneva Accords (1954):** Agreement temporarily dividing Vietnam at the 17th parallel; elections promised for 1956; USA refused to sign the elections clause
- **SEATO:** Southeast Asia Treaty Organisation (1954); US-led collective defence treaty; gave the US a formal commitment to Southeast Asian security
- **Vietcong (NLF):** National Liberation Front — the communist guerrilla organisation fighting Diem's South Vietnamese government; backed by North Vietnam; grew from rural discontent
- **ARVN:** Army of the Republic of Vietnam — South Vietnam's military; poorly led, underperforming, corruption-ridden despite massive US equipment and training
- **Agent Orange:** Chemical defoliant used under Operation Ranch Hand to destroy jungle cover; caused long-term cancers and birth defects in Vietnam and among US veterans
- **Strategic Hamlet Programme:** Kennedy-era policy of moving peasants into fortified villages to separate them from the Vietcong; failed and alienated the rural population further
- **Gulf of Tonkin Resolution (1964):** Congressional authorisation for LBJ to take military action in Vietnam without a formal declaration of war; used to justify the full escalation of the war from 1965

### Case studies / named examples
- **Battle of Ap Bac (January 1963):** 350 Vietcong fighters held off 2,000 ARVN troops equipped with US helicopters, armoured personnel carriers and artillery. The ARVN suffered 80 casualties and lost 5 helicopters; the Vietcong lost 18 fighters. American adviser John Paul Vann was furious — and his reports to Washington were suppressed. Ap Bac was the clearest early evidence that the ARVN could not win the war even with US equipment and advice.
- **Buddhist crisis (1963):** The South Vietnamese government banned Buddhist flags in Hue (May 1963). Police fired on crowds; protests spread. On 11 June 1963, Buddhist monk Thich Quang Duc sat down at a crossroads in Saigon and burned himself to death in protest. The photographs appeared on front pages worldwide. Diem's sister said she "would clap hands at seeing another monk barbecue show." The US government was appalled. The crisis directly accelerated the decision to allow the coup.
- **Gulf of Tonkin — the second incident:** The second reported attack on 4 August 1964 is now known not to have occurred. USS Maddox commander reported contacts on sonar — probably caused by a malfunctioning sonar array or reflections from the vessel's own propellers. Robert McNamara called LBJ from the Pentagon to say he was "pretty sure" the attack hadn't happened. LBJ used the report anyway to seek the Resolution. He later told an aide: "Those dumb, stupid sailors were just shooting at flying fish."

### Causes & effects
- Geneva Accords (1954) → elections cancelled by Diem (1956) → Ho Chi Minh's party denied the political victory it had earned → Vietcong formed 1960 to fight for reunification by force
- Domino theory + SEATO commitment → Eisenhower sends advisers + $1.6bn aid → Kennedy expands to 16,000 advisers
- Diem's land policies + Buddhist persecution → rural discontent → Vietcong recruitment grows → ARVN increasingly unable to hold territory
- Battle of Ap Bac (1963) → Kennedy government loses confidence in ARVN → Diem coup approved
- Gulf of Tonkin incident (2 August) + second report (4 August — disputed) → Gulf of Tonkin Resolution (7 August) → LBJ has blank cheque for escalation → Operation Rolling Thunder (February 1965) (Episode 9)
