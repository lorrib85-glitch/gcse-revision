### Section 2 — Learning Chunk 1
- **Purpose:** Nixon's strategy — Vietnamization and why it was supposed to work.
- **Proposed content for this episode:**
  - Nixon Doctrine (July 1969): the political context; why "peace with honour" required a South Vietnamese army that could fight
  - Vietnamization: the policy (train, equip, fund the ARVN; withdraw US troops); troop numbers (536,000 → 49,000 by 1972)
  - The problem from the start: ARVN had never been effective; corruption, desertion, poor leadership (all established in Episode 8); was Vietnamization just putting lipstick on the same problem?
  - Nixon's compensating strategy: bombing North Vietnam, Cambodia and Laos to buy time for Vietnamization
  - Secret bombing of Cambodia/Laos: Nixon targeted communist bases and supply routes outside Vietnam, but this widened the war and deepened distrust when it became known
- **Suggested component(s):**
  - `ExplainReveal` — cause-and-effect chain: domestic anti-war pressure (Episode 11) + Congress's Gulf of Tonkin repeal → Nixon needs an exit → Nixon Doctrine/Vietnamization → ARVN must be able to fight independently → training and equipment → ARVN's fundamental problems remain → Nixon compensates with bombing Cambodia/Laos/North Vietnam → the war narrows for US troops but widens geographically; reveals the gap between the strategy's logic and its reality
  - `QuickRecallScreen` — retrieval of ARVN failures (Episodes 8–9: Ap Bac, Search and Destroy, Tet), Cambodia anger (Episode 11), and Vietnamization facts; connects the history of ARVN ineffectiveness to why Vietnamization was structurally unlikely to work

### Section 3 — Learning Chunk 2
- **Purpose:** Testing Vietnamization — Operation Lam Son 719 and the Easter Offensive.
- **Proposed content for this episode:**
  - Operation Lam Son 719 (February 1971): ARVN incursion into Laos to cut the Trail; US air support only; the first real test of Vietnamization in a major operation; rapid ARVN retreat; soldiers clinging to helicopter skids; Nixon's public downplaying vs private acknowledgement
  - Easter Offensive (April 1972): North Vietnam launched a major conventional NVA attack, showing the war was no longer only guerrilla warfare; ARVN struggled and depended heavily on US air power
  - Operation Linebacker (April–October 1972): intensive US bombing helped stop the Easter Offensive and gave Nixon leverage in negotiations, but it also proved ARVN could not hold alone
  - The lesson: after two years of Vietnamization, the ARVN could not hold without US bombing; what would happen when the US left entirely?
  - Interleaving: connect to Ap Bac (Episode 8) and ARVN's consistent history of underperformance — this is the same pattern repeated at scale
- **Suggested component(s):**
  - `VisualNarrativeScreen` — beat-by-beat: Operation Lam Son 719 (ARVN invades Laos → rapid retreat → soldiers on helicopter skids → Nixon downplays it) → Easter Offensive (NVA launches conventional attack → ARVN struggles → Linebacker saves the situation through US air power) → the conclusion: two years of Vietnamization and the ARVN still can't fight alone
  - `QuickRecallScreen` — retrieval of Ap Bac (Episode 8), Gulf of Tonkin Resolution repeal (Episode 11), and Hearts and Minds/ARVN weakness (Episode 9) interleaved with Lam Son 719/Easter Offensive facts; the series-long ARVN failure thread completing

### Section 4 — Learning Chunk 3
- **Purpose:** Paris Peace Accords — the exit mechanism and its fatal flaws.
- **Proposed content for this episode:**
  - Kissinger and Le Duc Tho: secret negotiations; Paris talks; the terms of the Accords
  - The fundamental flaw: NVA troops remained in South Vietnam; no enforcement mechanism; North Vietnam signed knowing it would violate the ceasefire when ready
  - Le Duc Tho's Nobel Prize refusal: "peace has not actually been established in South Vietnam" — the most accurate assessment
  - Nixon's private promise to Thieu: US would respond militarily if NVA attacked; impossible to honour after Watergate
  - Watergate and its consequences: Nixon weakened → Nixon resigned August 1974 → Ford had less authority → Congress refused more money for South Vietnam → South Vietnam could not resist the 1975 North Vietnamese offensive
  - War Powers Act (November 1973): constrained future presidents and reflected Congress's reaction against the Gulf of Tonkin blank cheque
- **Suggested component(s):**
  - `InteractiveCollectionExplorer` — explore "Why the Accords couldn't hold" (NVA troops left in South / no enforcement mechanism / Le Duc Tho's Nobel refusal / Nixon's private promise to Thieu / Watergate ended Nixon's ability to act / Congress cut off funds); colour-coded; makes the structural failure visible rather than attributing it to bad luck
  - `MatchingTask` — match cause to consequence: (NVA troops left in South → could resume offensive after US withdrawal), (Watergate → Nixon resigned, promise to Thieu worthless), (Ford lacked Nixon's authority → weaker ability to defend South Vietnam), (Congress cut off funds → South Vietnam lacked resources), (War Powers Act → future presidents constrained); active processing of the domino of political failures

## 4. Build recommendations

1. **Le Duc Tho's Nobel Prize refusal as the episode's key insight:** The most elegant piece of knowledge in this episode is Le Duc Tho's refusal of the Nobel Peace Prize. It's a single detail that contains the entire argument: the most honest person in the Paris negotiations said publicly that no peace had been established. Students who cite this in Q2 answers demonstrate a level of sophistication that immediately separates them from the middle band. The `InteractiveCollectionExplorer` in Section 4 should give it a dedicated card.

2. **Watergate as a Vietnam factor:** Many students don't connect Watergate to Vietnam's outcome. The `InteractiveCollectionExplorer` in Section 4 should make explicit that Nixon's private promise to Thieu (he would respond militarily if NVA attacked) became worthless the moment Nixon resigned. This is the causal link that turns a list of events into an argument.

3. **The series conclusion in Section 5:** This episode closes the series as well as the Vietnam arc. The `ColSortBlock` in Section 5 should be designed as a final series-level synthesis activity — sorting evidence not just about the fall of Saigon but about the series' two battles together. The `ExaminerExplainsScreen` in Section 6 should include the brief "you've covered 21 years of American history" close — it's earned.

4. **"The domino had fallen anyway" as the through-line completion:** The series opened with the domino theory (Episode 8) as the US rationale for involvement. It closes with the domino falling. Students who can articulate this irony — the country intervened to stop the domino, the domino fell, and the broader domino effect across Asia didn't happen anyway — are writing at a high level. The `QuickRecallScreen` in Section 5 should include a domino theory recall question to bring the series full circle.

5. **The 2023 exam paper connection:** The 2023 paper Q2 was about peace negotiations 1972–73 — this episode's content. The `FaceTheExaminer` should offer this as an alternative Q2 question alongside the Saigon question, or the `ExaminerExplainsScreen` should explicitly reference the 2023 question type.

6. **Secret bombing and widened-war contradiction:** Claude should make the contradiction clear: Nixon promised to reduce US involvement, yet bombing and incursions in Cambodia/Laos widened the war geographically. This links Episode 11's Cambodia anger to Episode 12's Vietnamization strategy.

7. **Easter Offensive as the second Vietnamization test:** Lam Son 719 shows ARVN weakness in Laos; Easter Offensive shows ARVN weakness against a large conventional NVA attack. Both are needed to prove Vietnamization was fragile before the final collapse.

8. **Funding cut chain must be explicit:** This is the easiest Q2 chain to write and must be taught directly: Watergate weakens Nixon → Nixon resigns → Ford lacks authority → Congress refuses funding → South Vietnam cannot resist 1975 offensive.

9. **Claude build detail — exact implementation requirements:**
   - Do not frame Vietnamization as simply "US troops went down". Teach it as a flawed transfer strategy: US withdrawal depended on ARVN being able to fight, but ARVN weakness had been visible since Ap Bac.
   - The Section 2 `ExplainReveal` must show the logic gap: Nixon needs exit → trains/equips ARVN → ARVN must replace US troops → ARVN has structural weaknesses → Nixon compensates with bombing Cambodia/Laos/North Vietnam → policy is fragile from the start.
   - Section 3 should treat Lam Son 719 as the practical test of Vietnamization, not a side-detail. The student should leave knowing: Laos mission + US air support only + ARVN retreat = warning sign that Vietnamization was failing.
   - Section 3 should treat the Easter Offensive as a second test: NVA conventional attack + ARVN struggled + US air power/Linebacker saved the situation = Vietnamization still depended on US support.
   - Section 4 `InteractiveCollectionExplorer` must present the Paris Peace Accords as an exit mechanism, not a peace settlement. Use at least five cards: NVA troops left in South, no enforcement mechanism, Le Duc Tho refuses Nobel, Nixon promises Thieu support, Watergate/Congress make that promise worthless.
   - Include one misconception check: "The Paris Peace Accords ended the Vietnam War" = false; they ended US involvement, but fighting continued until Saigon fell in 1975.
   - Include one retrieval question that brings back Episode 8: "What was the domino theory, and what is ironic about Saigon falling in 1975?" Correct answer: the US intervened to stop South Vietnam falling to communism, but it fell anyway and the wider Asian domino effect did not happen as predicted.
   - Section 5 visuals should be cinematic and solemn, not action-movie. Use helicopter evacuation, empty offices, maps, abandoned equipment, crowds at gates, palace gates; avoid triumphant or explosive battle imagery.
   - The final Examiner screen should model two Q2 structures: Saigon fall and peace negotiations 1972–73. Keep each concise but explicit enough to show different answer routes.
