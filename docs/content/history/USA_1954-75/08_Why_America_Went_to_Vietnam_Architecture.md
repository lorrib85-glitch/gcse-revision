### Section 2 — Learning Chunk 1
- **Purpose:** Introduce the context — French Indochina, Geneva Accords, containment, the division of Vietnam, the domino theory.
- **Proposed content for this episode:**
  - French Indochina and Dien Bien Phu (May 1954): the French empire collapses; Ho Chi Minh victorious
  - Ho Chi Minh as nationalist as well as communist: his support came partly from fighting foreign rule
  - Geneva Accords (July 1954): temporary division at 17th parallel; elections promised 1956; USA refused to sign elections clause
  - Containment: Truman-era Cold War policy continued by Eisenhower, Kennedy and Johnson
  - Domino theory: Eisenhower's articulation; the Cold War logic that made Vietnam feel necessary
  - Why domino theory felt convincing: China 1949, Korean War 1950–53, McCarthyism and fear of being accused of being soft on communism
  - SEATO (1954): treaty commitment; the US locked in
- **Suggested component(s):**
  - `ExplainReveal` — cause-and-effect chain: China becomes communist + Korea stalemate + McCarthyism → containment hardens → French defeat at Dien Bien Phu → Geneva Accords divide Vietnam → elections clause (USA refused to sign) → 1956 elections cancelled → Vietcong forms 1960 → US feels obligated by SEATO + domino theory → escalating involvement; reveals the step-by-step path to entanglement
  - `QuickRecallScreen` — retrieval of Cold War context and containment interleaved with Geneva Accords/domino theory facts; this is the first KT3 episode so interleaving is cross-series rather than within-series

### Section 3 — Learning Chunk 2
- **Purpose:** Diem's failures — why the country America chose to defend couldn't win its own people's loyalty.
- **Proposed content for this episode:**
  - Diem's rigged 1955 referendum (98.2% claimed); 1956 elections cancelled
  - Land policy: forced peasants off land given to them by the Vietminh → rural discontent → Vietcong recruitment
  - Buddhist persecution: banned flags, police shootings, self-immolations, Diem's sister's "barbecues" comment
  - Battle of Ap Bac (January 1963): ARVN humiliated; US advisers ignored; the military situation was worse than admitted
  - Diem coup (November 1963): US-approved; but created political instability; a series of coups followed
  - Interleaving: connect to US claiming to be the leader of the "free world" (Episodes 1–2's Cold War thread) — how does supporting Diem fit that claim?
- **Suggested component(s):**
  - `VisualLearning` — cinematic sequence: Diem's rigged election → cancelled 1956 elections → land policies → Buddhist crisis (self-immolation photographs) → Ap Bac → Diem coup; each scene shows another layer of Diem's failure and the deepening US problem
  - `QuickRecallScreen` — retrieval of the civil rights movement's Cold War embarrassment (Episodes 1–2) interleaved with Diem's US-image problem; the same Cold War pressure that forced Eisenhower to act at Little Rock was now giving the USA a different international headache

### Section 4 — Learning Chunk 3
- **Purpose:** Kennedy's escalation — the slow slide from advisers to something more.
- **Proposed content for this episode:**
  - Kennedy inherits Eisenhower's commitment: 900 advisers in 1961 → 16,000 by November 1963
  - Operation Ranch Hand: Agent Orange, defoliation, long-term consequences
  - Strategic Hamlet Programme: the logic, the failure, the peasant alienation
  - What Kennedy knew: Ap Bac was suppressed; his advisers split; his own intentions remain debated
  - LBJ inherits Vietnam after Kennedy's assassination — with 16,000 US troops already in the country
- **Suggested component(s):**
  - `VisualNarrativeScreen` — beat-by-beat: Kennedy inherits 900 advisers → 16,000 advisers by 1963 → Strategic Hamlets (fail) → Ap Bac (ARVN humiliated) → Diem coup (November) → Kennedy assassinated (22 November) → LBJ inherits the war; the arc of escalation without admission
  - `MatchingTask` — match policy to outcome: (Strategic Hamlet Programme → peasant alienation, Vietcong inside hamlets), (Agent Orange / Ranch Hand → defoliation, long-term cancers), (Battle of Ap Bac → exposed ARVN weakness), (Diem coup → political instability); active processing of why Kennedy's policies failed

### Section 5 — Learning Chunk 4

## 4. Build recommendations

1. **The "defending democracy" misconception as the episode's hook:** The ChapterHookScreen opens with this — and every section should implicitly return to it. Diem's rigged elections, cancelled referendums and Buddhist persecution are the content that proves the misconception false. By the end of Section 3, students should be able to articulate why this framing was false — which earns marks in Q2 answers.

2. **Gulf of Tonkin second incident as a scepticism-building moment:** The detail that the second attack probably did not happen — and that LBJ used it anyway — is one of the most impactful pieces of knowledge in the entire series. The Section 5 `ColSortBlock` and the `VisualNarrativeScreen` in Section 4 should both set up the Gulf of Tonkin material as the culmination of a pattern of political choices that obscured military reality.

3. **Series bridge in Section 1:** This is the first KT3 episode and the first time the series shifts from civil rights to Vietnam. The `PriorKnowledgeRecall` should include a series-bridge question connecting the two halves: "While the civil rights movement was fighting for equality at home, what was happening in Southeast Asia?" This frames Vietnam as the other half of the through-line from the very opening of Section 1.

4. **The domino theory's internal logic:** Students who understand *why* the domino theory was compelling (China falling to communism in 1949, Korean War 1950–53, McCarthyism) write stronger Q2 answers. The `ExplainReveal` in Section 2 should teach this explicitly so domino theory feels like a real belief held by real people under political pressure, not just a slogan.

5. **Ho Chi Minh should not be flattened into just "communist":** Add the nationalist dimension early. Students need to understand that many Vietnamese saw him as the leader who had fought foreign rule. This helps avoid weak "communism vs democracy" answers.

6. **Containment should be named before domino theory:** Use Truman/containment as the broad policy, then domino theory as the Southeast Asia version of that policy. This gives students a cleaner causal hierarchy for Q2.

7. **Claude build detail — exact implementation requirements:**
   - Do not teach domino theory as a floating definition. Build the causal ladder: China 1949 → Korea 1950–53 → McCarthyism → containment pressure → domino theory → Vietnam.
   - Include one short retrieval question that asks: "Why did US politicians fear being accused of being soft on communism?" Correct answer must mention McCarthyism or the Cold War climate.
   - Include one misconception question: "Was Ho Chi Minh only popular because he was communist?" Correct answer: no, he was also a nationalist who fought foreign rule.
   - In the `ExplainReveal`, each reveal step should be one sentence maximum, with a clear arrow/chain relationship. Avoid a dense paragraph.
   - In the Examiner screen, model a Q2 paragraph using this structure: point = domino theory/containment; evidence = China/Korea/McCarthyism; explanation = presidents feared another Asian country falling to communism; link = this made Vietnam seem strategically important.
   - Do not over-teach Truman as a separate mini-topic. Name containment as the broader policy and keep Truman as context only.
