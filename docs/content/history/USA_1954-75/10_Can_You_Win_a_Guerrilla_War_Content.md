## 2. Storyline

### Core takeaway
DRAFT (for user confirmation): The Tet Offensive proved that the US military had been lying — to the public, to Congress and to itself. American commanders had claimed the war was being won. In January 1968, the Vietcong and North Vietnamese Army attacked over 100 South Vietnamese cities simultaneously — including the US Embassy in Saigon. They were eventually repulsed militarily. But the political damage was fatal. My Lai happened in March 1968, but when it was revealed in 1969 it destroyed much of the remaining moral authority of the US war effort.

*Drawn from recurring pattern: Tet is cited in every source as the turning point of the war; My Lai is a separately examinable event cited in 2022 mark scheme; the mark scheme for "why couldn't the US win" cites both Tet's political impact and My Lai's moral impact. The path to peace talks also begins here: Tet helped push Johnson towards a partial bombing halt and negotiations in Paris.*

### Evidence for the takeaway
- Tet Offensive (30–31 January 1968): coordinated attacks by 80,000 VC and NVA troops on 100+ South Vietnamese cities; Saigon US Embassy breached; Hue occupied for 25 days; Khe Sanh besieged for 77 days
- Military outcome: VC suffered enormous casualties (up to 45,000 killed); the attacks were eventually repelled; the Vietcong as an effective military force was essentially destroyed; NVA replaced them
- Political outcome: Westmoreland had told the US public the war was being won; Tet made the credibility gap impossible to ignore; Walter Cronkite (CBS anchor) broadcast from Vietnam that it was a "stalemate"; LBJ reportedly said "if I've lost Cronkite, I've lost middle America"; LBJ announced he would not run for re-election (March 1968)
- Peace-talk bridge: on 31 March 1968, Johnson announced a partial bombing halt and called for peace talks; talks opened in Paris in May 1968, although progress was slow
- 1968 crisis context: MLK was assassinated in April and Robert Kennedy in June; alongside Tet, urban unrest and the anti-war movement, these events made 1968 feel like a year of national crisis
- My Lai Massacre (16 March 1968): US troops from Charlie Company (1st Platoon, Lieutenant William Calley) killed between 347 and 504 unarmed Vietnamese civilians — mostly women, children and elderly — in the village of My Lai; no weapons found; Calley convicted of murder (1971) but released after 3.5 years; exposed the "free fire zone" culture and the failure of military discipline
- My Lai cover-up: the massacre was covered up for over a year; revealed by journalist Seymour Hersh in November 1969; published with photographs by photographer Ron Haeberle; caused national and international outrage

### Series through-line
Tet and My Lai together are the two events that destroyed American support for the war — the former by exposing the lie about military progress, the latter by exposing the moral corruption of the war itself once revealed in 1969. Episode 11 (The War Comes Home) charts the anti-war movement that Tet and My Lai accelerated, and Kent State as the moment domestic opposition reached a new level of violence. This episode also makes 1968 visible as the point where both series threads converged: Vietnam, civil rights, political assassination and unrest all collided.

## 3. Specification requirements

### Key Topic 4 — Tet Offensive, My Lai and peace-talk bridge

- **The Tet Offensive (January–February 1968):**
  - Named for Tet Nguyen Dan — the Vietnamese lunar new year; a period when a truce was traditionally observed; both sides had agreed to a brief ceasefire
  - 30–31 January 1968: 80,000+ VC and NVA troops attacked over 100 South Vietnamese cities simultaneously; broke the ceasefire agreement
  - Saigon: US Embassy compound breached (a small VC unit fought its way inside); Presidential Palace attacked; fighting in the streets of the capital; General Westmoreland held a press conference in the Embassy grounds amid the fighting, claiming the situation was under control
  - Hue: occupied by NVA for 25 days; mass graves later found — NVA executed between 2,800 and 6,000 South Vietnamese officials, military officers and civilians
  - Khe Sanh: US Marine base besieged for 77 days (January–April 1968); massive US air power used to hold it; compared to Dien Bien Phu
  - Military result: VC casualties extremely heavy — up to 45,000 killed; the VC never recovered as an independent military force; the North Vietnamese Army (NVA) took over the main fighting role after Tet
  - Political result: the US public had been told the war was being won; Tet proved otherwise; "credibility gap" between government claims and reality became undeniable

- **Credibility gap and media impact of Tet:**
  - Credibility gap already existed before Tet because government claims of progress did not match television images, casualty figures, body counts and reports from Vietnam
  - Television brought the war into American living rooms; Tet brought the war directly into Saigon's streets
  - Walter Cronkite (CBS News, "the most trusted man in America") flew to Vietnam and broadcast on 27 February 1968: "We are mired in stalemate. It is increasingly clear to this reporter that the only rational way out, then, will be to negotiate, not as victors, but as honourable people."
  - LBJ watched and reportedly said: "If I've lost Cronkite, I've lost middle America."
  - 31 March 1968: LBJ addressed the nation; announced a partial bombing halt; called for peace talks; announced he would not seek re-election
  - May 1968: Paris peace talks opened, although negotiations made slow progress

- **1968 national crisis context:**
  - April 1968: Martin Luther King Jr assassinated; riots followed in many US cities
  - June 1968: Robert Kennedy assassinated while campaigning for president; he had become an important anti-war and civil-rights figure
  - Alongside Tet, the anti-war movement and urban unrest, these assassinations made 1968 feel like a year of national crisis

- **My Lai Massacre (16 March 1968):**
  - Charlie Company, 1st Battalion, 20th Infantry Regiment; Captain Ernest Medina ordered a search-and-destroy mission on My Lai village complex (designated as VC-controlled)
  - Lieutenant William L. Calley's platoon (1st Platoon) entered My Lai: found no weapons, no VC fighters — only civilians; killed 347–504 unarmed men, women, children and babies; some women raped before being killed; bodies mutilated
  - Stopped only when Chief Warrant Officer Hugh Thompson, piloting a helicopter above, intervened: landed between Calley's troops and fleeing civilians; called in armed support to protect civilians; reported the massacre up the chain of command
  - Cover-up: Army investigated but suppressed findings; no report reached the public for 14 months
  - November 1969: Journalist Seymour Hersh published the story; photographer Ron Haeberle's photographs published in *Life* magazine; caused national and international outrage
  - Courts-martial: 26 soldiers charged; only Calley convicted; sentenced to life imprisonment; Nixon released him to house arrest after public pressure; served 3.5 years before released
  - Significance: destroyed the remaining moral authority of the US war effort once revealed; gave the anti-war movement its most powerful evidence; demonstrated the consequences of the "free fire zone" and body count culture
  - Hugh Thompson: the hero of My Lai; received the Soldier's Medal in 1998 — thirty years later; died 2006

- **Consequences of Tet and My Lai:**
  - LBJ announced he would not run for re-election (March 1968); the political cost of the war had made him politically finished
  - Partial bombing halt and Paris peace talks opened the path towards negotiation, although the war continued for years
  - Nixon elected November 1968 on a platform of ending the war; Vietnamization policy (Episode 12)
  - US public opinion shifted decisively: the majority of Americans now believed the war had been a mistake; anti-war movement accelerated (Episode 11)
  - Tet destroyed Westmoreland's credibility; he was replaced by General Creighton Abrams

## 4. Content reference pack

### Dates & timeline
- **30–31 January 1968:** Tet Offensive launches — 100+ cities attacked simultaneously
- **February–April 1968:** Battle of Hue (25-day NVA occupation); Khe Sanh siege (77 days)
- **27 February 1968:** Walter Cronkite's "stalemate" broadcast
- **16 March 1968:** My Lai Massacre
- **31 March 1968:** LBJ announces no re-election bid; partial bombing halt; call for peace talks
- **4 April 1968:** MLK assassinated — riots across US cities
- **May 1968:** Paris peace talks begin
- **5 June 1968:** Robert Kennedy assassinated while campaigning for president
- **November 1968:** Nixon elected on a promise to achieve "peace with honour"
- **November 1969:** Seymour Hersh publishes My Lai story; Ron Haeberle's photographs published
- **1971:** Lieutenant Calley convicted of murder; sentenced to life; later reduced and released

### Key people
- **General William Westmoreland:** MACV commander; his "we are winning" narrative was destroyed by Tet; replaced by General Abrams; Tet is inseparable from his credibility collapse
- **Walter Cronkite:** CBS News anchor; his 27 February 1968 editorial — calling the war a stalemate — is one of the most influential broadcasts in US television history
- **Lyndon B. Johnson:** President who escalated the war; after Tet announced a partial bombing halt, called for peace talks and withdrew from the 1968 election
- **Robert Kennedy:** Assassinated June 1968 while campaigning for president; his death added to the sense of national crisis in 1968
- **Lieutenant William Calley:** Officer convicted for the My Lai Massacre; became a controversial figure — many Americans saw him as a scapegoat; others as a war criminal; Nixon's intervention to release him was itself controversial
- **Hugh Thompson:** Helicopter pilot who stopped the My Lai killing and reported it; received no official recognition for 30 years; the moral clarity of his actions stands in stark contrast to Calley's
- **Seymour Hersh:** Journalist who broke the My Lai story in November 1969; won the Pulitzer Prize; his investigation is an example of investigative journalism changing public opinion

### Key terms & definitions
- **Tet Offensive:** Coordinated surprise attack by 80,000+ VC and NVA troops on 100+ South Vietnamese cities during Tet (Vietnamese New Year), January 1968; military defeat for North Vietnam but political catastrophe for the US
- **Credibility gap:** The growing disconnect between what the US government said about the war (winning, progress) and what was actually happening; existed before Tet but Tet made it impossible to ignore
- **Paris peace talks:** Negotiations opened in May 1968 after Johnson's partial bombing halt; made slow progress but marked the beginning of the formal negotiating route out of the war
- **My Lai Massacre:** The killing of 347–504 unarmed South Vietnamese civilians by US soldiers on 16 March 1968; covered up for over a year; when revealed, it destroyed the moral case for the war
- **Hugh Thompson:** Helicopter pilot who intervened to stop the killing at My Lai; the episode's moral counterpoint to Calley
- **Walter Cronkite:** "The most trusted man in America"; his editorial calling Vietnam a stalemate after Tet is the most cited single moment of media influence on US public opinion during the war

### Case studies / named examples
- **US Embassy, Saigon (30 January 1968):** A 19-man VC squad blasted through the Embassy wall and occupied the compound for 6 hours. They were all killed. No one reached the main Embassy building. Militarily, the attack failed. But the television images of fighting inside the US Embassy in Saigon — the heart of American power in South Vietnam — were devastating. Westmoreland held a press conference amid the chaos claiming the situation was "well in hand." No one believed him.
- **Walter Cronkite's broadcast (27 February 1968):** Cronkite flew to Vietnam, saw the Tet aftermath, and returned to deliver an editorial in place of his regular news broadcast. The phrase "mired in stalemate" entered American political vocabulary. LBJ's response — "if I've lost Cronkite, I've lost middle America" — became one of the most quoted moments in US political history.
- **1968 as national crisis:** Tet shook confidence in the war; MLK's assassination triggered riots; Robert Kennedy's assassination destroyed the hopes of many anti-war voters; urban unrest and protest made the USA feel close to breakdown. This helps students understand why Tet had such enormous political impact.
- **My Lai — Hugh Thompson's intervention:** Thompson's helicopter crew were flying above My Lai when they saw US troops shooting unarmed civilians. Thompson landed between Calley's platoon and a group of fleeing civilians; he told his gunner to open fire on the US troops if they attacked the civilians; he called in other helicopters to evacuate survivors. He filed a report. It was buried. Thompson later testified that he received threats from fellow soldiers for years after. He finally received the Soldier's Medal in 1998.

### Causes & effects
- Body count culture + Search and Destroy + free fire zones → My Lai Massacre (16 March 1968) → covered up for 14 months → revealed November 1969 → national and international outrage → anti-war movement strengthened
- Westmoreland's optimistic claims ("winning") + existing credibility gap → Tet Offensive (30 January 1968) → credibility gap exposed → Cronkite's "stalemate" editorial (27 February) → LBJ announces no re-election and partial bombing halt (31 March) → Paris peace talks open (May 1968)
- VC suffered 45,000 casualties in Tet → VC never recovered as independent force → NVA took over main fighting role → US now facing a conventional army, not just guerrillas → pressure to withdraw increased
- MLK assassination + Robert Kennedy assassination + urban unrest + Tet → 1968 perceived as national crisis → anti-war and civil-rights tensions intensify
- My Lai revelation (November 1969) → Lieutenant Calley's trial + conviction → Nixon's intervention to release him → controversy about accountability → anti-war movement's moral case strengthened

### Exam angles
- **Q2 (Explain why, 12 marks):** "Explain why the Tet Offensive was a turning point in the Vietnam War." Military dimension (VC destroyed as a force — a US military success) vs political dimension (credibility gap exposed, Cronkite editorial, LBJ's withdrawal from the election, bombing halt and peace talks) — students must address BOTH dimensions; strongest answers explain why a military "success" was a political catastrophe.
- **Q1 (Source inference):** 2022 paper Q1 source relates to Search and Destroy; My Lai is the most extreme consequence of Search and Destroy culture; inference about the human cost of the strategy.
- **Q2 opposition angle:** "Explain why opposition to the Vietnam War grew." Use Tet, credibility gap, My Lai revelation, living room war, draft inequality and 1968 crisis context.
- **Misconceptions worth a MisconceptionCheck:**
  - "Tet was a military victory for the Vietcong" — the VC were effectively destroyed militarily; but the political impact was a US defeat
  - "My Lai caused immediate public outrage in March 1968" — it was covered up for 14 months and only caused national outrage when revealed in November 1969
  - "My Lai was an isolated incident caused by one bad officer" — it was the product of free fire zone culture, body count pressure, and poor military discipline; 26 soldiers were charged; Calley was the only one convicted
  - "The US public turned against the war only because of Tet" — My Lai, the draft inequality, the human cost in US lives (58,000 killed), the living room war and the anti-war movement all contributed; Tet was the tipping point, not the sole cause
