## 2. Storyline

### Core takeaway

Act 1 Scene 5 is the most important scene for Lady Macbeth. Her soliloquy on receiving Macbeth's letter — and then her "unsex me here" invocation — establishes her as Shakespeare's most complex female character. She is ambitious, calculating and willing to use spiritually dangerous language; but Shakespeare complicates this immediately: her concern that Macbeth is "too full o' the milk of human kindness" reveals her own humanity. She understands goodness, which is why she knows she needs to suppress it in herself. This is not a one-dimensional villain; it is a woman who wills herself into evil because she sees no other path to what she wants. The tragedy of Lady Macbeth is that the suppression of her conscience leaves nothing behind — she has no tools left to cope when guilt arrives in Act 5.

### Evidence for the takeaway

- "Too full o' the milk of human kindness" — Lady Macbeth recognises Macbeth's moral hesitancy; she knows him better than he knows himself; this is a character who deeply understands goodness even as she plans to circumvent it
- "Come, you spirits / That tend on mortal thoughts, unsex me here" — Shakespeare presents her as if she is calling on spirits to strip her of female-coded qualities; for a Jacobean audience shaped by the 1604 Witchcraft Act and King James's interest in witchcraft, this would feel spiritually dangerous and transgressive
- "Take my milk for gall" — gall = bile; she wants her nurturing capacity replaced with poison; the inversion of maternal instinct (milk → gall) is deliberately shocking
- "Look like the innocent flower, but be the serpent under't" — she immediately provides the practical strategy Macbeth will follow; she is the planner and Macbeth, at this point, needs directing
- Macbeth asks darkness to hide his desires; Lady Macbeth asks darkness and spirits to remake her body and conscience. Shakespeare makes them independently choose concealment before they plan together
- "Leave all the rest to me" — at the end of the scene, she takes control; this power will diminish progressively until Act 5 when she collapses entirely

### Series throughline

Lady Macbeth's arc is the mirror of Macbeth's: she begins strong and ends broken; he begins morally aware and ends nihilistic. This episode establishes her at peak confidence and agency. Episode 6 (1.7) shows her manipulation of Macbeth; Episode 10 (3.2–3.4) shows the relationship inverting as Macbeth keeps secrets from her; Episode 12 (5.1) shows the sleepwalking — her suppressed conscience returning catastrophically. The phrase "take my milk for gall" in 1.5 reverberates in the sleepwalking scene's obsession with blood on her hands.

## 4. Content reference pack

### Dates & timeline

- Act 1 Scene 5: Lady Macbeth's first appearance; alone at Inverness castle
- She reads Macbeth's letter reporting the witches' prophecies
- First soliloquy: assessment of Macbeth's character and her plan
- Second soliloquy: "Come, you spirits" — the invocation to evil spirits
- Macbeth arrives; she immediately takes control: "look like the innocent flower"
- Duncan's arrival announced; Lady Macbeth takes charge of the plan

### Key people

- **Lady Macbeth:** Her soliloquies here give the audience her psychology at peak confidence; she is the dominant partner at this point; she understands both Macbeth's strengths ("brave" — she does not doubt his courage) and his weakness ("too full o' the milk of human kindness")
- **Macbeth:** Appears briefly; significantly more passive here than in his own soliloquy in 1.4; Lady Macbeth takes charge immediately on his arrival. His Episode 4 request for darkness to hide his desires is extended here by Lady Macbeth's request for darkness and spirits to remake her body and conscience
- **The messenger:** Brings news of Duncan's imminent arrival; provokes the second invocation

### Key terms & definitions

- **Soliloquy:** Extended speech when a character is alone; the audience accesses private thoughts; Lady Macbeth's two soliloquies in 1.5 are her most candid self-revelation before guilt distorts her
- **Imperatives:** Command verbs — "Come", "unsex", "take", "stop", "come thick night", "pall thee" — Lady Macbeth's language in the invocation is almost entirely commands; she attempts to direct supernatural forces as if they are servants
- **Milk / gall contrast:** Milk = nurture, motherhood, the domestic feminine role Jacobean women were expected to fulfil; gall = bile, poison; the contrast is an explicit inversion of expected female function
- **"Unsex me here":** "Unsex" does not mean make her a man; it means strip away the qualities (compassion, nurturing, hesitancy) that Jacobean culture associated with femininity and that Lady Macbeth sees as obstacles
- **Spirit invocation:** Shakespeare presents Lady Macbeth as if she is calling on spirits. For a Jacobean audience shaped by the 1604 Witchcraft Act and King James's interest in witchcraft, this would feel spiritually dangerous and transgressive
- **Serpent under the flower:** The imagery from the Gunpowder Plot medal — a serpent concealed by flowers; see AO3 context; Lady Macbeth instructs Macbeth to embody this exact image
- **Concealment motif:** Macbeth asks darkness to hide his desires in 1.4; Lady Macbeth asks darkness and spirits to remake her body and conscience in 1.5; together, the scenes show concealment becoming a shared pattern before the murder plot is finalised

### Case studies / named examples

- **"Too full o' the milk of human kindness":** Lady Macbeth's diagnosis of Macbeth. For AQA: the word "full" suggests an excess — it is not that Macbeth has *some* kindness but that he has *too much*. For AO3: she identifies his human moral capacity as the obstacle, and her solution is to suppress it through spiritual self-transformation in herself and manipulation in him. The word "milk" is part of the play's recurring maternal imagery — her goal in the invocation is to replace it with gall.
- **"Come, you spirits / That tend on mortal thoughts, unsex me here":** The invocation is addressed to spirits — "that tend on mortal thoughts" suggests spirits that attend to murderous human impulses. For a Jacobean audience shaped by the 1604 Witchcraft Act and King James's interest in witchcraft, Lady Macbeth's invocation would feel spiritually dangerous and transgressive. Lady Macbeth is crossing moral and religious boundaries, and Shakespeare presents this as part of her attempt to suppress conscience.
- **"Look like the innocent flower, but be the serpent under't":** She provides Macbeth with the appearance/reality strategy that defines the play's central theme. The serpent imagery connects to the devil in the Garden of Eden (AO3: biblical allusion; temptation) and to the Gunpowder Plot medal (AO3: Jacobean political context).
- **"Leave all the rest to me":** Her closing line to Macbeth in this scene. At this moment she is in complete control. The erosion of this control across subsequent episodes (she is excluded from the Banquo plot; she fails to control Macbeth at the banquet; she breaks down in 5.1) is the arc of the play's gender power reversal.
- **Concealment pair: "Stars, hide your fires" / "Come, thick night":** Macbeth asks darkness to hide his desires; Lady Macbeth asks darkness and spirits to remake her body and conscience. Shakespeare makes them independently choose concealment before they plan together, showing that both are already morally aligned before the murder plot becomes practical.

### Causes & effects

- Macbeth's letter arrives → Lady Macbeth immediately begins planning → she does not hesitate as Macbeth does; her ambition is immediate and unimpeded
- Lady Macbeth assesses Macbeth as "too kind" → she positions herself as the one who must supply the hardness → the marriage becomes a functional partnership divided by role (she plans; he executes)
- Invocation to spirits → attempts to suppress conscience and compassion → the tragedy is that suppressed conscience does not disappear; it surfaces later as sleepwalking guilt (5.1)
- "Unsex me here" → gender transgression in Jacobean terms → Lady Macbeth temporarily acquires what the play codes as "masculine" qualities (ruthlessness, decisiveness) at the cost of her mental health
- Macbeth chooses concealment in 1.4 → Lady Macbeth intensifies concealment in 1.5 → their shared secrecy makes Duncan's murder possible
- Duncan's arrival announced mid-scene → immediately Lady Macbeth begins working on Macbeth's appearance ("to beguile the time") → the performance of loyalty as concealment begins

**Key analytical moves for Lady Macbeth strength question:**
- YES she is strong: she plans the murder; she manipulates Macbeth; she invokes supernatural language; she remains calm after the murder when Macbeth cannot
- BUT: her "strength" requires the suppression of her natural conscience — she asks to be remade and stripped of compassion; this is not straightforward or secure strength
- NO she is not strong (whole play): she collapses in 5.1; she cannot say what has been done "undone" (3.2); the guilt she dismissed in 2.2 consumes her; she dies by suicide

**Misconceptions worth a MisconceptionCheck:**
- "Lady Macbeth is the villain of Macbeth" — OVERSIMPLIFIED: she is ambitious and manipulative but not without humanity; her diagnosis of Macbeth's "milk of human kindness" reveals her own understanding of goodness
- "'Unsex me here' means Lady Macbeth wants to be a man" — FALSE: she wants to be rid of qualities Jacobean culture coded as feminine; she is not seeking masculine identity but freedom from social constraints
- "Lady Macbeth controls Macbeth throughout the play" — FALSE: her control diminishes from Act 3 onwards; by the Banquo plot she is excluded entirely
- "Lady Macbeth is simply naturally strong" — OVERSIMPLIFIED: the invocation suggests she must suppress or transform parts of herself to become capable of the murder plot
