## Series throughline

A loyal warrior becomes a tyrant because ambition, supernatural temptation and moral weakness pull him away from loyalty, order and conscience. Shakespeare presents regicide as an act that corrupts the mind, destroys natural order, damages relationships and must finally be punished.

**The question every episode must answer:** How does Shakespeare use language, form and structure to present [theme/character] to a Jacobean audience, and how does this reflect the wider concerns of the play?

## Series-wide context reference

### Jacobean context (AO3)

- **King James I:** ascended 1603; personally fascinated by witchcraft; wrote *Daemonologie* (1597); Shakespeare wrote Macbeth c.1606 partly to flatter him
- **Gunpowder Plot (1605):** Catholic conspiracy to blow up Parliament; equivocation used as defence by Henry Garnet; shapes the Porter scene and the theme of treason
- **Divine right of kings:** kings appointed by God; killing a king = sin against God; Duncan's murder = sacrilege, not just crime
- **Great Chain of Being:** God â†’ angels â†’ king â†’ nobles â†’ common people â†’ animals â†’ plants; regicide shatters this hierarchy; unnatural events follow
- **Witchcraft:** Witches Act 1604; witchcraft a capital crime; Jacobeans genuinely feared demonic intervention
- **Jacobean gender:** women expected to be passive, obedient, domestic; Lady Macbeth's ambition and power-seeking reads as transgression
- **Tragedy as form:** Macbeth follows Aristotelian tragic structure â€” noble hero, fatal flaw (hamartia), downfall, anagnorisis (recognition), catharsis

### Key critical approaches (for AO1 sophistication)

- Macbeth is not simply evil â€” he is a capable man whose moral weakness is exploited by external forces and his own ambition
- Lady Macbeth is neither victim nor villain â€” she is a complex character whose power collapses under the guilt she initially refuses to feel
- The witches are ambiguous â€” they predict but do not compel; free will remains central
- Shakespeare uses the play to explore what happens when personal ambition overrides moral, social and divine order
