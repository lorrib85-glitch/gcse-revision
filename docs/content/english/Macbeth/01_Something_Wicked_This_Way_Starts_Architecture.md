## 2. Architecture checklist (tailored)

### Section 1 — Hook & Prior Recall
- **Purpose:** Surface existing knowledge, create narrative tension, prime retrieval. Warm the learner up to the act/scene territory before teaching begins.
- **Proposed content for this episode:**
  - What do students already know about witches in Shakespeare?
  - What does "fair is foul" mean? (pre-teach the paradox before the scene)
  - True/false warm-up: "The witches control Macbeth's actions" / "The play opens with a battle" / "Macbeth appears in the first scene"
  - Retrieval hook: Where do the witches appear in the play? (seeds episodes 3, 11)
- **Suggested components:**
  - `ChapterHookScreen` — true/false statements about the play's opening to surface misconceptions
  - `PriorKnowledgeRecall` — free-text recall of any prior knowledge about Macbeth/witches

### Section 2 — Plot & Dramatic Action
- **Purpose:** Teach what happens in the episode's scenes: events, character actions, dramatic turning points.
- **Proposed content for this episode:**
  - The storm: where? who? when? (a heath, three witches, before/during the battle)
  - The witches' 12-line scene: what do they say? who do they mention? when will they meet again?
  - Structural significance: why does Shakespeare open with this scene before introducing any human characters?
  - The scene's brevity: 12 lines — Shakespeare creates disorder then cuts away
  - Macbeth's name appears in line 8: "There to meet with Macbeth" — the protagonist is already associated with the witches before he appears
- **Suggested components:**
  - `VisualLearning` — cinematic scene sequence (storm → witches appear → they speak → they vanish) with narrated atmosphere
  - `ConceptReveal` — the dramatic function of the opening 12 lines

### Section 3 — Theme & Context
- **Purpose:** Connect the episode's events to the play's major themes and to AO3 Jacobean/Shakespearean context.
- **Proposed content for this episode:**
  - Theme: Supernatural — what Jacobean audiences believed about witches; King James I and *Daemonologie*; the Witches Act 1604
  - Theme: Appearance vs reality — introduced through paradox ("Fair is foul"); nothing can be trusted
  - Theme: Disorder — the storm as pathetic fallacy; the scene's structural position as chaos before order
  - Theme: Fate vs free will — the witches predict but do not compel; introduced here, answered across the whole play
  - Context: Holinshed's Chronicles (original source); Shakespeare changed "goddesses of destine" to witches to reflect Jacobean beliefs and flatter James I
  - Context: The word "Weird" — from Old English *wyrd* (fate); the Weird Sisters may be agents of fate itself
- **Suggested components:**
  - `TheoryCompareBlock` — contrast Jacobean belief about witches (real supernatural agents) with modern reading (psychological/metaphorical)
  - `SwipeSort` — sort context facts: "Jacobean belief" vs "Modern reading" vs "Shakespeare's craft"
  - `ExplainReveal` — cause-and-effect chain: King James → *Daemonologie* → witchcraft law → Shakespeare's witches → audience response

### Section 4 — Quote Analysis (AO2)
- **Purpose:** Active retrieval and analysis of key quotes from the episode.
- **Proposed content for this episode:**
  - **"Fair is foul, and foul is fair"**: paradox; moral inversion as the play's thesis; technique = chiasmus; effect = disorients the audience; whole-play link = Macbeth's echo in 1.3
  - **"When the battle's lost and won"**: equivocation introduced; a battle is *both* lost and won (by different sides); technique = paradox/equivocation; whole-play link = apparitions in 4.1
  - **"So foul and fair a day I have not seen"** (1.3 cross-reference): Macbeth's first words echo the witches; technique = echo/allusion; effect = implies connection
  - **"Double, double toil and trouble"** (4.1 cross-reference): rhyming couplets; incantatory rhythm; technique = rhyme and repetition
  - **"none of woman born"** (5.8): introduced here as a theme tag; the equivocation that will destroy Macbeth
  - Quote-to-analysis: students complete a PQAC (Point → Quote → Analysis → Context) frame for "Fair is foul, and foul is fair"
- **Suggested components:**
  - `MatchingTask` — match quote → technique → effect → whole-play link
  - `FillInTheBlanksBlock` — complete the analysis of "Fair is foul": "Shakespeare uses ______ to suggest that ______, which reflects the Jacobean belief that ______"
  - `InteractiveCollectionExplorer` — browse the episode's quotes by theme category

### Section 5 — Retrieval & Wider Play Links
- **Purpose:** Build whole-play awareness. Episode 1 has no earlier episodes to retrieve from, so it should make explicit forward links that will become retrieval anchors later.
- **Proposed content for this episode:**
  - Forward link: Where do the witches appear next? (1.3 — Episode 3) What do they do there?
  - Forward link: Macbeth's echo of "foul and fair" in 1.3 — what does it suggest about his connection to them?
  - Retrieval: What is equivocation? (seeds Episode 3, 8 Porter scene, Episode 11)
  - Retrieval: What is dramatic irony? (seeds Episodes 4, 6, 8)
  - Quick quiz: 3 questions on the scene's content and techniques
- **Suggested components:**
  - `QuickRecallScreen` — rapid-fire retrieval of the episode's key facts and terms
  - `RecoveryQuizPlayer` — 3-4 targeted questions if recall is weak

### Section 6 — Exam Practice
- **Purpose:** Final AQA-style practice moment. The final navigation/progress dot must land on exam prep.
- **Proposed content for this episode:**
  - Question stem: "Starting with this extract, explore how Shakespeare presents the supernatural in Macbeth." (Act 1.1 as practice extract)
  - Act 1 Scene 1 is high-value as a whole-play link for supernatural, disorder and appearance/reality questions. It can be used for practice, but students should understand it is more likely to support a wider-play argument than appear alone as a full exam extract.
  - Model paragraph: analysis of "Fair is foul, and foul is fair" using Point → Quote → Analysis → Context → Whole play
  - Guided response: students identify AO2 techniques in the scene, then use them in a structured paragraph
  - Mark scheme awareness: Level 3 = explains a technique; Level 4 = analyses + contextualises; Level 5 = conceptualised, convincing
- **Suggested components:**
  - `GuidedExamResponse` — scaffolded paragraph building on "Fair is foul" as the analytical anchor
  - `ExaminerExplainsScreen` — animated word-by-word reveal of what a Level 4/5 answer does differently

## 4. Build recommendations

1. **Storyline integration:** The core takeaway (the witches establish moral inversion as the play's founding principle) should be stated in Section 3 and practised in Section 6. In Section 3, teach the "Fair is foul" paradox as more than a quote — it is the structural logic of the entire play. In Section 6, the model paragraph should demonstrate how a student can use this as a conceptualised argument, not just a language point.

2. **Section 1 must address the primary misconception:** "The witches make Macbeth commit murder." The ChapterHookScreen true/false should include this statement and use the reveal to establish from the start that Macbeth has free will — this frames the entire series correctly.

3. **Section 2 — keep it short and atmospheric:** This is a 12-line scene. Do not pad it. Two or three screens on the dramatic function of brevity and disorder; the storm; Macbeth's name appearing before he appears. VisualLearning should be cinematic (dark, weather, minimal) — no cartoon cauldrons.

4. **Section 4 — prioritise "Fair is foul" for exam practice:** This is the quote most students know but most students cannot analyse properly beyond "it means good is bad." The FillInTheBlanksBlock should push them toward the play-wide implications of the paradox (the *structural* principle, not just the local meaning).

5. **Section 5 — use this episode to preview Episode 3:** Since there are no earlier episodes to retrieve from, use Section 5 to prime the learner for what comes next. The forward link to Macbeth's echo in 1.3 establishes continuity and seeds retrieval for the next session.
