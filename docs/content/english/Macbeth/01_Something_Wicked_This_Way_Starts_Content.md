## 2. Storyline

### Core takeaway

Shakespeare opens Macbeth with immediate moral disruption: before any human character speaks, three witches establish a world where normal categories — good and evil, winning and losing, fair and foul — cannot be trusted. The opening's power lies not in what the witches do but in what they represent: a universe where appearances are deceptive and where supernatural forces can exploit human ambition. This scene seeds every major theme of the play and conditions the audience to distrust everything that follows.

### Evidence for the takeaway

- The witches' opening appearance in a storm immediately signals chaos and the supernatural before any plot has developed
- "Fair is foul, and foul is fair" — paradox as the play's founding principle: moral inversion will define everything Macbeth does
- "When the battle's lost and won" — equivocation introduced in line 4; the witches speak truths that mislead; this same technique will destroy Macbeth in Act 4
- The witches' rhyming couplets create a ritual, incantatory atmosphere separate from the iambic pentameter of the human world
- "So foul and fair a day I have not seen" (1.3) — Macbeth's first words unconsciously echo the witches, implying he is already connected to them
- The scene's brevity (12 lines) is itself a structural choice: disorder is introduced, then cut — the audience is unsettled before the play properly begins
- Jacobean audiences would have recognised the witches as genuinely dangerous supernatural agents, not theatrical metaphors

### Series throughline

This episode is the keystone of the series. Every subsequent episode traces the consequences of the moral inversion the witches introduce here. The witches reappear in Episode 3 (1.3, prophecies), Episode 11 (4.1, apparitions) and are referenced in Episode 12 (5.8, the fulfilled equivocations). The appearance-vs-reality theme they introduce runs through every episode. The fate-vs-free-will tension they create is the spine of Macbeth's character arc.

### Exam framing

This episode's core takeaway is a winning wider-play argument for:
- "How does Shakespeare present the supernatural in Macbeth?" → The witches' function is not to cause events but to create a world where moral certainty is impossible
- "How does Shakespeare create disorder and chaos in Macbeth?" → The play's opening 12 lines establish the theme of disordered nature with unusual speed and force
- Extract-to-whole-play questions on supernatural, appearance/reality, fate/free will or disorder → Act 1 Scene 1 gives students concise evidence to connect the extract to the play's opening pattern

Act 1 Scene 1 is high-value as a whole-play link for supernatural, disorder and appearance/reality questions. It can be used for practice, but students should understand it is more likely to support a wider-play argument than appear alone as a full exam extract.

## 4. Content reference pack

### Dates & timeline

- c.1606 — Macbeth written, probably performed at court for James I
- 1597 — King James I writes *Daemonologie*, a treatise on witchcraft
- 1604 — Witches Act passed in England under James I, making witchcraft a capital crime
- 1605 — Gunpowder Plot (shapes equivocation theme, Porter scene)
- Act 1 Scene 1: brief opening scene, storm, three witches; 12 lines only

### Key people

- **The Three Witches (the Weird Sisters):** supernatural beings — ambiguously agents of fate or tempters; their prophecies activate Macbeth's ambition but do not compel action; the word "weird" derives from Old English *wyrd* (fate)
- **King James I:** James VI of Scotland, became James I of England 1603; wrote *Daemonologie* (1597); believed in witchcraft; Shakespeare likely shaped the witches partly to appeal to him
- **Hecate (Act 3 Scene 5):** goddess of witchcraft; appears later as the witches' superior — this later scene is often considered a non-Shakespearean interpolation

### Key terms & definitions

- **Paradox:** a statement that contradicts itself but contains truth — "Fair is foul, and foul is fair" — used to signal that normal moral categories are inverted in this play
- **Equivocation:** saying something true but misleading; speaking in ways that can be interpreted in multiple ways; central to the witches' prophecies throughout
- **Pathetic fallacy:** the storm at the opening mirrors the moral chaos the witches represent — weather as emotional/moral signal
- **Rhyming couplets:** the witches speak in rhyme, creating a ritual, incantatory tone separate from human speech; signals their otherness
- **Iambic pentameter:** the standard metre of Shakespearean verse — the witches deviate from it, emphasising their separation from the human world
- **The Weird Sisters:** the witches' alternative name; *wyrd* = fate in Old English — implies they may be agents of fate itself, not merely evil
- **Daemonologie:** James I's 1597 treatise on witchcraft; belief in witches as real supernatural agents was mainstream in Jacobean England

### Case studies / named examples

- **The storm opening:** Shakespeare opens not with a scene of court or battle but with chaos — a structural choice that signals disorder as the play's fundamental condition
- **"Fair is foul, and foul is fair" as the play's thesis statement:** this paradox encapsulates the entire play — every character who appears virtuous (Duncan: trusting, generous) is destroyed; every character who appears powerful (Macbeth at his peak) is morally hollow
- **Macbeth's echo (1.3.38):** "So foul and fair a day I have not seen" — Macbeth's first words unconsciously mirror the witches' paradox; Shakespeare signals his connection to them before the audience is told of the prophecies
- **The scene's brevity:** 12 lines; Shakespeare establishes the supernatural threat then cuts away — the audience is unsettled, curious, and prepared to distrust appearances

### Causes & effects

- Storm opens → audience associates witches with chaos and disorder before any human character appears
- Witches speak in rhyming couplets → creates ritual atmosphere; signals otherness from the human world
- "Fair is foul" paradox → audience primed to distrust all appearances throughout the play
- "When the battle's lost and won" → equivocation introduced immediately; Macbeth will later trust prophecies that use exactly this technique to mislead him
- Macbeth echoes "foul and fair" in 1.3 → audience understands he is already connected to the witches' moral world before the prophecies are made
- Jacobean audience context → witches perceived as genuinely dangerous supernatural agents, not metaphor; stakes are higher than for a modern reader
- King James's interest in witchcraft → Shakespeare's inclusion of the witches has a political/flattering dimension, giving the play cultural weight beyond entertainment

### Exam angles

**Common question types for this episode:**
- "How does Shakespeare present the supernatural in this extract and in the play as a whole?" (Act 1.1 can be used as a practice extract, but is more valuable as a whole-play link)
- "How does Shakespeare use the witches to explore the theme of appearance and reality?"
- "How does Shakespeare create atmosphere in the opening of Macbeth?"

**Mark scheme patterns / keywords to target:**
- AO1: "Shakespeare presents the witches as..." + embedded quote + development
- AO2: Name the technique ("paradox", "rhyme", "pathetic fallacy") → explain effect → contextualise
- AO3: Jacobean belief in witchcraft; King James; *Daemonologie*; the word "weird" and fate

**High-value analytical points:**
- The paradox "Fair is foul" is not just a witty line — it is the structural thesis of the entire play
- The rhyming couplets are not decorative — they signal the witches operate by different rules than the human world
- Macbeth's echo in 1.3 is structural foreshadowing — Shakespeare planned his connection to the witches from the start
- The witches predict but do not compel — free will remains; Macbeth makes his own choices

**Misconceptions worth a MisconceptionCheck:**
- "The witches cause Macbeth to commit murder" — FALSE: they predict, they tempt, but Macbeth chooses; Banquo hears the same prophecies and makes different choices
- "Fair is foul means good is bad" — OVERSIMPLIFIED: it means appearances cannot be trusted; moral categories are unreliable, not simply inverted
- "The witches are just theatrical atmosphere" — FALSE for a Jacobean audience: witches were believed to be genuinely dangerous agents of the Devil
- "The storm is just weather" — FALSE: pathetic fallacy; the storm is Shakespeare's signal that nature itself is disturbed
