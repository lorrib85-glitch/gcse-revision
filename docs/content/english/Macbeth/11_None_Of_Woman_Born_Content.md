## 2. Storyline

### Core takeaway

Act 4 is the act of consequences and foils. The witches' second visit in 4.1 completes the trap that the first visit began: having made Macbeth trust them in Act 1, they now give him prophecies that sound reassuring but are designed to destroy him. Macbeth hears certainty where Shakespeare gives ambiguity. That is the whole danger of equivocation: the words are literally true, but Macbeth's interpretation is fatally wrong.

At the same time, Act 4 widens the play from Macbeth's mind to Scotland's suffering. Lady Macduff and her son show the innocent domestic world Macbeth's tyranny destroys. Macduff's grief in 4.3 shows a genuine human response to evil: not ambition, not performance, but love, guilt, sorrow and righteous anger. Malcolm's testing of Macduff also matters: it defines what a good king should be by imagining what a terrible king would be. Macbeth is not just personally corrupt; he is politically unfit.

### Evidence for the takeaway

- "Something wicked this way comes" — the first witch's description of Macbeth; the witches now see him as a force of evil approaching them, not a victim they are tempting
- "Beware Macduff" — first apparition; the only warning Macbeth should treat seriously
- "None of woman born / Shall harm Macbeth" — second apparition; Macbeth interprets this as total safety, but the wording is equivocal
- "Macbeth shall never vanquished be until / Great Birnam Wood to high Dunsinane Hill / Shall come against him" — third apparition; Macbeth hears impossibility, but Shakespeare has planted a literal fulfilment
- The show of kings — Banquo's descendants appear as a line of future kings; this confirms that Macbeth has not defeated the original prophecy
- "From this moment / The very firstlings of my heart shall be / The firstlings of my hand" — Macbeth collapses the gap between thought and action; moral hesitation has vanished
- "He wants the natural touch" — Lady Macduff suggests Macduff lacks natural feeling by leaving his family; this complicates Macduff and keeps him from becoming a flat hero
- "Poor bird! thou'dst never fear the net nor lime" — Lady Macduff's image of her son as a vulnerable bird intensifies the innocence of the household Macbeth attacks
- "Bleed, bleed, poor country" — Macduff presents Scotland itself as a wounded body under Macbeth's rule
- "This tyrant, whose sole name blisters our tongues" — Malcolm presents Macbeth as so diseased and corrupt that even naming him causes pain
- "Fit to govern? No, not to live" — Macduff rejects Malcolm's pretend vices, showing that his loyalty is to Scotland, not blind ambition
- "The king-becoming graces" — Malcolm lists the virtues of good kingship, directly contrasting Macbeth's tyranny
- "All my pretty ones? / Did you say all? O hell-kite! All?" — Macduff's grief at the loss of his family; repetition performs devastation
- "He has no children" — Macduff's grief turns towards Macbeth; this can imply Macbeth cannot understand parental loss, or that revenge cannot fully repay what Macbeth has done
- "Dispute it like a man" / "I shall do so; / But I must also feel it as a man" — Macduff redefines masculinity as feeling grief, not suppressing it
- "Front to front / Bring thou this fiend of Scotland and myself" — Macduff's resolve; unlike Macbeth, his violence is motivated by justice, grief and the restoration of Scotland

### Series throughline

This episode completes the equivocation arc that began in Episode 1 (witches establish paradox) and deepened in Episode 3 through Banquo's warning: "instruments of darkness... win us with honest trifles, to betray's in deepest consequence". The witches do exactly that in Episode 11. They give Macbeth statements with truthful surfaces and deadly meanings.

The Banquo line also returns through the show of kings, proving Macbeth's violence has failed to control the future. Macbeth's decision to act on the "firstlings" of his heart continues the Act 3 blood-cycle logic: once he believes he is too deep in blood to return, he acts faster and more brutally. Macduff's grief and Malcolm's kingship test set up Episode 12: Scotland will be restored not by personal ambition, but by legitimate rule and morally directed resistance.

### Exam framing

High priority for:
- "How does Shakespeare present the supernatural?" — the second visit to the witches is the crucial comparison with the first; the equivocations are more sophisticated and more fatal
- "How does Shakespeare present Macduff?" — 4.2 and 4.3 establish him as Macbeth's moral foil, but also complicate him through his absence from his family
- "How does Shakespeare present tyranny?" — the attack on Macduff's household is the clearest act of tyranny because it targets innocence and family
- "How does Shakespeare present kingship?" — Malcolm's testing scene defines good kingship against Macbeth's tyranny
- "How does Shakespeare present masculinity?" — Macduff insists that feeling grief is part of being a man, directly challenging Macbeth and Lady Macbeth's earlier brutal model of masculinity

## 4. Content reference pack

### Dates & timeline

- Act 4 Scene 1: Macbeth visits the witches; the cauldron ritual; the three apparitions; the show of kings confirming Banquo's line; Macbeth learns of Macduff's flight and decides to "seize upon Fife"
- Act 4 Scene 2: Lady Macduff and her son discuss Macduff's absence; their conversation humanises the household before Macbeth's tyranny reaches them
- Act 4 Scene 3: Malcolm and Macduff in England; Malcolm tests Macduff's loyalty by falsely claiming vices; Ross arrives; Macduff learns what has happened to his family; the English army prepares

### Key people

- **The Three Witches (second visit):** Their role has shifted. They no longer need to tempt Macbeth directly; he seeks them out. They give him language that sounds like certainty but works as a trap.
- **The Three Apparitions:** First: armed head, warning Macbeth to beware Macduff and also foreshadowing violent conflict. Second: bloody child, linked to Macduff's unusual birth and the "none of woman born" equivocation. Third: crowned child with tree, linked to Malcolm/Birnam Wood and the restoration of legitimate rule. Each image is symbolic; Macbeth cannot read the symbols correctly.
- **Macbeth:** Now acts on information rather than moral deliberation. His decision to act on the "firstlings" of his heart shows he has eliminated the gap between impulse and action.
- **Lady Macduff:** A brief but important character. Her anger at Macduff's absence complicates him, while her affection for her son makes Macbeth's tyranny feel domestic and human rather than abstract.
- **Macduff's son:** His exchange with his mother is witty and vulnerable. His innocence makes the household scene emotionally important, not just a plot device.
- **Macduff:** Absent when his family is attacked; this absence becomes the source of his guilt as well as grief. His response in 4.3 is central to Shakespeare's presentation of humane masculinity.
- **Malcolm:** Tests Macduff by pretending to be a worse ruler than Macbeth. This verifies that Macduff's loyalty is to Scotland, not personal advantage, and allows Shakespeare to define good kingship.
- **Ross:** Messenger figure who delays the full truth before telling Macduff. His hesitation increases dramatic tension and makes the grief scene unfold painfully.

### Key terms & definitions

- **Equivocation:** Deliberately misleading half-truths. The witches' prophecies are not simply false; they are literally true but designed to make Macbeth reach the wrong conclusion.
- **Apparition:** A supernatural vision. In 4.1 the apparitions are symbolic stage images, not just messages.
- **Show of kings:** A line of kings descended from Banquo. This confirms the prophecy Macbeth has been trying to stop since Act 3.
- **Foil:** A character used to contrast another. Macduff is Macbeth's moral foil: both use violence, but one acts from tyranny and one from grief/justice.
- **Tyranny:** Rule through fear, cruelty and self-interest. Macbeth's Act 4 behaviour shows tyranny moving from political threat to domestic suffering.
- **"The firstlings of my heart shall be / The firstlings of my hand":** Macbeth has collapsed the moral gap between impulse and action; this is the fullest expression of his moral deterioration.
- **Caesarean birth (AO3):** In the 17th century, a Caesarean section was usually associated with extreme danger to the mother. "Untimely ripped" makes Macduff's birth outside the normal process, which is why the prophecy's wording can technically hold.
- **Henry Garnet and equivocation (AO3):** The doctrine of equivocation — saying something literally true but morally misleading — was associated with Jesuit priest Henry Garnet after the Gunpowder Plot trial. Shakespeare references equivocation in the Porter scene and dramatises it through the witches.
- **King-becoming graces:** Malcolm's list of kingly virtues: justice, truth, temperance, mercy, generosity, perseverance, courage, fortitude and patience. The list defines what Macbeth lacks.

### Case studies / named examples

- **"Something wicked this way comes":** The first witch says this of Macbeth before he arrives. For AQA: the reversal is striking — in 1.3, the witches were the wicked figures approaching Macbeth; now Macbeth is the wicked thing approaching them. This is the clearest single-line indication of Macbeth's transformation from tempted hero to active evil.

- **The three apparitions (equivocation exam practice):**
  - "Beware Macduff" — the direct warning Macbeth should obey
  - "None of woman born shall harm Macbeth" — equivocal; Macbeth hears invincibility, but the wording leaves space for Macduff
  - "Macbeth shall never vanquished be until / Great Birnam Wood to high Dunsinane Hill / Shall come against him" — equivocal; Macbeth hears impossibility, but the soldiers later carry branches
  For AQA: the key analytical point is that the prophecies are designed to be misread. Shakespeare lets the audience see the linguistic trap while Macbeth hears reassurance.

- **The show of kings:** Macbeth demands to know if Banquo's line will reign, and the vision confirms it. For AO1, this means Macbeth's Act 3 violence has failed. For AO2, the stage image of repeated kings creates a visual future Macbeth cannot stop.

- **"From this moment / The very firstlings of my heart shall be / The firstlings of my hand":** Macbeth no longer debates, hesitates or imagines consequences as he did in 1.7. The repetition of "firstlings" makes action immediate and impulsive. Heart and hand collapse together: desire becomes deed.

- **Lady Macduff and the son:** Their scene gives tyranny a human cost. Lady Macduff's "He wants the natural touch" criticises Macduff's absence and introduces the idea of natural family feeling. Her bird imagery — "Poor bird!" — presents the household as vulnerable and innocent.

- **"Bleed, bleed, poor country":** Macduff imagines Scotland as a wounded body. This is excellent for essays on tyranny because Macbeth's rule damages the whole nation, not just individual enemies.

- **"This tyrant, whose sole name blisters our tongues":** Malcolm's image makes Macbeth's name physically painful. For AO2, "blisters" suggests disease, burning and infection. Macbeth has become a contaminating force.

- **Malcolm's false confession:** Malcolm pretends to be lustful, greedy and cruel to test whether Macduff cares about Scotland or merely wants power. Macduff's cry — "O Scotland, Scotland!" — proves his loyalty is national and moral, not selfish.

- **"Fit to govern? No, not to live":** Macduff rejects the idea that a corrupt ruler should rule simply because he is next in line. This gives students a sharp contrast with Macbeth: legitimate kingship requires moral fitness, not just power.

- **"All my pretty ones? / Did you say all?":** Macduff's grief. For AQA: the repetition of "all" performs the mind trying to absorb incomprehensible loss. For AO2: the rhetorical question refuses to accept the information; it is the sound of grief before it becomes anger.

- **"He has no children":** This is deliberately compressed and interpretable. It may mean Macbeth cannot understand the loss of children; it may also mean Macduff cannot take equivalent revenge. Either way, Shakespeare presents grief as something revenge cannot fully satisfy.

- **"Give sorrow words; the grief that does not speak / Whispers the o'erfraught heart and bids it break":** Malcolm's advice to Macduff. This is the play's clearest statement about the need to express grief. It contrasts with Lady Macbeth's suppressed guilt and Macbeth's distorted public performances.

- **"Dispute it like a man" / "I shall do so; / But I must also feel it as a man":** Malcolm initially frames response as masculine combat; Macduff corrects this. Shakespeare presents real masculinity as including feeling, grief and love — not just violence.

### Causes & effects

- Macbeth seeks out the witches → agency reverses from 1.3 → he actively seeks supernatural certainty → the witches exploit this desire through equivocation
- Apparitions give false security → Macbeth believes himself protected → he acts on impulse → tyranny accelerates
- The show of kings confirms Banquo's line → Macbeth realises he cannot control succession → his violence becomes more frantic and less rational
- Macduff's household is attacked → Macduff's grief becomes righteous anger → his motivation is love and justice, not ambition
- Malcolm tests Macduff → Macduff proves loyalty to Scotland → the play defines legitimate kingship by contrast with Macbeth
- Macduff speaks grief → he becomes capable of morally directed action → contrast with Macbeth and Lady Macbeth, whose unprocessed guilt destroys them

### Exam angles

**Common question types:**
- "How does Shakespeare present the supernatural?" — compare 1.3 and 4.1: first visit tempts; second visit traps through false security; equivocation is more sophisticated in Act 4
- "How does Shakespeare present Macduff?" — 4.2 complicates him through absence; 4.3 defines him through grief, loyalty and moral courage
- "How does Shakespeare present tyranny?" — Macbeth's rule damages families, Scotland and natural order
- "How does Shakespeare present kingship?" — Malcolm's testing scene defines good kingship and exposes Macbeth by contrast
- "How does Shakespeare present masculinity?" — Macduff shows that feeling grief is not weakness; it is part of moral manhood
- "How does Shakespeare present Scotland?" — the country is wounded, diseased and waiting for restoration

**Misconceptions worth a MisconceptionCheck:**
- "The witches' prophecies are just wrong" — FALSE: they are equivocal, literally true but misleading
- "The witches tell Macbeth he cannot be killed" — FALSE: Macbeth misreads the wording; they do not say he is invincible
- "Macbeth attacks Macduff's household because the witches order it" — FALSE: Macbeth chooses to act after hearing Macduff has fled; this shows his tyranny
- "Macduff is a perfect hero" — TOO SIMPLE: Lady Macduff criticises his absence, and Macduff later has to face guilt as well as grief
- "Malcolm's testing scene is irrelevant" — FALSE: it defines legitimate kingship and proves Macduff's loyalty to Scotland
- "Feeling grief makes Macduff weak" — FALSE: Shakespeare presents grief as morally necessary; Macduff must feel before he can fight justly
