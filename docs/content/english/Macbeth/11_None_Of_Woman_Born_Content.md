## 2. Storyline

### Core takeaway

Act 4 is the act of consequences and foils. The witches' second visit (4.1) completes the trap that the first visit began: having made Macbeth trust them in Act 1, they now give him prophecies that seem reassuring but are designed to destroy him. His overconfidence ("the spirits that know / All mortal consequences have pronounced me thus: / 'Fear not, Macbeth'") is the final expression of a character who has learned nothing from the first set of prophecies. Meanwhile, Act 4 introduces Macduff as Macbeth's moral counter — the murder of Macduff's family (4.2) and Macduff's grief (4.3) show what genuine human response to tyranny looks like: not ambition, not supernatural assistance, but love, loss and righteous anger.

### Evidence for the takeaway

- "Something wicked this way comes" — the first witch's description of Macbeth; the witches now see him as a force of evil approaching them, not a victim they are tempting; the reversal is significant
- "Beware Macduff" — first apparition; the only warning that proves directly true; the others are all equivocations
- "None of woman born / Shall harm Macbeth" — second apparition; Macbeth interprets "none" to mean "no person ever born"; it means "no person born in the conventional way"; Macduff's Caesarean birth is the equivocation
- "Birnam Wood to Dunsinane" — third apparition; Macbeth interprets it as impossible; it comes true through the soldiers carrying branches; both prophecies are equivocal — literally true, not as Macbeth understood
- "All my pretty ones? / Did you say all? O hell-kite! All? / What, all my pretty chickens and their dam / At one fell swoop?" — Macduff's grief at the murder of his family; the repetition of "all" performs devastation; Shakespeare contrasts this genuine loss with Macbeth's hollow power
- "Front to front / Bring thou this fiend of Scotland and myself" — Macduff's resolve; unlike Macbeth, his motivation is love and grief, not ambition; this is the legitimate use of violence contrasted with Macbeth's

### Series throughline

This episode completes the equivocation arc that began in Episode 1 (witches establish paradox) and deepened in Episode 3 (Banquo's warning: "instruments of darkness... win us with honest trifles, to betray's in deepest consequence"). The Banquo warning in Episode 3 is exactly what happens to Macbeth in Episode 11. The introduction of Macduff as the avenger completes the setup for Episode 12 (5.8 — "Macduff was from his mother's womb / Untimely ripped"). Act 4.3's Malcolm/Macduff scene also introduces Malcolm as a legitimate heir — the restoration of order that will close the play.

### Exam framing

High priority for:
- "How does Shakespeare present the supernatural?" — the second visit to the witches is the crucial comparison with the first (1.3); the equivocations are more sophisticated and more fatal
- "How does Shakespeare present Macduff?" — 4.2 and 4.3 establish him as the moral foil to Macbeth
- "How does Shakespeare present tyranny in Macbeth?" — the murder of Macduff's family is the most clearly tyrannical act; Macbeth has no strategic reason to kill children

## 4. Content reference pack

### Dates & timeline

- Act 4 Scene 1: Macbeth visits the witches; the three apparitions; the eighth king — Banquo's line; Macbeth learns of Macduff's flight and decides to "seize upon Fife"
- Act 4 Scene 2: Lady Macduff and her son; the murderers arrive; mother and children are killed
- Act 4 Scene 3: Malcolm and Macduff in England; Malcolm tests Macduff's loyalty by falsely claiming vices; Macduff's grief on learning of his family's murder; the English army prepares

### Key people

- **The Three Witches (second visit):** Their role has shifted — they no longer tempt Macbeth but confirm him in his path; he seeks them out this time; the power dynamic has reversed (they serve his demand for information now, but only because this will destroy him)
- **The Three Apparitions:** First (armed head): beware Macduff. Second (bloody child): none of woman born. Third (crowned child with tree): Macbeth safe until Birnam Wood comes to Dunsinane. Each is literally true and fatally misleading.
- **Macbeth:** Now acts on information rather than moral deliberation; his decision to kill Macduff's family is reactive and savage — "the firstlings of my heart shall be / The firstlings of my hand" — he has eliminated the gap between impulse and action
- **Lady Macduff:** A brief but important character; her murder is the starkest example of innocent victims of tyranny; her conversation with her son about her husband's "flight" humanises the family before they are killed
- **Macduff's son:** His exchange with his mother ("And must they all be hanged that swear and lie?") is both comic and heartbreaking; he understands the political world better than might be expected; his death is one of the most affecting in the play
- **Macduff:** Absent when his family is killed; this absence (he has gone to England) will become the source of his guilt as well as his grief; "All my pretty ones" is the emotional climax of Act 4
- **Malcolm:** Testing Macduff by pretending to be a worse tyrant than Macbeth (lust, avarice, cruelty) — this is a device to verify that Macduff's loyalty is to Scotland, not to personal ambition; it also defines what good kingship looks like by negative example

### Key terms & definitions

- **Equivocation:** Deliberately misleading half-truths; "none of woman born" is literally true (Macduff was born by Caesarean section; some editions/interpretations say he was surgically removed before birth, making him "not of woman born" in the technical sense); the witches' language is always literally true and fatally misleading — this is equivocation in action
- **Apparition:** A supernatural vision; each apparition takes a symbolic form (armed head = Macduff; bloody child = Macduff untimely ripped; crowned child = Malcolm); Macbeth cannot read these symbols correctly
- **"The firstlings of my heart shall be / The firstlings of my hand":** Macbeth has collapsed the moral gap between impulse and action; he will now act on every thought without deliberation; this is the fullest expression of his moral collapse
- **Caesarean birth (AO3):** In the 17th century, a Caesarean section was performed only when the mother was dead or dying; "untimely ripped" means surgically removed from a dead or dying mother; Macduff's birth is not supernatural but it is outside the normal process — this is the equivocation's "literal truth"
- **Henry Garnet and equivocation (AO3):** The doctrine of equivocation (saying something literally true but morally misleading) was used by Jesuit priest Henry Garnet as a defence in the Gunpowder Plot trial (1606); he was executed; Shakespeare references him in the Porter scene (2.3); the witches embody equivocation as a structural principle of the play

### Case studies / named examples

- **"Something wicked this way comes":** The first witch says this of Macbeth before he arrives. For AQA: the reversal is striking — in 1.3, the witches were the wicked thing approaching Macbeth; now he is the wicked thing approaching them. This is the clearest single-line indication of Macbeth's transformation from victim to agent of evil.

- **The three apparitions (equivocation exam practice):** 
  - "Beware Macduff" — literally and straightforwardly true; the only honest prophecy
  - "None of woman born shall harm Macbeth" — equivocal; Macduff is untimely ripped; Macbeth hears it as a guarantee; it is a trap
  - "Macbeth shall never vanquished be until / Great Birnam Wood to high Dunsinane Hill / Shall come against him" — equivocal; the soldiers carry branches; Macbeth hears "impossible" and finds false security
  For AQA: the key analytical point is that the prophecies are designed to be misread. Shakespeare ensures the audience sees the equivocal language but Macbeth does not.

- **"All my pretty ones? / Did you say all?":** Macduff's grief. For AQA: the repetition of "all" (three times) performs the mind trying to absorb an incomprehensible loss. For AO2: the rhetorical question — "Did you say all?" — refuses to accept the information; it is the sound of grief before it becomes anger. For AO3: the Jacobean concept of household as microcosm of the state; Macbeth has destroyed a family/household, which mirrors his destruction of Scotland itself.

- **"Give sorrow words; the grief that does not speak / Whispers the o'erfraught heart and bids it break":** Malcolm's advice to Macduff. For AQA: this is the play's clearest statement about the function of language in processing grief. It also contrasts with Macbeth, whose psychological collapse comes precisely because his grief/guilt *cannot* be expressed publicly. Speaking sorrow is healthy; suppressing it (Lady Macbeth) or performing it falsely (Macbeth at the banquet) is destructive.

### Causes & effects

- Macbeth seeks out the witches → reversal of agency from 1.3 → he is now actively seeking supernatural reassurance → the witches exploit this desire for certainty to give him equivocal prophecies he will believe
- Apparitions give false security → Macbeth believes himself invulnerable → "The firstlings of my heart shall be the firstlings of my hand" → he murders Macduff's family without strategic calculation → this is his most clearly tyrannical act
- Macduff's family murdered → Macduff's grief becomes righteous anger → his motivation is love, not ambition → he becomes the instrument of legitimate justice
- Malcolm tests Macduff in 4.3 → the testing scene defines legitimate kingship (it includes: justice, truth, moderation, mercy, liberality, perseverance, courage, devotion, patience, etc.) → contrasts with Macbeth's tyranny → prepares the restoration of Act 5

### Exam angles

**Common question types:**
- "How does Shakespeare present the supernatural?" — compare 1.3 and 4.1: first visit tempts; second visit traps through false security; the equivocation is more sophisticated in Act 4
- "How does Shakespeare present Macduff in Macbeth?" — 4.2 (his absent family) and 4.3 (his grief and resolve) are the essential evidence
- "How does Shakespeare present tyranny?" — the murder of Macduff's family is the clearest act of naked tyranny without strategic necessity

**Misconceptions worth a MisconceptionCheck:**
- "The witches' prophecies are just wrong" — FALSE: they are equivocal — literally true but misleading; "none of woman born" is literally true (Macduff's untimely birth); this is the sophistication of the trap
- "Macduff is a straightforward hero" — NUANCED: in 4.3, Malcolm criticises him for leaving his family unprotected; Macduff must grapple with this; he is not without fault
- "The second visit to the witches is the same as the first" — FALSE: in 1.3 the witches approach Macbeth; in 4.1 he seeks them out; the agency has reversed; this shows how far he has deteriorated
