## 2. Storyline

### Core takeaway

The murder of Duncan is the pivot of the entire play. Shakespeare does not dramatise the murder itself — it happens offstage — but surrounds it with the richest psychological imagery in English drama. Before the murder: Macbeth hallucinates a dagger; his language fractures; he hears words that unsettle him. After the murder: he cannot say "Amen"; he hears a voice declare "Macbeth does murder sleep"; he brings the daggers back when he should have left them; he is paralysed. Lady Macbeth attempts practical damage control but her confidence is already defensive rather than genuine. Shakespeare's argument is clear: the murder damages the murderer immediately, catastrophically, and permanently. There is no moment of triumph.

### Evidence for the takeaway

- "Is this a dagger which I see before me?" — the hallucination precedes the murder; Macbeth's mind is already unravelling under the pressure of what he is about to do; or the supernatural is participating
- "I go, and it is done" — Macbeth's speech before committing the murder is almost minimalist; the elaborate moral reasoning of 1.7 is replaced by a soldier's clipped command to himself
- "I could not say 'Amen'" — immediately after the murder, Macbeth feels spiritually cut off from God's blessing and forgiveness; his first words after killing are about spiritual rupture
- "Macbeth does murder sleep" — the voice Macbeth hears generalises the crime: sleep is universal human restoration; to murder sleep is to murder all of humanity's peace, not just Duncan's
- "Will all great Neptune's ocean wash this blood clean from my hand?" — immediately establishes that the murder cannot be undone; blood = guilt; no natural force, even the ocean, can cleanse it
- "A little water clears us of this deed" — Lady Macbeth's dismissal of the blood imagery; dramatically ironic given her sleepwalking scene obsession with blood

### Series throughline

Every subsequent episode is a consequence of this one. The hallucination returns (Banquo's ghost, 3.4). The sleep motif threads through (Lady Macbeth's sleepwalking, 5.1). The blood motif expands from Macbeth's question in 2.2 to Lady Macbeth's "the smell of the blood still" in 5.1. The "Amen" moment — Macbeth's first indication that he feels spiritually severed from God's blessing — anticipates Episode 8 (2.3–2.4, the unnatural events that follow regicide) and Episode 12's nihilism. Lady Macbeth's dismissal "A little water" makes her collapse in 5.1 more powerful: she said it wouldn't matter; it does.

## 4. Content reference pack

### Dates & timeline

- Act 2 Scene 1: Late night at Inverness; Banquo and Fleance cross the courtyard; Macbeth is preparing; the dagger hallucination
- Act 2 Scene 2: Immediately after the murder; Macbeth returns with the daggers; Lady Macbeth attempts control; the scene ends with knocking at the south gate

### Key people

- **Macbeth:** At his most psychologically complex in Act 2; his language moves from the controlled heroism of Act 1 to fractured, hallucinatory, guilt-saturated speech; the murder damages him instantaneously
- **Lady Macbeth:** Ostensibly in control — she notes she had drugged the grooms; she returns the daggers when Macbeth cannot; she provides the practical voice; but her confidence is brittle ("I am afraid to think what I have done" — she says she's *not* afraid but the admission of the possibility is telling)
- **Banquo:** Present in 2.1; cannot sleep; has been praying; establishes the motif of difficulty sleeping before the murder that anticipates "Macbeth does murder sleep"
- **Duncan:** Murdered offstage; mentioned as a guest who will "not be disturbed" — black dramatic irony

### Key terms & definitions

- **Hallucination:** The air-drawn dagger; it may be supernatural or psychological; Shakespeare deliberately leaves this ambiguous; it functions as both — a sign of Macbeth's unstable mind and a possible supernatural omen
- **Motif:** A recurring image or idea; blood and sleep are the two central motifs of this episode and they run through the entire play
- **Stagecraft:** The decision to keep the murder offstage is a deliberate artistic choice — the audience's imagination creates something more disturbing than any staging could; it also means the murder is always filtered through description and hallucination, never directly shown
- **"Amen":** In Christian theology, "Amen" seals a prayer and invokes God's blessing; Macbeth's inability to say it suggests he feels spiritually cut off from God's blessing and forgiveness
- **Neptune:** Roman god of the sea; "Will all great Neptune's ocean" — the greatest natural force imaginable cannot cleanse the guilt; Shakespeare is using classical mythology to express the immense weight of the crime

### Case studies / named examples

- **"Is this a dagger which I see before me?":** The dagger hallucination is one of the most famous moments in English drama. For AQA AO2: the rhetorical question is addressed to the dagger itself, not to the audience — Macbeth is genuinely unsure of his own perception. The repeated soft /s/ sounds create a whispering, uncertain rhythm; Macbeth seems to be testing reality as he speaks. The word "sensible" (able to be felt by the senses) reveals he tries to touch it and cannot. The phrase "heat-oppressed brain" suggests he half-attributes it to fever — he is trying to rationalise a supernatural event and failing.

- **AQA Nov 2021 extract (Act 2.2) — Relationship analysis:**
  - Macbeth: "Methought I heard a voice cry 'Sleep no more'" — he is reporting a supernatural experience; he is passive, shocked, destabilised
  - Lady Macbeth: "Who was it that thus cried? Why, worthy thane, / You do unbend your noble strength to think / So brain-sickly of things" — she attempts to redirect him practically; the word "brain-sickly" is interesting: she diagnoses his response as illness rather than conscience
  - Macbeth: "I'll go no more. / I am afraid to think what I have done; / Look on't again, I dare not" — he refuses to return the daggers; his paralysis is moral, not physical
  - Lady Macbeth: "Infirm of purpose! / Give me the daggers" — she takes control; "Infirm" echoes masculine/feminine coding (weakness = infirmity); she positions herself as the stronger one
  - AQA Nov 2021 indicative content (AO1): "Lady Macbeth taking control of the situation in the extract and elsewhere"; "Macbeth's shielding Lady Macbeth from the planned murder of Banquo/Fleance"
  - AQA Nov 2021 indicative content (AO2): "contrast between language of Macbeth/Lady Macbeth in the extract"; "tracking the change in the relationship — almost switching roles"
  - AQA Nov 2021 indicative content (AO3): "gender issues/roles within their relationship: shifts of power/control"; "ideas about conscience in relation to Macbeth and Lady Macbeth"

- **"Will all great Neptune's ocean wash this blood clean from my hand?":** For AQA: the contrast with Lady Macbeth's "A little water clears us of this deed" in the same scene is the most important dramatic irony in the episode. She is wrong, as Act 5 proves. Macbeth is right: the blood is permanent. The ocean/water contrast also sets up the sleepwalking scene (5.1) where she is obsessed with trying to "wash" blood from her hands.

- **"Macbeth does murder sleep":** Sleep = the "innocent" thing murdered alongside Duncan; sleep is the natural restorative of all humanity; to murder it is to murder peace, recovery, innocence. The repetition of "sleep no more" (three times in quick succession) creates a mantra-like quality — this is both a hallucinated voice and a self-fulfilling prophecy.

### Causes & effects

- Macbeth hallucinates dagger → ambiguity about supernatural involvement → or his mind is already collapsing under the pressure of the decision → his psychological state is fragile before he commits the act
- Murder committed offstage → audience imagination engaged → the horror is personal/interpreted, not spectacle → Shakespeare's choice raises the moral weight rather than the theatrical impact
- "I could not say Amen" → Macbeth feels spiritually cut off from God's blessing and forgiveness → this begins the arc toward nihilism ("Tomorrow" speech in 5.5)
- Macbeth brings back the daggers → Lady Macbeth must return them → she enters the murder chamber; Macbeth cannot → this is the first role reversal; she is more capable of action; he is more consumed by conscience
- Lady Macbeth dismisses blood as easily washed → "A little water clears us of this deed" → the dramatic irony: in 5.1 she cannot stop washing her hands in her sleep

### Exam angles

**AQA Nov 2021 actual exam question:**
- Extract: Act 2.2 (Macbeth and Lady Macbeth immediately after the murder)
- Question: "How does Shakespeare present the relationship between Macbeth and Lady Macbeth?"
- **Model paragraph (extract focus, AO2-led):** "In the extract, Shakespeare presents Lady Macbeth as the more practically capable partner through her use of imperatives. When she commands 'Give me the daggers', the monosyllabic force of the command contrasts with Macbeth's fragmented sentences, suggesting she has maintained composure while he has collapsed. However, the word 'Infirm' — meaning weak-willed — echoes the masculine/feminine coding of their earlier exchange in Act 1.7, where she challenged his manhood to compel the murder. Now the roles are reinforced: Lady Macbeth performs strength; Macbeth performs collapse."

**Misconceptions worth a MisconceptionCheck:**
- "The dagger hallucination proves the witches caused Macbeth to commit murder" — UNCERTAIN: the hallucination may be supernatural (the witches' influence) or psychological (his own unstable mind); Shakespeare deliberately leaves it ambiguous
- "Lady Macbeth is calm and unaffected after the murder" — FALSE: her later sleepwalking proves the guilt she dismisses here is merely suppressed; she is not unaffected, she is performing control
- "Macbeth feels no guilt about killing Duncan" — FALSE: the Amen moment and the Neptune speech prove instant and overwhelming guilt
