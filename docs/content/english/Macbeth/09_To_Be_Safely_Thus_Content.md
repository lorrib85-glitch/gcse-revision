## 2. Storyline

### Core takeaway

Act 3 Scene 1 marks the transition of Macbeth from an influenced murderer to an independent tyrant. He now holds the crown but cannot enjoy it: his fear of Banquo, whose prophesied royal line threatens to make Macbeth's achievement "barren", is as paralysing as his earlier fear of being discovered. Shakespeare presents power as self-defeating — the more Macbeth gains, the more he fears losing. His decision to murder Banquo and Fleance is made without direct prompting from the witches or Lady Macbeth. This is new: in Act 1, Lady Macbeth pushed him; in Act 3, he pushes himself.

The scene also matters because Macbeth is now able to perform kingship in public while plotting murder in private. He politely invites Banquo to the feast and calls him his "chief guest", then immediately reveals that Banquo is the person he fears most. That contrast between public performance and hidden tyranny is central to AQA whole-play answers on power, appearance and reality, ambition, guilt and kingship.

### Evidence for the takeaway

- "Thou hast it now: king, Cawdor, Glamis, all" — Banquo recognises that the witches' prophecy has come true for Macbeth
- "And I fear / Thou play'dst most foully for't" — Banquo suspects Macbeth but does not act; he becomes a moral threat because he knows too much
- "May they not be my oracles as well" — Banquo also thinks about the prophecy benefiting his own descendants; Shakespeare makes him morally better than Macbeth, but not ambition-free
- "Fail not our feast" / "My lord, I will not" — Macbeth performs friendly kingship in public while privately preparing violence
- "To be thus is nothing, but to be safely thus" — he has the crown but not security; the word "nothing" is devastating: achievement means nothing without permanence
- "Our fears in Banquo stick deep" — "stick deep" suggests roots, wounds or splinters; the fear is embedded and structural
- "There is none but he / Whose being I do fear" — Macbeth admits Banquo alone has the moral strength and prophetic connection to threaten him
- "Fruitless crown" and "barren sceptre" — the crown cannot produce legitimate heirs; Macbeth's power is sterile because it cannot continue through a bloodline
- "For Banquo's issue have I filed my mind" — "filed" means defiled or polluted; Macbeth has corrupted his mind and soul for Banquo's descendants, not his own
- "Mine eternal jewel / Given to the common enemy of man" — Macbeth recognises that he has given his immortal soul to the devil
- "Come fate into the list, / And champion me to the utterance" — he challenges fate itself; once the crown is not enough, he commits to a violent war against the future
- "Both of you / Know Banquo was your enemy" — Macbeth manipulates the murderers by redirecting their anger towards Banquo
- "Ay, in the catalogue ye go for men" — Macbeth attacks the murderers' masculinity, echoing Lady Macbeth's earlier manipulation of him in 1.7
- "Fleance his son... must embrace the fate / Of that dark hour" — Macbeth understands that killing Banquo is not enough; the child must die because succession is the real threat

### Series throughline

This episode is the midpoint of Macbeth's arc. He has completed the transition from tempted hero (Episodes 3–6) to deliberate murderer ordering others (Episode 9 onwards). The "fruitless crown" imagery connects backwards to Episode 3, where Banquo is told he will "get kings", and forwards to Episode 11, where the apparitions confirm Banquo's line through the show of kings. The "eternal jewel" given to the devil connects to Episode 4 ("deep damnation"), Episode 7 (guilt after Duncan's murder) and Episode 12 (the nihilism of "Tomorrow, and tomorrow, and tomorrow").

Macbeth's isolation also starts to become structural here. Lady Macbeth is not present in the murder plot in 3.1. In 3.2, this develops into Macbeth telling her to "be innocent of the knowledge". The relationship has shifted from partnership in crime to separation, secrecy and psychological collapse.

## 4. Content reference pack

### Dates & timeline

- Act 3 Scene 1: Macbeth is now king; Banquo is at the palace
- Banquo's soliloquy opens the scene: he suspects Macbeth but remembers that the witches also promised kings for Banquo's descendants
- Macbeth publicly invites Banquo to the feast and questions his movements for the day
- Macbeth's soliloquy follows: the "to be thus is nothing" speech reveals that kingship has brought fear, not peace
- Macbeth identifies Banquo and Fleance as threats because the prophecy depends on Banquo's line continuing
- Macbeth manipulates two murderers into agreeing to kill Banquo and Fleance; a third murderer appears later in 3.3
- The murder is planned for that night; Fleance must also die because Macbeth wants to stop the line of succession

### Key people

- **Macbeth:** The soliloquy in 3.1 is his most politically aware so far — he is thinking strategically about succession, permanence and threat. His manipulation of the murderers shows calculated cruelty. He no longer needs Lady Macbeth's direction. He also performs a public mask: friendly host in front of Banquo, paranoid tyrant in private.
- **Banquo:** His suspicion of Macbeth is established in his opening soliloquy: "Thou hast it now: king, Cawdor, Glamis, all / As the weird women promised, and I fear / Thou play'dst most foully for't." He is more morally restrained than Macbeth, but his line "May they not be my oracles as well" shows he has not forgotten the prophecy that benefits him.
- **Fleance:** Politically important because he represents the continuation of Banquo's line. Macbeth must target Fleance because Banquo's murder alone would not stop the prophecy.
- **The Murderers:** Macbeth recruits them through emotional manipulation. He tells them Banquo has ruined their lives and attacks their identity as men. This echoes Lady Macbeth's method in 1.7, but Macbeth now turns the tactic outward.
- **Lady Macbeth:** Not involved in the Banquo plot in 3.1. This absence matters. The partnership from Acts 1–2 is beginning to fracture; in 3.2 Macbeth makes this explicit with "be innocent of the knowledge".

### Key terms & definitions

- **Soliloquy:** Macbeth's "To be thus is nothing" speech; reveals his private fear and motivation without Banquo, the court or Lady Macbeth hearing him
- **Dramatic irony:** Macbeth publicly behaves as Banquo's friend while the audience hears his private plan to destroy him
- **Tyrant:** A ruler who uses power violently for personal security rather than ruling justly for the good of the kingdom
- **Proxy violence:** Macbeth uses hired murderers instead of killing Banquo himself; this shows political calculation and moral decline
- **Succession:** The line of inheritance. In Jacobean politics, stable succession mattered deeply; Macbeth's lack of an heir makes his kingship insecure
- **"Fruitless crown":** A crown that will not produce legitimate heirs; Macbeth has no secure line; Banquo's descendants will inherit according to the witches' prophecy
- **"Barren sceptre":** The sceptre of kingship that will be taken by "an unlineal hand"; the fertility imagery reinforces the idea of sterile power
- **"Eternal jewel":** The immortal soul; Macbeth has given it "to the common enemy of man" (the devil); the value comparison exposes the terrible exchange he has made
- **Banquo's royal line and James I (AO3):** Shakespeare's audience would have known that James I claimed descent from Banquo. The prophecy that Banquo's descendants would be kings flatters James, while Macbeth's attempt to murder that line makes Macbeth's villainy politically and morally obvious

### Case studies / named examples

- **Banquo's opening soliloquy:** "Thou hast it now" sounds like a list of Macbeth's prizes, but "I fear / Thou play'dst most foully for't" turns celebration into suspicion. For AO1, Banquo becomes a threat because he can interpret Macbeth's rise. For AO2, the shift from fulfilled prophecy to fearful suspicion creates immediate dramatic tension.

- **Macbeth's public mask:** Macbeth calls Banquo his "chief guest" and tells him to "fail not our feast". For AO2, Shakespeare structures the scene so public performance comes before private confession. This makes Macbeth look more politically skilled and more dangerous than he was before Duncan's murder.

- **"To be thus is nothing, but to be safely thus":** The opening of Macbeth's soliloquy. For AQA: the word "nothing" is the pivot — having the crown is worthless without safety. This is the moment when Shakespeare reveals that ambition cannot be satisfied. For AO2: the repetition of "thus" creates a tautology — being king only feels meaningful if it feels secure; security has replaced the crown as Macbeth's new desire.

- **"Our fears in Banquo stick deep":** The word "stick" is physical — the fear is embedded like a splinter or wound. The word "deep" makes fear seem rooted and permanent. Contrast with Act 1, where Macbeth feared moral consequences; now his fear is political survival.

- **"For Banquo's issue have I filed my mind, / For them the gracious Duncan have I murdered, / Put rancours in the vessel of my peace / Only for them":** The repeated "for them" performs Macbeth's paranoid logic. Every crime has been committed to create a future that will benefit someone else, not Macbeth. For AO1, this is Macbeth's clearest expression of the futility of his ambition.

- **"Fruitless crown" / "barren sceptre":** The paired fertility images show that Macbeth's power cannot reproduce itself. He has gained the symbols of kingship, but not the future of kingship. This links to Duncan's "I have begun to plant thee" in 1.4, because images of growth and inheritance have turned into images of sterility.

- **"Mine eternal jewel / Given to the common enemy of man":** Macbeth describes his soul as a precious object and admits it has been handed to the devil. For AO3, this matters because a Jacobean audience would understand regicide and murder as spiritual crimes, not only political ones.

- **Macbeth manipulating the murderers:** He says "Both of you / Know Banquo was your enemy" and then challenges their manhood: "Ay, in the catalogue ye go for men." For AO2, the animal catalogue that follows reduces human identity into breeds and ranks. For whole-play structure, Macbeth has absorbed Lady Macbeth's tactic from 1.7 and now uses it as a tyrant.

- **Fleance as the real problem:** Macbeth says Fleance's "absence is no less material". Banquo is dangerous because he suspects Macbeth, but Fleance is dangerous because he represents the future. This makes the murder plot colder: Macbeth is willing to kill a child to fight succession.

### Causes & effects

- Macbeth has the crown → but the witches' prophecy means Banquo's descendants will inherit → "fruitless crown" → Macbeth decides Banquo and Fleance must die
- Banquo suspects Macbeth → Macbeth sees him as morally and politically dangerous → Banquo becomes the first target of Macbeth's reign
- Macbeth acts without Lady Macbeth → the partnership begins to diverge → Episode 10's disconnect follows when Macbeth tells her to "be innocent of the knowledge"
- Macbeth recruits murderers → he is ordering killings rather than committing them → new phase: the tyrant who uses others as instruments of violence → this continues with the Macduff family murder in 4.2
- Fleance escapes in 3.3 → Macbeth's attempt to control the future fails → the Banquo line returns symbolically in the eight kings apparition in 4.1
- Banquo is suspicious but silent → he does not expose Macbeth → his caution does not save him
