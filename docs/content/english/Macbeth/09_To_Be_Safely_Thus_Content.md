## 2. Storyline

### Core takeaway

Act 3 Scene 1 marks the transition of Macbeth from an influenced murderer to an independent tyrant. He now holds the crown but cannot enjoy it: his fear of Banquo (whose prophesied royal line threatens to make Macbeth's achievement "barren") is as paralysing as his earlier fear of being discovered. Shakespeare presents power as self-defeating — the more Macbeth gains, the more he fears losing. His decision to murder Banquo is made entirely alone, without prompting from the witches or Lady Macbeth. This is new: in Act 1, Lady Macbeth pushed him; in Act 3, he is pushing himself. The regicide that was meant to secure his future has made the future feel more precarious than ever.

### Evidence for the takeaway

- "To be thus is nothing, but to be safely thus" — he has the crown but not the security; the word "nothing" is devastating: achievement means nothing without permanence
- "Our fears in Banquo stick deep" — "stick deep" suggests roots, not surface anxiety; this fear is embedded, structural; he cannot function as king while Banquo lives
- "Fruitless crown" and "barren sceptre" — the crown cannot produce legitimate heirs; the imagery of agricultural sterility echoes Duncan's "plant thee" in 1.4 but now in defeat; Macbeth's murder of Duncan has not secured anything
- "For Banquo's issue have I filed my mind" — "filed" means defiled, contaminated; Macbeth has corrupted his eternal soul for the benefit of Banquo's descendants, not his own
- "Mine eternal jewel / Given to the common enemy of man" — the eternal soul given to the devil; this is the fullest statement of what Macbeth has sacrificed; he has traded heaven for a "barren sceptre"
- "Come fate into the list, / And champion me to the utterance" — he challenges fate itself; having decided to murder Banquo, he commits to the path of total war against fate

### Series throughline

This episode is the midpoint of Macbeth's arc. He has completed the transition from tempted hero (Episodes 3–6) to deliberate murderer ordering others (Episode 9 onwards). The "fruitless crown" imagery connects to Episode 3 (the prophecy about Banquo's descendants) and Episode 11 (4.1 confirms the Banquo line in the apparitions). The "eternal jewel" given to the devil connects to Episode 4 ("deep damnation") and Episode 12 (the nihilism of "tomorrow and tomorrow and tomorrow"). Macbeth's increasing isolation — Lady Macbeth is not involved in the Banquo plot — anticipates Episode 10's relationship collapse.

### Exam framing

High priority for:
- "How does Shakespeare present Macbeth as a tyrant?" — 3.1 is where he acts autonomously and cruelly for the first time
- "How does Shakespeare present power in Macbeth?" — the "to be thus is nothing" speech is the most direct statement that power without security is meaningless
- "How does Shakespeare present ambition after the murder?" — 3.1 shows that ambition cannot be satisfied; achieving the goal only generates new fears

## 4. Content reference pack

### Dates & timeline

- Act 3 Scene 1: Macbeth is now king; Banquo is at the palace
- Banquo's soliloquy opening the scene: he suspects Macbeth but expects his own descendants to be kings
- Macbeth's soliloquy: the "to be thus is nothing" speech
- Macbeth manipulates the murderers (two, then three) into agreeing to kill Banquo and Fleance
- The murder is planned for that night; Fleance must also die to prevent the line from continuing

### Key people

- **Macbeth:** The soliloquy in 3.1 is his most politically aware — he is thinking strategically about succession and permanence; his manipulation of the murderers shows a new capability for calculated cruelty; he no longer needs Lady Macbeth's direction
- **Banquo:** His suspicion of Macbeth is established in his opening soliloquy ("Thou hast it now: king, Cawdor, Glamis, all / As the weird women promised, and I fear / Thou play'dst most foully for't"); he is the moral conscience of this scene
- **The Murderers:** Macbeth recruits them through emotional manipulation — he tells them Banquo has ruined their lives; this is the same technique Lady Macbeth used on him in 1.7, now turned outward
- **Lady Macbeth (briefly):** Present at the opening; the scene establishes that she is not involved in the Banquo plot; Macbeth is acting independently

### Key terms & definitions

- **Soliloquy:** Macbeth's "To be thus is nothing" speech; reveals his private fear and motivation without Lady Macbeth's knowledge
- **"Fruitless crown":** A crown that will not produce legitimate heirs; Macbeth has no children; Banquo's descendants will inherit according to the witches' prophecy; the agricultural imagery connects to Duncan's "plant thee" — the "fruit" of the planting belongs to Banquo, not Macbeth
- **"Barren sceptre":** The sceptre of kingship that will be taken by "an unlineal hand" (not Macbeth's descendants) — the phallic/fertility imagery of the "sceptre" being "barren" reinforces the theme of sterile power
- **"Eternal jewel":** The immortal soul; Macbeth has given it "to the common enemy of man" (the devil); the value comparison — eternal soul traded for a "barren sceptre" — is his own devastating self-assessment
- **Banquo's royal line and James I (AO3):** Shakespeare's audience would have known that James I claimed descent from Banquo; the witches' prophecy that Banquo's descendants would be kings is a compliment to the king watching the play; Macbeth's attempts to murder that line makes his villainy even clearer

### Case studies / named examples

- **"To be thus is nothing, but to be safely thus":** The opening of Macbeth's soliloquy. For AQA: the word "nothing" is the pivot — having the crown is worthless without safety; this is the moment when Shakespeare reveals that ambition cannot be satisfied; achieving the goal merely generates new anxiety. For AO2: the repetition of "thus" (twice in the same phrase) creates a tautology — being king is only being king if it *feels* secure; security has become the new desire replacing the crown itself.

- **"Our fears in Banquo stick deep":** The word "stick" is physical — the fear is embedded, rooted; it is not surface anxiety but structural dread. For AQA AO2: the preposition "deep" extends the image — fear is underground, invisible, permanent. Contrast with Act 1 where his fears were about moral consequences; now his fears are purely about political survival.

- **"For Banquo's issue have I filed my mind, / For them the gracious Duncan have I murdered, / Put rancours in the vessel of my peace / Only for them":** The anaphora of "for them" / "for Banquo's issue" performs the treadmill of paranoid logic — every crime has been committed to create a future that will benefit someone else, not Macbeth. For AO1: this is Macbeth's clearest expression of the futility of his ambition.

- **Macbeth manipulating the murderers:** He uses the same technique Lady Macbeth used on him — attacking their identity (telling them they are not "the patient, holy, and forgiving" type but men who have been wronged) to override their reluctance. For AQA: this shows Macbeth has learned Lady Macbeth's manipulation tactics and now applies them without prompting.

### Causes & effects

- Macbeth has the crown → but the witches' prophecy means Banquo's descendants will inherit → "fruitless crown" → Macbeth must murder Banquo and Fleance to prevent the prophecy from fulfilling itself
- Macbeth acts without Lady Macbeth → she is not involved → their relationship begins to diverge → Episode 10's disconnect (he tells her "be innocent of the knowledge") follows from this
- Macbeth recruits murderers → he is ordering killings rather than committing them → new phase: the tyrant who uses others as instruments of violence → this pattern continues with the Macduff family murder (4.2)
- Banquo's suspicious but silent → his loyalty to the promise of his own line's kingship keeps him complicit → he does not act on his suspicion → his murder is the consequence of this passivity
