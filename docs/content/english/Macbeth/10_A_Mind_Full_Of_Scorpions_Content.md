## 2. Storyline

### Core takeaway

Act 3 Scenes 2 and 4 contain the moment when Macbeth's private psychological collapse becomes a public event. At the banquet — a set piece of kingship, designed to display legitimacy, hospitality and order — Macbeth sees Banquo's ghost, speaks to it, breaks the ceremony and is managed by Lady Macbeth with diminishing success. The banquet that should prove he is king reveals that he is not capable of kingship.

The relationship between Macbeth and Lady Macbeth has reversed. In 1.7 she controlled him; now she cannot. In 3.2, Macbeth cryptically tells her to "be innocent of the knowledge, dearest chuck". This exclusion from the Banquo plot marks the end of their partnership. They are now separately deteriorating, not deteriorating together. Lady Macbeth can still perform control in public, but she can no longer truly control Macbeth.

### Evidence for the takeaway

- "Nought's had, all's spent / Where our desire is got without content" — Lady Macbeth's soliloquy in 3.2; she has achieved what she wanted but feels no satisfaction
- "Better be with the dead" — Macbeth envies Duncan's peace; kingship has brought mental torment, not safety
- "O, full of scorpions is my mind, dear wife" — Macbeth's image of his own psychological state; scorpions suggest venom, pain, multiple fears and internal attack
- "Be innocent of the knowledge, dearest chuck" — Macbeth excludes Lady Macbeth from the Banquo plot; the tenderness of "dearest chuck" contrasts with secrecy and violence
- "Come, seeling night" — Macbeth calls for darkness to cover what is coming; he now actively invites concealment rather than being pushed into it
- "Things bad begun make strong themselves by ill" — Macbeth understands that one crime creates pressure for further crime
- "There the grown serpent lies; the worm that's fled" — Macbeth hears Banquo is dead but Fleance has escaped; his succession anxiety survives
- "But now I am cabined, cribbed, confined, bound in / To saucy doubts and fears" — Fleance's escape instantly traps Macbeth back inside paranoia
- "Thou canst not say I did it" — Macbeth speaks to Banquo's ghost; the line tries to deny direct responsibility, but exposes guilt
- "Never shake thy gory locks at me" — the ghost's silent presence becomes a visual accusation
- "Are you a man?" — Lady Macbeth echoes her challenge from 1.7; the same tactic that once pushed him towards murder now fails as damage control
- "This is the very painting of your fear" — Lady Macbeth links the ghost to Macbeth's earlier dagger hallucination
- "My worthy lord, / Your noble friends do lack you" — Lady Macbeth tries to pull Macbeth back into public kingship, but he cannot perform the role
- "Blood will have blood" — Macbeth accepts that violence perpetuates itself
- "I am in blood / Stepp'd in so far" — Macbeth knows he has gone too far to return; the blood motif becomes a metaphor for moral entrapment
- "Strange things I have in head, that will to hand" — Macbeth's thoughts are turning into actions faster and more dangerously

### Series throughline

This episode is the mid-collapse point of both characters. Lady Macbeth's "nought's had" in 3.2 mirrors Macbeth's "to be thus is nothing" in 3.1 — both characters have independently reached the same conclusion that achievement is hollow. Macbeth's "full of scorpions" develops the insecurity of Episode 9 into psychological torment.

The banquet scene reverses the power structure of 1.7. Lady Macbeth still reaches for the same weapon — masculine shame — but it no longer works. The ghost of Banquo connects to the dagger hallucination in 2.1; both are supernatural or psychological stage images that reveal Macbeth's guilty mind. Episode 12 (5.1) is the completion of Lady Macbeth's arc: her sleepwalking is the private version of what Macbeth enacts publicly at the banquet.

## 4. Content reference pack

### Dates & timeline

- Act 3 Scene 2: Lady Macbeth privately admits that achieving the crown has not brought contentment
- Act 3 Scene 2: Macbeth reveals his mind is "full of scorpions" and excludes Lady Macbeth from the Banquo/Fleance plot
- Act 3 Scene 2: Macbeth calls on night and darkness, showing he now actively chooses concealment and further violence
- Act 3 Scene 4: The banquet begins as a public display of kingship, hospitality and order
- Act 3 Scene 4: The murderer reports that Banquo is dead but Fleance has escaped
- Act 3 Scene 4: Banquo's ghost appears in Macbeth's seat; Macbeth speaks to it; only Macbeth and the audience are normally staged as seeing it
- Act 3 Scene 4: Lady Macbeth tries to explain Macbeth's behaviour and dismisses the guests
- Act 3 Scene 4: Macbeth reflects on the cycle of bloodshed and decides to return to the witches

### Key people

- **Macbeth:** His psychological state is now externally visible. Fleance's escape means his security problem remains unresolved. The ghost makes his private guilt public. He cannot control his reactions in the way public kingship requires.
- **Lady Macbeth:** Her power has shifted from motivator to damage controller. Her strategy ("Are you a man?") is the same as in 1.7 but its function is now defensive, not offensive. She can cover for him briefly but cannot restore real control.
- **Banquo's ghost:** Usually visible to Macbeth and the audience, but invisible to the lords. This staging choice is crucial: it makes Macbeth appear unstable to the court while allowing the audience to see the visual form of his guilt.
- **Fleance:** Absent from the banquet but crucial to Macbeth's mental state. His escape means Banquo's line may still continue, so Macbeth's attempt to secure the future has failed.
- **Lennox, Ross and the lords:** Present at the banquet; they witness Macbeth's breakdown. Their polite withdrawal does not openly rebel yet, but it marks the erosion of public trust that becomes more explicit later.

### Key terms & definitions

- **Stagecraft:** The practical choices Shakespeare creates for performance: ghost visibility, seating, pauses, Macbeth's movements, Lady Macbeth's interruptions and the guests' reactions
- **Ghost ambiguity (AO3):** In Protestant theology, ghosts could be demonic illusions or psychological projections; in Catholic theology, ghosts could be souls connected to purgatory. Shakespeare uses this uncertainty to make Banquo's ghost both supernatural and psychological
- **Public kingship:** A king was expected to display order, dignity, hospitality and control. Macbeth's banquet failure is therefore political as well as personal
- **Hospitality:** Hosting a feast should show generosity and stability. Macbeth turns a feast into a scene of fear and suspicion, corrupting the social role of a king
- **Power reversal:** The role swap between Macbeth and Lady Macbeth — she moves from controlling to containing him; he moves from directed to secretive and self-directed
- **"Dearest chuck":** An affectionate diminutive. The tenderness of the term contrasts with what Macbeth is hiding from her; it marks a shift from equal partners to concealment
- **"Cabined, cribbed, confined":** A triple phrase of restriction. Macbeth has power but feels trapped; Fleance's escape makes kingship feel like imprisonment
- **"Blood will have blood":** A proverbial-sounding statement that violence creates more violence; Macbeth recognises the cycle but does not step out of it
- **"I am in blood / Stepp'd in so far":** Macbeth imagines himself wading through blood; returning would be as difficult as continuing, so he chooses further violence

### Case studies / named examples

- **"Nought's had, all's spent":** Lady Macbeth's private admission. For AQA: the contrast between "nought" and "all" reveals that the murder has produced the opposite of what she planned — not gain, but total loss. For AO1: this is Lady Macbeth's moment of self-knowledge; she sees the futility before Macbeth reaches his later nihilism in 5.5.

- **"Better be with the dead":** Macbeth envies Duncan because death looks more peaceful than guilty kingship. This reverses ambition: the crown was meant to make life better, but Macbeth now imagines death as preferable.

- **"O, full of scorpions is my mind":** The plural "scorpions" implies multiple sources of anxiety: guilt, fear of discovery, fear of Fleance, fear of Banquo's line and fear of the future. The image is visceral — scorpions sting and poison. For AO2, the metaphor turns psychological suffering into physical attack.

- **"Be innocent of the knowledge, dearest chuck":** The phrase sounds affectionate, but it excludes Lady Macbeth from Macbeth's plans. "Innocent" is ironic because neither of them is truly innocent. This is a key relationship pivot: Macbeth no longer needs her to direct him.

- **"Come, seeling night":** A "seeling" was the stitching shut of a hawk's eyelids during training. Macbeth imagines night blinding the world. For AO2, this makes darkness active and violent; Macbeth wants moral sight closed.

- **"Things bad begun make strong themselves by ill":** Macbeth recognises escalation. One bad act needs another to protect it. For whole-play structure, this explains the movement from Duncan to Banquo to Macduff's family.

- **Fleance escapes — "cabined, cribbed, confined":** The triple alliteration compresses Macbeth's fear. He has just become king, but the language makes him sound imprisoned. This is excellent for essays on power because Shakespeare shows power creating confinement, not freedom.

- **Banquo's ghost — stagecraft analysis:** When the ghost occupies Macbeth's seat at the table, Shakespeare stages a visual metaphor: Banquo's presence interrupts Macbeth's kingship. Macbeth may wear the crown, but Banquo's memory and bloodline still occupy the future. Lady Macbeth's explanation ("He has these fits from his youth") is a public lie trying to protect a private secret.

- **"Thou canst not say I did it":** Macbeth tries to create an alibi because he did not personally carry out the attack on Banquo. However, the line exposes his guilt because no one has accused him aloud. His denial becomes a confession to the audience.

- **"Are you a man?":** Lady Macbeth echoes 1.7, but the function is opposite. In 1.7, the challenge motivated Macbeth to act. Here, it tries to stop him from exposing himself. The recycled challenge shows Lady Macbeth has only one tool, and it is no longer working.

- **"This is the very painting of your fear":** Lady Macbeth links the ghost to the dagger hallucination. This is a useful whole-play bridge: both visions expose Macbeth's guilty imagination, but the dagger appears before murder while the ghost appears after murder.

- **"Blood will have blood":** The repeated noun makes the line sound circular and inevitable. Macbeth is no longer shocked by violence; he now sees it as a law that governs his life.

- **"I am in blood / Stepp'd in so far":** The metaphor imagines Macbeth wading through blood. For AO2, the physical depth of the image shows moral entrapment. For AO1, Macbeth understands his own corruption but chooses to continue rather than repent.

- **Relationship timeline for exam answers (whole play arc):**
  1. 1.5: Lady Macbeth in control ("leave all the rest to me")
  2. 1.7: Lady Macbeth manipulates Macbeth into agreeing to murder
  3. 2.2: Lady Macbeth manages the aftermath; she is more capable than him
  4. 3.2: Lady Macbeth is excluded from the Banquo/Fleance plot; Macbeth acts independently
  5. 3.4: Lady Macbeth attempts damage control at banquet; she fails
  6. 5.1: Lady Macbeth's collapse; Macbeth is distant; their connection is severed

### Causes & effects

- Macbeth excludes Lady Macbeth from the Banquo/Fleance plot → the partnership of shared guilt ends → they deteriorate separately → her sleepwalking and his nihilism become separate endpoints
- Fleance escapes → Macbeth's succession fear survives → he feels "cabined, cribbed, confined" → he seeks more certainty from the witches in 4.1
- Banquo's ghost appears at the banquet → Macbeth cannot maintain the performance of kingship → the lords witness his breakdown → public trust begins to erode
- Lady Macbeth repeats "Are you a man?" → the challenge fails to restore control → demonstrates the limits of her manipulation → her power over him is effectively ended
- "Blood will have blood" and "I am in blood" → Macbeth accepts the cycle of violence as inevitable → he moves further towards tyranny

**Misconceptions worth a MisconceptionCheck:**
- "Banquo's ghost proves the supernatural is controlling Macbeth" — UNCERTAIN: the ghost may be a hallucination of guilt; the other lords cannot see it; Shakespeare leaves the ambiguity deliberate
- "Lady Macbeth is still in control at the banquet" — FALSE: she manages the situation temporarily but cannot restore it; the lords leave early; the performance fails
- "Macbeth tells Lady Macbeth everything" — FALSE: "Be innocent of the knowledge" shows he is now hiding plans from her
- "Banquo's death solves Macbeth's fear" — FALSE: Fleance escapes, so the succession threat survives
- "Macbeth is only frightened of ghosts" — TOO SIMPLE: he is frightened of guilt, exposure, loss of power, succession and the future
