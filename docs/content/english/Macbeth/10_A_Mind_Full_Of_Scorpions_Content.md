## 2. Storyline

### Core takeaway

Act 3 Scenes 2 and 4 contain the moment when Macbeth's private psychological collapse becomes a public event. At the banquet — a set piece of kingship, designed to display legitimacy and order — Macbeth sees Banquo's ghost, speaks to it, breaks the ceremonial occasion and is managed by Lady Macbeth with diminishing success. The banquet that should prove he is king reveals that he is not capable of kingship. The relationship between Macbeth and Lady Macbeth has reversed: in 1.7 she controlled him; now she cannot. And in 3.2, the moment where Macbeth cryptically tells her to "be innocent of the knowledge, dearest chuck" — the exclusion of Lady Macbeth from the Banquo plot — marks the end of their partnership. They are now separately deteriorating, not deteriorating together.

### Evidence for the takeaway

- "Nought's had, all's spent / Where our desire is got without content" — Lady Macbeth's soliloquy in 3.2; she has achieved what she planned and feels nothing; the cost was everything
- "Full of scorpions is my mind" — Macbeth's image of his own psychological state; scorpions = venomous, multiple, stinging; the mind as an infested space
- "Be innocent of the knowledge, dearest chuck" — Macbeth excludes Lady Macbeth from the Banquo plot; the word "innocent" is darkly ironic; their partnership of shared guilt is over
- "Thou canst not say I did it" — Macbeth speaks to Banquo's ghost; addressed to a figure only he can see; the "I" is his alibi (he didn't personally kill Banquo) but it is also his guilt speaking
- "Never shake thy gory locks at me" — the ghost's physical presence is not verbal but visual; "gory locks" identifies the wounds on the murder victim; Macbeth is confronted by the physical evidence of his crime
- "Are you a man?" — Lady Macbeth echoes her challenge from 1.7; she used it to push him toward murder; now she uses it to drag him back from exposure; the tactic is now damage control, not motivation
- "Blood will have blood" — Macbeth's philosophical acceptance that violence perpetuates itself; he cannot stop now because stopping would expose everything; the cycle is self-sustaining

### Series throughline

This episode is the mid-collapse point of both characters. Lady Macbeth's "nought's had" in 3.2 mirrors Macbeth's "to be thus is nothing" in 3.1 — both characters have independently reached the same conclusion that achievement is hollow. The banquet scene reverses the power structure of 1.7. The ghost of Banquo connects to the dagger hallucination in 2.1 — both are hallucinatory manifestations of guilt. Episode 12 (5.1) is the completion of Lady Macbeth's arc: her sleepwalking is the private version of what Macbeth enacts publicly at the banquet.

## 4. Content reference pack

### Dates & timeline

- Act 3 Scene 2: Private conversation between Macbeth and Lady Macbeth; he hints at further murders without telling her; she speaks of the futility of what they have achieved
- Act 3 Scene 4: The banquet; Banquo's ghost appears (visible to audience and Macbeth; invisible to other characters); Lady Macbeth attempts to manage the situation; Macbeth's behaviour exposes his guilt; guests leave; "blood will have blood"

### Key people

- **Macbeth:** His psychological state is now externally visible; the ghost makes his private guilt public; he cannot control his reactions in the way that the performance of kingship requires
- **Lady Macbeth:** Her power has shifted from motivator to damage controller; her strategy ("Are you a man?") is the same as in 1.7 but its function is now defensive, not offensive; she fails to restore control
- **Banquo's ghost:** Only visible to Macbeth (and the audience); invisible to the lords; this staging choice is crucial — it makes Macbeth appear mad to the court; the ghost is the externalized form of guilt that Macbeth cannot suppress
- **Lennox, Ross (the lords):** Present at the banquet; witnessing Macbeth's breakdown; their growing suspicion is established here; by Act 5 the Scottish lords have abandoned Macbeth and joined Malcolm

### Key terms & definitions

- **Stagecraft:** The director/Shakespeare must decide whether Banquo's ghost is "real" (visible to the audience) or whether Macbeth is hallucinating; most productions make it visible to the audience; this is a key AQA performance analysis point
- **Ghost beliefs (AO3):** In Catholic theology, ghosts are souls in purgatory; in Protestant theology, ghosts are either demonic illusions or psychological projections; Jacobean England was officially Protestant but many retained Catholic ghost beliefs; the ghost's ambiguity is Shakespeare exploiting this theological uncertainty
- **Power reversal:** The role swap between Macbeth and Lady Macbeth — she moves from controlling to containing him; he moves from directed to directing himself (but now disastrously)
- **"Dearest chuck":** An affectionate diminutive (chuck = a term of endearment, also a cluck, bird reference); the tenderness of the term contrasts with what he is hiding from her; it marks a shift from equal partners to a relationship of concealment
- **"Blood will have blood":** The statement is almost proverbial; blood = violence; violence generates further violence; Macbeth is acknowledging he is trapped in a cycle of killing that cannot be stopped

### Case studies / named examples

- **"Nought's had, all's spent":** Lady Macbeth's private admission. For AQA: the chiasmus of "nought" (nothing) and "all's spent" (everything consumed) reveals that the murder has produced the opposite of what she planned — not gain but total loss. For AO1: this is Lady Macbeth's moment of self-knowledge; she sees the futility before Macbeth does; contrast with his later "tomorrow" speech (5.5) which reaches the same conclusion more nihilistically.

- **"Full of scorpions is my mind":** For AQA: the plural "scorpions" implies multiple sources of anxiety (guilt, fear of discovery, fear of Banquo's line, fear of the witches' other prophecies). The image is visceral — scorpions sting; they are venomous; the mind is not merely troubled but actively poisoned. For AO2: the metaphor positions Macbeth's mental suffering as physical — he experiences guilt as bodily pain.

- **Banquo's ghost — stagecraft analysis:** For AQA performance analysis: when the ghost occupies Macbeth's seat at the table, Shakespeare is staging a visual metaphor — Banquo's rightful place has been stolen; he returns to occupy it. Lady Macbeth's attempt to explain Macbeth's behaviour ("He has these fits from his youth") is the public performance of a private lie. The lords' polite withdrawal ("Good-night, and better health / Attend his majesty") marks the moment when public trust begins to erode.

- **"Are you a man?":** Lady Macbeth echoes 1.7 exactly, but the function is opposite. In 1.7, the challenge motivated Macbeth to act; here, it is trying to stop him from acting (speaking to the ghost, revealing his guilt). The recycled challenge shows Lady Macbeth has only one tool; it is no longer working.

- **Relationship timeline for exam answers (whole play arc):**
  1. 1.5: Lady Macbeth in control ("leave all the rest to me")
  2. 1.7: Lady Macbeth manipulates Macbeth into agreeing to murder
  3. 2.2: Lady Macbeth manages the aftermath; she is more capable than him
  4. 3.2: Lady Macbeth excluded from Banquo plot; Macbeth acts independently
  5. 3.4: Lady Macbeth attempts damage control at banquet; she fails
  6. 5.1: Lady Macbeth's collapse; Macbeth is distant; their connection is severed

### Causes & effects

- Macbeth excludes Lady Macbeth from Banquo plot → the partnership of shared guilt ends → they deteriorate separately → her sleepwalking (private guilt) and his nihilism (public bravado) are the separate endpoints
- Banquo's ghost appears at the banquet → Macbeth cannot maintain the performance of kingship → the lords witness his breakdown → suspicion grows → Scotland begins to resist him
- Lady Macbeth repeats "Are you a man?" → the challenge fails to restore control → demonstrates the limits of her manipulation → her power over him is effectively ended
- "Blood will have blood" → Macbeth accepts the cycle of violence as inevitable → he plans the second witches visit (4.1) → his confidence becomes false security rather than genuine
