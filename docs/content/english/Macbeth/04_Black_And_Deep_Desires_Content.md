## 2. Storyline

### Core takeaway

Act 1 Scene 4 is the scene of maximum dramatic irony in Act 1: Duncan expresses absolute trust in Macbeth ("I have begun to plant thee, and will labour / To make thee full of growing"), names Malcolm as his heir (which removes Macbeth's chance of legitimate succession), and is met with concealed plotting. Macbeth hides his "black and deep desires" behind the language of loyalty and service. Shakespeare presents this scene as the moment the tragedy becomes inevitable: Macbeth now knows that the path to the crown requires murder, not merit.

### Evidence for the takeaway

- Duncan's speech echoes agricultural metaphors (planting, growing) — he sees himself as a nurturing king, not knowing his "plant" will destroy him
- Malcolm's naming as Prince of Cumberland removes any legitimate route to kingship for Macbeth → murder becomes the only option if Macbeth is to fulfil the prophecy
- "The Prince of Cumberland! That is a step / On which I must fall down, or else o'erleap" — Macbeth immediately converts Malcolm into an obstacle that ambition must overcome
- "Stars, hide your fires; / Let not light see my black and deep desires" — Macbeth's first conscious attempt to conceal his ambition; the darkness/concealment motif begins
- "The eye wink at the hand" — Macbeth wants his own conscious mind to be unaware of what his hand will do; a fragmentation of the self that becomes his defining psychological condition
- "He was a gentleman on whom I built an absolute trust" (about Cawdor) — Duncan's words about the betrayed Cawdor apply exactly to what Macbeth is about to become; Shakespeare's deepest dramatic irony in the scene

### Series throughline

This episode establishes the concealment theme that threads through the entire series. Lady Macbeth's "look like the innocent flower but be the serpent under't" (Episode 5) is the tactical application of what Macbeth begins here. Duncan's "no art to find the mind's construction in the face" (said about Cawdor, applied to Macbeth) remains true throughout the play — until the mask finally cracks in Episode 10 (3.4, the banquet). Episode 8 (2.3–2.4) revisits Duncan's description of himself as "anointed temple" — his death ruptures the natural and divine order that this scene presents at its most intact.

## 4. Content reference pack

### Dates & timeline

- Act 1 Scene 4: Duncan's court; immediately after the announcement of Macbeth's Cawdor title
- Duncan executes the old Thane of Cawdor (reported)
- Macbeth and Banquo arrive; Duncan praises them effusively
- Duncan names Malcolm as Prince of Cumberland (heir to the throne)
- Macbeth exits to prepare Inverness for Duncan's visit — already concealing his ambition

### Key people

- **Duncan:** Presented at his most idealistic — generous, grateful, trusting, openly emotional about loyalty; his inability to see treachery is both noble and fatal; represents the legitimate, divinely-ordained order that Macbeth will destroy
- **Macbeth:** In direct opposition to Duncan's openness, Macbeth is immediately concealing — the audience watches him construct his loyal public face while his private thoughts are murderous
- **Malcolm:** Named Prince of Cumberland; he becomes the obstacle Macbeth explicitly notes; his eventual return in Act 5 to restore order makes this naming dramatically significant
- **Banquo:** Present but brief; his honest loyalty contrasts with Macbeth's concealment in the same scene

### Key terms & definitions

- **Dramatic irony:** The audience knows Macbeth is plotting; Duncan does not — this is the scene's primary technique
- **Divine right of kings:** The Jacobean belief that kings are appointed by God; Duncan's murder is therefore not just regicide but sacrilege; this contextualises Macbeth's later guilt and Scotland's "unnaturalness"
- **Prince of Cumberland:** The heir-designate to the Scottish throne; Malcolm's naming as such creates the obstacle Macbeth must overcome
- **Concealment motif:** First established here ("Stars, hide your fires"); runs through to Lady Macbeth's "look like the innocent flower" and Macbeth's "False face must hide what the false heart doth know"
- **Agricultural metaphor:** Duncan's language of planting and growing → kingship as cultivation → Macbeth's violence against Duncan = destroying the garden
- **Obstacle metaphor:** Macbeth imagines Malcolm as a "step" he must either fall down before or leap over; Shakespeare turns political succession into a physical barrier in Macbeth's mind

### Case studies / named examples

- **"There's no art to find the mind's construction in the face":** Duncan says this about Cawdor immediately before Macbeth enters, so the audience applies it to the new Cawdor. Shakespeare makes Duncan's inability to read treachery repeat itself. High-value quote for appearance vs reality.
- **"The Prince of Cumberland! That is a step / On which I must fall down, or else o'erleap":** This metaphor presents Malcolm as a physical obstacle in Macbeth's mind. "Fall down" suggests surrender or failure; "o'erleap" suggests ambition jumping over moral and political boundaries. It makes Macbeth's ambition active immediately after Malcolm is named heir.
- **"Stars, hide your fires":** Macbeth's first explicit invocation of darkness to hide his intentions. The stars are associated with providence and divine order; asking them to "hide" is asking God not to witness what he is about to do. Contrast with Lady Macbeth's "Come thick night" in 1.5 — both characters invoke darkness independently, suggesting the plotting is mutual even before they speak.
- **"I have begun to plant thee":** Duncan's agricultural metaphor — he is the nurturing king, Macbeth is the loyal plant. The irony: Macbeth will "unplant" Duncan by killing him. For AO2: the metaphor reveals Duncan's vision of kingship as paternal cultivation.
- **The Gunpowder Plot connection (AO3):** A Jacobean audience, especially under James I after the Gunpowder Plot, may have recognised the anxiety around trusted nobles becoming traitors. "There's no art to find the mind's construction in the face" speaks to the fear that treachery can hide behind loyalty.

### Causes & effects

- Duncan names Malcolm as Prince of Cumberland → Macbeth loses any legitimate path to the crown → murder is now the only route to fulfilling the prophecy
- Malcolm becomes a "step" in Macbeth's imagination → Macbeth begins thinking in terms of overcoming lawful succession rather than accepting it
- Duncan expresses absolute trust in Macbeth → Macbeth immediately responds with hidden plotting → dramatic irony maximised
- Old Cawdor is described as "a gentleman on whom I built an absolute trust" → Macbeth is the *new* Cawdor → Macbeth is being positioned by Shakespeare as history repeating itself
- Macbeth exits ahead of the king → he is planning the murder before Duncan has even arrived at Inverness → Lady Macbeth's "unsex me here" (1.5) follows directly

**Mark scheme patterns:**

- AO2: Dramatic irony; light/dark imagery; agricultural metaphor; obstacle metaphor
- AO1: Duncan as innocent victim makes Macbeth's later actions more heinous — establishes sympathy for Duncan before the murder

**Misconceptions worth a MisconceptionCheck:**
- "Macbeth murders Duncan mainly because of Lady Macbeth's pressure" — PARTLY TRUE but INCOMPLETE: Act 1.4 shows Macbeth already planning *before* Lady Macbeth is involved; "Stars, hide your fires" is internal, not prompted by his wife
- "Duncan is naive and deserves what happens to him" — FALSE: his trust is a kingly virtue, not a character flaw; Shakespeare presents his murder as a tragedy *because* he is a good king
- "Malcolm being named heir is just background plot" — FALSE: this is the moment Macbeth recognises he cannot become king legitimately and begins thinking in terms of obstacles to overcome
