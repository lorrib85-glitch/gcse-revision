## 2. Storyline

### Core takeaway

Act 5 brings every major arc to its endpoint. Lady Macbeth — who dismissed blood as easily washed in Act 2.2 and invoked spirits to suppress her conscience in Act 1.5 — can no longer control what she has buried. Her sleepwalking scene turns private guilt into involuntary performance: her body and language repeat the crimes her waking self tried to deny.

Macbeth has travelled a different route. He once felt the full moral weight of Duncan's murder; by Act 5, guilt has curdled into numbness, false courage and nihilism. The "Tomorrow" speech is not simple grief for Lady Macbeth. It is the final result of a life emptied by ambition: time, love, kingship and identity all seem meaningless.

The ending restores order through Malcolm and Macduff, but Shakespeare leaves a final tension. Malcolm calls Macbeth and Lady Macbeth "this dead butcher and his fiend-like queen". Politically, that verdict helps close the tragedy and restore moral order. Dramatically, it is too simple for the complex characters the audience has watched.

### Evidence for the takeaway

- "Out, damned spot! out, I say!" — Lady Macbeth relives the murder; the imperative is aimed at a bloodstain only she perceives; "damned" invokes spiritual judgement
- "Yet who would have thought the old man to have had so much blood in him?" — Duncan's blood returns through memory; she now dwells on what she earlier tried to dismiss
- "The Thane of Fife had a wife. Where is she now?" — Lady Macbeth's guilt reaches beyond Duncan to the later victims of Macbeth's tyranny
- "What, will these hands ne'er be clean?" — the dramatic irony of "A little water clears us" is complete; her hands can never be imaginatively clean
- "Here's the smell of the blood still" — guilt has become sensory; she smells what no one else can smell
- "All the perfumes of Arabia will not sweeten this little hand" — hyperbole and scale expose the impossibility of cleansing guilt
- "More needs she the divine than the physician" — the Doctor's diagnosis; Lady Macbeth's condition is spiritual/psychological, not physically curable
- "I am sick at heart" — Macbeth's Act 5 despair; the sickness imagery links personal corruption to the sick kingdom
- "My way of life / Is fall'n into the sere, the yellow leaf" — autumn imagery; Macbeth recognises moral and emotional withering
- "Honour, love, obedience, troops of friends, / I must not look to have" — Macbeth lists the human goods his ambition has cost him
- "Canst thou not minister to a mind diseased" — Macbeth asks the Doctor to cure Lady Macbeth, but the phrase also reflects his own diseased mind and kingdom
- "I have almost forgot the taste of fears" — Macbeth is no longer brave in the heroic Act 1 sense; he has become emotionally blunted by horror
- "Tomorrow, and tomorrow, and tomorrow / Creeps in this petty pace from day to day" — Macbeth's nihilism after Lady Macbeth's death; time becomes repetitive, slow and meaningless
- "Life's but a walking shadow, a poor player" — theatrical metaphor; life is performance without substance
- "It is a tale / Told by an idiot, full of sound and fury, / Signifying nothing" — Macbeth's final philosophical position: human life has no meaning
- "I bear a charmed life" — Macbeth still clings to the witches' equivocal promises even as they unravel
- "Macduff was from his mother's womb / Untimely ripped" — the "none of woman born" equivocation is revealed
- "I will not yield" — Macbeth refuses humiliation and chooses violent resistance even when hope has collapsed
- "Yet I will try the last" — Macbeth's final fighting instinct; he dies as the soldier he began as, but morally ruined
- "The time is free" — Macduff announces that Macbeth's tyranny has ended
- "This dead butcher and his fiend-like queen" — Malcolm's official verdict; politically useful, but dramatically reductive
- "By the grace of Grace" — Malcolm frames restoration as divinely sanctioned order returning after Macbeth's unnatural rule

### Series throughline

Episode 12 is the synthesis of the entire series. Every theme, motif and character arc completes here:

- **Supernatural:** the witches' equivocations are fulfilled; "none of woman born" and Birnam Wood prove their language was technically true but fatally misleading
- **Blood motif:** Lady Macbeth's hands complete the arc from "A little water clears us" to "Out, damned spot"
- **Sleep motif:** "Macbeth does murder sleep" returns through Lady Macbeth's sleepwalking
- **Guilt:** Macbeth's guilt moves from fear and hallucination to numb nihilism; Lady Macbeth's guilt moves from suppression to involuntary confession
- **Ambition:** "To be thus is nothing" becomes "signifying nothing" — the crown has produced emptiness, not meaning
- **Masculinity:** Macbeth dies fighting, but Macduff has already redefined manhood as feeling grief as well as acting
- **Kingship:** Macbeth's diseased rule is removed; Malcolm restores lawful succession and social order
- **Tragic form:** the protagonist falls, order returns, and the audience experiences catharsis — pity, fear and moral clarity

## 4. Content reference pack

### Dates & timeline

- Act 5 Scene 1: Lady Macbeth sleepwalks; the Doctor and Gentlewoman witness her involuntary confession
- Act 5 Scene 2: Scottish nobles describe Macbeth's forces and Scotland's condition; sickness/medicine imagery prepares Act 5.3
- Act 5 Scene 3: Macbeth at Dunsinane; defiant military posture; the Doctor's report about Lady Macbeth; "I am sick at heart"; "My way of life"; "Canst thou not minister to a mind diseased"
- Act 5 Scene 4: Malcolm's army camouflages itself with Birnam Wood branches
- Act 5 Scene 5: Lady Macbeth's death is reported; Macbeth gives the "Tomorrow" speech; Birnam Wood appears to move
- Act 5 Scene 8: Macbeth meets Macduff; learns the equivocation about "none of woman born"; chooses to fight anyway
- Act 5 Scene 9: Macduff presents Macbeth's defeat; Malcolm's closing speech restores order

### Key people

- **Lady Macbeth:** Her sleepwalking scene is both her most psychologically exposed moment and the completion of her arc. She re-enacts the murder and refers to Duncan, Banquo and Lady Macduff. Her off-stage death is consistent with the play's pattern of keeping some crucial acts unseen while showing their psychological consequences.
- **Macbeth:** His Act 5 behaviour is fragmented — defiant, sickened, nihilistic and finally combative. He dies a soldier, which is both his origin and his ending: the violence that once made him heroic now leaves him isolated and condemned.
- **Macduff:** His defeat of Macbeth is the play's justice. His motivation remains grief and Scotland, not ambition. He is the agent through whom the equivocation is exposed.
- **Malcolm:** His restoration re-establishes legitimate succession. His verdict on Macbeth and Lady Macbeth is politically necessary but dramatically incomplete.
- **The Doctor:** A witness figure. His inability to cure Lady Macbeth reinforces that her problem is not physical illness alone; it is conscience, sin and spiritual disorder.
- **The Gentlewoman:** Protects herself by refusing to repeat Lady Macbeth's words without a witness. Her caution shows the danger of truth in Macbeth's Scotland.

### Key terms & definitions

- **Sleepwalking (5.1):** The body acting out what the conscious mind suppresses. Lady Macbeth's sleepwalking is the physical manifestation of guilt she has tried to deny since 2.2.
- **Somatic guilt:** Psychological guilt experienced through the body: washing hands, smelling blood, disturbed sleep, compulsive speech.
- **Nihilism:** The belief that life is meaningless. Macbeth's "Tomorrow" speech is the endpoint of ambition emptied of moral purpose.
- **Catharsis:** The emotional release or purging associated with tragedy; the audience moves through pity and fear towards moral clarity.
- **Tragic ending:** The restoration of order after disorder. In Jacobean tragedy, legitimate authority must be re-established after the violation of divine and natural order.
- **False security:** Macbeth's confidence based on the witches' equivocal language; it collapses when Birnam Wood moves and Macduff reveals his birth.
- **"This dead butcher and his fiend-like queen":** Malcolm's verdict. Useful for high-level AQA answers because it is both valid as political judgement and too simple as a reading of Shakespeare's characterisation.

### Case studies / named examples

- **Act 5.1 — The sleepwalking scene:**
  - "Out, damned spot! out, I say!" — the imperative is addressed to an imaginary spot; "damned" invokes theological judgement
  - "One; two: why, then 'tis time to do't" — she relives the timing of Duncan's murder
  - "Yet who would have thought the old man to have had so much blood in him?" — the physical reality of Duncan's death now overwhelms the woman who once dismissed the blood
  - "The Thane of Fife had a wife. Where is she now?" — Lady Macbeth's conscience reaches the later innocent victims, not just Duncan
  - "What, will these hands ne'er be clean?" — the dramatic irony of "A little water clears us" is complete
  - "Here's the smell of the blood still: all the perfumes of Arabia will not sweeten this little hand" — smell, scale and smallness combine; guilt has become sensory and uncleanable
  - Contrast with 2.2: "A little water clears us of this deed" — she was wrong; no water can cleanse moral guilt

- **AQA Jun 2023 extract (Act 5.3) — Macbeth as changing male character:**
  - "I am sick at heart" — the warrior of Act 1 is now inwardly diseased; the phrase also links Macbeth's mind to Scotland's condition
  - "My way of life / Is fall'n into the sere, the yellow leaf" — autumn imagery; "sere" means dried up; natural metaphor for moral decay; contrast with Duncan's "plant thee" in 1.4
  - "Honour, love, obedience, troops of friends, / I must not look to have" — list structure totals the human losses caused by ambition
  - "Canst thou not minister to a mind diseased" — Macbeth asks for Lady Macbeth to be cured, but the phrase reflects his own state too; he still thinks inner corruption can be treated like a physical illness
  - "Throw physic to the dogs; I'll none of it" — when medicine cannot fix the moral/spiritual problem, Macbeth rejects it entirely
  - AQA Jun 2023 indicative content (AO1): comments on Macbeth in the extract, e.g. his despair; details about Macbeth at the end of the play
  - AQA Jun 2023 indicative content (AO2): language used to present Macbeth's despair; use of soliloquies to reveal Macbeth's character/inner thoughts
  - AQA Jun 2023 indicative content (AO3): Macbeth's use of violence in war/to fulfil ambition; Macbeth's attitude to religious issues; kingship and expectations

- **"Tomorrow, and tomorrow, and tomorrow":**
  - Structure: triple repetition of "tomorrow" mimics the endless procession of time; each tomorrow is the same as the last
  - "Creeps in this petty pace" — the verb "creeps" is both slow and low; "petty" suggests contemptible smallness
  - "Life's but a walking shadow" — shadow = no substance; the theatrical metaphor begins
  - "A poor player / That struts and frets his hour upon the stage / And then is heard no more" — self-referential theatre metaphor; Macbeth is a character in a play describing life as performance
  - "It is a tale / Told by an idiot, full of sound and fury, / Signifying nothing" — existence as meaningless noise; this is Macbeth's final philosophical position

- **The equivocations fulfilled:**
  - Birnam Wood "moves" because Malcolm's soldiers carry branches; Macbeth's interpretation collapses
  - "None of woman born" is fulfilled through Macduff's "untimely ripped" birth; the witches were technically truthful but morally misleading
  - "I bear a charmed life" shows Macbeth still clinging to the wording until the final revelation

- **Macbeth's death: "Yet I will try the last" / "I will not yield":** Macbeth has learned that the witches equivocated; he knows he can be defeated; but he chooses to fight anyway. For AQA, this is morally complex: courage, pride, despair and soldierly identity are all present. He dies as a soldier, which deliberately mirrors how he entered the play.

- **Malcolm's restoration:**
  - "The time is free" — Macduff announces liberation from tyranny
  - "This dead butcher and his fiend-like queen" — official judgement; useful but reductive
  - "By the grace of Grace" — restoration is framed through divine order; legitimate kingship returns after unnatural rule
  - High-level move: contrast Malcolm's simple verdict with Shakespeare's complex characterisation

### Causes & effects

- Lady Macbeth's suppressed conscience → sleepwalking scene → guilt resurfaces through body and speech → she cannot function → she dies off-stage
- Macbeth's bravado in Act 5 → false confidence from equivocal prophecies → Birnam Wood moves → Macduff reveals his birth → his false security collapses
- Lady Macbeth's death → "Tomorrow" speech → nihilism voiced fully → ambition's endpoint is meaninglessness
- Macduff reveals his untimely birth → equivocation exposed → Macbeth chooses to fight → he dies in battle
- Malcolm's restoration → "The time is free" → order restored → catharsis for Jacobean audience → the disruption of the Great Chain of Being is resolved

- **Model paragraph (whole play, AO2-led):** "Shakespeare charts Macbeth's change through a progression of soliloquies. In Act 1.7, his soliloquy reveals an intact moral compass: 'He's here in double trust' catalogues his obligations with the precision of a man who understands every bond he is about to break. By Act 5.3, this moral vocabulary has been replaced by defeat: 'My way of life / Is fall'n into the sere, the yellow leaf' uses the natural imagery of autumn to signal moral decay rather than seasonal change. The shift from the complex moral reasoning of 1.7 to the simple botanical image of 5.3 shows not just a changed man but a diminished one — the very capacity for extended ethical argument has been eroded by guilt, fear and the consequences of his crimes."

**Misconceptions worth a MisconceptionCheck:**
- "The 'Tomorrow' speech shows Macbeth feels guilty" — NUANCED: the speech shows nihilism, not active guilt; guilt dominated Acts 2–3, but by Act 5 he has moved into emptiness
- "The 'Tomorrow' speech is mainly Macbeth grieving for Lady Macbeth" — TOO SIMPLE: her death triggers it, but the speech is about time, life and meaninglessness more than personal mourning
- "Lady Macbeth's sleepwalking shows she was always weak" — FALSE: earlier she could suppress guilt powerfully; the sleepwalking shows the limit of suppression, not simple weakness
- "Macbeth dies bravely, so he is redeemed" — TOO SIMPLE: he regains soldierly courage, but he does not repent or repair what he has done
- "Malcolm's verdict is Shakespeare's own final judgement" — FALSE: Malcolm's verdict is a political judgement needed to restore order; Shakespeare has shown something more complex
