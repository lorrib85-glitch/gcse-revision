## 2. Storyline

### Core takeaway

Act 5 brings both arcs to their conclusions, and Shakespeare ensures each character gets the ending their choices have earned. Lady Macbeth — who dismissed blood as easily washed in Act 2.2, who invoked spirits to suppress her conscience in Act 1.5 — cannot sleep; she is trapped in a compulsive re-enactment of the murder, her suppressed guilt returning as her conscious control fails. Macbeth — who once saw the full moral weight of what he was about to do (1.7), who was destroyed by a hallucinated ghost (3.4) — has gone through the other side of guilt into emptiness; his "Tomorrow" speech is nihilism, not self-pity. He has felt everything there is to feel about his crimes and emerged unable to feel anything. The play ends with order restored, but Shakespeare does not celebrate this: the restoration comes at the cost of every human relationship Macbeth destroyed. Malcolm's final words call Macbeth "this dead butcher and his fiend-like queen" — a verdict, but one that understates the complexity Shakespeare has shown.

### Evidence for the takeaway

- "Out, damned spot! out, I say!" — Lady Macbeth reliving the murder; the imperative directed at a spot of blood that no one else can see; the guilt she dismissed in 2.2 has taken over her sleeping mind
- "Here's the smell of the blood still" — her senses are trapped in the murder; she cannot clean what she told Macbeth could be washed away with "a little water"
- "More needs she the divine than the physician" — the Doctor's diagnosis; Lady Macbeth's affliction is spiritual/psychological, not medical; it cannot be cured by physical medicine
- "I have almost forgot the taste of fears" — Macbeth in Act 5.3; his relationship with fear has been so extreme that it has hollowed out; he has gone through the emotion, not past it
- "Tomorrow, and tomorrow, and tomorrow / Creeps in this petty pace from day to day" — the most famous speech in the play; Macbeth's nihilism on learning of Lady Macbeth's death; life is meaningless, time is purposeless, existence is theatrical performance without significance
- "Life's but a walking shadow, a poor player / That struts and frets his hour upon the stage / And then is heard no more" — the theatrical metaphor; life as performance; the metaphor is darkly self-referential (a character in a play is describing life as a play)
- "Birnam Wood be come to Dunsinane" — the messenger's report; the equivocal prophecy is fulfilling; Macbeth's false security begins to crack
- "Macduff was from his mother's womb / Untimely ripped" — the equivocation revealed; Macbeth's reaction is not fear but a strange exhausted recognition
- "This dead butcher and his fiend-like queen" — Malcolm's verdict; the final official judgement; but by this point the audience has seen far more than this summary acknowledges

## 4. Content reference pack

### Dates & timeline

- Act 5 Scene 1: Lady Macbeth's sleepwalking; the Doctor and Gentlewoman witness it
- Act 5 Scene 3: Macbeth at Dunsinane; defiant military posture; the Doctor's report about Lady Macbeth ("a mind diseased"); "I have almost forgot the taste of fears"
- Act 5 Scene 4–5: Malcolm's army camouflages with Birnam Wood branches; Lady Macbeth's death reported; the "Tomorrow" speech
- Act 5 Scene 8: Macbeth meets Macduff; learns the equivocation about "none of woman born"; chooses to fight anyway
- Act 5 Scene 9: Malcolm's closing speech; verdict on Macbeth and Lady Macbeth; restoration of order

### Key people

- **Lady Macbeth:** Her sleepwalking scene (5.1) is both her most psychologically exposed moment and the completion of her arc; she re-enacts the murder, speaks of Duncan, Banquo and Lady Macduff — she has registered every death even while claiming not to; her suicide (reported off-stage) is consistent with the play's pattern of keeping its most significant moments off-stage
- **Macbeth:** His Act 5 behaviour is fragmented — defiant ("I'll fight till from my bones my flesh be hacked"), nihilistic ("Tomorrow"), briefly tender about Lady Macbeth ("She should have died hereafter"), resolute at the end ("Yet I will try the last"); he dies a soldier, which is both his downfall (violence) and his origin (Act 1 heroism); the ending is deliberately ambiguous
- **Macduff:** His killing of Macbeth is the play's justice; his motivation (loss of family) is love, not ambition; he does not kill out of cruelty but grief
- **Malcolm:** His restoration restores the legitimate succession; but his verdict ("this dead butcher and his fiend-like queen") is too simple for what the play has shown; Shakespeare's complexity exceeds his character's judgement

### Key terms & definitions

- **Sleepwalking (5.1):** In Jacobean medicine, sleepwalking was associated with a troubled conscience; the body acting out what the conscious mind suppresses; Lady Macbeth's sleepwalking is the physical manifestation of guilt she has suppressed since 2.2
- **Nihilism:** The belief that life is meaningless; Macbeth's "Tomorrow" speech is the purest expression of nihilism in English literature; it arises from the total emptying of meaning through crime
- **Catharsis:** The Aristotelian concept of emotional purging through watching tragedy; the audience's pity and fear for Macbeth and Lady Macbeth are "purged" at the play's end, leaving clarity about the consequences of ambition
- **Tragic ending:** The restoration of order marks the end of the tragic disruption; in Jacobean tragedy, legitimate authority must be re-established; Malcolm's kingship restores the divine order that Macbeth violated
- **"This dead butcher and his fiend-like queen":** Malcolm's verdict; deliberately reductive; for AQA high-level answers: Shakespeare has shown us a far more complex pair; the reductive summary is part of the restoration of a simple moral order that the play has complicated

### Case studies / named examples

- **Act 5.1 — The sleepwalking scene:**
  - "Out, damned spot! out, I say!" — the imperative is addressed to an imaginary spot; the word "damned" invokes theological damnation; she is in hell before she dies
  - "One; two: why, then 'tis time to do't" — she is reliving the moment of the murder; the counting mimics the clock that struck as she waited
  - "Here's the smell of the blood still: all the perfumes of Arabia will not sweeten this little hand" — the "little" is agonising; the grandiosity of "all the perfumes of Arabia" contrasts with the smallness ("little hand") of the human being who cannot escape what she has done
  - Contrast with 2.2: "A little water clears us of this deed" — the dramatic irony is complete; she was wrong; no water can cleanse it

- **AQA Jun 2023 extract (Act 5.3) — Macbeth as changing male character:**
  - "I am sick at heart" — the man who "unseam'd" enemies in battle is now "sick"; his confidence is fragile; the phrase is also literal (his heart has been morally sickened)
  - "My way of life / Is fall'n into the sere, the yellow leaf" — the autumn imagery; "sere" means dried up; "yellow leaf" is a leaf past its peak; he is using natural metaphor for his own moral decay; contrast with Duncan's "plant thee" (1.4) — the language of cultivation has become the language of autumn
  - "Honour, love, obedience, troops of friends, / I must not look to have" — the enumeration of everything he has lost through his crimes; the list performs the totalling of damage
  - "Canst thou not minister to a mind diseased" — he asks the Doctor to cure Lady Macbeth; ironically, he is the one who needs a physician for his own mind; the displacement reveals his refusal to acknowledge his own psychological state
  - AQA Jun 2023 indicative content (AO1): "comments on Macbeth in the extract, eg his despair"; "details about Macbeth at the end of the play"
  - AQA Jun 2023 indicative content (AO2): "language used to present Macbeth's despair at this point"; "use of soliloquies to reveal Macbeth's character/inner thoughts"
  - AQA Jun 2023 indicative content (AO3): "Macbeth's use of violence in the context of war/to fulfil his ambition"; "Macbeth's attitude to religious issues at different points"; "kingship and expectations thereof"

- **"Tomorrow, and tomorrow, and tomorrow":**
  - Structure: the triple repetition of "tomorrow" mimics the endless procession of time — each tomorrow is the same as the last; time has no direction, no purpose
  - "Creeps in this petty pace" — the verb "creeps" is both slow and low; "petty" is contemptible smallness; time is both agonisingly slow and insignificant
  - "Life's but a walking shadow" — shadow = no substance; the theatrical metaphor begins; a "walking shadow" is a performance without reality
  - "A poor player / That struts and frets his hour upon the stage / And then is heard no more" — the actor metaphor is self-referential (Macbeth is himself a character in a play); "struts and frets" implies pompous anxiety — the behaviour of a king without real authority
  - "It is a tale / Told by an idiot, full of sound and fury, / Signifying nothing" — the conclusion: existence is meaningless narration by a fool; "sound and fury" without meaning; this is Macbeth's final philosophical position

- **Macbeth's death: "Yet I will try the last":** He has learned that the witches equivocated; he knows he can be killed; but he chooses to fight anyway. For AQA: this is the most morally complex moment of his Act 5 arc — is this courage, despair, or dignity? The answer is probably all three. He dies as a soldier, which is both his beginning (Act 1) and his end; the symmetry is deliberate.

### Causes & effects

- Lady Macbeth's suppressed conscience → sleepwalking scene → guilt resurfaces as somatic symptom → she cannot function → she dies by suicide (off-stage, reported)
- Macbeth's bravado in Act 5 → false confidence from equivocal prophecies → armies advance; Birnam Wood moves; the equivocations begin unravelling → his bravado cannot hold
- "Tomorrow" speech → Lady Macbeth's death is the trigger → but the nihilism predates her death; it is the endpoint of his entire arc from Episode 3 (ambition) to here
- Macduff reveals his untimely birth → the equivocation is exposed → Macbeth chooses to fight → he dies in battle → the death is proportionate (as a soldier he lived; as a soldier he dies)
- Malcolm's restoration → "The time is free" → order restored → catharsis for Jacobean audience → the disruption of the Great Chain of Being is resolved → the play's moral argument is complete

### Exam angles

**AQA Jun 2023 actual exam question:**
- Extract: Act 5.3 (Macbeth hearing of approaching army, asking about Lady Macbeth)
- Question: "How far Shakespeare presents Macbeth as a male character who changes during the play"
- **Model paragraph (whole play, AO2-led):** "Shakespeare charts Macbeth's change through a progression of soliloquies. In Act 1.7, his soliloquy reveals an intact moral compass: 'He's here in double trust' catalogues his obligations with the precision of a man who understands every bond he is about to break. By Act 5.3, this moral vocabulary has been replaced by defeat: 'My way of life / Is fall'n into the sere, the yellow leaf' uses the natural imagery of autumn to signal moral decay rather than seasonal change. The shift from the complex moral reasoning of 1.7 to the simple botanical image of 5.3 shows not just a changed man but a diminished one — the very capacity for extended ethical argument has been eroded by guilt, fear and the consequences of his crimes."

**Misconceptions worth a MisconceptionCheck:**
- "The 'Tomorrow' speech shows Macbeth feels guilty" — NUANCED: the speech shows nihilism, not guilt; guilt is what he felt in Acts 2–3; by Act 5, he is *beyond* guilt into emptiness — a more complete form of moral destruction
- "Lady Macbeth's sleepwalking shows she was always going to break down" — FALSE: her earlier behaviour shows genuine capability for suppression; the sleepwalking shows the limit of that suppression, not its inevitability
- "Malcolm's verdict 'this dead butcher and his fiend-like queen' is Shakespeare's own verdict" — FALSE: this is Malcolm's verdict — a political judgement needed to restore order; Shakespeare has shown us something far more complex; high-level answers use the gap between the verdict and the play's complexity
