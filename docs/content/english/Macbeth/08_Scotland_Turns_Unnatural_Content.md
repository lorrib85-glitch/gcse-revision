## 2. Storyline

### Core takeaway

Act 2 Scenes 3 and 4 show the consequences of regicide radiating outward from Macbeth's private guilt to the public world of Scotland. Duncan's murder is not merely a personal crime; it is a cosmic rupture. Shakespeare presents this through two registers simultaneously: the Porter scene (comic, low, earthy) and the unnatural events reported in Act 2.4 (darkness in daytime, an owl killing a falcon, horses eating each other). Both registers say the same thing: the natural order that held Scotland together has been violated, and the violation is visible everywhere. For Jacobean audiences who believed in the Great Chain of Being — a divinely ordered hierarchy from God to the lowest creature — regicide was not metaphorically unnatural; it was *literally* so.

### Evidence for the takeaway

- "Porter of hell-gate" — the Porter's comedy immediately positions Inverness castle as hell; the comic speech is a Jacobean audience signal that something spiritually significant is happening
- "Most sacrilegious murder hath broke ope / The Lord's anointed temple" — Macduff's description of Duncan's murder as an attack on God's church; the "temple" metaphor identifies Duncan's body with God's sanctuary
- "Dark night strangles the travelling lamp" — the sun cannot rise on the morning after the murder; darkness is the natural world's response to regicide
- "'Tis unnatural / Even like the deed that's done" — the connection between Macbeth's act and the unnatural events is made explicit; the word "like" is key: the universe mirrors the moral state of its king
- "A falcon, tow'ring in her pride of place, / Was by a mousing owl hawk'd at and kill'd" — a superior bird (falcon) killed by an inferior (owl); the hierarchy of nature has inverted, mirroring the inversion of political hierarchy
- "Duncan's horses... eat each other" — the most extreme image; even domesticated animals have become wild and cannibalistic; the disorder goes all the way down the Chain of Being

### Series throughline

This episode is the clearest expression of the AO3 context theme — the Great Chain of Being — in the entire play. It connects directly to Episode 1 (the witches establishing disorder), Episode 4 (Duncan as God's anointed representative), and Episode 12 (Malcolm's restoration of order in Act 5.9 reverses the unnaturalness here). The Porter scene's comedy also provides tonal contrast — Shakespeare's use of comic relief here is a structural choice to create a moment of audience release before the discovery and its aftermath.

## 4. Content reference pack

### Dates & timeline

- Act 2 Scene 3: Early morning; the knocking at the gate (heard at the end of 2.2); the Porter's comedy; Macduff and Lennox arrive; Macbeth greets them; Macduff discovers the body; alarm; Macbeth "discovers" the grooms as murderers; Malcolm and Donalbain flee
- Act 2 Scene 4: The Old Man and Ross discuss the unnatural events; Macduff reports that Malcolm and Donalbain are suspected of the murder; Macbeth has gone to Scone to be crowned

### Key people

- **The Porter:** A comic character; his speech about being "porter of hell-gate" is dark comedy — he is listing the types of people who would belong in hell; the comic mode allows Shakespeare to establish the spiritual stakes while providing audience relief
- **Macduff:** His discovery of Duncan's body establishes him as the moral opposite of Macbeth; his language is extreme in its moral weight ("most sacrilegious murder"); he is introduced as the play's conscience before he becomes its avenger
- **Macbeth:** Present at the discovery; his performance of grief is his first major public deception since agreeing to the murder; Shakespeare gives him little to say — his "performance" has to be read in his silences
- **Lady Macbeth:** Enters after the discovery; her faint is either calculated (to distract attention from Macbeth) or a genuine physical response to what she has done — Shakespeare leaves it deliberately ambiguous
- **Malcolm and Donalbain:** Duncan's sons; they flee separately — "our separated fortune / Shall keep us both the safer" — their flight will be used as evidence of guilt; Macbeth is immediately the beneficiary
- **The Old Man and Ross:** In 2.4, these two figures discuss the unnatural events; the Old Man is a structural device — he represents ordinary Scotland's experience of the cosmic rupture; Ross will become increasingly complicit in Macbeth's tyranny across the play

### Key terms & definitions

- **Great Chain of Being:** The Jacobean cosmological belief that all of creation exists in a divinely ordained hierarchy: God → angels → king → nobles → common people → animals → plants → stones; regicide — killing the king — disrupts every level of this hierarchy simultaneously
- **Pathetic fallacy:** When the natural environment reflects the emotional or moral state of characters; in 2.4, the pathetic fallacy is extended to the entire natural world, not just weather
- **Comic relief:** Shakespeare's deliberate use of the Porter's comedy to release the tension of 2.2; also provides a contrast that makes the discovery more shocking; and frames the castle as hell through the Porter's own analogy
- **Sacrilege:** The violation of something sacred; Macduff's word "sacrilegious" elevates the murder from a crime to a sin; Duncan's body is a "temple" and the murder is a desecration
- **Equivocator (Porter scene):** The Porter lists types deserving hell; the "equivocator" reference connects to the Gunpowder Plot and Henry Garnet (see AO3 below); this is a topical joke for 1606 audiences

### Case studies / named examples

- **The Porter's "hell-gate" speech:** The Porter pretends to be the gatekeeper of hell, admitting sinners. For AQA AO2: this is comic relief but it is *thematically precise* — Inverness castle *is* a hell-gate at this moment; three people who have died or will die (Duncan, the two grooms Macbeth has killed) are inside. The comedy does not undermine the horror; it contextualises it within a moral framework the Jacobean audience recognised (the gateway to hell was a familiar image from mystery plays).

- **Equivocator reference (AO3 — high value):** The Porter mentions "an equivocator, that could swear in both the scales against either scale, who committed treason enough for God's sake, yet could not equivocate to heaven." This is a direct reference to Henry Garnet, the Jesuit priest executed in 1606 for his involvement in the Gunpowder Plot, who used the doctrine of equivocation (deliberate ambiguity in speech) as his defence. For an AQA audience: this reference dates the play precisely and connects the themes of equivocation (witches in 1.3), deception (Macbeth's "false face") and treachery (Gunpowder Plot) into a single moment. It is also funny — the Porter's timing makes it dark comedy rather than lecture.

- **"Most sacrilegious murder hath broke ope / The Lord's anointed temple":** Macduff's description is the theological thesis of the play. Duncan's body is a "temple" — a place of God's presence — and murder has "broke ope" (forced open / desecrated) it. This language positions Macbeth's crime not just as murder but as blasphemy. For AQA AO3: this is the clearest statement of divine right of kings in the play; it explains why the consequences are cosmic, not merely personal.

- **The unnatural events (2.4):** The inversion of hierarchy is the key pattern: dark night strangles the "travelling lamp" (sun — the highest celestial object after God); a mousing owl (lowest predator) kills a falcon (noble bird); Duncan's horses (domesticated animals) eat each other. Each example moves the inversion down the Great Chain: cosmic → avian → mammalian. Shakespeare is showing that regicide ruptures the entire created order, not just the political one.

### Causes & effects

- Regicide committed → Scotland's natural order destabilised → unnatural events follow → Jacobean audience understands these as evidence of God's displeasure
- Porter jokes about hell-gate → frames Inverness as hell → positions Macbeth as a Satanic figure without stating it directly → allows Shakespeare to convey theological meaning through comedy
- Macduff discovers body → his response establishes the moral weight of the crime → he becomes the play's conscience and eventually its instrument of justice → his family's murder in 4.2 is the act that fully makes Macbeth a tyrant without moral complexity
- Malcolm and Donalbain flee → their absence used as evidence of guilt → Macbeth is free to take the crown without obvious suspicion → his usurpation appears legitimate at this moment
- Macbeth is crowned at Scone → formal assumption of the stolen crown → Scotland now has an illegitimate, murdering king → everything that follows (Banquo's murder, Macduff's family's murder) flows from this

### Exam angles

**Common question types linking to this episode:**
- "How does Shakespeare present order and disorder in Macbeth?" — 2.3–2.4 are the purest expressions of disorder's consequences
- "How does Shakespeare use the natural world in Macbeth?" — unnatural events as pathetic fallacy; contrast with Act 5.9's restoration
- "How does Shakespeare present the consequences of ambition/regicide?" — the cosmic consequences in 2.4 show that Macbeth's ambition has damaged more than he intended

**Misconceptions worth a MisconceptionCheck:**
- "The unnatural events in Act 2 are just superstition" — FALSE for a Jacobean audience: belief in the Great Chain of Being was mainstream; natural disorder following regicide was theologically expected, not superstitious
- "The Porter scene is irrelevant to the play's themes" — FALSE: the Porter's hell-gate analogy is precise; the equivocator reference is historically specific (Gunpowder Plot/Henry Garnet); it is thematically integrated comic relief
- "Lady Macbeth faints because she is guilty" — UNCERTAIN: Shakespeare leaves it ambiguous; it may be calculated distraction or genuine response; high-level answers acknowledge both interpretations
