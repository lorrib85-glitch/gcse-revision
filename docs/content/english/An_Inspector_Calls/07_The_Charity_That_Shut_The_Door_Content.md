## 2. Storyline

### Core takeaway

Mrs Birling exposes the cruelty of class prejudice disguised as respectability and charity. She refuses Eva's appeal to the Brumley Women's Charity Organisation not because Eva does not need help, but because Eva does not fit Mrs Birling's idea of a deserving poor woman. Eva is pregnant, desperate and at the end of the chain, but Mrs Birling judges her language, class, sexual status and use of the Birling name instead of responding to her need.

This episode is especially powerful because Mrs Birling believes she is morally right. She is not acting like a cartoon villain. She is acting as if charity should protect respectable society from the undeserving poor. Priestley's criticism is therefore bigger than one cold woman: he attacks a whole system where the rich decide whether the poor deserve survival.

The dramatic irony is brutal. Mrs Birling insists the father of Eva's child should be made responsible, not knowing that the father is Eric. In condemning the unknown young man, she condemns her own son.

### Evidence for the takeaway

- "a rather cold woman" — opening stage direction; emotional coldness presented before her interrogation
- "social superior" — her class status is part of her power
- "I didn't like her manner" — she judges Eva's behaviour/style rather than her need
- "used the name Mrs Birling" — Mrs Birling treats the name as an insult to her status
- "girls of that class" — class prejudice; Eva is reduced to a category before she is understood as a person
- "a girl of that sort" — moral and sexual judgement wrapped in class language
- "claiming elaborate fine feelings and scruples that were simply absurd in a girl in her position" — she refuses to believe a poor pregnant woman can have genuine moral sensitivity
- "not a good case" — bureaucratic/euphemistic language; human desperation reduced to eligibility
- "I used my influence to have it refused" — she abuses social power while making it sound respectable
- "slammed the door in her face" — the Inspector's direct phrase exposes the cruelty her language hides
- "Go and look for the father of the child. It's his responsibility" — she tries to transfer all blame to the unknown father
- "make sure that he's compelled to confess in public his responsibility" — she unknowingly demands public judgement of Eric
- Sheila's growing alarm — the younger generation realises the truth before Mrs Birling does
- "Eric" / his return — the dramatic irony lands: Mrs Birling has condemned her own son

### Series throughline

Episode 7 is the penultimate link in Eva's destruction. Birling removes Eva's job; Sheila removes her shop work; Gerald offers temporary security and then leaves; Mrs Birling removes her final access to help. The chain is almost complete before Eric's role is exposed.

Mrs Birling also develops Priestley's critique of charity. Charity should be compassionate, but in the play it becomes another gatekeeping system controlled by the upper classes. Help is offered only if the poor perform respectability in a way the rich approve.

The episode sharpens the older/younger divide. Sheila senses the truth and tries to warn her mother; Mrs Birling is so certain of her own class judgement that she cannot hear the danger in her own words. This becomes crucial in Episode 8.

## 4. Content reference pack

### Dates & timeline

- **After Gerald ends the relationship:** Eva/Daisy becomes increasingly vulnerable
- **After Eric:** Eva becomes pregnant and refuses stolen money once she suspects its source
- **Around two weeks before Eva's death:** Eva appeals to the Brumley Women's Charity Organisation
- **Charity refusal:** Mrs Birling uses her influence to have the claim refused
- **Final consequence:** Eva loses her last possible support and later dies
- **Act 2 climax:** Mrs Birling condemns the father; Eric's role is about to be exposed

### Key people

- **Mrs Sybil Birling:** "rather cold"; socially superior to Arthur; class-conscious; believes her judgement is moral. Her danger lies in certainty, not obvious malice.
- **Eva Smith / Daisy Renton:** pregnant, desperate and at the end of the chain. She still tries to act with moral scruple, especially around Eric's money.
- **Sheila Birling:** increasingly perceptive. She understands the implication about Eric before Mrs Birling does and tries to stop her mother condemning him.
- **Eric Birling:** absent for most of this interrogation but structurally present. Mrs Birling's words prepare his reveal.
- **Inspector Goole:** forces Mrs Birling's polite language into moral clarity, especially through "slammed the door in her face".

### Key terms & definitions

- **Deserving poor:** Victorian/Edwardian idea that poor people must prove moral worthiness before receiving help.
- **Undeserving poor:** those judged immoral, idle, deceitful or socially unacceptable; often denied charity.
- **Charity gatekeeping:** when people with status control access to help and decide who deserves it.
- **Influence:** Mrs Birling's polite word for social power; she uses it to block Eva's claim.
- **Dramatic irony:** the audience/Sheila can see that Mrs Birling is condemning Eric before she realises.
- **Hypocrisy:** claiming moral respectability while acting without compassion.
- **Class prejudice:** judging someone's worth because of their class background.
- **Moral absolutism:** certainty that one's judgement is right, even when evidence suggests otherwise.

### Case studies / named examples

- **"Rather cold woman":** The stage direction is concise but powerful. Priestley makes Sybil's emotional temperature part of her identity before she speaks.

- **"Girls of that class" / "a girl of that sort":** These phrases erase Eva as an individual. Mrs Birling sees category before person. This is perfect evidence for class-prejudice essays.

- **"I didn't like her manner":** Mrs Birling judges tone and behaviour rather than need. Priestley exposes how charity can become a performance test controlled by the privileged.

- **"Claiming elaborate fine feelings and scruples":** Mrs Birling cannot accept that a poor pregnant woman could have moral principles. The adjective "elaborate" makes Eva's feelings seem artificial; "absurd" dismisses them completely.

- **"Not a good case":** The phrase is bureaucratic and cold. It turns a desperate pregnant woman into an administrative problem.

- **"I used my influence to have it refused":** Mrs Birling's power is social, not just formal. "Influence" sounds respectable, but in practice it means blocking help.

- **"Slammed the door in her face":** The Inspector strips away euphemism. Mrs Birling says she refused a case; the Inspector says she shut out a desperate woman.

- **"Go and look for the father of the child. It's his responsibility":** The sentence is morally important because she is partly right: the father is responsible. But she uses that truth to avoid her own responsibility.

- **"Make sure that he's compelled to confess in public his responsibility":** This is the dramatic irony climax. She demands a public confession from her own son without knowing it.

- **Sheila's alarm:** Sheila's reactions show her moral and structural understanding. She sees the trap before Mrs Birling because she is listening morally, not protecting class pride.

### Causes & effects

- Eva becomes pregnant → needs support → approaches charity
- Eva uses the name Mrs Birling → Mrs Birling feels insulted → uses influence against her
- Mrs Birling judges Eva's class/manner → refuses help → Eva's last support disappears
- Mrs Birling transfers blame to the father → unknowingly condemns Eric → dramatic irony reaches climax
- Sheila recognises the implication → tries to warn her mother → older/younger divide sharpens
- Mrs Birling refuses responsibility → becomes key example of older generation denial

**Grade 9 essay move:**
Do not write that Mrs Birling is simply cruel. A stronger answer argues that Priestley presents her cruelty as institutional: she embodies a charity system where the rich decide whether the poor are morally worthy of help.

**Useful paragraph stem:**
Priestley presents Mrs Birling as dangerous because her cruelty is disguised as respectable judgement: the language of charity allows her to deny help while believing herself morally correct.

**Key misconceptions for MisconceptionCheck:**
- "Mrs Birling was right to refuse Eva because she lied about her name" — false in moral terms: Eva's need was genuine
- "Mrs Birling is just evil" — too simple: she believes she is acting properly, which makes Priestley's criticism of the system stronger
- "The charity was generous by existing" — too simple: Priestley shows charity can reinforce class power if it judges rather than helps
- "The father alone is responsible" — false: Eric is responsible, but the whole chain still matters
- "Mrs Birling changes when Eric is revealed" — false/mostly false: she is shocked but does not truly accept her own responsibility
