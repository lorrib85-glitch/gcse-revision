## 2. Storyline

### Core takeaway
Mrs Birling exposes the cruelty of class prejudice disguised as respectability and charity. She refused Eva's appeal to the Brumley Women's Charity Organisation — not because Eva didn't need help, but because Eva used the name "Mrs Birling" (perhaps identifying herself as the mother of Birling's child), which Mrs Birling found offensive. Her response was personal vanity masquerading as principle. Worse, she calls for the father of Eva's child to be "made an example of" — unknowingly condemning her own son Eric. This is the play's most perfect dramatic irony.

### Evidence for the takeaway
- "cold woman" — stage direction; she is described before she speaks through her temperature
- "social superior" — she is Birling's social superior; she has more class capital than anyone in the room
- "girls of that class" — class prejudice expressed as a noun phrase; Eva is reduced to a social category
- "not a good case" — the euphemism of charity bureaucracy; human suffering assessed for worthiness
- "used my influence to have it refused" — she abused her position; this is what power looks like without accountability
- "slammed the door in her face" — the Inspector's phrase, not Mrs Birling's; he names what she euphemised
- "go and look for the father" — her moral judgment on the unnamed father; dramatic irony — it is her own son

### Series throughline
Mrs Birling represents the cruellest aspect of Edwardian class society: charity with strings attached, help conditional on proving you deserve it, and a system designed to protect the comfortable from the consequences of their own actions. Her refusal to accept responsibility, even after Eric is revealed, is the play's bleakest statement about the older generation's incapacity for change.

## 4. Content reference pack

### Dates & timeline
- **Two weeks before Eva's death (approximately):** Eva approaches the Brumley Women's Charity Organisation; is refused
- This is the final blow in Eva's chain of misfortunes; the charity refusal is her last hope

### Key people
- **Mrs Sybil Birling:** "a rather cold woman"; Arthur's "social superior"; highly class-conscious; chairs the charity; her prejudice is more dangerous than Birling's because it is disguised as principle
- **Eva Smith (at the charity):** pregnant; desperate; used the name "Mrs Birling"; was hoping for financial support; was refused

### Key terms & definitions
- **Deserving poor:** Victorian concept that the poor must prove moral worthiness before receiving charity; only those deemed "deserving" receive help; explicitly used in context of Eva's case
- **Influence:** Mrs Birling's word for her power to refuse Eva's claim; she used social position to override the organisation's decision
- **Gatekeeping:** using power to block others' access to resources; Mrs Birling gatekept charity funding from Eva
- **Dramatic irony:** Mrs Birling calls for the father to "be made an example of" not knowing the father is Eric; the audience/Sheila recognise this before she does

### Case studies / named examples
- **"She was claiming elaborate fine feelings and scruples that were simply absurd in a girl in her position":** Mrs Birling dismissed Eva's moral arguments because she believed working-class women couldn't have genuine moral sensibilities — a direct expression of class prejudice
- **"Used the name of Birling":** Eva gave a false name that happened to be Mrs Birling's own surname; Mrs Birling took this as an insult and a deceit; she prioritised her own offended pride over a desperate woman's need
- **"Go and look for the father":** her demand in Act 2; she means it as a moral judgment (the man should take responsibility); in Act 3 it becomes a devastating dramatic irony as Eric is revealed as that father

### Causes & effects
- Eva approaches charity → uses name "Mrs Birling" → offends Mrs Birling
- Mrs Birling "uses influence" → Eva's claim is refused → Eva's last hope is gone
- Eva refused charity → no money, no support, pregnant → dies
- Mrs Birling calls for father to "make an example of himself" → Eric revealed as the father → dramatic irony destroys her credibility
- Mrs Birling refuses all guilt → even after Eric's reveal → she is the play's most fixed and immovable character
