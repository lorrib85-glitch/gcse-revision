## 2. Storyline

### Core takeaway

The final phone call destroys the family's false comfort and proves that Priestley will not let the Birlings escape the moral lesson. After the apparent hoax, the older generation thinks the danger has passed. Then the phone rings: a girl has died, and a real police inspector is on his way.

The ending matters because it repeats the beginning. The family are back where they started: comfortable room, sudden phone call, inspector coming, questions about a dead young woman. But now the audience knows what the family has done. Priestley turns the ending into a trap for characters who refused to learn.

The Inspector's exact identity remains unresolved. He could be read as real, supernatural, symbolic, prophetic, or part of Priestley's interest in time. But the exam point is not to "solve" him. The stronger argument is that Priestley deliberately keeps the ending ambiguous so the audience focuses on the moral truth: responsibility cannot be dodged forever.

### Evidence for the takeaway

- "the telephone rings sharply" — sound interrupts false comfort, just as the doorbell interrupted Birling earlier
- "a girl has just died" — the death is now confirmed after the family tried to dismiss it
- "on her way to the Infirmary" — echoes the Inspector's original announcement
- "after swallowing some disinfectant" — repeated detail; cyclical structure and moral shock
- "a police inspector is on his way here" — the threat returns; this time apparently official
- "to ask some questions" — the interrogation is about to begin again
- Birling is "panic-stricken" — his fear shows exposure anxiety, not genuine guilt
- The curtain falls before the inspector arrives — Priestley withholds resolution and leaves the audience to judge
- Goole / ghoul — supports supernatural possibility, but not a single fixed answer
- The ending mirrors the opening but reverses its emotional meaning: earlier confidence becomes panic

### Series throughline

Episode 10 asked whether the family had learned without legal pressure. Episode 11 shows the consequence of their failure. The older generation used the possible hoax to restore comfort; the final phone call strips that comfort away.

The ending also completes the play's structure. Episode 1 introduced a respectable family about to be disrupted. Episode 11 returns them to that moment, but now respectability has collapsed. The same structure now feels like judgement.

Sheila and Eric do not need the phone call in the same way as Arthur, Sybil and Gerald do. They already understand guilt. The call is aimed at the characters — and audience members — who need external shock before they accept responsibility.

## 4. Content reference pack

### Dates & timeline

- **Same evening:** the whole play happens in one continuous evening
- **After the hoax relief:** the family are briefly allowed to think they have escaped
- **Final moment:** the phone rings and a new inspector is coming
- **1945/46 audience:** they have lived through war and social upheaval; the repeated warning feels urgent, not abstract

### Key people

- **Arthur Birling:** ends "panic-stricken". This is fear, not repentance.
- **Sybil Birling:** the phone call exposes the weakness of her relief and denial.
- **Gerald Croft:** his attempt to reset everything is immediately undermined.
- **Sheila and Eric:** already morally changed; the phone call confirms what they were saying.
- **Inspector Goole:** absent but still dominant; his function survives even when his identity remains uncertain.
- **Audience:** left without resolution; forced to judge what responsibility means.

### Key terms & definitions

- **Cyclical structure:** a narrative that returns to a version of its starting point.
- **Cliff-hanger:** an ending that stops before resolution, keeping tension active.
- **Ambiguity:** deliberate uncertainty with more than one possible interpretation.
- **Structural echo:** a repeated event or image that connects different points in the play.
- **Morality play:** drama designed to teach a moral lesson; characters can function as moral examples.
- **Audience judgement:** the audience is positioned like a jury, deciding what lesson should be taken.
- **Time-play reading:** a higher-level interpretation connected to Priestley's interest in non-linear time.

### Case studies / named examples

- **"The telephone rings sharply":** The adverb "sharply" makes the call feel like a cut through comfort. It mirrors the earlier doorbell: sound interrupts complacency.

- **Repeated announcement:** The final call repeats the details of Eva's death. This structural echo makes the ending feel like punishment, warning and reset all at once.

- **"Panic-stricken":** This is the most efficient evidence for Birling's failure to change. He is not described as remorseful, ashamed or compassionate. He is frightened.

- **"A police inspector is on his way here":** The earlier Inspector's legal status was questioned; now the threat of official investigation returns. Priestley turns the family's relief against them.

- **The curtain falls:** Priestley ends before the new inspector arrives. This is not unfinished storytelling. The missing scene is the point: the audience has to carry the lesson out of the theatre.

- **Multiple interpretations:** The ending supports several readings. Goole could be supernatural, prophetic, symbolic, or simply part of a clever structure. Strong answers explain why Priestley keeps it unresolved.

- **Time theory extension:** Priestley was interested in time and wrote other plays exploring non-linear time. This can support an advanced cyclical/time-loop reading, but it should not replace the core structural argument.

### Causes & effects

- Older generation celebrates the possible hoax → final phone call arrives → false comfort collapses
- Inspector's warning ignored → new inspector coming → lesson returns with more force
- Ending mirrors opening → cyclical structure suggests history repeats until people learn
- Birling panics → his lack of moral change is exposed
- Curtain falls → audience becomes responsible for interpreting the warning

**Useful paragraph stem:**
Priestley's cyclical ending refuses the Birlings' escape: by repeating the phone call and ending before the inspector arrives, he turns the play's structure into a warning that responsibility returns if it is ignored.

**Key misconceptions for MisconceptionCheck:**
- "The Inspector is definitely a ghost" — too certain: it is one reading, not the answer
- "The ending proves the first Inspector was real" — too certain: the ending confirms the moral warning, not a single explanation
- "The ending is only a twist" — false: it is structural and political, not just surprising
- "Birling changes because he panics" — false: panic is fear of exposure, not moral learning
- "The play ends before the real point" — false: the unresolved ending is the point
