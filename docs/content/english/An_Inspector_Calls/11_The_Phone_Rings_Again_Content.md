## 2. Storyline

### Core takeaway
The ending forces the audience to judge the characters and consider whether society will learn before it is too late. The second phone call — that a girl has just died, that a police inspector is on his way — cannot be explained by any single interpretation. Is this a real inspector arriving now? Is the play cycling back to repeat itself unless the characters change? Is Goole a supernatural figure who has arranged the actual consequences? The ambiguity is deliberate: Priestley does not want the audience to resolve it. He wants them to sit with the question. The curtain falls before the inspector arrives. The audience is left to decide.

### Evidence for the takeaway
- "a girl has just died — on her way to the Infirmary — after swallowing some disinfectant" — the identical announcement from Act 1; the structure is cyclical
- "a police inspector is on his way here — to ask some questions" — identical function to Goole's arrival in Act 1
- Birling described as "panic-stricken" — his fear is the only learning he has shown, and it is the fear of exposure, not guilt
- "Goole" — his name rhymes with "ghoul" — the supernatural reading
- "curtain falls" — Priestley's stagecraft; the audience does not see what happens next; they are responsible for the next act
- The cyclical structure: the play begins with a celebration and an Inspector's arrival; it ends with another imminent Inspector's arrival; nothing has changed in the world, only in the characters who choose to change
- Sheila's line from Episode 10: "you're pretending everything's just as it was before" — the ending proves the opposite: it is the same, because the family have not changed

### Series throughline
The ending is the play's most discussed element. It confirms that the Inspector's message was real regardless of his identity. It punishes the older generation's complacency. It rewards Sheila and Eric's moral growth by sparing them from the panic (they already know they are guilty). And it leaves the audience with a question: what will you do? The ending is Priestley addressing the 1945 audience directly: learn the lesson before the next catastrophe arrives.

## 4. Content reference pack

### Dates & timeline
- The ending takes place moments after the investigation in Acts 1–3 — the same evening
- 1945: Priestley writes this for a specific audience who has lived through the consequences of social selfishness twice; the cyclical structure warns them they can do it again

### Key people
- **Arthur Birling:** "panic-stricken" in the final stage direction — fear rather than guilt; his change is of the wrong kind
- **Sheila and Eric:** already know they are guilty; the phone call does not surprise them; they have not needed the confirmation
- **Inspector Goole (absent):** his identity is finally irrelevant; the ending proves his function was real regardless of his nature

### Key terms & definitions
- **Cyclical structure:** a narrative that returns to its starting point; in AIC the ending is the same situation as the opening (a family about to be questioned by an inspector about Eva Smith's death)
- **Time plays:** Priestley's series of plays exploring time (including Time and the Conways); AIC is in this tradition — the cyclical ending implies that history repeats until lessons are learned
- **Cliff-hanger:** the play ends before the resolution; Priestley deliberately withholds the "answer"
- **Morality play ending:** morality plays traditionally posed moral questions rather than resolving them; the audience is the jury
- **Supernatural reading:** Goole as a ghost/supernatural figure who has arranged for a real consequence; supported by his omniscience and his name

### Case studies / named examples
- **J.W. Dunne's time theory:** Priestley was influenced by Dunne's 'An Experiment with Time' (1927), which proposed that time is not linear; people might experience different time-streams; the cyclical ending can be read through this lens
- **"Panic-stricken":** the stage direction for Birling in the final moments; his only response to the moral lesson is fear of exposure — he has not changed; he is afraid of the same consequences, not the same guilt
- **The curtain:** "The curtain falls" — Priestley ends before the resolution; this is a deliberate choice; the audience is left in the same position as Sheila and Eric — knowing what has happened, choosing what to do next

### Causes & effects
- Older generation celebrates the "hoax" → does not change → phone rings → they are trapped again
- The cyclical structure → implies history repeats → the only escape is learning the lesson
- Birling is "panic-stricken" → his fear is of exposure not guilt → Priestley confirms he has not changed
- The curtain falls before the inspector arrives → the audience is left to imagine → Priestley makes the audience participants
