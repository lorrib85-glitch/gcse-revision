## 2. Storyline

### Core takeaway
This episode does not teach new plot or content. It teaches students how to build arguments using everything they have learned. A GCSE essay on An Inspector Calls is not a character summary and it is not a quote list. It is an argument, structured around a thesis, supported with close AO2 analysis, and contextualised with AO3. This episode trains the machinery: thesis first, evidence second, analysis third, context fourth. Students who have learned the content across Episodes 1–11 now learn how to turn that content into top-band responses.

### Evidence for the takeaway
- AQA Paper 2 Section A: one 30-mark question (+ 4 SPaG), whole-play essay, no extract; AO1 (12), AO2 (12), AO3 (6)
- Grade 9 requires: clear argument with a thesis beyond simple description; AO2 embedded in the argument (not as "the writer uses..."); AO3 woven in as evidence for the argument (not as a separate context paragraph)
- The ten essay routes trained in this episode cover every AIC question type that has appeared at GCSE
- The mark scheme rewards students who argue throughout, not students who cover lots of points without a throughline

### Series throughline
This is the series capstone. Every episode has built towards this: the facts, quotes, themes, character arcs, and context from Episodes 1–11 all become material for the essay machine. The episode's job is synthesis, not new content. Students who can write a confident, well-structured, analytically sharp essay on any of the ten question routes have fully internalised the series.

### Exam framing
This episode directly serves every possible AIC exam question. The ten routes trained:
1. How does Priestley present responsibility?
2. How does Priestley present social class?
3. How does Priestley present Mr Birling?
4. How does Priestley present Inspector Goole?
5. How does Priestley present Sheila as a character who changes?
6. How does Priestley present Eva Smith as a victim of society?
7. How does Priestley present younger and older generations?
8. How does Priestley present gender inequality?
9. How does Priestley use the ending?
10. How does Priestley use stagecraft to create tension?

## 3. Specification requirements

### What the exam requires (AQA Paper 2 Section A)
- **Format:** One question on the modern text chosen (An Inspector Calls); whole-play essay only; no extract provided
- **Marks:** 30 marks + 4 SPaG = 34 marks total
- **AO1 (12 marks):** Maintain a critical style and informed personal response; use textual references; construct a coherent, reasoned argument
- **AO2 (12 marks):** Analyse language, form, and structure; use subject terminology accurately; explain effects on the reader/audience
- **AO3 (6 marks):** Show understanding of the relationship between texts and contexts (social, historical, biographical, literary)
- **AO4 / SPaG (4 marks):** Accurate spelling, punctuation and grammar; varied vocabulary; clear sentence structures

### What top-band (Grade 7–9) looks like
- A clear thesis stated in the opening that the essay defends throughout
- AO2 embedded in the argument: not "Priestley uses the technique of..." but "the adjective 'panic-stricken' confirms that Birling's only growth is in fear of exposure, not guilt"
- AO3 used as evidence for the thesis, not as a separate context paragraph
- Whole-play links: at least three different moments across Acts 1, 2 and 3
- Counter-argument or nuance: the essay acknowledges complexity and responds to it

### What mid-band (Grade 4–6) looks like
- Some argument but it collapses into retelling or character description
- AO2 named but not embedded: "the writer uses the technique of metaphor to..."
- AO3 bolted on: "Written in 1945, Priestley..." appears in a separate paragraph rather than woven into the analysis
- Evidence confined to one or two moments rather than across the whole play
- SPaG errors in vocabulary range or sentence structure

### Common misconceptions
- "A longer essay scores higher" — FALSE: a focused 5-paragraph essay with consistent argument and embedded AO2 outscores a sprawling 8-paragraph summary
- "I should write about every character" — FALSE: the question names what to focus on; staying on the question is AO1
- "Context goes in its own paragraph" — FALSE: AO3 is woven into analytical paragraphs as evidence for interpretive claims
- "I should start with 'In this essay I will...'" — FALSE: start with the thesis; the plan should be invisible

### The ten essay routes — thesis and key evidence per route

#### Route 1: How does Priestley present responsibility?
- **Thesis:** Priestley presents responsibility as the moral test that divides the Birling family — those who accept it are changed, those who refuse it are condemned
- **Key evidence:** Inspector's final speech ("we are responsible for each other"); Sheila's refusal to celebrate the hoax ("you're pretending everything's just as it was before"); Birling's opening speech ("a man has to look after himself"); the chain of dismissal — each character's responsibility for Eva Smith's death
- **AO3 link:** 1945 post-war moral reckoning; the welfare state; "members of one body" as political programme

#### Route 2: How does Priestley present social class?
- **Thesis:** Priestley presents social class as a system that allows the privileged to destroy the powerless and feel no guilt — until the Inspector arrives
- **Key evidence:** Birling sacking Eva for striking ("twelve percent to our labour costs"); Sheila's abuse of social power at Milwards ("I threatened them"); Mrs Birling's "girls of that class" (contempt as class reflex); Gerald's "keeping" of Daisy Renton; Eva's powerlessness at every stage
- **AO3 link:** Edwardian class rigidity; Priestley's socialism; the contrast with post-war welfare state aspirations

#### Route 3: How does Priestley present Mr Birling?
- **Thesis:** Priestley presents Birling as the embodiment of everything wrong with Edwardian capitalism — self-interest dressed as common sense, willful ignorance dressed as authority
- **Key evidence:** "The Titanic — unsinkable, absolutely unsinkable" (dramatic irony); "a man has to look after himself and his family" (social Darwinism); "community and all that nonsense" (contempt for collective responsibility); "I was Lord Mayor" (status obsession); "panic-stricken" at the end (fear of exposure, not guilt)
- **AO3 link:** Priestley's direct criticism of 1912 capitalist ideology; the dramatic irony is Priestley's weapon against an audience who knows what happened next

#### Route 4: How does Priestley present Inspector Goole?
- **Thesis:** Priestley presents the Inspector as a deliberately ambiguous figure — what matters is not who he is but what he does: he forces each character to face the human consequences of their choices
- **Key evidence:** "massiveness, solidity and purposefulness" (moral rather than physical authority); "burnt her inside out" (the language of consequence); "one person and one line of inquiry" (the method of cumulative responsibility); the final speech ("millions and millions of Eva Smiths"); his departure before the truth is confirmed; multiple possible identities (real detective, supernatural, conscience, Priestley's voice)
- **AO3 link:** The morality play tradition; Priestley's political voice; the Inspector as the socialist alternative to Birling's capitalism

#### Route 5: How does Priestley present Sheila as a character who changes?
- **Thesis:** Priestley presents Sheila as the play's most important agent of moral growth — she moves from complicit to conscience, becoming the audience's guide to what responsibility looks like
- **Key evidence:** Act 1 "pink and intimate" (surface happiness); Milwards abuse of power ("I'll never, never do it again" — genuine remorse); adopting the Inspector's role (asking questions the family avoids); "you're pretending everything's just as it was before" (the clearest statement of the play's moral argument); Sheila already knows she is guilty when the second phone call arrives — she does not need the confirmation
- **AO3 link:** Young women in 1912 vs 1945; the generational argument as a political argument about who will build the post-war world

#### Route 6: How does Priestley present Eva Smith as a victim of society?
- **Thesis:** Priestley presents Eva as the invisible casualty of a society that reduces people to their economic and social utility — she is destroyed not by one villain but by a system
- **Key evidence:** "these girls aren't cheap labour — they're people" (the Inspector's corrective); the chain of five actions (sacked, dismissed, dismissed, kept then abandoned, rejected); "Burnt her inside out"; "millions and millions of Eva Smiths and John Smiths" (she is universalised); Eva's moral superiority — she refuses Eric's stolen money; Eva's choices are never presented as the cause of her death
- **AO3 link:** Working-class women in Edwardian England; Priestley's socialism; Eva as the unnamed face of all those harmed by laissez-faire capitalism

#### Route 7: How does Priestley present younger and older generations?
- **Thesis:** Priestley presents the generational divide as the play's central political argument: the older generation will not change, so the responsibility for building a better society falls to the young
- **Key evidence:** Sheila and Eric accept responsibility; Birling and Mrs Birling celebrate the hoax; Gerald reverts ("everything's all right now, Sheila"); "you don't seem to have learnt anything" (Sheila's verdict on her parents); Sheila and Eric already know they are guilty when the second phone call arrives — the older generation is still learning nothing
- **AO3 link:** The 1945 election; the welfare state as the young generation's project; Priestley's deliberate structural choice to vindicate Sheila and Eric

#### Route 8: How does Priestley present gender inequality?
- **Thesis:** Priestley exposes gender inequality as a structural force that leaves women without protection and without voice — Eva is destroyed partly because she is a woman in a world run by men
- **Key evidence:** Gerald's affair presented in the language of rescue ("the wonderful Fairy Prince") — the irony that his "rescue" ends with her destruction; Mrs Birling's refusal to support Eva because she "used a different name" (the cruelest hypocrisy — a woman rejecting a woman); Sheila is the only character who consistently moralises correctly (her gender does not limit her moral clarity); Eva's powerlessness at every stage — she cannot keep her job, she cannot keep a relationship, she cannot access charity
- **AO3 link:** Women's rights in 1912 (pre-suffrage); the contrast with 1945 when women had voted and served in the war; Priestley's feminist strand

#### Route 9: How does Priestley use the ending?
- **Thesis:** Priestley uses the ending to ensure that the audience, not the characters, carries the moral question — the curtain falls before resolution, making the audience responsible for the next act
- **Key evidence:** The second phone call is identical to the first (cyclical structure); Birling is "panic-stricken" (fear, not guilt — he has not changed); Sheila and Eric are not surprised (they already know); "the curtain falls" before the new inspector arrives; multiple interpretations of the ending are all valid; the 1945 audience is the jury
- **AO3 link:** Morality play tradition (the ending poses a question, not a resolution); Priestley's time plays (J.W. Dunne); the 1945 moment — the audience can still choose a different outcome

#### Route 10: How does Priestley use stagecraft to create tension?
- **Thesis:** Priestley uses stagecraft throughout — lighting, stage directions, dialogue rhythm, and structural decisions — to make the audience feel the moral weight of the Inspector's investigation
- **Key evidence:** "The lighting should be pink and intimate" becoming "brighter and harder" (the transition from comfortable illusion to interrogation); the Inspector's entrance before his announcement (the audience knows before the family); "one person and one line of inquiry" (the method creates claustrophobic focus); the deliberate placing of the final phone call after the family's false relief (structural manipulation of the audience's hope); "panic-stricken" as a stage direction delivering character judgment; "the curtain falls" as Priestley's withheld resolution
- **AO3 link:** Priestley as an experienced theatre practitioner; the morality play form; the three-act structure collapsing into one rising moral pressure that the ending refuses to release

## 4. Content reference pack

### Dates & timeline
- All events from 1912 (the family's actions) to the play's closing phone call on the same evening
- 1945: the writing and performance date — Priestley's intended moment; the audience who would build the welfare state or not
- 1912–1945: the gap between setting and performance is part of the argument; the audience knows what happened in between

### Key people (essay-route roles)
- **Arthur Birling:** Route 3 (his own essay route); the capitalist foil to the Inspector; dramatic irony engine; does not change
- **Inspector Goole:** Route 4 (his own essay route); the moral voice; the structuring device; the ambiguous figure
- **Sheila Birling:** Route 5 (her own essay route); the audience's moral guide; the character whose change is most argued
- **Eva Smith:** Route 6 (her own essay route); the absent centre; universalised into "millions of Eva Smiths"; the human face of capitalist harm
- **Gerald Croft:** The reversion character; his return to the older generation's logic is Route 7's sharpest evidence
- **Eric Birling:** The young generation alongside Sheila; his confession (Route 8, Route 7) adds gender complexity
- **Mrs Birling:** The cruelest character; Route 8's most powerful example; "girls of that class"

### Key terms & definitions
- **Thesis:** A clear, argumentative claim that the essay defends throughout — not a topic sentence, but a whole-essay argument
- **AO1 (argument):** The coherent, reasoned critical response — the student's own interpretation of the text
- **AO2 (analysis):** Close language, form, and structure analysis; embedded within the argument rather than bolted on
- **AO3 (context):** The historical, social, and literary context used as evidence for interpretive claims
- **AO4/SPaG:** Spelling, punctuation, grammar — 4 marks; accurate vocabulary and sentence variety matter
- **Embedded quotation:** A quotation woven into the student's own sentence rather than introduced with a colon and a separate sentence
- **Whole-play essay:** No extract provided — the student must select their own evidence from across all three acts
- **Counter-argument:** Acknowledging complexity or an alternative interpretation strengthens the essay's critical voice
- **PEE / PEEL / PEEC:** Framework mnemonics — acceptable at GCSE but the best essays don't show the scaffold; the argument is seamless

### Causes & effects
- Essay without thesis → paragraphs feel like character notes → loses AO1 marks throughout
- AO2 as "the writer uses..." → AO2 not embedded → mark drops from Band 4 to Band 3
- AO3 as separate context paragraph → feels bolted on → marker cannot credit it as integrated analysis
- Whole-play evidence from only one act → essay reads as plot summary → loses AO1 reward for critical range
- Counter-argument included → essay sounds like a genuine literary argument → highest AO1 band

### Exam angles
**Common question types:**
- Character questions: "How does Priestley present [character]?"
- Theme questions: "How does Priestley present [theme]?"
- Craft questions: "How does Priestley use [technique] to present [effect]?"
- Effect questions: "How effective is [element] in An Inspector Calls?"
- All routes require the same essay structure — thesis → evidence → analysis → context → whole-play link → counter-argument

**Mark scheme keywords:** thesis / argument throughout; embedded AO2; AO3 as evidence not background; whole-play range; counter-argument / complexity; subject terminology accurate and purposeful; SPaG varied and accurate

**Key misconceptions for MisconceptionCheck:**
- "Longer essays score higher" — FALSE: a focused argument beats a sprawling summary
- "Context should go in its own paragraph" — FALSE: AO3 is woven into analysis
- "I should cover as many points as possible" — FALSE: depth with three strong points beats breadth with eight weak ones
- "Starting with 'In this essay I will...' is correct" — FALSE: start with the thesis
- "The Inspector is definitely supernatural — I should state this as fact" — FALSE: the exam rewards multiple interpretations argued with evidence, not assertions

**Techniques to name:**
- Dramatic irony (the Titanic; the audience knowing what the 1912 characters do not)
- Staging / stagecraft (lighting; entrances; curtain; stage directions as character judgment)
- Cyclical structure (ending mirrors beginning)
- Symbolism (Eva Smith / John Smith as all exploited people)
- Semantic field (economic language: "price", "cost", "paid", "cheap labour")
- Tripling (the Inspector's speech: "millions and millions and millions"; "lives, hopes and fears")
- Short declarative sentences (the Inspector's moral certainty: "We don't live alone. We are members of one body.")
- Naming (Goole / ghoul; Eva / Eve — the first woman?)
