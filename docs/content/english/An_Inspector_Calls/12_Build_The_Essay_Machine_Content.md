## 2. Storyline

### Core takeaway

This episode does not teach new plot. It teaches students how to build a strong AQA essay using what they already know. A good *An Inspector Calls* essay is not a character biography, a quote dump, or a tour of the plot. It is an argument.

The machine is simple:

1. **Thesis first:** answer the question with a clear argument.
2. **Evidence second:** choose moments from across the whole play.
3. **AO2 third:** analyse Priestley's language, form and structure.
4. **AO3 fourth:** weave context into the point, not in a separate history paragraph.
5. **Judgement throughout:** keep linking back to Priestley's purpose.

The final goal is that students can face any AQA-style *An Inspector Calls* question and quickly build a focused whole-play answer.

### Evidence for the takeaway

- AQA Paper 2 Section A asks for one whole-play modern text essay; there is no printed extract.
- The answer is marked for AO1, AO2, AO3 and AO4/SPaG.
- AO1 rewards a coherent argument and well-selected references.
- AO2 rewards analysis of language, form and structure.
- AO3 rewards relevant context linked to interpretation.
- The strongest answers do not bolt context on at the end; they use it to explain Priestley's choices and audience effect.
- The strongest answers use fewer quotes better, rather than listing lots of quotations without analysis.

### Series throughline

Episodes 1–11 built the knowledge. Episode 12 turns that knowledge into essays.

The whole series has trained three things:

- **Content:** what happens and who is responsible.
- **Meaning:** what Priestley is saying about society.
- **Method:** how Priestley uses stagecraft, structure and language to make the audience feel that message.

This chapter connects them into one exam system.

## 3. Specification requirements

### AQA Paper 2 Section A essentials

- **Format:** one essay on the modern prose/drama text studied.
- **For *An Inspector Calls*:** whole-play answer; no extract is provided.
- **Marks:** 30 marks plus 4 marks for SPaG.
- **AO1:** critical, exploratory response; coherent argument; textual references.
- **AO2:** language, form and structure analysis.
- **AO3:** relevant context connected to the text and question.
- **AO4/SPaG:** accurate spelling, punctuation, grammar and vocabulary.

### What Grade 7–9 writing does

- Begins with a thesis, not a vague introduction.
- Maintains one argument across the essay.
- Chooses evidence from different parts of the play.
- Analyses short quotations closely.
- Connects methods to Priestley's purpose.
- Weaves context into analysis.
- Handles complexity: characters can be guilty and remorseful; Gerald can help and exploit; the Inspector can be ambiguous.

### What Grade 4–6 writing often does

- Retells what happens.
- Describes characters as simply good or bad.
- Names techniques without explaining effects.
- Adds context in a separate paragraph.
- Uses long quotations without zooming in.
- Repeats the question rather than building an argument.

### Common misconceptions

- "A longer essay scores higher" — false. A focused, coherent answer beats a long plot summary.
- "I need to write about every character" — false. The question tells you the focus.
- "Context goes in its own paragraph" — false. It should support analysis.
- "PEE/PEEL guarantees high marks" — false. It is a scaffold, not a top-band style by itself.
- "I need loads of quotes" — false. A small bank of flexible, well-analysed quotes is better.

## 4. The ten essay routes

### Route 1: Responsibility

**Thesis:** Priestley presents responsibility as the moral test that divides the characters: those who accept it can change, while those who reject it are condemned by the structure of the play.

**Best evidence:**
- Birling: "a man has to make his own way"
- Inspector: "We are responsible for each other"
- Sheila: "you're pretending everything's just as it was before"
- Eric: "we did her in all right"
- Final phone call: responsibility returns when dodged

**AO3:** 1912 individualism versus Priestley's 1945/post-war support for collective responsibility.

---

### Route 2: Social class

**Thesis:** Priestley presents social class as a system that lets privileged people harm the vulnerable while protecting themselves from guilt.

**Best evidence:**
- Opening: "substantial and heavily comfortable but not cosy and homelike"
- Birling: "lower costs and higher prices"
- Sheila: "these girls aren't cheap labour — they're people"
- Mrs Birling: "girls of that class"
- Inspector: "millions and millions and millions of Eva Smiths and John Smiths"

**AO3:** Edwardian class hierarchy, workers' rights, Priestley's socialist viewpoint.

---

### Route 3: Mr Birling

**Thesis:** Priestley presents Birling as the embodiment of capitalist complacency: confident, status-obsessed and repeatedly wrong.

**Best evidence:**
- "a hard-headed practical man of business"
- "The Titanic — unsinkable, absolutely unsinkable"
- "community and all that nonsense"
- "it's my duty to keep labour costs down"
- "panic-stricken"

**AO3:** Dramatic irony works because a 1945/46 audience knows Birling's confident 1912 predictions were wrong.

---

### Route 4: Inspector Goole

**Thesis:** Priestley presents the Inspector as a deliberately ambiguous moral force whose function matters more than his literal identity.

**Best evidence:**
- "massiveness, solidity and purposefulness"
- "speaks carefully, weightily"
- "one person and one line of inquiry at a time"
- "We are members of one body"
- Final ambiguity: new inspector coming / Goole's identity unresolved

**AO3:** Morality play tradition; Priestley's socialist message; post-war audience judgement.

---

### Route 5: Sheila

**Thesis:** Priestley presents Sheila as flawed but teachable, using her change to show that responsibility means admitting harm and changing behaviour.

**Best evidence:**
- Opening: "very pleased with life and rather excited"
- "these girls aren't cheap labour — they're people"
- "I know I'm to blame — and I'm desperately sorry"
- "I'll never, never do it again to anybody"
- "you're pretending everything's just as it was before"

**AO3:** Young women in 1912 had limited public power, but Priestley gives Sheila moral clarity and change.

---

### Route 6: Eva Smith

**Thesis:** Priestley presents Eva as the absent centre of the play: an individual victim and a symbol of the millions harmed by class, gender and economic power.

**Best evidence:**
- "a good worker"
- "these girls aren't cheap labour — they're people"
- "Daisy Renton" as survival identity
- Eva refuses Eric's stolen money
- "millions and millions and millions of Eva Smiths and John Smiths"

**AO3:** Working-class women in Edwardian Britain had limited protection against employers, customers, wealthy men and charity gatekeepers.

---

### Route 7: Younger and older generations

**Thesis:** Priestley presents the generational divide as a political argument: the young are not innocent, but they are more capable of learning than the older generation.

**Best evidence:**
- Sheila's remorse and refusal to reset
- Eric: "the money's not the important thing"
- Eric: "we did her in all right"
- Birling and Sybil celebrating the possible hoax
- Gerald: "everything's all right now, Sheila"

**AO3:** Priestley writes in 1945/46, encouraging a post-war audience to choose social change over old complacency.

---

### Route 8: Gender inequality

**Thesis:** Priestley exposes gender inequality as a structure that makes Eva vulnerable to male power, sexual double standards and female class prejudice.

**Best evidence:**
- Gerald: "young and fresh and charming"
- Gerald: "She became my mistress"
- Sheila's Milwards complaint and later remorse
- Mrs Birling judging "a girl of that sort"
- Eric: "she didn't want me to go in" / "a chap easily turns nasty"

**AO3:** Edwardian sexual double standards; working-class women had fewer protections and higher reputational risks.

---

### Route 9: The ending

**Thesis:** Priestley uses the cyclical ending to refuse the Birlings' escape: the phone call repeats the beginning, but now the audience understands the family's guilt.

**Best evidence:**
- "the telephone rings sharply"
- repeated death announcement
- "a police inspector is on his way here"
- Birling: "panic-stricken"
- "The curtain falls"

**AO3:** Morality play and post-war warning; the audience is left to judge and carry the lesson out of the theatre.

---

### Route 10: Stagecraft / tension / structure

**Thesis:** Priestley uses stagecraft and structure to turn a comfortable family celebration into a public moral trial.

**Best evidence:**
- Lighting: "pink and intimate" to "brighter and harder"
- Inspector entrance after Birling's individualist speech
- Photograph shown privately
- "one person and one line of inquiry at a time"
- Final phone call and curtain fall

**AO3:** Priestley writes for performance; the play's single-room structure creates pressure and moral exposure.

## 5. The essay machine method

### Step 1: Build a thesis

Weak thesis: "Priestley presents responsibility in many ways."

Strong thesis: "Priestley presents responsibility as the moral test that divides the characters: those who accept it can change, while those who reject it are condemned by the structure of the play."

### Step 2: Choose three whole-play moments

Use this pattern:

- **Beginning:** set up the idea.
- **Middle:** complicate or develop it.
- **End:** show Priestley's final judgement.

Example for responsibility:

- Beginning: Birling's individualism.
- Middle: Inspector's chain of questioning.
- End: Sheila/Eric learn; older generation try to escape; phone rings again.

### Step 3: Embed short quotations

Weak: Priestley uses the quote "panic-stricken". This shows Birling is scared.

Stronger: By ending with Birling "panic-stricken", Priestley shows that Birling has not developed guilt, only fear of exposure.

### Step 4: Weave context into the analysis

Weak AO3: Priestley wrote the play in 1945 after two world wars.

Stronger AO3: For a 1945/46 audience who had lived through war, the Inspector's warning of "fire and blood and anguish" makes collective responsibility feel urgent rather than theoretical.

### Step 5: Keep returning to the question

Every paragraph should answer the question, not just explain a quote.

Use sentence stems like:

- "Priestley uses this moment to suggest..."
- "This matters because..."
- "For a 1945/46 audience..."
- "Structurally, this moment..."
- "This contrasts with..."

## 7. SPaG quick wins

AO4 is worth 4 marks. Quick fixes:

- Use character names consistently: **Sheila**, **Sybil**, **Gerald**, **Inspector Goole**.
- Write the play title correctly: *An Inspector Calls*.
- Avoid vague words like "stuff", "things", "bad", "nice".
- Use precise terms: **collective responsibility**, **dramatic irony**, **cyclical structure**, **class prejudice**, **capitalism**, **socialism**, **stage direction**.
- Keep sentences controlled. Long, panicky sentences lose clarity.
