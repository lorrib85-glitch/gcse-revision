# An Inspector Calls — Series Map

## Series identity

- **Qualification:** AQA GCSE English Literature (8702)
- **Text:** An Inspector Calls — J.B. Priestley
- **Assessment:** Paper 2 Section A (Modern Text)
- **Format:** One question from a choice of two; students write a whole-play essay (no printed extract)
- **Marks available:** 30 (including 4 SPaG marks)
- **Time allowed:** ~45–50 minutes
- **Series episode count:** 12 episodes
- **First written/performed:** Written 1945; first performed Moscow/Leningrad 1945; London 1946; Broadway 1947

---

## Series throughline

A wealthy Edwardian family celebrates itself as respectable and successful, but Inspector Goole exposes how each character has helped destroy Eva Smith. Priestley uses the play to attack selfish capitalism, class prejudice, gender inequality and moral irresponsibility, while urging a post-war 1945 audience to build a fairer, more socially responsible society.

**The question every episode must answer:** How does Priestley use language, form, structure and stagecraft to present [theme/character] to a 1945 audience, and what does this reveal about his message?

---

## AQA assessment objectives

| AO | Description | Weighting in AIC |
|----|-------------|-----------------|
| AO1 | Informed personal response; use of textual references | Core — must be present in every paragraph; 12 marks |
| AO2 | Language, form, structure and their effects | Core — must be present in every paragraph; 12 marks |
| AO3 | Contexts (Edwardian, 1945, Priestley's politics, reception) | Supporting — integrated not bolted on; 6 marks |
| SPaG | Spelling, punctuation, grammar | 4 of the 30 marks |

**AO balance note:** AO1 and AO2 carry equal weight (12 marks each). AO3 is worth 6 marks — contextual points must illuminate interpretation, not replace it.

**Mark scheme tiers (whole-play essay):**
- Level 1 (1–6): simple; some awareness of text; little AO2
- Level 2 (7–12): some understanding; some language comment; some context
- Level 3 (13–18): clear; explained; relevant AO2; some context integrated
- Level 4 (19–24): perceptive; analysed; AO2 purposefully selected; context embedded
- Level 5 (25–30): convincing; compelling; discerning AO2; context illuminates

---

## Key difference from extract-based papers

Unlike Macbeth (Paper 1 — extract plus whole play), AIC Paper 2 is **whole-play only**. There is no printed extract. Students must:
- Select their own evidence from across all three acts
- Build a sustained argument about a theme or character across the whole play
- Show how Priestley's methods (stagecraft, dramatic irony, structure) operate at play level, not just scene level
- Integrate AO3 context into their argument, not as a separate paragraph

This means every episode must teach **whole-play links and argument-building**, not just local scene analysis.

## Module architecture for English Literature (An Inspector Calls)

Every episode must build a 6-section module. The sections are locked in sequence.

### Section 1 — Hook & Prior Recall
**Purpose:** Surface existing knowledge, create narrative tension, prime retrieval. Warm the learner up to the act/scene territory before teaching begins.
- Typical components: `ChapterHookScreen`, `PriorKnowledgeRecall`, `MisconceptionCheck`

### Section 2 — Plot & Dramatic Action
**Purpose:** Teach what happens in the episode's scenes: events, character actions, dramatic turning points and stagecraft. The learner must be able to narrate the episode from memory.
- Typical components: `VisualLearning`, `VisualNarrativeScreen`, `TimelineChain`, `ConceptReveal`, `ExplainReveal`
- **AIC rule:** Every Section 2 must note the relevant **stagecraft device** Priestley uses (lighting, stage directions, entrances/exits, props, dialogue control).

### Section 3 — Theme & Context
**Purpose:** Connect the episode's events to the play's major themes and to AO3 Edwardian/1945 context. Every theme taught here must link to a quote in Section 4.
- Typical components: `TheoryCompareBlock`, `ColSortBlock`, `KeyFigureReveal`, `ExplainReveal`, `SwipeSort`
- **AIC rule:** Context must be integrated into thematic interpretation — not presented as a separate history block. Always frame as "this tells the audience in 1945 that..."

### Section 4 — Quote Analysis (AO2)
**Purpose:** Active analysis of the episode's key quotes — language choice, technique, effect, structure. Every quote taught must be retrievable in paragraph-ready form (Point → Quote → Analysis → Context).
- Typical components: `MatchingTask`, `InteractiveCollectionExplorer`, `FillInTheBlanksBlock`, `ColSortBlock`, `QuickRecallScreen`
- **Rule:** No quote-dump tables. Every quote must be taught through an active recall, sorting, matching, ranking or paragraph-building task.
- **AIC rule:** Quote analysis must always consider **Priestley's purpose** — what he wants the 1945 audience to think or feel.

### Section 5 — Exam Practice
**Purpose:** At least one AQA-style whole-play practice moment per episode. Must include the question type, a model approach, and a guided/independent writing activity. Feeds `GuidedAnswerCoach` and `FaceTheExaminer`.
- Typical components: `FaceTheExaminer`, `GuidedAnswerCoach`, `GuidedExamResponse`, `ExaminerExplainsScreen`
- **Exam question types for AIC:** "How does Priestley present [theme/character] in An Inspector Calls?"
- **AIC rule:** Every practice moment must include at least one whole-play link — evidence from a different act to the episode's primary content.

### Section 6 — Retrieval & Wider Play Links
**Purpose:** Interleave knowledge from earlier episodes. Require the learner to connect this episode's content to characters and scenes across the whole play. Build the whole-play argument the AQA mark scheme rewards.
- Typical components: `QuickRecallScreen`, `MatchingTask`, `RecoveryQuizPlayer`, `PriorKnowledgeRecall`
- **Rule:** Every episode's Section 6 must contain at least one explicit cross-reference to an earlier episode's content.

---

## 10-point English Literature Module Completion Test (An Inspector Calls)

Every episode, before marking it complete, must pass all 10 checks:

- [ ] Students can narrate the episode's plot events from memory (Section 2)
- [ ] Students can name and explain at least 2 themes from the episode (Section 3)
- [ ] Students can identify at least 1 key AO3 context point and link it to the text (Section 3)
- [ ] Students have actively retrieved at least 4 key quotes from the episode (Section 4)
- [ ] Students can analyse each key quote using Point → Quote → Analysis → Context (Section 4)
- [ ] Students can identify and name at least 2 language/structural techniques from the episode (Section 4)
- [ ] Students have practised at least one AQA-style whole-play written response (Section 5)
- [ ] Students have been shown or produced a mark-scheme-aware paragraph (Section 5)
- [ ] Students have retrieved content from at least one earlier episode (Section 6)
- [ ] The episode's core takeaway (how it contributes to the series throughline / Priestley's message) has been stated and practised (Section 3 or 5)

## Series-wide context reference

### J.B. Priestley biographical context (AO3)

- Born 1894, Bradford, Yorkshire; father a headmaster and proud Socialist
- Left school at 16; worked as junior clerk while writing at night; saw Bradford's industrial poverty
- Served in WWI 1914–19 as private, lance-corporal, then officer; front-line service; buried alive by shell; gas attack; many friends killed; emerged with "a big, heavy chip on my shoulder"
- After war: Cambridge (Modern History and Political Science); moved to London; became essayist, novelist, radio broadcaster
- WWII BBC broadcaster: 'Postscripts' after the news; called for a fairer post-war society
- 1942: co-founded the Common Wealth Party (Socialist); helped Labour win 1945 election
- Key themes in his work: responsibility; theories of time; effects of individual actions over time
- Wrote An Inspector Calls in a single week, 1945; first performed Moscow/Leningrad 1945 (no London theatre available); London 1946

### 1912 context (AO3)

- **Class system:** rigid, near-zero social mobility; upper/lower classes lived separate lives; 'old money' (Crofts) vs 'new money' (Birlings); factory owners controlled workers' wages and conditions
- **Workers' rights:** no minimum wage, no workplace safety laws, no enforceable employment rights; trade unions growing but suppressed; national coal strike 1912 (37 days, minimum wage demand)
- **Capitalism:** Liberal government under Asquith; laissez-faire economics; business owners had near-total freedom to exploit workers; workers' wages cited in the play: 22s 6d (rejected raise to 25s)
- **Women 1912:** no vote; under father's then husband's control; no right to own property (until 1882 Married Women's Property Act, but stigma remained); couldn't work in legal profession or civil service; no equal pay protection; rape within marriage not a crime; abortion illegal; 'fallen women' ostracised
- **Titanic:** sailed April 1912; sank April 1912, 1500+ dead; upper-class passengers given lifeboats first; Birling's praise of it as "absolutely unsinkable" — dramatic irony audible to any 1945 audience
- **Pre-WWI tensions:** German naval expansion; Balkan crises; Birling's dismissal of war as "fiddlesticks" — dramatic irony for any audience post-1914

### 1945 context (AO3)

- **Labour landslide:** Conservative Churchill defeated by Clement Attlee's Labour; 10.7% swing — largest in British history; country voted Socialist after six years of shared war experience
- **Beveridge Report (1942):** identified five 'giant evils' (want, disease, ignorance, squalor, idleness); recommended welfare state including NHS, National Insurance, housing policy
- **Welfare State 1945–46:** National Insurance Act; Industrial Injuries Act; NHS founded 1948; nationalisation of coal, gas, electricity, railways; Labour's manifesto: 'Let Us Face the Future'
- **Post-war mood:** desire for a fairer, more equal Britain; class distinctions reduced by shared wartime experience; rationing levelled material differences; community spirit from Blitz
- **Women 1945:** Representation of the People Act 1918 gave women over 30 the vote; 1928 extended to all women over 21; entered workforce in both wars but expected to leave again after; marital rape still legal; abortion still illegal; no equal pay; gender roles still constrained

### 'Drawing room play' and morality play form (AO3)

- **Drawing room plays:** Victorian/Edwardian genre; events confined to a single room; Priestley uses the form to present the Birlings as a microcosm of Edwardian society
- **Morality play:** Inspector functions as allegorical figure — conscience, social conscience, supernatural force, or Priestley's mouthpiece — not simply a realistic police detective
- **Unities:** play observes the classical unities of time (one evening), place (dining room), and action (single investigation) — creates claustrophobia and inevitability
- **Time plays:** Priestley was fascinated by theories of time (J.W. Dunne's 'An Experiment with Time'); the cyclical ending and the 1912/1945 split reflect his interest in time and consequences
- **Name 'Goole':** pronounced identically to 'ghoul' — supernatural/ghostly connotation; also a town in Yorkshire; suggests he is not a realistic character

### Capitalism vs Socialism framework (AO3)

- **Capitalism:** private ownership of production; competition drives down wages; workers are 'cheap labour'; Birling embodies this — profit before people; laissez-faire government in 1912
- **Socialism:** collective ownership; everyone contributes and receives; workers cannot be exploited; Inspector embodies this — "we are members of one body"
- **Priestley's argument:** capitalism destroys Eva Smith; socialism offers the alternative; the play is his political argument, dramatised
- **New money vs old money:** Birlings are nouveau riche (manufacturing); Crofts are nobility ('old money'); Gerald's family are social superiors — tension in the Birling/Croft engagement

### Key critical approaches (for AO1 sophistication)

- The Inspector is not a real police inspector — his name, his knowledge, his departure, the phone call, and the doubt in Act 3 all signal he is something else (conscience, prophet, Priestley's voice, supernatural figure, time-loop figure)
- Eva Smith is allegorical — she represents all exploited working-class women; she changes her name (Smith → Renton) to show she is not one person but a symbol
- Sheila and Eric represent Priestley's hope for the younger generation; Mr and Mrs Birling represent the older generation's refusal to change
- Gerald sits in between — more sympathetic than the Birlings but ultimately reverts to their logic by Act 3
- The play's form (drawing room, one evening, one location) traps the Birlings and forces a reckoning

## Design notes

- **Palette:** dark academic — forest green/charcoal (`#1A2E1A`), deep slate (`#1C2533`), brass/gold (`#C9A84C`), aged parchment (`#E8D5A3`), interrogation amber (`#D4A017`), near-black (`#0A0C0F`)
- **Tone:** cinematic tension — dining-room shadows, telephone, hard interrogation light, formal dining table, closed doors, inspection atmosphere
- **No:** detective-cartoon aesthetic, magnifying glass game styling, neon crime-board strings, childish 1920s murder mystery pastiche, Halloween/ghost styling for Goole
- **Typography:** Manrope for headings, Sora for body (per app type system)
- **Imagery direction:** Edwardian dining table with candlelight bleeding into harsh inspector's light; closed curtains; formal dress; a ringing telephone
