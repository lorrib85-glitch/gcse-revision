## 2. Storyline

### Core takeaway
Eric reveals the hidden corruption inside the respectable family and exposes the failure of parental authority. He forced himself on Eva, got her pregnant, and stole money from his father to help her. He is not innocent — but unlike his parents, he shows genuine remorse and anger at the family's failure to face the truth. Priestley handles Eric carefully: he is not a monster but a product of a family that never saw him clearly. "Not quite at ease" from his first appearance; drinking heavily for two years; unable to talk to his father. His breakdown is the family's breakdown.

### Evidence for the takeaway
- "not quite at ease, half shy, half assertive" — opening stage direction; his instability is there from the start
- "squiffy" — he was drunk when he forced himself on Eva; the colloquial word makes his admission simultaneously more and less shocking
- "you're not the kind of father a chap could go to when he's in trouble" — the most damning line in the play for Birling as a parent; Eric indicts the whole family system
- "I was in that state when a chap easily turns nasty — and I threatened to make a row" — the most explicit admission of the sexual exploitation; handled with care and specificity
- "stole some money" — from his father's office; the stolen money was to help Eva
- "my child" — Eric claims the baby; unlike Gerald he accepts the pregnancy's reality
- "you killed them both" — directed at Mrs Birling; she refused help to the woman carrying his child
- "the money's not the important thing" — he grasps what his father cannot: moral responsibility > financial reputation

### Series throughline
Eric is the younger generation's flawed representative: he has done serious harm, but he accepts it and tries to act. His confrontation with his parents in Act 3 is one of the play's most emotionally raw scenes. His genuine guilt — contrasted with his parents' refusal to accept any — reinforces the young vs old divide that is the play's central structural argument.

## 4. Content reference pack

### Dates & timeline
- **Late 1911 / early 1912:** Eric and Eva's relationship; Eva becomes pregnant
- **Early 1912:** Eva rejects Eric's stolen money; approaches Mrs Birling's charity; is refused; dies
- This timeline overlaps with Gerald's departure (autumn 1911) and Mrs Birling's refusal (two weeks before Eva's death)

### Key people
- **Eric Birling:** "not quite at ease, half shy, half assertive"; drinking; sexually exploited Eva; stole money; genuinely remorseful; unable to talk to his father; the family's secret shame
- **Eva Smith (at this stage):** she was "desperate" by the time she met Eric; she did not love him; she did "try to break off with him" after she found she was pregnant; she refused the stolen money on moral grounds

### Key terms & definitions
- **Sexual exploitation:** using someone's vulnerability for personal gratification; Eric exploits Eva's desperation
- **Edwardian masculinity:** a cultural code in which drinking, male entitlement and lack of sexual restraint were normalised for upper-class men; Eric is simultaneously a product of and a victim of this code
- **Guilt vs remorse:** guilt is feeling bad; remorse is genuinely wishing you hadn't done something; Eric shows remorse; his parents show neither guilt nor remorse

### Case studies / named examples
- **"I was in that state when a chap easily turns nasty":** the most careful admission of sexual exploitation in the text; Priestley doesn't use the word "rape" but the implication is clear — Eva didn't want him there; he threatened her; this is the play's darkest moment and must be handled with appropriate care in any screen
- **Eva's moral code:** she refused Eric's stolen money when she discovered where it came from; even at her most desperate she had a clearer moral code than the family that destroyed her; this detail is Priestley's most explicit tribute to Eva as a person
- **"You killed them both":** Eric's accusation of Mrs Birling; "them both" = Eva and the unborn child; it is the most direct statement of blame in the play; his mother refuses to hear it

### Causes & effects
- Eric's drinking and instability → he forces himself on Eva → Eva pregnant
- Eva discovers Eric stole money → refuses it on moral grounds → she cannot take money she believes is stolen
- Eva refuses Eric's money → approaches Mrs Birling's charity → is refused → leads to her death
- Eric's confession → Birling's first concern is money (the stolen fifty pounds) → Eric says "the money's not the important thing" → the generational divide confirmed
- Eric accuses Mrs Birling → she cannot accept it → older generation's moral failure is total
