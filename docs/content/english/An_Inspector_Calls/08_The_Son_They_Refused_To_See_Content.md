## 2. Storyline

### Core takeaway

Eric reveals the hidden corruption inside the respectable Birling family and exposes the failure of parental authority. He exploits Eva, gets her pregnant, and steals money from his father's office to help her. He is guilty of serious harm. However, unlike Arthur and Sybil, he shows genuine remorse, accepts that the money is not the central issue, and is horrified by the family's attempt to reduce the situation to scandal or finance.

Priestley handles Eric as a complex younger-generation character. Eric is not innocent, and his drinking does not excuse his behaviour. But he is also not presented as fixed in the way his parents are. His remorse matters because it shows he understands the moral horror of what happened.

Eric's confession also completes the chain. The family have refused to see both Eva and Eric properly. Eva was reduced to labour, shop assistant, mistress, charity case and scandal. Eric was reduced to awkward son, drunk son and family embarrassment. Priestley shows a family system that hides problems until they become catastrophic.

### Evidence for the takeaway

- "not quite at ease, half shy, half assertive" — opening stage direction; Eric's instability is signalled from Act 1
- Sheila says Eric has been "steadily drinking too much for the last two years" — the family knew something was wrong but did not face it
- "I was in that state when a chap easily turns nasty" — careful textual evidence for sexual exploitation; drunkenness is description, not excuse
- "she didn't want me to go in" — Eva's lack of consent is clear; handle maturely and briefly
- "you're not the kind of father a chap could go to when he's in trouble" — direct indictment of Birling as parent
- "I wasn't in love with her or anything" — emotional emptiness; contrast with the scale of consequence for Eva
- "my child" — Eric recognises the pregnancy and names his connection to it
- "I insisted on giving her enough money to keep her going" — attempt at responsibility, but built on stolen money
- "I got it — from the office" / "I intended to pay it back" — theft from his father; morally wrong but motivated by panic and partial responsibility
- Eva refuses the stolen money when she discovers the source — her integrity is stronger than the family's
- "you killed them both" — Eric's accusation of Mrs Birling; morally raw and direct
- "the money's not the important thing" — Eric understands moral consequence better than Arthur
- "we did her in all right" — Eric accepts shared responsibility and the chain of harm

### Series throughline

Eric completes the chain of responsibility. The family's respectable surface is now fully exposed: Arthur exploited Eva as a worker; Sheila used consumer power; Gerald used class/gender privilege; Eric exploited her sexually and then tried to help with stolen money; Sybil refused charity. No single person can carry all blame alone, but every link matters.

Eric also completes the young/old contrast. Sheila and Eric are both guilty, but they accept that guilt means something. Arthur and Sybil mainly worry about reputation, money and legal consequences. Priestley is not saying young people are automatically good; he is saying they are more capable of learning.

Eric's accusation of his parents makes the family failure explicit. The Birlings have not only failed Eva; they have failed their own son by creating a household where reputation matters more than honesty.

## 3. Specification requirements

- Eric's opening characterisation: "not quite at ease, half shy, half assertive" — instability seeded early
- His drinking: Sheila says he has been "steadily drinking too much for the last two years"
- The encounter with Eva must be handled maturely: Eric was drunk, Eva did not want him to enter, and Eric says he was in a state where a man can "turn nasty"
- Do not sensationalise the scene; name it as sexual exploitation/abuse of vulnerability and move to the moral argument
- Eva becomes pregnant and later does not tell Eric everything immediately
- Eric steals money from his father's office to support her
- Eva refuses Eric's stolen money when she discovers its source — key evidence of her moral integrity
- Eva approaches Mrs Birling's charity after rejecting Eric's money
- Eric's confrontation with Mrs Birling: "you killed them both — you killed her and the child"
- Eric's confrontation with Arthur: "you're not the kind of father a chap could go to" and "the money's not the important thing"
- Eric's shared-responsibility line: "we did her in all right"
- AO3: Edwardian masculinity, drinking, male entitlement, sexual double standards, family reputation, lack of emotional openness in patriarchal households

## 4. Content reference pack

### Dates & timeline

- **Act 1 opening:** Eric already appears uneasy and awkward
- **Previous two years:** Eric has been drinking heavily enough for Sheila to notice
- **Late 1911 / early 1912:** Eric meets Eva after Gerald has ended the relationship
- **After the encounter:** Eva becomes pregnant
- **After pregnancy:** Eric steals money to support her
- **After Eva discovers the money is stolen:** she refuses it and cuts off that support
- **Around two weeks before Eva's death:** Eva approaches Mrs Birling's charity and is refused
- **Act 3:** Eric returns; the chain is completed and the family collapse becomes explicit

### Key people

- **Eric Birling:** awkward, emotionally neglected, drinking heavily, guilty of serious exploitation, but remorseful and morally more awake than his parents by the end.
- **Eva Smith:** desperate and vulnerable when she meets Eric, but still morally principled. Her refusal of stolen money prevents students reducing her to passive victim only.
- **Arthur Birling:** exposed as emotionally unavailable father; cares first about money and public consequence.
- **Sybil Birling:** condemned the unknown father, then discovers he is Eric. Her certainty collapses but her responsibility does not fully awaken.
- **Sheila Birling:** sees the implications before the adults and aligns more closely with Eric's moral seriousness than with her parents' denial.

### Key terms & definitions

- **Sexual exploitation:** using another person's vulnerability, dependence or lack of power for sexual purposes. This term is accurate and mature without becoming graphic.
- **Consent:** agreement freely given. Eva's statement that she did not want Eric to enter is central to how the scene should be understood.
- **Remorse:** genuine sorrow and moral recognition after wrongdoing. Eric shows remorse; this does not erase guilt.
- **Guilt:** responsibility for wrongdoing. Eric is guilty and remorseful.
- **Family failure:** when a family system hides or ignores problems instead of supporting people honestly.
- **Edwardian masculinity:** social expectations that could normalise male drinking, entitlement and sexual double standards.
- **Generational divide:** the contrast between the younger characters' remorse and the older characters' self-protection.
- **Moral integrity:** acting according to moral principles even under pressure; Eva refusing stolen money is key evidence.

### Case studies / named examples

- **"Not quite at ease, half shy, half assertive":** Eric's opening stage direction plants instability before the audience understands why. Priestley does not make his confession come from nowhere.

- **"Steadily drinking too much for the last two years":** Sheila's line shows the family had warning signs. This makes the parents' ignorance less excusable.

- **"She didn't want me to go in":** This is the key line for mature discussion of consent. It should be handled briefly, clearly and without graphic expansion.

- **"I was in that state when a chap easily turns nasty":** Eric does not excuse himself; he describes the frightening male entitlement the play criticises. The colloquial "chap" generalises the behaviour into a wider masculine culture.

- **"You're not the kind of father a chap could go to":** This line shifts the focus from individual scandal to family failure. Birling has provided money and status, but not emotional safety.

- **"My child":** Eric claims connection to the unborn baby. He is guilty, but he does not erase the child's existence in the way his parents try to reduce everything to scandal.

- **The stolen money:** Eric's money is morally compromised. He tries to help, but his help is unstable and dishonest because he has never learned mature responsibility.

- **Eva refuses the money:** Priestley gives Eva a moral code. She is desperate but will not knowingly live on stolen money. This is crucial for Eva essays.

- **"You killed them both":** Eric's accusation is blunt and emotionally raw. It directly links Mrs Birling's charity refusal to Eva and the unborn child.

- **"The money's not the important thing":** Arthur focuses on the stolen money; Eric focuses on Eva's death. This contrast crystallises young vs old.

- **"We did her in all right":** Eric uses collective responsibility. The pronoun "we" shows he understands the shared chain more clearly than his parents.

### Causes & effects

- Eric's drinking and emotional isolation → exploitative encounter with Eva → pregnancy
- Pregnancy → Eric steals money to support Eva → Eva discovers source and refuses it
- Eva refuses stolen money → approaches charity → Mrs Birling refuses help → Eva's last support disappears
- Eric returns → family learn he is the father → Mrs Birling's dramatic irony lands
- Eric confronts parents → family failure becomes explicit → young/old divide hardens
- Birling focuses on stolen money → Eric says money is not the main issue → moral priorities exposed
- Eric accepts "we" responsibility → prepares for Inspector's final speech on collective responsibility

**Grade 9 essay move:**
Do not present Eric as either excused victim or simple villain. Priestley presents him as guilty but remorseful, using him to expose both male entitlement and the emotional failure of the Birling family.

**Useful paragraph stem:**
Priestley presents Eric as deeply guilty but morally capable of remorse: his confession exposes male entitlement, but his later anger at his parents reveals a clearer sense of responsibility than theirs.

**Key misconceptions for MisconceptionCheck:**
- "Eric's drinking excuses his behaviour" — false: it explains context but does not excuse exploitation
- "Eric is just like his father" — too simple: both do harm, but Eric shows remorse and accepts shared responsibility
- "Eva is only a helpless victim" — too simple: she is vulnerable, but her refusal of stolen money shows moral integrity
- "The stolen money is the main problem" — false: Arthur thinks this, but Eric and Priestley show moral harm matters more
- "Eric is redeemed completely because he feels guilty" — too simple: remorse matters, but it does not erase harm
