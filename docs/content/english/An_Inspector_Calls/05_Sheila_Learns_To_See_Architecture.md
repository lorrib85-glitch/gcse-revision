## 2. Architecture checklist (tailored)

### Section 1 — Hook & Prior Recall
- **Purpose:** Retrieve Eva's journey so far and Sheila's early moral challenge; prime the learner to see Sheila as flawed but teachable.
- **Proposed content:**
  - True/false: "Sheila has done nothing wrong at the start of the play" — FALSE: she had Eva dismissed from Milwards
  - True/false: "Sheila is completely innocent because she feels sorry" — FALSE: remorse matters, but real harm was done
  - True/false: "Sheila has no power because she is a woman in 1912" — TOO SIMPLE: she has less patriarchal power than men but class/customer power over Eva
  - Recall Episode 1: Sheila is "very pleased with life and rather excited"
  - Recall Episode 4: Eva leaves Birling's factory and finds work at Milwards; Sheila has already said "these girls aren't cheap labour — they're people"
  - Hook question: "Is saying sorry enough if your action changed someone else's life?"
- **Suggested component(s):**
  - `ChapterHookScreen` — true/false on Sheila's innocence, remorse and class power
  - `PriorKnowledgeRecall` — Eva's journey from factory to Milwards and Sheila's early moral challenge
  - `MisconceptionCheck` — remorse vs responsibility vs change

### Section 2 — Plot & Dramatic Action
- **Purpose:** Teach Sheila's interrogation as a character-change sequence: recognition, confession, Milwards, remorse, warning, pseudo-Inspector role.
- **Proposed content:**
  - Sheila recognises the photograph and realises her involvement
  - Milwards context: Eva works in a smart shop after leaving Birling's factory
  - Dress incident: Sheila tries on a dress that does not suit her; Eva smiles/laughs with another assistant
  - Sheila feels mocked and complains to the manager
  - Sheila admits she was "in a furious temper"
  - She accepts blame directly: "I know I'm to blame — and I'm desperately sorry"
  - She commits to changed behaviour: "I'll never, never do it again to anybody"
  - She understands the Inspector's method quickly: "you knew it was me all the time"
  - She begins warning others: "He's giving us the rope — so that we'll hang ourselves"
  - She refuses emotional/class distancing: "You mustn't try to build up a kind of wall"
- **Suggested component(s):**
  - `VisualNarrativeScreen` — sequence: opening Sheila → Eva at Milwards → dress incident → complaint → dismissal → confession → commitment → pseudo-Inspector warning
  - `ConceptReveal` — pseudo-Inspector role: Sheila starts to see what the Inspector sees
  - `TimelineChain` — Eva's chain: factory → Milwards → Gerald; Sheila's chain: privilege → harm → remorse → change

### Section 3 — Theme & Context
- **Purpose:** Connect Sheila to young vs old, responsibility, gender, class power and Priestley's hope.
- **Proposed content:**
  - Theme: responsibility — Sheila accepts guilt and changes behaviour
  - Theme: young vs old — Sheila is contrasted with Arthur/Sybil's refusal to learn
  - Theme: class — customer power lets Sheila damage Eva's employment
  - Theme: gender — Sheila is constrained as an Edwardian young woman but still has power over a working-class woman
  - Theme: appearance vs reality — initially childish/materialistic, later morally perceptive
  - Theme: change — Sheila's arc is the clearest proof that Priestley believes people can learn
  - AO3: Edwardian gender expectations — young women judged by marriageability, respectability and appearance
  - AO3: consumer culture/class hierarchy — wealthy customers could influence shop management
  - AO3: 1945 audience — younger generation as hope for a more socially responsible post-war Britain
  - AO3: Priestley's socialist purpose — guilt should create social change, not just private regret
- **Suggested component(s):**
  - `SwipeSort` — accepts responsibility / refuses responsibility / partly understands
  - `TheoryCompareBlock` — Sheila at opening vs Sheila after the Inspector
  - `ExplainReveal` — class power + gender nuance: Sheila has less power than men, but more power than Eva
  - `ColSortBlock` — sort Sheila quotes into remorse / responsibility / warning / change

### Section 4 — Quote Analysis (AO2)
- **Purpose:** Active retrieval and analysis of Sheila's key quotes across the play.
- **Proposed content:**
  - **"very pleased with life and rather excited"** — AO2: sheltered, naive opening; structural contrast with later seriousness
  - **"Oh — how horrible!"** — AO2: immediate emotional response; shows receptiveness before accusation
  - **"I was in a furious temper"** — AO2: adjective reveals pettiness and lack of justice behind her action
  - **"I know I'm to blame — and I'm desperately sorry"** — AO2: direct confession; dash gives emotional pause; no evasion
  - **"I'll never, never do it again to anybody"** — AO2: repetition shows commitment; "anybody" widens lesson beyond Eva
  - **"these girls aren't cheap labour — they're people"** — AO2: antithesis; restores human identity; early moral awakening
  - **"You mustn't try to build up a kind of wall"** — AO2: wall metaphor; rejects class/emotional separation
  - **"He's giving us the rope — so that we'll hang ourselves"** — AO2: metaphor; Sheila understands the Inspector's method
  - **"it frightens me the way you talk"** — AO2: reversal; younger generation judges older moral blindness
  - **"we all started like that — so confident, so pleased with ourselves"** — AO2: inclusive pronoun; she sees family complacency as a shared condition
  - PQAC: analyse "I'll never, never do it again to anybody" as evidence of responsibility and change
  - PQAC extension: compare "cheap labour" / "people" to show Sheila's moral contrast with Birling
- **Suggested component(s):**
  - `MatchingTask` — match Sheila quote to act/phase: opening / confession / pseudo-Inspector / Act 3 resistance
  - `FillInTheBlanksBlock` — complete paragraph analysis on repetition, metaphor and contrast
  - `InteractiveCollectionExplorer` — Sheila quote bank with technique, effect, context and whole-play use
  - `QuoteLadder` — simple meaning → method → character change → Priestley's purpose

## 4. Build recommendations

1. **Storyline integration:** Teach Sheila as flawed but teachable. Her hopefulness comes from change, not innocence.

2. **Section 2 — make Milwards concrete:** Students need the dress/smile/manager sequence because it shows how petty emotion becomes structural harm when backed by class power.

3. **Section 3 — handle gender carefully:** Do not imply Sheila is simply powerful or simply powerless. She has less patriarchal power than men but more class power than Eva.

4. **Section 4 — prioritise contrast:** Opening Sheila vs Act 3 Sheila; Birling's cheap labour vs Sheila's people; remorse vs actual change.

5. **Section 5 — avoid the weak paragraph:** "Sheila changes because she feels guilty" is not enough. Stronger: Priestley uses Sheila to show that responsibility means accepting harm and changing behaviour.

6. **Design:** Use mirror/dress-shop imagery sparingly — no fashion-app vibe. The image should feel like judgement in a fitting-room mirror, not a shopping game.
