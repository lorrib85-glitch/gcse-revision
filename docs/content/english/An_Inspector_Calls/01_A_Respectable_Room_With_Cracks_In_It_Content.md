## 2. Storyline

### Core takeaway

Priestley opens with comfort, wealth and celebration, but the stage directions already suggest the Birling family is not truly warm, secure or morally healthy. The dining room is a microcosm of Edwardian upper-middle-class society: on the surface, prosperous and respectable; underneath, hollow, complacent and vulnerable. Before Eva Smith is even named, Priestley has encoded his criticism of this class into the room itself.

This matters for AQA because *An Inspector Calls* is a whole-play essay text. There is no printed extract. Students need to be able to use the opening as flexible evidence for questions on social class, stagecraft, dramatic irony, responsibility, appearance vs reality, and Priestley's purpose.

### Evidence for the takeaway

- "a fairly large suburban house" — respectable, prosperous, not aristocratic; signals upper-middle-class aspiration
- "belonging to a prosperous manufacturer" — the family's comfort is rooted in industry and capitalism
- "good solid furniture" — material security and status; the room looks stable before the Inspector exposes moral instability
- "substantial and heavily comfortable but not cosy and homelike" — the room has weight, money and comfort but lacks genuine warmth; the phrase is the opening crack in the family facade
- "pink and intimate" → "brighter and harder" — the lighting change when the Inspector arrives encodes the moral shift from comfortable self-deception to harsh truth
- "pleased with themselves" — the characters begin in complacent self-satisfaction
- "celebrating a special occasion" — the engagement celebration is real but ironic because what it represents — marriage, family, status, business alliance — will be dismantled
- Edna clearing plates and serving — a working-class woman is present from the start but nearly invisible; this silently introduces the class hierarchy the play will expose
- Gerald's family, "Sir George and Lady Croft" — the engagement links the Birlings' new manufacturing wealth to old social status
- The single-room setting — Priestley traps the family in one space so their respectable facade can be stripped away in front of the audience

### Series throughline

This episode establishes the **appearance vs reality** axis that drives the entire play. The Inspector's work is to strip away the facade — and the facade is built, brick by brick, in the opening stage directions. The contrast between "pink and intimate" and "brighter and harder" is the play's central dramatic image: the shift from Edwardian self-satisfaction to moral accountability.

The opening also sets up the play's form. It looks like a conventional drawing-room drama about an engagement, manners and family status, but Priestley bends that form into a morality play. The comfortable room becomes a moral courtroom.

### Exam framing

This episode provides strong essay openings for:
- "How does Priestley present social class?" — the room's comfort is built on capitalist wealth and class hierarchy
- "How does Priestley use stagecraft?" — the lighting change is one of the most important stage directions in the play
- "How does Priestley present appearance and reality?" — the family looks secure but is immediately framed as emotionally hollow
- "How does Priestley present responsibility?" — the opening's self-satisfaction is the world the Inspector arrives to challenge
- "How effective is the opening of the play?" — Priestley has already built the critique before the Inspector speaks

**AQA format reminder:** *An Inspector Calls* appears in Paper 2 Section A. Students choose one of two whole-play questions and are expected to spend about 45 minutes. There is no printed extract, so students must select their own evidence from across the play.

## 3. Specification requirements

- AQA English Literature Paper 2 Section A: Modern text, whole-play essay, one question from a choice of two
- AO1: informed personal response supported by references and quotations
- AO2: analysis of language, form and structure — especially stage directions, lighting, props, entrances/exits and dramatic irony
- AO3: integrated context — 1912 Edwardian society, 1945 post-war audience, Priestley's socialism, capitalism, class, gender and responsibility
- AO4: accurate, controlled writing under timed conditions
- Setting of the play: spring evening 1912, Brumley, North Midlands, in the dining room of a prosperous manufacturer's house
- The Birling family: Arthur Birling, Sybil Birling, Sheila Birling, Eric Birling, Gerald Croft and Edna
- Edna: the maid — represents the servant class; seen but not centred; emblematic of class divisions
- Opening stage directions: "substantial and heavily comfortable but not cosy and homelike"; "pink and intimate" lighting initially
- The lighting change: "pink and intimate" → "brighter and harder" when the Inspector arrives — Priestley's moral instruction to the audience
- The engagement dinner: context for the family's self-satisfaction, class ambition and business alliance
- Mr Birling's toasting speeches: prosperity, business, self-interest — establishes the worldview that later episodes challenge
- Port, cigars, champagne glasses and the dining table: props that encode wealth, masculinity, ritual and class performance
- The genre: drawing-room play, well-made play, detective/mystery form and morality play
- Do not call the text a novel. It is a play written for performance.

## 4. Content reference pack

### Dates & timeline

- **1901–1910:** Edwardian era — the cultural world the Birlings represent
- **Spring 1912:** the play's setting; a moment of peak Edwardian confidence before the Titanic's sinking and the First World War
- **April 1912:** Titanic sinks shortly after the play's setting; Birling's later confidence becomes dramatic irony
- **1914–1918:** First World War; 1945/46 audiences know Birling's "no war" confidence is absurd
- **1939–1945:** Second World War; Priestley's first audiences had just lived through another national crisis
- **1945:** Labour landslide and demand for social reform; the play's socialist message lands in a post-war Britain thinking about responsibility and welfare

### Key people in this episode

- **Arthur Birling:** "a heavy-looking, rather portentous man"; self-made manufacturer; magistrate; seeking social status; confident, pompous and class-anxious
- **Sybil Birling:** Arthur's social superior; cold, formal and controlled; her status makes the family hierarchy visible before the Inspector arrives
- **Sheila Birling:** "a pretty girl in her early twenties, very pleased with life and rather excited"; initially naive and materialistic, but not fixed in that state
- **Eric Birling:** "not quite at ease, half shy, half assertive"; his unease and drinking are early warning signs
- **Gerald Croft:** "very much the well-bred young man-about-town"; son of Sir George and Lady Croft; old-money status gives Birling social opportunity
- **Edna:** the maid; her practical work enables the family's comfort but she is treated as background

### Key terms & definitions

- **Play:** a text written for performance on stage; students should discuss audience, staging, lighting, props and dialogue, not just plot
- **Stage directions:** Priestley's instructions for performance; in AIC they carry meaning, especially the setting and lighting
- **Dramatic irony:** when the audience knows more than the characters — central because a 1945/46 audience knows about WWI, WWII and the Titanic
- **Drawing-room play:** a theatrical genre set in a respectable middle/upper-class domestic room
- **Well-made play:** a structured nineteenth-century dramatic form built on secrets, withheld information, investigation and revelation; Priestley uses and subverts it
- **Morality play:** a dramatic form designed to teach a moral lesson; the Inspector functions like a conscience, judge or moral messenger
- **Microcosm:** a small-scale version of something larger — the Birling dining room represents Edwardian capitalist society
- **Facade:** an outward appearance that hides an uncomfortable truth
- **New money / old money:** Birlings = manufacturing wealth; Crofts = inherited social prestige
- **Stagecraft:** the playwright's practical dramatic choices: lighting, props, setting, entrances, exits, sound and positioning

### Case studies / named examples

- **The opening room description:** "The dining room is of a fairly large suburban house, belonging to a prosperous manufacturer." This immediately fixes class, place and wealth. The family is successful but not aristocratic. Priestley places the audience inside capitalist comfort before dismantling it.

- **"Substantial and heavily comfortable but not cosy and homelike":** This is the episode's signature opening quote. "Substantial" and "comfortable" suggest status and security, but "heavily" adds oppressiveness. "Not cosy and homelike" directly tells us that this house lacks emotional warmth. The home is respectable, not humane.

- **The lighting shift:** "The lighting should be pink and intimate until the Inspector arrives and then it should be brighter and harder." The soft lighting suits the family's self-image: safe, flattering, private. The harder light exposes truth. This is AO2 gold because it is stagecraft working as metaphor.

- **The dinner table and props:** Dessert plates, champagne glasses, port, cigars and evening dress create a ritual of wealth and respectability. But these props also stage exclusion: Edna serves; the family consumes.

- **Edna's presence:** She clears, serves and exits. She does not join the conversation. Priestley quietly shows a working-class woman supporting upper-middle-class comfort before Eva Smith's story even begins.

- **Gerald's class function:** The engagement is romantic on the surface, but socially and economically useful underneath. Birling later treats it as a business alliance. The Crofts' title and status expose Birling's class anxiety.

- **The opening as well-made play:** The family do not yet know the secret that will disturb the evening. Priestley uses the familiar structure of an investigation into hidden past events, but turns it into a moral and political lesson.

- **The opening as morality play:** The Inspector has not arrived yet, but the room is ready for judgement. The family are arranged like suspects before the judge enters.

### Causes & effects

- "Pink and intimate" atmosphere → characters feel safe, private and unchallenged
- Inspector's arrival → lighting becomes "brighter and harder" → comfort is exposed as false
- Room described as "not cosy and homelike" → prepares audience for the revelation that the family is not truly loving or united
- Engagement celebration → creates a class/business alliance between Birlings and Crofts → Priestley critiques marriage as social/economic transaction
- Edna's presence as silent maid → introduces class hierarchy → later echoed through Eva Smith as another working-class woman treated as disposable
- Single-room setting → traps characters and audience in the investigation → creates pressure and inevitability
