## 2. Storyline

### Core takeaway
Priestley opens with comfort, wealth and celebration, but the stage directions already suggest the Birling family is not truly warm, secure or morally healthy. The setting is a microcosm of Edwardian upper-middle-class society: on the surface, prosperous and respectable; underneath, hollow, complacent and vulnerable. Before a single line of dialogue is spoken about Eva Smith, Priestley has encoded his criticism of this class into the room itself.

### Evidence for the takeaway
- "substantial and heavily comfortable" — the room has weight and money but lacks warmth; the adverb "heavily" implies excess, even oppressiveness
- "not cosy and homelike" — Priestley directly tells us what this room is NOT; the family cannot provide genuine warmth
- "pink and intimate" → "brighter and harder" — lighting change when the Inspector arrives encodes the moral shift from comfortable self-deception to harsh truth
- "pleased with themselves" — the characters are complacent before the Inspector's arrival; self-satisfaction makes them blind
- "celebrating a special occasion" — the celebration is real but ironic; what they celebrate (the engagement, the family's success) will be dismantled
- Edna the maid — a servant present but invisible; a silent emblem of the class divisions the play will expose
- Gerald's family ("Sir George and Lady Croft") vs the Birlings' "new money" — the room contains the class tension of the whole play in miniature

### Series throughline
This episode establishes the **appearance vs reality** axis that drives the entire play. The Inspector's work is to strip away the facade — and the facade is built, brick by brick, in this opening scene. The contrast between "pink and intimate" and "brighter and harder" is the play's central dramatic image: the shift from Edwardian self-satisfaction to moral accountability.

### Exam framing
This episode provides strong essay openings for:
- "How does Priestley present social class?" — the room's comfort is built on the exploitation exposed later
- "How does Priestley use stagecraft to create tension?" — the lighting change is the key stagecraft moment
- "How effective is the opening of the play?" — use the stage directions as evidence Priestley has already built in his critique

## 3. Specification requirements

- Setting of the play: spring evening 1912, Brumley, North Midlands (fictional industrial city)
- The Birling family: Arthur Birling (wealthy businessman, possible knighthood), Sybil Birling (Arthur's social superior), Sheila Birling (daughter, engaged), Eric Birling (son), Gerald Croft (engaged to Sheila, upper-class 'old money')
- Edna: the maid — represents the servant class; seen but not heard; emblematic of class divisions
- The dining room as a drawing-room play setting: single location, one evening, classical unities
- Opening stage directions: "substantial and heavily comfortable but not a home"; "pink and intimate" lighting initially
- The engagement dinner: context for the family's self-satisfaction and celebration
- Mr Birling's toasting speeches: prosperity, business, self-interest — establishes his worldview before the Inspector arrives
- Port and cigars: props that encode wealth, masculinity, upper-class ritual
- The lighting change: "pink and intimate" → "brighter and harder" as Inspector arrives — Priestley's stage direction, not just atmosphere; it is a moral instruction to the audience
- The genre: drawing-room play; morality play elements present from the start

## 4. Content reference pack

### Dates & timeline
- **1901–1910:** Edwardian era (Edward VII's reign) — the cultural period the play is set within
- **Spring 1912:** the play's setting; a moment of peak Edwardian confidence before WWI, the Titanic's sinking
- **1945:** the play's writing and first performance — the gap between setting and writing is central to its dramatic irony
- **April 1912 (Titanic sinking):** referenced by Birling; the play is set contemporaneously

### Key people in this episode
- **Arthur Birling:** "heavy looking, rather portentous man in his middle fifties"; self-made businessman; magistrate; seeking a knighthood; new money; arrogant, pompous
- **Sybil Birling:** Arthur's "social superior" — she is from a higher-class background than him; cold, controlled, formal
- **Sheila Birling:** "a pretty girl in her early twenties, very pleased with life and rather excited" — naive, happy, materialistic at this stage
- **Eric Birling:** "in his early twenties, not quite at ease, half-shy, half-assertive" — already signals instability; he is drinking
- **Gerald Croft:** "an attractive chap about thirty, rather too manly to be a dandy but very much the easy well-bred young man-about-town" — upper-class, confident, charming; son of Sir George Croft
- **Edna:** the maid; no personality given in stage directions; emblematic of the invisible serving class

### Key terms & definitions
- **Dramatic irony:** when the audience knows something the characters do not — central to AIC because the 1945 audience knows about WWI, WWII, and the Titanic
- **Stage directions:** Priestley's instructions for how the play looks; in AIC they carry as much meaning as dialogue
- **Drawing-room play:** a theatrical genre set in an upper-class domestic space, popularised in the Victorian/Edwardian era
- **Morality play:** a theatrical form (medieval origin) in which allegorical characters represent moral forces; the Inspector functions as a morality-play figure
- **Microcosm:** a small-scale representation of something larger — the Birling household is a microcosm of Edwardian capitalist society
- **Facade:** outward appearance that masks an interior reality — the key tension of the opening
- **New money / old money:** the Birlings' wealth is from manufacturing (new money); the Crofts' is from hereditary nobility (old money); this causes social anxiety for the Birlings

### Case studies / named examples
- **The lighting stage direction:** "The lighting should be pink and intimate until the Inspector arrives, and then it should be brighter and harder" — one of the most exam-cited stage directions in GCSE English Literature; marks the moral shift of the whole play in a single sentence
- **The room description:** "The dining room of a fairly large suburban house, belonging to a prosperous manufacturer. It has good solid furniture of the period. The general effect is substantial and heavily comfortable but not a home." — every word is deliberate: 'substantial' and 'comfortable' but NOT a home
- **Gerald's family vs the Birlings:** Birling mentions "Sir George and Lady Croft" — he is impressed by Gerald's family connections; this signals the new money/old money divide and Birling's social anxiety

### Causes & effects
- "Pink and intimate" atmosphere → characters feel safe, self-congratulatory, unchallenged
- Inspector's arrival → lighting changes to "brighter and harder" → the comfort is exposed as false
- Room described as "not a home" → prepares audience for the revelation that the family is not truly united or loving
- Engagement celebration → surfaces the class alliance (Birlings + Crofts) that Priestley will critique
- Edna's presence as silent maid → foreshadows the theme of invisible, exploited working-class women

**Key misconceptions for MisconceptionCheck:**

- "The drawing-room setting is just realistic detail" — FALSE: Priestley chose this genre deliberately; the confined, comfortable room traps the family and makes their exposure inescapable
