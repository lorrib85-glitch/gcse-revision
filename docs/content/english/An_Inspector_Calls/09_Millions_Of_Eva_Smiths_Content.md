## 2. Storyline

### Core takeaway
The Inspector's final speech turns Eva's story into Priestley's warning about society. Eva Smith is not one woman: she is "millions and millions of Eva Smiths and John Smiths" — all the exploited, powerless people whose "lives, hopes and fears" are "intertwined" with those of the comfortable classes. The speech is Priestley's direct address to his 1945 audience: if you do not learn the lesson of social responsibility now, "they will be taught it in fire and blood and anguish." This is not a metaphor — it is a reference to WWI, WWII, and what follows from selfishness unchecked.

### Evidence for the takeaway
- "millions and millions and millions of Eva Smiths and John Smiths" — the tripling of "millions" for emphasis; Eva becomes collective; the generic surname confirms she is symbolic
- "their lives, their hopes and fears, their suffering, and chance of happiness, all intertwined with our lives" — the semantic field of connection; "intertwined" is the key metaphor
- "We don't live alone. We are members of one body." — the collective noun "body"; biological metaphor: if one part suffers, the whole suffers
- "We are responsible for each other." — the central moral proposition of the play, stated plainly
- "if men will not learn that lesson, then they will be taught it in fire and blood and anguish" — prophetic; "fire and blood and anguish" = war; the Inspector warns of what happens when his lesson is refused
- "She wanted twenty-five shillings a week instead of twenty-two and six. You made her pay a heavy price for that. And now she'll make you pay a heavier price still." — the motif of price; justice has a cost

### Series throughline
This speech is the play's moral climax. Everything in Episodes 1–8 has built to this moment. Eva's individual story has been assembled; now the Inspector universalises it. The speech is also the series' central lesson: we are responsible for each other. Every episode in this series has shown how refusing that responsibility destroys lives. This episode is where Priestley makes his argument explicitly.

## 4. Content reference pack

### Dates & timeline
- **1945 — Priestley's moment:** Labour won the 1945 election; the welfare state was created; "We are members of one body" is not just a metaphor but a political programme
- The Inspector's speech was written for this precise historical moment: a 1945 audience that had just survived "fire and blood and anguish" and needed to be persuaded to build a fairer society

### Key people
- **Inspector Goole:** delivers the speech and departs; his identity remains unresolved; the speech is more important than who delivers it
- **Eva Smith / John Smith:** universalised in the speech; "John Smith" = the male equivalent; the Inspector names the category not the individual

### Key terms & definitions
- **Collective responsibility:** the idea that all members of society share responsibility for each other's wellbeing; the Inspector's central proposition
- **"Intertwined":** the metaphor of threads woven together; implies that no life is truly separate from others; the opposite of Birling's "every man for himself"
- **"Members of one body":** biological metaphor; one body is the organic image of community; if one part is damaged the whole suffers
- **Prophetic language:** language that predicts future consequences; "fire and blood and anguish" is prophetic — to the 1945 audience it is a reference to what has already happened

### Case studies / named examples
- **The price motif:** "She wanted twenty-five shillings a week instead of twenty-two and six. You made her pay a heavy price for that. And now she'll make you pay a heavier price still." — the word "price" runs throughout the play (capitalism reduces everything to cost); here the Inspector inverts it: those who refused to pay a fair wage will pay a greater price in guilt and consequence
- **"Fire and blood and anguish":** in 1945, anyone hearing this speech had lived through this reality; it is not an abstract threat but a recalled experience; Priestley turns the memory of WWII into the argument for socialism

### Causes & effects
- Inspector delivers final speech → he departs → the family is left to respond
- Eva's individual story → universalised as "millions of Eva Smiths" → Priestley's social argument crystallised
- "if men will not learn that lesson" → WWI, WWII happened → Priestley's lesson proved correct for the 1945 audience
- The speech leaves the Birlings' responses open → this is the moment that divides young from old in Episode 10
