## 2. Storyline

### Core takeaway

The Inspector's final speech turns Eva's individual story into Priestley's warning about society. Eva is no longer only one dead woman; she becomes a symbol of "millions and millions and millions of Eva Smiths and John Smiths" — ordinary people harmed by selfishness, class power and refusal of responsibility.

This is the moral climax of the play. Episodes 4–8 build the chain of harm. Episode 9 explains what that chain means: society is connected, actions have consequences, and privileged people cannot safely pretend other people's suffering has nothing to do with them.

For a 1945/46 audience, "fire and blood and anguish" would carry the memory of two world wars. Priestley is not just preaching kindness; he is arguing for the kind of collective responsibility associated with post-war social reform and the 1945 Labour landslide.

### Evidence for the takeaway

- "millions and millions and millions of Eva Smiths and John Smiths" — tripling creates scale; Eva becomes symbolic; "John Smith" widens the message beyond one woman
- "with their lives, their hopes and fears, their suffering, and chance of happiness" — listing gives the poor full inner lives, not just economic need
- "all intertwined with our lives, with what we think and say and do" — metaphor of connection; no action is isolated
- "We don't live alone" — short declarative sentence; directly rejects Birling's individualism
- "We are members of one body" — organic metaphor; society is one living system
- "We are responsible for each other" — the play's central moral thesis
- "if men will not learn that lesson" — conditional warning; change is possible, but refusal has consequences
- "fire and blood and anguish" — prophetic triad; for the first audience it evokes war and social collapse
- "She wanted twenty-five shillings a week instead of twenty-two and six" — returns to the small wage demand that began the chain
- "You made her pay a heavy price for that. And now she'll make you pay a heavier price still" — price motif; capitalist language becomes moral consequence

### Series throughline

This speech locks the series together. The chain is now complete: factory, Milwards, Gerald, Eric, charity. The Inspector universalises that chain and turns it into Priestley's social argument.

The clearest whole-play contrast is:
- Birling: "a man has to make his own way" and "community and all that nonsense".
- Inspector: "We are members of one body. We are responsible for each other."

This contrast can power essays on responsibility, capitalism, socialism, social class, Eva, the Inspector, young vs old and Priestley's purpose.

### Exam framing

This episode is essential for almost every AQA *An Inspector Calls* essay. The final speech is not just a quote bank; it is the play's thesis statement.

Essential for essays on:
- "How does Priestley present responsibility?"
- "How does Priestley present Inspector Goole?"
- "How does Priestley present Eva Smith?"
- "How does Priestley criticise capitalism?"
- "How does Priestley present social class?"
- "How does Priestley use structure?"

**AQA format reminder:** *An Inspector Calls* is a whole-play essay text. There is no printed extract. Students need to know the final speech accurately and link it backwards to the chain and forwards to how characters respond after the Inspector leaves.

## 4. Content reference pack

### Key terms

- **Collective responsibility:** society shares responsibility for the welfare of others.
- **Symbolism:** Eva Smith becomes more than one character; she represents many exploited people.
- **Intertwined:** a metaphor suggesting lives are woven together.
- **Members of one body:** an organic metaphor for society as one connected organism.
- **Prophetic language:** warning language that sounds like a prediction or moral judgement.
- **Price motif:** economic language from capitalism turned into moral cost.

### Case studies / named examples

- **The chain universalised:** Eva's story begins as one case but becomes a social pattern. The Inspector wants the family and audience to see that there are millions of people like her.

- **"Members of one body":** This directly attacks Birling's individualism. If society is one body, then harming one part harms the whole.

- **"Fire and blood and anguish":** In 1912 this sounds like a warning. For a 1945/46 audience it sounds like a memory of two world wars. Priestley uses history to strengthen his moral argument.

- **The price motif:** Eva wanted a small wage rise. The Birlings made her pay a "heavy price". The Inspector turns capitalist language against the capitalist characters.

### Causes & effects

- The complete chain is revealed → Inspector universalises Eva's story → social responsibility becomes explicit
- Birling's individualism is exposed → Inspector gives opposite moral thesis → audience is asked to choose
- Inspector leaves → characters must respond → Episodes 10–11 test whether they have learned
- Warning about war/social collapse → 1945 audience recognises consequences of refusing responsibility
