## 2. Storyline

### Core takeaway

The Inspector's final speech turns Eva's individual story into Priestley's warning about society. Eva Smith is not only one woman; she becomes a symbol of "millions and millions and millions of Eva Smiths and John Smiths" — ordinary people whose lives are damaged when the powerful act selfishly and refuse responsibility.

This speech is the moral climax of the play. The Inspector takes the completed chain from Episodes 4–8 and universalises it. The lesson is not simply "be kind to Eva". It is: society is connected, actions have consequences, and the privileged cannot live safely while ignoring the suffering of others.

For a 1945/46 audience, the warning about "fire and blood and anguish" would carry enormous force. Priestley wrote after two world wars, when Britain was debating how to build a fairer post-war society. The speech supports the kind of collective responsibility associated with post-war social reform and the Labour landslide of 1945.

### Evidence for the takeaway

- "millions and millions and millions of Eva Smiths and John Smiths" — tripling creates scale; Eva becomes collective and symbolic; "John Smith" widens the point beyond one woman
- "with their lives, their hopes and fears, their suffering, and chance of happiness" — listing humanises the poor through inner life, not just economic need
- "all intertwined with our lives, with what we think and say and do" — metaphor of connection; no action is isolated
- "We don't live alone" — short declarative sentence; directly rejects Birling's individualism
- "We are members of one body" — organic metaphor; society is one living system, so one person's suffering harms the whole
- "We are responsible for each other" — plain moral thesis of the play
- "if men will not learn that lesson" — conditional warning; learning is possible, but refusal has consequences
- "fire and blood and anguish" — prophetic triad; for a 1945/46 audience, it evokes the destruction of war and the cost of ignoring social responsibility
- "She wanted twenty-five shillings a week instead of twenty-two and six" — returns to the small wage demand that began the chain
- "You made her pay a heavy price for that. And now she'll make you pay a heavier price still" — price motif; capitalist language is turned into moral consequence

### Series throughline

This speech is where the entire series locks together. Episodes 1–8 build the chain: respectable room, capitalist speech, Inspector entrance, factory sacking, Milwards dismissal, Gerald's temporary rescue, charity refusal, Eric's confession. Episode 9 explains what the chain means.

The speech also creates the central whole-play opposition:
- Birling says: look after yourself; community is "nonsense".
- The Inspector says: "We are members of one body. We are responsible for each other."

Students should learn this contrast as an essay engine. It can power answers on responsibility, capitalism, socialism, Inspector Goole, Eva Smith, social class, young vs old and Priestley's purpose.

### Exam framing

This episode is essential for almost every AQA *An Inspector Calls* essay. The final speech is not just a quote bank; it is the play's thesis statement.

Essential for essays on:
- "How does Priestley present responsibility?" — the speech states the play's moral argument
- "How does Priestley present Inspector Goole?" — the Inspector becomes Priestley's moral/political mouthpiece
- "How does Priestley present Eva Smith?" — Eva becomes symbolic of millions
- "How does Priestley criticise capitalism?" — the price motif and Birling contrast are central
- "How does Priestley present social class?" — the poor are humanised and connected to the comfortable classes
- "How does Priestley use structure?" — the completed chain is universalised before the family responds

**AQA format reminder:** *An Inspector Calls* is a whole-play essay text. There is no printed extract. Students need to know the final speech accurately and link it backwards to the chain and forwards to how characters respond after the Inspector leaves.
