## 2. Storyline

### Core takeaway
Eva Smith's first loss shows how capitalism treats workers as disposable. Birling sacked her not because she was bad at her job ("good worker") but because she organised a strike for a modest wage increase — one that would have cost him 12% more in labour. Eva is not just one person: she is "cheap labour" in Birling's worldview but a "person" in the Inspector's. This episode teaches class, labour rights, and Priestley's most direct critique of employers who protect profit before people.

### Evidence for the takeaway
- "good worker" — even Birling admits she was capable; her sacking was purely economic/political
- "asked for more money" / "ringleader" — Eva exercised collective rights and was punished for it
- "refused, of course" — the "of course" is chilling; to Birling it is self-evident that workers cannot demand higher wages
- "twelve percent to our labour costs" — workers reduced to statistics; Eva's life vs a profit margin
- "cheap labour" — Birling's term; reveals how he categorises people
- "these girls aren't cheap labour — they're people" — Sheila's line; the moral correction is given to Sheila, not the Inspector, suggesting even the Birlings' own children can see the truth Birling refuses
- "a chain of events" — the Inspector's phrase; the first link in Eva's destruction

### Series throughline
Episode 4 establishes the causal chain that the Inspector tracks. It also establishes Eva as a symbol, not just a character. Her sacking from the factory is the first of five episodes in which a Birling/Croft destroys part of her life. This episode's lesson — that workers are people, not cheap labour — is the economic dimension of the Inspector's final speech in Episode 9.

## 4. Content reference pack

### Dates & timeline
- **1910 (approximately):** Eva sacked from Birling's factory — approximately two years before the play's 1912 setting
- **1911:** Liverpool general transport strike — context for labour unrest
- **1912:** national coal strike, 37 days, minimum wage demand — exact year of the play's setting
- **Pre-WWI:** trade unions growing; employers resisting; Birling's attitude represents the dominant employer class

### Key people
- **Eva Smith:** "a good worker too"; organised a strike; was a "ringleader"; sacked; her response is dignified — she moved on, she didn't become bitter in the way that would justify Birling's view of her
- **Arthur Birling:** his interrogation in this scene reveals him most clearly as a capitalist; "refused, of course"; "twelve percent"
- **Sheila Birling:** present but not interrogated yet; her line "these girls aren't cheap labour — they're people" is the moral touchstone of this episode

### Key terms & definitions
- **Strike:** workers collectively stop working to demand better wages or conditions; Eva's strike in 1910 was for a wage of 25s/week
- **Trade union:** workers' collective organisation; in 1912 largely suppressed; the Liverpool transport strike (1911) was an early successful union action
- **Labour costs:** what an employer pays in wages; Birling reduces Eva's life to "twelve percent of our labour costs"
- **Ringleader:** Birling's word for Eva; implies criminality; dehumanises a worker exercising collective rights
- **Chain of events:** the Inspector's phrase; explicitly describes how one act of selfishness causes a cascade of harm

### Case studies / named examples
- **Eva's strike:** workers at Birling's factory averaged 22s 6d per week; they wanted 25s; "That's not a lot," the Inspector says — Birling's refusal was to protect a small competitive advantage, not out of genuine financial necessity
- **Birling's "twelve percent" calculation:** he reduces the human cost to a percentage; Priestley shows how capitalism trains people to think about workers as cost items, not as people with lives

### Causes & effects
- Eva organises a strike → Birling sacks her as "ringleader" → Eva loses her factory job
- Eva sacked → she must find work elsewhere → she ends up at Milwards (Episode 5)
- Birling refuses to take responsibility → "I can't accept any responsibility" → establishes the older generation's failure
- Eva is "good worker" but sacked for political reasons → Priestley exposes capitalism: merit doesn't protect you, only profit matters
- Sheila says "these girls aren't cheap labour — they're people" → seeds Sheila's moral awakening (Episode 5) → marks the young vs old divide
