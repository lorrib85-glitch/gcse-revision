## 2. Storyline

### Core takeaway

Eva Smith's first loss shows how capitalism treats workers as disposable. Birling sacks her not because she is bad at her job — he admits she was a "good worker" — but because she helps organise a strike for a small wage increase. Priestley makes this the first link in Eva's destruction because he wants the audience to see that social harm does not begin with one dramatic act of cruelty. It begins with ordinary, respectable business decisions.

This episode is where the play's ideological battle becomes concrete. In Episode 2, Birling talked about profit, capital and looking after yourself. In Episode 4, we see what that worldview does to a real working-class woman. Eva is "cheap labour" in Birling's language, but "people" in Sheila's. That contrast is the moral centre of the episode.

### Evidence for the takeaway

- "a good worker" — even Birling admits Eva was capable; she is not dismissed for poor performance
- "they'd suddenly decided to ask for more money" — Birling presents a wage demand as unreasonable disruption
- "twenty-two and six" → "twenty-five shillings a week" — the workers ask for a modest rise, not luxury
- "I refused, of course" — the phrase makes refusal sound automatic; to Birling, profit protection is common sense
- "it's my duty to keep labour costs down" — Birling presents exploitation as duty, not selfishness
- "she'd had a lot to say — far too much" — Eva is punished for having a voice
- "she had to go" — blunt dismissal; Birling treats sacking as unavoidable
- "ringleaders" — Birling criminalises worker organisation
- "if you don't come down sharply on some of these people, they'd soon be asking for the earth" — his fear is not this one raise, but workers gaining confidence
- "Why shouldn't they try for higher wages?" — Sheila challenges the logic of employer power
- "we try for the highest possible prices" — Sheila exposes Birling's hypocrisy: employers maximise profit but deny workers the same self-interest
- "these girls aren't cheap labour — they're people" — Sheila's moral correction; workers become human again in language
- "what happened to her then may have determined what happened to her afterwards" — Inspector introduces the chain of consequences
- "It's better to ask for the earth than to take it" — Inspector's counter-argument: demanding fairness is less immoral than exploiting others

### Series throughline

Episode 4 establishes the causal chain that the Inspector tracks. Eva's sacking from the factory is the first of several acts in which a Birling or Croft damages her life, then tries to minimise responsibility. This episode's lesson — that workers are people, not cheap labour — is the economic dimension of the Inspector's final speech.

It also plants Sheila's awakening before her own interrogation in Episode 5. Her line about "cheap labour" shows she has begun to see through her father's capitalist language. She has not yet faced her own guilt, but she has already understood something Birling refuses to understand: class does not cancel personhood.

### Exam framing

This episode is essential for essays on:
- "How does Priestley present social class?" — workers are treated as costs, not people
- "How does Priestley present capitalism?" — Birling's profit-first logic directly harms Eva
- "How does Priestley present Eva Smith?" — she is principled, capable, working-class and vulnerable to employer power
- "How does Priestley present responsibility?" — Birling refuses responsibility even when his action begins the chain
- "How does Priestley present Sheila?" — her moral awakening begins before she is personally exposed
- "How does Priestley present the older and younger generations?" — Sheila challenges Birling's dehumanising language

## 4. Content reference pack

### Dates & timeline

- **1910:** Eva is sacked from Birling's factory after the wage dispute
- **1911:** major transport/labour unrest in Britain; context for fears about organised workers
- **1912:** national coal strike and wider anxiety about labour disputes; the play is set in a period of real industrial conflict
- **1912:** no modern minimum wage or employment protection as GCSE students would understand it; employers had far more power to dismiss workers
- **1945/46:** post-war audience more receptive to welfare, collective responsibility and workers' rights after shared national sacrifice

### Key people

- **Eva Smith:** capable, principled and collective-minded. She is not presented as lazy or incompetent. Her vulnerability comes from class and gender, not from personal failure.
- **Arthur Birling:** revealed as capitalist employer in action, not just in speech. He treats wages as costs and worker organisation as a threat.
- **Sheila Birling:** her moral intelligence begins here. She recognises that workers are people and challenges her father's hypocrisy before being interrogated herself.
- **Inspector Goole:** frames the event as part of a chain. He refuses to let Birling treat the sacking as an isolated business decision.

### Key terms & definitions

- **Strike:** workers collectively stop working to demand better wages or conditions.
- **Trade union:** a workers' organisation that protects rights, wages and conditions.
- **Labour costs:** the money a business spends on workers' wages; Birling uses the term to reduce people to expense.
- **Ringleader:** Birling's word for Eva; it implies criminality or troublemaking rather than leadership.
- **Exploitation:** taking advantage of someone because they have less power.
- **Collective action:** people acting together to improve their situation; Eva's strike activity is a form of collective action.
- **Chain of events:** the Inspector's concept that one action leads to another; responsibility spreads through consequences.
- **Dehumanisation:** language that makes people seem less human; "cheap labour" does this.
- **Socialist reading:** the episode shows why Priestley believes society needs collective responsibility and workers' protection.

### Case studies / named examples

- **Eva as "good worker":** Birling admits she was capable. This matters because Priestley removes the excuse that she deserved dismissal for incompetence. She is punished because she challenges power.

- **The wage demand:** The workers move from "twenty-two and six" to asking for "twenty-five shillings a week". Priestley makes the demand sound modest so the audience sees Birling's refusal as mean-spirited and ideological, not financially unavoidable.

- **"I refused, of course":** The adverbial phrase makes Birling's decision sound automatic. For him, the natural response to workers asking for more is refusal. Priestley uses that certainty to expose how capitalism normalises exploitation.

- **"It's my duty to keep labour costs down":** Birling disguises self-interest as duty. The phrase is useful for essays because it shows how moral language can be twisted to defend profit.

- **"She'd had a lot to say — far too much":** Eva is punished for speaking. The dash sharpens Birling's irritation: he sees a working-class woman's voice as excess.

- **"Ringleaders":** This is dysphemistic language. It gives collective worker organisation a criminal tone. Priestley wants us to notice how employers use language to make justice look like troublemaking.

- **"Asking for the earth":** Birling exaggerates the workers' demand. Priestley contrasts the small wage rise with Birling's fear of working-class power.

- **"It's better to ask for the earth than to take it":** The Inspector turns Birling's phrase back against him. The workers asked; employers take. This is one of the play's sharpest anti-capitalist reversals.

- **"These girls aren't cheap labour — they're people":** Sheila rejects her father's dehumanising vocabulary. The plural "girls" matters: she is not only thinking of Eva, but of a wider class of exploited young women.

- **"Chain of events":** The Inspector's metaphor shows causality. Birling wants to isolate the sacking as a business choice; the Inspector makes it the first link in Eva's destruction.

### Causes & effects

- Eva becomes a leading voice in the strike → Birling labels her a ringleader → she is dismissed
- Birling refuses the wage rise → workers lose the dispute → Eva is punished as an example to others
- Eva loses factory work → she later finds work at Milwards → Sheila's misuse of power becomes the next link
- Birling treats workers as labour costs → Sheila challenges him → generational and moral divide begins
- Inspector frames the sacking as part of a chain → Birling cannot fully isolate his action → audience sees responsibility as cumulative
- Eva is dehumanised as "cheap labour" → Sheila restores personhood through language → Priestley's moral argument becomes explicit

**Grade 9 essay move:**
Do not write that Birling simply "fires Eva because she wanted more money". A stronger answer argues that Priestley presents Eva's dismissal as the first visible consequence of capitalist ideology: the employer's language turns people into costs, while Sheila's language restores their humanity.

**Useful paragraph stem:**
Priestley uses Eva's dismissal to expose the human cost of capitalism: Birling sees a percentage on his labour costs, but Sheila and the Inspector force the audience to see a person.

**Key misconceptions for MisconceptionCheck:**
- "Eva was sacked because she was a bad worker" — false: Birling admits she was a good worker
- "Birling had no choice" — false in moral terms: he chose profit and employer control over fairness
- "The strike was greedy" — too simple: the requested rise was modest, and Birling himself seeks higher prices
- "Sheila's important change starts only when she confesses" — false: she begins seeing morally in this episode
- "This episode is only about Eva" — too narrow: it is about capitalism, class, workers' rights and the whole chain of responsibility

- Dysphemism: "ringleader"
- Hyperbole: "asking for the earth"
- Structural metaphor: "chain of events"
- Dramatic contrast: Birling's logic vs Sheila/Inspector's moral language
