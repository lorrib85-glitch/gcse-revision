## 2. Architecture checklist (tailored)

### Section 1 — Hook & Prior Recall
- **Purpose:** Surface existing knowledge about the play's opening; prime retrieval of character names and first impressions; create curiosity about what the celebration is hiding.
- **Proposed content for this episode:**
  - True/false hook: "The Birlings are a genuinely warm, loving family" (FALSE — stage directions say "not cosy and homelike")
  - Prior knowledge retrieval: who is at the engagement dinner? (Arthur, Sybil, Sheila, Eric, Gerald, Edna)
  - Prior knowledge retrieval: what is the play about, in one sentence?
  - Misconception trap: is this a happy scene? (surface yes, beneath no)
- **Suggested component(s):**
  - `ChapterHookScreen` — true/false warm-up using the appearance vs reality misconception
  - `PriorKnowledgeRecall` — free-text recall of who the Birlings are and what they're celebrating

### Section 2 — Plot & Dramatic Action
- **Purpose:** Teach the opening scene's events: the engagement dinner, Mr Birling's speeches, the mood, the characters' first impressions, the staging, and the lighting change when the Inspector arrives.
- **Proposed content for this episode:**
  - The setting: Brumley dining room, spring 1912, engagement of Sheila and Gerald
  - Each character's entrance impression (use stage directions verbatim)
  - Mr Birling's toasting speeches: prosperity, self-interest, the Titanic, no war
  - Edna's presence as silent servant
  - The doorbell: a sharp ring that changes everything
  - The lighting: "pink and intimate" → "brighter and harder"
  - The Inspector's entrance and first impression of "massiveness, solidity and purposefulness"
- **Suggested component(s):**
  - `VisualNarrativeScreen` — beat-based sequence: setting → characters → Birling's speeches → doorbell → lighting change → Inspector enters
  - `ConceptReveal` — the lighting change as the moral pivot of the play

### Section 3 — Theme & Context
- **Purpose:** Connect the opening to the play's major themes (appearance vs reality, class, Priestley's purpose) and to AO3 context (Edwardian society, drawing-room form, 1912 vs 1945).
- **Proposed content for this episode:**
  - Theme: appearance vs reality — the room looks comfortable but is "not a home"
  - Theme: class — new money (Birlings) vs old money (Crofts); Edna's silent presence
  - Theme: stagecraft as message — Priestley uses the form of the drawing-room play deliberately
  - AO3: Edwardian society — class system, what this kind of dinner party meant in 1912
  - AO3: 1912 vs 1945 — the audience watching in 1945 knows what's coming (Titanic, WWI, WWII)
  - AO3: the drawing-room play genre — chosen deliberately to trap the characters and audience together
  - AO3: morality play — the Inspector as an allegorical figure is signalled from the start
- **Suggested component(s):**
  - `TheoryCompareBlock` — "what the room looks like" vs "what the room means" (appearance vs reality side-by-side)
  - `ExplainReveal` — chain: comfortable setting → false security → Inspector strips it away → 1945 audience warned

### Section 4 — Quote Analysis (AO2)
- **Purpose:** Active retrieval and analysis of the episode's 5 key quotes. Every quote practised in paragraph-ready form.
- **Proposed content for this episode:**
  - "substantial and heavily comfortable but not a home" — juxtaposition; 'heavily' connotations of oppressiveness; 'not a home' — the family lacks real warmth
  - "pink and intimate" — the lighting as self-deception; softness of colour = softness of moral vision
  - "brighter and harder" — the Inspector brings clarity and harshness of truth
  - "pleased with themselves" — self-satisfaction as complacency; Priestley's irony
  - "celebrating a special occasion" — dramatic irony; what they celebrate will be dismantled
- **Suggested component(s):**
  - `MatchingTask` — match quote to technique (juxtaposition / dramatic irony / metaphor / symbolism)
  - `FillInTheBlanksBlock` — complete the analysis: "The adjective _____ suggests..."
  - `InteractiveCollectionExplorer` — five quotes, each with technique name, analysis note and context link

## 4. Build recommendations

1. **Storyline integration:** The core takeaway — the comfortable room is a facade that Priestley builds to dismantle — should be threaded through Sections 2, 3 and 5. In Section 2 it is shown (the stage directions); in Section 3 it is named (appearance vs reality + AO3 context); in Section 5 the learner should be able to articulate it in a paragraph. The lighting change ("pink and intimate" → "brighter and harder") is the single most powerful image for this, and should be the episode's signature quote.

2. **Section 2 — `VisualNarrativeScreen`:** Use a beat sequence that respects the pace of the original scene. Do not rush to the Inspector. Dwell on the dinner table, the port, the self-congratulatory speeches. The contrast only works if the comfort is established first.

3. **Section 3 — AO3 integration:** Resist making the Edwardian context a history lesson. Frame every context point as: "this tells the audience in 1945 that..." e.g. "The drawing-room setting tells the 1945 audience that the Birlings are trapped in a pre-war world that is already over."

4. **Section 4 — avoid the quote-dump:** Five quotes is the right number for Episode 1. Each needs one technique name, one analytical note and one context link. Use `MatchingTask` for technique identification and `FillInTheBlanksBlock` for building the analysis sentence.

5. **Section 5 — whole-play link requirement:** Even in Episode 1, the exam paragraph must reference something beyond the opening. Use the cyclical structure (Episode 11, the second phone call mirrors the first) as the whole-play link — it shows Priestley has designed the opening as a deliberate structural frame.

6. **Design:** Open with the dining room in amber/gold light. When the Inspector arrives, shift to harder white/green light. This is a direct application of Priestley's stage direction in the UI itself.
