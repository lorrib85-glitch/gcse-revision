## 2. Architecture checklist (tailored)

### Section 1 — Hook & Prior Recall
- **Purpose:** Surface existing knowledge about the play's opening; prime retrieval of character names and first impressions; create curiosity about what the celebration is hiding.
- **Proposed content for this episode:**
  - True/false hook: "The Birlings are a genuinely warm, loving family" — FALSE: stage directions say "not cosy and homelike"
  - True/false hook: "An Inspector Calls is a novel" — FALSE: it is a play written for performance; stagecraft matters
  - True/false hook: "AIC questions always give you a printed extract" — FALSE: AQA Paper 2 asks a whole-play essay from a choice of two questions
  - Prior knowledge retrieval: who is at the engagement dinner? Arthur, Sybil, Sheila, Eric, Gerald and Edna
  - Prior knowledge retrieval: what is the play about, in one sentence? Use: respectable family → Inspector arrives → Eva Smith reveals hidden responsibility
  - Misconception trap: is this a happy scene? Surface yes; beneath no
- **Suggested component(s):**
  - `ChapterHookScreen` — true/false warm-up using appearance vs reality, play-not-novel and no-extract misconceptions
  - `PriorKnowledgeRecall` — free-text recall of who the Birlings are and what they're celebrating
  - `MisconceptionCheck` — "looks comfortable" does not mean "morally secure"

### Section 2 — Plot & Dramatic Action
- **Purpose:** Teach the opening scene's events: the engagement dinner, the room, the props, first impressions, the staging, and the lighting change when the Inspector arrives.
- **Proposed content for this episode:**
  - The setting: Brumley dining room, spring 1912, engagement of Sheila and Gerald
  - The room: "fairly large suburban house" belonging to a "prosperous manufacturer"
  - The effect: "substantial and heavily comfortable but not cosy and homelike" — material comfort without emotional warmth
  - The props and rituals: dessert plates, champagne glasses, port, cigars, evening dress, dining table; wealth as performance
  - Each character's first impression using stage directions verbatim:
    - Arthur Birling: "heavy-looking, rather portentous"
    - Sybil Birling: "rather cold" and Arthur's "social superior"
    - Sheila: "very pleased with life and rather excited"
    - Eric: "not quite at ease, half shy, half assertive"
    - Gerald: "very much the well-bred young man-about-town"
    - Edna: working in the background, serving and clearing
  - The lighting: "pink and intimate" → "brighter and harder" when the Inspector arrives
  - The doorbell: a sharp interruption that breaks the self-congratulatory atmosphere
  - The Inspector's entrance belongs properly to Episode 3, but this episode may preview his arrival as the moment the room's lighting and moral temperature change
- **Suggested component(s):**
  - `VisualNarrativeScreen` — beat-based sequence: room → props → character first impressions → self-satisfied dinner → doorbell → lighting shift
  - `ConceptReveal` — the lighting change as the moral pivot of the play
  - `ColSortBlock` — sort opening details into: wealth/status / emotional coldness / class hierarchy / dramatic warning sign

### Section 3 — Theme & Context
- **Purpose:** Connect the opening to the play's major themes and to AO3 context: Edwardian society, 1912 vs 1945, drawing-room play, well-made play and morality play.
- **Proposed content for this episode:**
  - Theme: appearance vs reality — the room looks comfortable but is "not cosy and homelike"
  - Theme: class — new money (Birlings) vs old money (Crofts); Edna's silent presence; the engagement as social/business alliance
  - Theme: capitalism — the house belongs to a "prosperous manufacturer"; comfort is funded by industry and labour
  - Theme: stagecraft as message — Priestley uses set, lighting, props and silence to build meaning before dialogue
  - AO3: Edwardian society — class hierarchy, domestic rituals, gender expectations and servant labour
  - AO3: 1912 vs 1945 — a 1945/46 audience knows the confidence of 1912 will be shattered by the Titanic, war and social change
  - AO3: drawing-room play — Priestley uses a respectable domestic genre to expose the moral rot inside respectability
  - AO3: well-made play — hidden past events will be investigated through a chain of revelations
  - AO3: morality play — the Inspector becomes a moral force rather than just a detective
- **Suggested component(s):**
  - `TheoryCompareBlock` — what the room looks like vs what the room means
  - `ExplainReveal` — comfortable setting → false security → Inspector strips it away → 1945 audience warned
  - `SwipeSort` — drawing-room play / well-made play / morality play: what each form contributes

### Section 4 — Quote Analysis (AO2)
- **Purpose:** Active retrieval and analysis of the episode's key quotes. Every quote practised in paragraph-ready form.
- **Proposed content for this episode:**
  - **"a fairly large suburban house"** — AO2: respectable but not grand aristocracy; suburban implies aspirational comfort
  - **"belonging to a prosperous manufacturer"** — AO2/AO3: the family's comfort is rooted in industry, capitalism and labour
  - **"substantial and heavily comfortable but not cosy and homelike"** — AO2: juxtaposition; "heavily" suggests oppressive material comfort; "not" directly exposes emotional lack
  - **"pink and intimate"** — AO2: flattering lighting; softness creates a protected illusion of privacy and warmth
  - **"brighter and harder"** — AO2: harsh exposure; truth becomes visual; Inspector's arrival changes the moral atmosphere
  - **"pleased with themselves"** — AO2: self-satisfaction and complacency; Priestley invites the audience to judge before the Inspector speaks
  - **"heavy-looking, rather portentous"** — AO2: Arthur Birling looks important but Priestley's wording makes him seem inflated and self-important
  - **"not quite at ease"** — AO2: Eric is an early warning sign; his discomfort suggests the family facade is already unstable
  - **Edna's silence/action** — AO2/stagecraft: she is present through labour, not voice; this is class hierarchy shown on stage
- **Suggested component(s):**
  - `MatchingTask` — match quote to technique: stage directions / juxtaposition / dramatic irony / symbolism / microcosm / stagecraft
  - `FillInTheBlanksBlock` — complete analysis sentences: "The adjective ____ suggests..."
  - `InteractiveCollectionExplorer` — opening quotes with technique, analysis and context link
  - `QuoteLadder` — simple meaning → method → audience effect → Priestley's purpose

### Section 5 — Exam Practice
- **Purpose:** Practise writing an AQA-style whole-play paragraph using evidence from the opening scene, with a whole-play link.
- **Proposed content for this episode:**
  - Format reminder: AQA Paper 2 Section A gives one whole-play essay from a choice of two questions; there is no printed extract; students should spend around 45 minutes
  - Exam question: "How does Priestley use stagecraft to present appearance and reality in An Inspector Calls?"
  - Alternative question: "How does Priestley use the opening of the play to introduce important ideas?"
  - Model opening argument: Priestley presents the Birling dining room as a microcosm of Edwardian capitalist respectability: materially impressive, emotionally cold, and ready to be exposed
  - Guided paragraph: use "substantial and heavily comfortable but not cosy and homelike" plus whole-play link to Act 3 phone call/cyclical structure
  - Guided paragraph: use "pink and intimate" → "brighter and harder" plus whole-play link to the Inspector's final speech and moral exposure
  - Examiner explains: Level 4 vs Level 5 — context must be embedded into interpretation, not bolted on
  - Grade 9 move from attached essay: begin with a big controlling idea, e.g. "Priestley uses the Birling household as a microcosm of Edwardian capitalist confidence, only to expose its moral emptiness."
- **Suggested component(s):**
  - `ExaminerExplainsScreen` — what AO2 on stagecraft looks like at Level 4 and Level 5
  - `GuidedExamResponse` — guided paragraph on the lighting change and room description
  - `FaceTheExaminer` — student upgrades a basic paragraph into a big-idea paragraph

### Section 6 — Retrieval & Wider Play Links
- **Purpose:** For Episode 1 this is primarily forward-linking, but it must already build the habit of whole-play evidence selection because AQA does not provide extracts.
- **Proposed content for this episode:**
  - Retrieval: name the 6 people at the opening dinner and their relationships
  - Retrieval: identify three pieces of stagecraft from the opening: room, props, lighting
  - Whole-play link: the ending — the second phone call recreates the Inspector's arrival; the structure is cyclical
  - Whole-play link: the Inspector's final speech will turn the "brighter and harder" truth into explicit moral warning
  - Whole-play link: Edna's background labour anticipates Eva Smith as another working-class woman used by the upper classes
  - Forward-link: each comfortable first impression will crack later: Arthur's confidence, Sybil's control, Sheila's naivety, Eric's unease, Gerald's charm
- **Suggested component(s):**
  - `QuickRecallScreen` — character matching: name → role → first impression → later crack
  - `MatchingTask` — opening detail → later whole-play payoff
  - `PriorKnowledgeRecall` — what might the Inspector's arrival reveal about this room?

## 4. Build recommendations

1. **Storyline integration:** The core takeaway — the comfortable room is a facade Priestley builds to dismantle — should be threaded through Sections 2, 3 and 5. In Section 2 it is shown; in Section 3 it is named; in Section 5 the learner writes it.

2. **Section 2 — dwell on stagecraft:** Do not rush to the Inspector. The episode should make students look at the room like detectives: furniture, props, lighting, Edna, seating and first impressions all contain evidence.

3. **Section 3 — AO3 integration:** Do not turn Edwardian context into a history dump. Frame every context point as: "this tells the 1945 audience that..." Example: "The drawing-room setting tells a 1945 audience that the Birlings are trapped in a pre-war world that is already morally obsolete."

4. **Section 4 — avoid quote dump:** Use active analysis. Each quote needs one technique, one inference, one audience effect and one Priestley purpose. QuoteLadder is ideal here.

5. **Section 5 — whole-play link requirement:** Because AIC has no extract, every paragraph must practise choosing evidence. Use the cyclical structure, Edna/Eva, or lighting/final speech as the whole-play link.

6. **Design:** Open with the dining room in amber/pink warmth. On the Inspector preview, shift to harder green-white light. Keep the tone dark academic, not neon. The UI itself should enact Priestley's lighting direction.
