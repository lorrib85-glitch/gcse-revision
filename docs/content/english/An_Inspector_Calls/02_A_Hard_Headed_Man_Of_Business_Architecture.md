## 2. Architecture checklist (tailored)

### Section 1 — Hook & Prior Recall
- **Purpose:** Prime retrieval of Episode 1's setting and opening; create curiosity about Birling's character; set up the dramatic irony trap.
- **Proposed content:**
  - True/false hook: "Mr Birling is presented as a wise and reliable figure whose views we should trust" — FALSE
  - True/false hook: "The Titanic line is just a random historical reference" — FALSE: it is dramatic irony and symbolism
  - True/false hook: "Capitalism vs socialism is only about money" — FALSE: in the play it links to morality, responsibility, class and survival
  - Recall from Episode 1: what was the mood at the opening dinner? who was there? what was the lighting?
  - Recall from Episode 1: what does "not cosy and homelike" suggest about the Birling household?
  - Misconception trap: "Birling's predictions show he is practical and experienced" — FALSE: Priestley makes every major prediction wrong
- **Suggested component(s):**
  - `ChapterHookScreen` — true/false using Birling-as-reliable, Titanic-as-detail and capitalism-as-money-only misconceptions
  - `PriorKnowledgeRecall` — recall the room, the occasion, the characters and the facade from Episode 1
  - `MisconceptionCheck` — "sounds confident" does not mean "is trustworthy"

### Section 2 — Plot & Dramatic Action
- **Purpose:** Teach Birling's speeches scene by scene: business alliance, prosperity, labour unrest, Titanic, war, Russia, socialist writers, self-interest and reputation. Show how each speech sets up dramatic irony.
- **Proposed content:**
  - The engagement toast: Birling uses the occasion to lecture Gerald, Eric and Sheila
  - Engagement as business deal: Crofts and Birlings may work together for "lower costs and higher prices"
  - "Interests of Capital" speech: employers are protecting their profits and authority
  - Labour unrest: miners' strike, "possible labour trouble", "Capital versus Labour agitations" — sets up Eva's later dismissal
  - Prosperity speech: "steadily increasing prosperity" — forward prediction undercut by what the 1945 audience knows
  - War speech: Kaiser, German officers, Balkans and "fiddlesticks" — comic/tragic dramatic irony
  - Titanic speech: "unsinkable, absolutely unsinkable" — peak dramatic irony and symbol of capitalist hubris
  - Russia/Bernard Shaw/H. G. Wells references: Birling dismisses progressive/socialist thinking
  - Self-interest speech: "a man has to make his own way" and "community and all that nonsense"
  - Knighthood/reputation anxiety: "just a knighthood" and fear of scandal/"police court" foreshadow Act 3
- **Suggested component(s):**
  - `ExplainReveal` — Birling says X → audience in 1945 knows Y → Priestley's purpose is Z
  - `VisualNarrativeScreen` — beat sequence through the speeches, with audience knowledge/reaction layered in
  - `TimelineChain` — 1912 claim → real historical event → Priestley's message

### Section 3 — Theme & Context
- **Purpose:** Name and explain capitalism, dramatic irony, class anxiety and the 1912/1945 split; connect Birling's speeches to Priestley's political message.
- **Proposed content:**
  - Theme: capitalism — profit, competition, employer solidarity, labour as cost, family as business alliance
  - Theme: social responsibility — Birling's self-interest creed is the negative thesis the Inspector later overturns
  - Theme: dramatic irony — how the 1945/46 audience's knowledge reverses Birling's authority
  - Theme: class — Birling's new money and anxiety around the Crofts' old money
  - Theme: appearance vs reality — Birling sounds stable and practical, but his judgement is unreliable
  - AO3: 1912 Liberal government / laissez-faire economics — why Birling's worldview could seem normal in 1912
  - AO3: workers' rights and industrial unrest — miners' strike, trade union activity, employer fear of labour demands
  - AO3: Priestley's socialism — writing in 1945/46 after two wars and the Labour landslide, when collective responsibility felt urgent
  - AO3: Titanic as symbol — huge, luxurious, class-divided, supposedly unsinkable; a perfect image of Edwardian capitalist hubris
  - AO3: socialist/progressive writers — Bernard Shaw and H. G. Wells signal the ideas Birling wants young people to reject
- **Suggested component(s):**
  - `TheoryCompareBlock` — capitalism (Birling's worldview) vs socialism (Inspector's later worldview)
  - `ExplainReveal` — 1912 context vs 1945 audience reaction
  - `ColSortBlock` — sort Birling's lines into capitalism / dramatic irony / class anxiety / reputation
  - `MisconceptionCheck` — Birling is not presented sympathetically as a reliable guide

### Section 4 — Quote Analysis (AO2)
- **Purpose:** Active retrieval and analysis of 8 high-frequency Birling quotes. Every quote should be taught with technique, effect, context and Priestley's purpose.
- **Proposed content:**
  - **"a hard-headed business man" / "hard headed, practical man of business"** — repeated self-advertising; comic pomposity; authority undercut by irony
  - **"lower costs and higher prices"** — semantic field of business; capitalism colonises family and marriage
  - **"the interests of Capital"** — abstract noun gives capitalism almost sacred status for Birling; profit becomes identity
  - **"steadily increasing prosperity"** — confident future prediction; dramatic irony after war, hardship and rationing
  - **"fiddlesticks"** — childish/comic dismissal of war; makes him look absurd to a post-war audience
  - **"unsinkable, absolutely unsinkable"** — repetition and adverb of certainty; Titanic as symbol of false confidence
  - **"Russia... always behindhand naturally"** — dramatic irony and ideological blindness; ignores coming revolution/social change
  - **"a man has to make his own way — has to look after himself"** — capitalist individualism; direct contrast with Inspector's collective responsibility
  - **"community and all that nonsense"** — dismissive noun; Priestley sets up the Inspector's moral answer
  - **"just a knighthood" / "police court or start a scandal"** — reputation anxiety; public image over moral substance
- **Suggested component(s):**
  - `MatchingTask` — match quote to technique + effect + later whole-play link
  - `InteractiveCollectionExplorer` — Birling quote bank with technique, analysis, context and essay use
  - `ColSortBlock` — sort quotes by theme: capitalism / dramatic irony / class / self-interest / reputation
  - `QuoteLadder` — simple meaning → method → 1945 audience effect → Priestley's purpose

## 4. Build recommendations

1. **Storyline integration:** Dramatic irony is the engine. Every Section 2 screen should show: Birling says → audience knows → Priestley's purpose. This makes the method visible and teachable.

2. **Section 2 — sequence matters:** Build the dramatic irony cumulatively: prosperity → labour unrest → war → Titanic → Russia → self-interest. By the end, learners should feel both the comedy and the warning.

3. **Section 3 — capitalism as a concept:** Give a brief, teen-friendly definition: capitalism here means profit, competition, self-interest and workers treated as costs. Then compare it with the Inspector's later social responsibility.

4. **Section 4 — don't quote-dump:** Use sorting and matching first. Students need to know what each quote proves before they analyse it.

5. **Section 5 — reinforce whole-play habit:** Because AIC has no printed extract, every exam paragraph should connect Act 1 speeches to later moments: Eva's dismissal, Inspector's final speech, Birling's Act 3 reputation panic, or the final phone call.

6. **Avoid:** making this a simplistic "Birling is bad" episode. Priestley's technique is subtler: he makes Birling sound confident, then lets history and the Inspector expose him.
