## 2. Storyline

### Core takeaway

Gerald appears kinder than Arthur Birling, but his relationship with Eva/Daisy still exposes male privilege, class power and moral weakness. Priestley makes him morally complex on purpose. Gerald does help Eva. He removes her from immediate danger, gives her somewhere to stay and shows some tenderness. But he also benefits from her vulnerability, "keeps" her as a mistress, then ends the relationship when it no longer suits his life.

This makes Gerald exam-useful because he cannot be reduced to simple villain or simple rescuer. He shows how exploitation can look polite, romantic and even generous on the surface. Priestley's point is uncomfortable: a gentleman can still participate in a system that uses vulnerable women.

Sheila's response is equally important. She recognises the affair's moral complexity more clearly than Gerald does. Her phrase "wonderful Fairy Prince" punctures Gerald's romantic self-image. By returning the ring, she rejects the engagement facade built in Episode 1.

### Evidence for the takeaway

- "Daisy Renton" — Eva's name-change suggests reinvention, vulnerability and survival; different men meet different versions of her
- Gerald's reaction to the name — he gives himself away before the Inspector fully exposes him
- "the Palace bar" — a space associated with sexual availability and male privilege; Eva/Daisy is exposed to danger there
- "young and fresh and charming" — Gerald aestheticises Eva; she is presented through his male gaze
- "I didn't feel about her as she felt about me" — Gerald admits emotional imbalance
- "She became my mistress" — a clear admission of the relationship's sexual/social status
- "I became at once the most important person in her life" — reveals dependence and power imbalance
- "I want you to understand that I didn't install her there so that I could make love to her" — defensive phrasing; he wants moral credit for his intention, but the power imbalance remains
- "kept her" — euphemistic phrase for financially supporting a mistress; soft language hides dependency
- "the wonderful Fairy Prince" — Sheila's irony deflates his rescue narrative
- "I rather respect you more than I've ever done before" — Sheila can recognise honesty while still rejecting the engagement
- "No, not yet. It's too soon" — Sheila refuses the ring; she has changed too much to resume the old relationship immediately
- "everything's all right now, Sheila" — Gerald's Act 3 return; he wants the hoax possibility to erase moral responsibility

### Series throughline

Gerald is positioned between the older and younger generations. He is younger than Arthur and Sybil, and at moments he seems more emotionally honest than them. But by Act 3 he behaves like the older generation: he searches for technical escape rather than moral responsibility.

His relationship with Eva/Daisy continues the chain begun by Birling and Sheila. Factory exploitation pushes Eva to Milwards; Milwards dismissal leaves her more vulnerable; Gerald's temporary rescue becomes another stage of dependence and abandonment. Priestley shows that Eva is not destroyed by one monster but by a sequence of socially acceptable behaviours.

This episode also deepens Sheila's transformation. She sees Gerald's behaviour with painful clarity. Her decision to return the ring proves that her moral awakening affects her own future, not just her feelings about Eva.

## 4. Content reference pack

### Dates & timeline

- **After Milwards dismissal:** Eva becomes Daisy Renton and is increasingly vulnerable
- **Summer 1911:** Gerald meets Daisy at the Palace bar and begins the affair
- **Summer 1911:** Gerald houses Daisy in rooms at Morgan Terrace and gives her money
- **Autumn 1911:** Gerald ends the relationship; gives her enough money to "go on for a while"
- **Late 1911 / early 1912:** Eva/Daisy becomes more desperate and later meets Eric
- **Act 3:** Gerald returns with information suggesting Inspector Goole may not be a real police inspector; he uses this as emotional relief rather than moral challenge

### Key people

- **Gerald Croft:** aristocratic, charming, "well-bred young man-about-town"; son of Sir George and Lady Croft; engaged to Sheila; capable of tenderness but protected by gender/class privilege.
- **Eva Smith / Daisy Renton:** after two dismissals, she reinvents herself under a new name. Her dignity and sensitivity make Gerald's abandonment more morally uncomfortable.
- **Sheila Birling:** no longer the naive girl from the opening. Her response to Gerald shows her developing judgement and refusal to return to the old engagement fantasy.
- **Inspector Goole:** exposes Gerald's hidden history and forces the private sexual relationship into public moral judgement.

### Key terms & definitions

- **Kept woman / mistress:** a woman financially supported by a man in a sexual relationship outside marriage; in Edwardian society the woman's reputation was far more vulnerable than the man's.
- **Gender double standards:** men and women judged by different sexual rules; men's affairs could be tolerated, while women were shamed and excluded.
- **Fallen woman:** Victorian/Edwardian term for a woman considered sexually immoral or no longer respectable; Eva/Daisy is pushed towards this category by poverty and male power.
- **Male privilege:** social freedom and protection given to men because of gender; Gerald can return to respectability in ways Eva cannot.
- **Class power:** Gerald has money, housing and family status; Eva/Daisy has dependence and insecurity.
- **Euphemism:** softening language. "Kept her" hides the transactional and unequal nature of the relationship.
- **Male gaze:** a woman presented through a man's aesthetic/sexual perspective; "young and fresh and charming" shows Daisy through Gerald's eyes.
- **Ring symbolism:** the engagement ring symbolises the respectable relationship/future Gerald expects; Sheila's refusal shows that future has been morally interrupted.

### Case studies / named examples

- **Daisy Renton as name-change:** The new name matters. Eva has moved from factory worker to shop assistant to a more precarious social identity. Priestley shows how society forces vulnerable women to reinvent themselves to survive.

- **Gerald at the Palace bar:** Gerald presents himself as rescuer, but the setting is already revealing. He is a privileged man in a space where vulnerable women may be sexually/economically available. Priestley complicates the rescue narrative from the start.

- **"Young and fresh and charming":** Gerald's description objectifies Daisy. The adjectives make her attractive and appealing, but they reduce her to qualities Gerald notices. This is useful for gender essays.

- **"I didn't feel about her as she felt about me":** Gerald recognises the emotional imbalance. Daisy is more dependent and emotionally invested than he is.

- **"I became at once the most important person in her life":** The phrase exposes the danger of his power. He might intend kindness, but he becomes central because she has so little else.

- **"She became my mistress":** This blunt admission shows the social reality. In 1912, Gerald can confess and later return to respectability; Daisy bears the risk of shame, abandonment and poverty.

- **"Kept her":** The key phrase. It makes the relationship sound controlled and civilised, but it means Daisy is financially dependent on Gerald. The softness of the phrase is the danger.

- **"The wonderful Fairy Prince":** Sheila's irony exposes Gerald's self-image. A fairy prince should rescue someone permanently; Gerald's rescue is temporary and tied to his own desire.

- **"I rather respect you more than I've ever done before":** Sheila's response is nuanced. She recognises Gerald's honesty and perhaps his partial kindness, but this does not cancel his exploitation.

- **"No, not yet. It's too soon":** Sheila's refusal of the ring shows changed standards. She will not let the engagement return to its comfortable pre-Inspector state.

- **"Everything's all right now, Sheila":** Gerald's Act 3 line exposes his regression. He thinks procedural doubt can restore the relationship and erase responsibility. Sheila knows it cannot.

### Causes & effects

- Eva loses Milwards job → becomes more vulnerable → enters the Palace bar environment as Daisy Renton
- Gerald sees Daisy at the Palace bar → intervenes and houses her → she becomes dependent on him
- Gerald supports Daisy → relationship becomes unequal and sexually/socially risky for her
- Gerald ends the affair → Daisy is left again with only temporary money → she moves closer to desperation
- Sheila hears Gerald's confession → returns the ring → her moral transformation affects her personal choices
- Gerald leaves → returns in Act 3 with hoax evidence → shows desire for relief, not full moral change
- Daisy's treatment by Gerald → prepares the audience for Eric's later, more obviously exploitative behaviour

**Grade 9 essay move:**
Do not argue that Gerald is simply kind or simply cruel. Priestley presents him as morally complex to show that exploitation can be hidden inside politeness, charm and temporary rescue.

**Useful paragraph stem:**
Priestley presents Gerald's relationship with Daisy as superficially compassionate but structurally exploitative: his money and status allow him to rescue her temporarily while still controlling when the relationship ends.

**Key misconceptions for MisconceptionCheck:**
- "Gerald loved Eva, so he is not responsible" — too simple: affection does not erase power imbalance or consequences
- "Gerald is exactly the same as Birling" — too simple: he is more emotionally complex, but still self-protective
- "Gerald is the most moral character" — debatable: his Act 3 response shows he wants relief more than responsibility
- "Daisy Renton is a totally different person from Eva Smith" — false: it is the same woman under a new name/identity
- "Keeping Daisy was purely generous" — false/too simple: support and exploitation can coexist
