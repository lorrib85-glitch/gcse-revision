## 2. Storyline

### Core takeaway
Gerald appears kinder than the Birlings, but his relationship with Eva/Daisy still exposes male privilege, class power and moral weakness. He "kept" her (the word Priestley chooses), provided for her temporarily, and then ended the relationship when his life required it — with no regard for her vulnerability. He is useful for nuanced essays because Priestley presents him as charming and sometimes sympathetic, but ultimately still part of the system that uses vulnerable women. By Act 3 he reverts entirely to the older generation's self-protective logic.

### Evidence for the takeaway
- "Daisy Renton" — her name-change signals her changing identity; she is a different person to different men
- "young and fresh and charming" — Gerald's description of Eva; she is an aesthetic object in his account
- "easy well-bred young man-about-town" — his opening stage direction; his charm is a class performance
- "kept her" — the euphemism conceals the reality of the power imbalance; he supported her financially in exchange for sexual availability
- "the wonderful Fairy Prince" — Sheila's sarcastic summary; romantic framing vs realistic exploitation
- "respect myself" — after ending the affair, Gerald frames his departure in terms of his own self-image, not her welfare
- "everything's all right now, Sheila" — his Act 3 line after "disproving" the Inspector; he has reverted to self-interest

### Series throughline
Gerald represents the middle ground in Priestley's moral spectrum — between Birling's overt self-interest and Sheila/Eric's genuine remorse. His relationship with Eva shows that even "kind" expressions of class privilege are still exploitation. His Act 3 reversion to the older generation's values is Priestley's warning that charm and surface decency are not the same as moral change.

## 4. Content reference pack

### Dates & timeline
- **Summer 1911:** Gerald begins the affair with Eva/Daisy Renton
- **Autumn 1911:** Gerald ends the relationship; gives her money to "go on for a while"
- **Around December 1911 / early 1912:** Eva (now at her lowest) meets Eric

### Key people
- **Gerald Croft:** aristocratic, charming, "easy well-bred young man-about-town"; son of Sir George Croft; engaged to Sheila; had an affair with Eva; genuinely seemed to care for her but ultimately protected himself
- **Eva Smith / Daisy Renton:** Eva changed her name to "Daisy Renton" when she moved in the circles of the Palace bar; the name change reflects how she had to reinvent herself to survive; her "sensitivity" is noted by Gerald

### Key terms & definitions
- **Kept woman / mistress:** a woman financially supported by a man in exchange for sexual availability; in 1912 this was tacitly accepted for upper-class men; for the woman, it was social ruin if discovered
- **Double standards (gender):** men and women were judged by different moral standards; men's affairs were accepted or ignored; women's were grounds for dismissal and social exclusion
- **Fallen woman:** Victorian/Edwardian term for a woman who had lost her "virtue" (had sexual relations outside marriage); Eva is progressively pushed into this category
- **Euphemism:** "kept her" — softens what is actually a financially-transactional relationship built on a fundamental power imbalance

### Case studies / named examples
- **"The wonderful Fairy Prince":** Sheila's ironic term for Gerald's version of his own role; he saw himself as rescuing Eva; Sheila (and Priestley) see him as exploiting her vulnerability to satisfy his own needs with a clear conscience
- **Morgan Terrace:** the specific address where Gerald "kept" Eva; the detail of a place name makes the reality concrete and inescapable
- **The Palace bar:** a venue Gerald knew was frequented by women available for money; the fact that he was there at all, and that he noticed Eva there, is already morally revealing

### Causes & effects
- Eva dismissed from Milwards → destitute → resorts to the Palace bar → Gerald finds her there
- Gerald offers money and a place to stay → Eva grateful but financially dependent → he has power she doesn't
- Gerald ends the affair → Eva "accepted it" with "dignity" → she is left again with nothing
- Gerald gives her money to "go on for a while" → not enough → Eva continues to struggle → eventually meets Eric
- Sheila learns of the affair → returns the ring → begins to see Gerald clearly
- Gerald leaves Act 2 → returns in Act 3 trying to prove Inspector was a hoax → reverts to self-interest
