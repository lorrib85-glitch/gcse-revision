## 2. Architecture checklist (tailored)

### Section 1 — Hook & Prior Recall
- **Purpose:** Retrieve Birling's capitalist worldview and the Inspector's method; prime the learner to see Eva's dismissal as the first link in a chain, not an isolated factory incident.
- **Proposed content:**
  - True/false hook: "Eva was sacked because she was a bad worker" — FALSE: Birling calls her a "good worker"
  - True/false hook: "The strike was only about greed" — FALSE/too simple: the rise was modest, and employers also seek higher prices
  - True/false hook: "Sheila only starts changing when she is personally exposed" — FALSE: her moral challenge begins here
  - Recall Episode 2: Birling's "lower costs and higher prices" and "interests of Capital"
  - Recall Episode 3: the Inspector's method — "one person and one line of inquiry at a time"
  - Hook question: "When does a business decision become a moral decision?"
- **Suggested component(s):**
  - `ChapterHookScreen` — true/false on Eva, strike and Sheila misconceptions
  - `PriorKnowledgeRecall` — retrieve Birling's capitalist language and Inspector's method
  - `MisconceptionCheck` — legal business choice vs moral responsibility

### Section 2 — Plot & Dramatic Action
- **Purpose:** Teach the factory interrogation as the first link in Eva's chain: photograph, recognition, wage demand, refusal, strike, sacking, Sheila's challenge.
- **Proposed content:**
  - The Inspector shows Birling the photograph; Birling recognises Eva Smith
  - Birling admits Eva was "a good worker"
  - Eva and other workers ask for a rise from around 22s 6d to 25 shillings a week
  - Birling says "I refused, of course" and "it's my duty to keep labour costs down"
  - Eva is labelled a "ringleader" and punished for having "a lot to say — far too much"
  - Birling fears that if employers do not "come down sharply", workers will be "asking for the earth"
  - Inspector reframes the phrase: "It's better to ask for the earth than to take it"
  - Sheila challenges Birling: "Why shouldn't they try for higher wages?" and "these girls aren't cheap labour — they're people"
  - The Inspector introduces the chain logic: what happened then may have determined what happened afterwards
  - Eva's factory dismissal pushes her towards Milwards, setting up Episode 5
- **Suggested component(s):**
  - `VisualNarrativeScreen` — sequence: photograph → recognition → good worker → wage demand → refusal → strike → ringleader → sacking → Sheila challenge → chain begins
  - `ExplainReveal` — Eva's factory story as a cause/effect chain
  - `TimelineChain` — first link of Eva's downfall: factory → Milwards → Gerald → charity → Eric → death

### Section 3 — Theme & Context
- **Purpose:** Connect the episode to capitalism, class, workers' rights, gender, responsibility and Sheila's emerging generational difference.
- **Proposed content:**
  - Theme: capitalism — workers are reduced to labour costs, percentages and cheap labour
  - Theme: social class — employer power vs working-class vulnerability
  - Theme: exploitation — Eva is good at her job but punished for challenging unfair conditions
  - Theme: collective action — strike, wage demand and worker organisation are presented as morally reasonable
  - Theme: dehumanisation — Birling's "cheap labour" vs Sheila's "people"
  - Theme: young vs old — Sheila begins to challenge Birling's values before her own interrogation
  - Theme: responsibility — the first link in the chain shows responsibility is cumulative
  - AO3: no modern minimum wage/employment protection; employers had strong power over wages and dismissal
  - AO3: labour unrest in 1911–12 and employer fear of organised workers
  - AO3: working-class women had limited economic and legal protection
  - AO3: Priestley's 1945 audience would be more open to arguments about welfare, workers' protection and collective responsibility
- **Suggested component(s):**
  - `TheoryCompareBlock` — Birling's language vs Sheila/Inspector's language: costs/ringleaders/cheap labour vs people/fairness/responsibility
  - `ColSortBlock` — sort quotes into capitalism / dehumanisation / responsibility / Sheila's awakening
  - `ExplainReveal` — 1912 labour context, kept lean: no minimum wage, weak protection, employer power
  - `SwipeSort` — business decision or moral decision? Learner sorts Birling's claims

### Section 4 — Quote Analysis (AO2)
- **Purpose:** Active retrieval and analysis of factory-scene quotes, with focus on language contrast.
- **Proposed content:**
  - **"good worker"** — AO2/AO1: removes the excuse of incompetence; Eva is dismissed for challenging power
  - **"I refused, of course"** — AO2: adverbial phrase normalises refusal; exploitation is presented as common sense to Birling
  - **"it's my duty to keep labour costs down"** — AO2: moral word "duty" twisted into profit protection; business language dehumanises
  - **"a lot to say — far too much"** — AO2: dash marks irritation; working-class female voice treated as excess
  - **"ringleaders"** — AO2: criminalising dysphemism; leadership reframed as troublemaking
  - **"asking for the earth"** — AO2: hyperbole; exaggerates a modest wage demand
  - **"It's better to ask for the earth than to take it"** — AO2: Inspector's reversal; moral contrast between asking fairly and taking exploitatively
  - **"these girls aren't cheap labour — they're people"** — AO2: antithesis; Sheila replaces economic label with human identity
  - **"chain of events"** — AO2: metaphor of linked consequences; responsibility extends beyond the first action
  - PQAC: analyse "these girls aren't cheap labour — they're people" as a turning point in Sheila's moral awareness
  - PQAC extension: analyse "it's my duty to keep labour costs down" as Priestley's critique of capitalist language
- **Suggested component(s):**
  - `MatchingTask` — match quote to speaker and theme
  - `InteractiveCollectionExplorer` — quote bank with technique, effect, context and whole-play use
  - `ColSortBlock` — Birling language vs Sheila/Inspector language
  - `QuoteLadder` — simple meaning → language method → Priestley's purpose → whole-play link

## 4. Build recommendations

1. **Storyline integration:** Make the chain visible. This chapter should make learners feel, "This is link one." Every later link should be understood as consequences building from this point.

2. **Section 2 — show value systems clashing:** The plot is not just that Eva is sacked. The real learning is the clash between Birling's economic language and Sheila/Inspector's moral language.

3. **Section 3 — keep context lean:** Use only what helps interpretation: no minimum wage, weak employment protection, employer power, labour unrest. Do not turn it into a history lesson.

4. **Section 4 — prioritise language contrast:** The chapter's highest-value AO2 pair is "cheap labour" vs "people". Make learners actively manipulate that contrast.

5. **Section 5 — avoid retelling:** The weak paragraph says: "Birling fired Eva because she wanted more money." The stronger paragraph explains how Priestley uses Birling's language to expose capitalism's dehumanising logic.

6. **Design:** The screen language should feel industrial but not grimy-generic: use dark factory silhouettes, ledger/wage-slip motifs, and a subtle chain visual. No neon factory dashboard.
