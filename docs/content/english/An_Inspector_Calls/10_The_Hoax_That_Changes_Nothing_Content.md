## 2. Storyline

### Core takeaway
After the Inspector leaves, the family splits between those who understand the moral lesson and those who only care about escaping consequences. Gerald investigates the Inspector's identity; the infirmary reports no death; the older generation celebrates. But Sheila and Eric recognise that the moral truth is unchanged: they did harm, regardless of whether there was a real police investigation. The "hoax" does not undo what they did. The play's key exam argument: morality is not the same as legality.

### Evidence for the takeaway
- "that man wasn't a police inspector" — Gerald's first move is to question the Inspector's identity, not to reflect on the family's behaviour
- "no girl has died" — the infirmary report; the older generation treats this as exoneration
- "the whole story" — Birling: if there's no body, there's no crime; he conflates legal innocence with moral innocence
- "you're pretending everything's just as it was before" — Sheila's devastating line; she refuses the logic
- "the point is, you don't seem to have learnt anything" — Sheila to her parents; the generational divide is now complete
- "you began to learn something" — the Inspector's earlier line, quoted back
- "everything's all right now, Sheila" — Gerald's Act 3 line; he has reverted; Sheila's response is silence

### Series throughline
This episode is the play's moral turning point after the climax. The Inspector has gone; the question is what remains. For the older generation: relief. For the younger: horror at the relief. The split confirms that social change must come from the young, not the old. This is Priestley's most direct argument: you cannot wait for the older generation to change.

## 4. Content reference pack

### Dates & timeline
- This entire episode takes place in the immediate aftermath of the Inspector's departure — the same evening as the whole play

### Key people
- **Gerald Croft:** drives the investigation into the Inspector's identity; his motivation is to undo the damage rather than learn from it; he reverts to the older generation's values
- **Sheila Birling:** refuses to join the celebration; "you don't seem to have learnt anything"; holds the moral line
- **Eric Birling:** equally appalled; sides with Sheila; the generational alliance confirmed
- **Arthur and Sybil Birling:** immediate relief; "the whole thing" is a hoax; Birling's knighthood chances restored; no learning, no change

### Key terms & definitions
- **Morality vs legality:** a distinction the play insists on; the characters may not be legally guilty (no confirmed crime) but they are morally guilty (they each contributed to a person's destruction)
- **Self-deception:** the older generation's willingness to believe the "hoax" is real because it is convenient; Priestley shows this as a moral failing
- **Reversion:** Gerald's return to the older generation's logic; he had some sympathy during the interrogation but ultimately chooses comfort over growth

### Case studies / named examples
- **Birling's knighthood:** his first thought after relief is his "knighthood" prospects; this is Priestley's most damning detail — even after everything, his primary concern is social advancement
- **"You're pretending everything's just as it was before":** Sheila names what the older generation is doing; the word "pretending" is key — it is conscious self-deception, not ignorance

### Causes & effects
- Gerald investigates the Inspector → no police record → the older generation concludes it was a hoax
- Infirmary has no record → Birling and Mrs Birling celebrate → Sheila and Eric refuse to join
- Older generation reverts → the generational split is permanent → Priestley's argument about social change confirmed
- Morality vs legality crystallised → no legal case does not mean no moral responsibility
