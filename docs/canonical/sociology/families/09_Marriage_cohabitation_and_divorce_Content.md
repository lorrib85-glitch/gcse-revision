# Episode 9: Marriage, cohabitation and divorce — Content

## Series: Families and households (AQA 8192, Paper 1 Section A)

---

## Big question

Why are people less likely to marry — and more likely to leave?

---

## Syllabus coverage

- Marriage trends: declining marriage rate since the 1970s, rising age at first marriage, rising remarriage and changing meaning of marriage
- Cohabitation: increasing, especially among younger adults; may be seen as a trial marriage, an alternative to marriage, or part of a long-term relationship
- Living Apart Together (LAT): intimate relationships where partners remain in separate households
- Same-sex relationships: civil partnerships and same-sex marriage as examples of legal and social change
- Divorce trends since 1945: long-term rise after legal changes, later decline overall, increase among some older age groups
- Reasons for increased divorce: legal changes, changing social attitudes, secularisation, changing status of women, financial independence, rising expectations and individualisation
- Giddens: pure relationship — relationships continue only while they are rewarding for both partners
- Consequences of divorce: lone-parent families, reconstituted families, remarriage, extended-family changes and effects on children/adults
- Different sociological views: Functionalist, Feminist, New Right and Postmodern/Late modern views
- Interpreting official statistics on marriage, cohabitation and divorce

## Key concepts / vocabulary

- **Marriage:** a legally recognised relationship between two people.
- **Marriage rate:** number of marriages per 1,000 unmarried adults in the population.
- **Remarriage:** marrying again after divorce or widowhood.
- **Serial monogamy:** a pattern where people have a series of exclusive relationships or marriages across their lifetime.
- **Cohabitation:** living together as a couple without being married.
- **Living Apart Together (LAT):** being in an intimate relationship while living in separate households.
- **Civil partnership:** a legally recognised partnership introduced for same-sex couples by the Civil Partnership Act 2004 and later extended to opposite-sex couples.
- **Same-sex marriage:** marriage between two people of the same sex; legal in England and Wales from 2014.
- **Divorce:** legal ending of a marriage.
- **Separation:** when a couple stops living together as partners but may still be legally married.
- **Secularisation:** declining influence of religion on social life and behaviour.
- **Stigma:** social shame or disapproval attached to a behaviour or status.
- **Divorce Reform Act 1969:** made irretrievable breakdown of marriage sufficient grounds for divorce, making divorce easier to obtain.
- **Pure relationship:** Giddens' idea that relationships continue only while both partners find them satisfying and rewarding.
- **Individualisation:** the process where people make more personal choices about relationships, identity and family life rather than simply following tradition.
- **Reconstituted family:** family where one or both partners have children from previous relationships.
- **Lone-parent family:** family with one parent living with dependent children; may result from divorce, separation, bereavement or choice.

## Marriage trends

Marriage has not disappeared, but its pattern and meaning have changed.

Key trends:

- fewer people marry than in the past
- people tend to marry later
- cohabitation before marriage has become more common
- remarriage remains important because divorce and re-partnering create new family forms
- same-sex marriage and civil partnerships show legal recognition of more diverse relationships
- some couples choose LAT relationships rather than sharing one household

Possible reasons for declining marriage include:

- secularisation: less pressure to marry for religious reasons
- less stigma around cohabitation, divorce and sex before marriage
- women’s increased economic independence
- longer education and later transitions into work, housing and parenthood
- changing expectations of relationships and personal fulfilment
- rising cost of weddings, housing and family life
- more legal and social acceptance of alternatives to marriage

---

## Cohabitation and LAT relationships

Cohabitation means living together as a couple without being married. It may be:

- a step before marriage
- an alternative to marriage
- a long-term arrangement
- a practical response to housing, money or work pressures

Some sociologists link rising cohabitation to secularisation and declining stigma. Others link it to individualisation: people may feel freer to choose relationship forms that fit their lives rather than following one expected path.

Living Apart Together relationships show that intimacy and family life do not always require a shared household. LAT relationships can happen because of work, children from previous relationships, caring responsibilities, personal independence or housing constraints.

---

## Divorce trends and reasons

Divorce rose after the Second World War, especially after legal changes made it easier to divorce. The Divorce Reform Act 1969 was a major turning point because it allowed irretrievable breakdown of marriage to be used as grounds for divorce.

Reasons for increased divorce include:

- **Legal changes:** divorce became easier, cheaper and less blame-based.
- **Changing social attitudes:** divorce became less stigmatised and more socially accepted.
- **Secularisation:** religion has less influence over whether people stay married.
- **Changing status of women:** women have more legal rights, education, employment and financial independence.
- **Welfare support:** some people may be more able to leave unhappy or unsafe relationships.
- **Rising expectations:** people expect love, emotional fulfilment, sexual satisfaction and equality from relationships.
- **Individualisation:** people may prioritise personal happiness rather than staying married because tradition expects it.
- **Media and romantic expectations:** films, television and social media may create high expectations of relationships.

Important nuance: divorce has not simply risen every year. It rose strongly after legal change, but has declined in some recent periods. Some older people are more likely to divorce than in the past, sometimes described as "silver splitters".

---

## Giddens and the pure relationship

Giddens argues that modern relationships are increasingly based on the pure relationship.

This means relationships are no longer held together mainly by tradition, religion, law or economic dependence. Instead, they continue only while both partners feel the relationship is rewarding.

This helps explain:

- rising divorce
- greater cohabitation
- later marriage
- more relationship choice
- higher expectations of emotional satisfaction

Best exam sentence:

> Giddens argues that relationships have become more individualised, so people are more likely to leave if the relationship no longer provides emotional fulfilment.

---

## Consequences of divorce and changing relationships

Divorce and changing relationship patterns can affect family structure.

Possible consequences include:

- more lone-parent families
- more reconstituted families
- more step-parents and step-siblings
- more remarriage and serial monogamy
- more complex extended-family relationships, such as step-grandparents
- possible emotional, financial or housing difficulties for adults and children
- escape from unhappy, violent or oppressive relationships
- greater personal freedom and choice for adults

Do not write as if divorce has only negative consequences. For some people, divorce may create hardship. For others, it may improve wellbeing, safety or equality.

## Sociological perspectives

### Functionalist view

Functionalists usually see stable families as important for socialisation and emotional support. They may argue that high divorce rates threaten family stability and make socialisation more difficult.

### New Right view

The New Right sees divorce, lone-parent families and cohabitation as signs of moral decline and weakening commitment. Murray links lone-parent families and welfare dependency to the development of an underclass.

This view is criticised for blaming lone parents and ignoring poverty, domestic abuse, women’s independence and the possibility that divorce can be better than remaining in a harmful relationship.

### Feminist view

Feminists often see divorce more positively because it may allow women to leave patriarchal, unequal, violent or oppressive marriages. Rising divorce can show that women have more independence and legal rights.

However, Feminists also note that divorce can leave women financially worse off, especially if they have taken responsibility for unpaid childcare and domestic labour.

### Postmodern / late modern view

Giddens argues that relationships are more based on choice, satisfaction and negotiation than tradition. Divorce and cohabitation are signs of individualisation and changing expectations rather than simply decline.

## Likely misconceptions

- MISCONCEPTION: "Divorce has been rising continuously since 1945." Correction: it rose sharply after legal changes but has declined in some recent periods, while some older age groups have seen increases.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Secularisation alone explains rising divorce." Correction: secularisation is one factor among several, including legal change, women's economic independence and changing expectations.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Cohabitation means people reject commitment." Correction: cohabitation can be a trial before marriage, an alternative to marriage or a committed long-term relationship.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Divorce is always bad for children." Correction: divorce can create difficulties, but staying in a high-conflict or harmful family can also be damaging.
- MISCONCEPTION: "The New Right and Functionalists say exactly the same thing." Correction: both value family stability, but the New Right makes a more moral and political argument about welfare dependency and nuclear-family decline.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Giddens is a Marxist or Feminist." Correction: Giddens writes from a late-modern / postmodern perspective about individual choice and relationship satisfaction.

## Methods link

Official statistics are useful for studying marriage, cohabitation and divorce because they provide large-scale data over time.

Strengths:

- They allow sociologists to identify long-term trends.
- They are useful for comparing marriage, divorce and remarriage rates across time.
- They can show links between legal changes and divorce patterns.

Limitations:

- They record legal status, not relationship quality.
- Cohabitation and LAT relationships can be harder to measure than marriage and divorce.
- Definitions and laws change over time, making direct comparison difficult.
- Statistics show patterns but not personal meanings, so interviews may be useful for understanding why people marry, cohabit, separate or divorce.
