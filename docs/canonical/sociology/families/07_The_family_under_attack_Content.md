# Episode 7: The family under attack — Content

## Series: Families and households (AQA 8192, Paper 1 Section A)

---

## Big question

Is the family a safe place — or a place of control, exploitation and harm?

---

## Syllabus coverage

- Marxist critique of the family, with Eli Zaretsky as the core named sociologist
- Zaretsky's argument that the family supports capitalism through unpaid domestic labour, reproduction of workers, consumption and the illusion of private life
- Feminist critique of the family, with Christine Delphy and Diana Leonard as the core named sociologists
- Delphy and Leonard's argument that the family is a patriarchal economic system where men benefit from women's labour
- The dark side of the family: domestic abuse, neglect, power and control, and dysfunctional families
- General criticisms of the family: isolation, unrealistic idealisation, loss of traditional functions and lack of contact with wider kinship networks
- Difference feminist challenge: not all women experience family life in the same way; for some women, the family may also be a refuge from racism, class inequality or wider social hostility
- Comparison with Functionalism: Parsons sees the family as a warm bath, but conflict perspectives ask who pays the cost of family life

---

## Key concepts / vocabulary

- **Conflict perspective:** a sociological view that focuses on inequality, power and competing interests rather than harmony and consensus.
- **Capitalism:** an economic system based on private ownership, profit and wage labour.
- **Unit of consumption:** the family as a major consumer unit in capitalism, targeted by advertisers and encouraged to buy goods and services.
- **Unpaid domestic labour:** housework, childcare and caring work performed without wages, often by women.
- **Reproduction of labour power:** the way families raise, feed, care for and socialise the next generation of workers.
- **Private life:** the idea that the family is separate from work and the economy. Zaretsky argues this separation is partly an illusion.
- **Alienation:** under capitalism, workers may feel separated from control, creativity and satisfaction in their work. Zaretsky argues the family can cushion this alienation while still supporting capitalism.
- **Patriarchy:** a system of social organisation in which men hold dominant power over women.
- **Family hierarchy:** an unequal structure inside the family, with some members having more power, authority or benefit than others.
- **Domestic abuse:** physical, emotional, financial, coercive, controlling or sexual abuse within an intimate or family relationship.
- **Coercive control:** a pattern of threats, monitoring, isolation, humiliation or control that limits another person's freedom.
- **Dark side of the family:** harmful experiences within family life, such as abuse, neglect, control, violence or exploitation.
- **Difference feminism:** feminist strand arguing that women's experiences differ by race, class, sexuality, ethnicity, religion and age; not all families oppress all women in the same way.
- **Idealisation:** presenting the family as happier, safer or more positive than it may really be.
- **Wider kinship network:** relatives beyond the nuclear family, such as grandparents, aunts, uncles and cousins.

## Core named evidence

### Eli Zaretsky — Marxist criticism

Zaretsky argues that the family supports capitalism. He does not simply say the family is bad; he says the family helps capitalism continue while appearing to be a private escape from it.

Key points:

- The family reproduces the workforce by raising and caring for future workers.
- Women often provide unpaid domestic labour that keeps current workers healthy and ready for paid work.
- The family acts as a unit of consumption because families buy goods, services, housing, toys, technology and leisure products.
- The family cushions workers from alienation by offering emotional comfort after stressful work.
- This comfort can make capitalism easier to tolerate, so the system continues.
- The family can help pass on class advantage through inheritance, housing, education and contacts.

Best exam sentence:

> Zaretsky argues that the family appears to be a private safe haven, but actually supports capitalism by reproducing workers, relying on unpaid domestic labour and encouraging consumption.

---

### Christine Delphy and Diana Leonard — feminist criticism

Delphy and Leonard argue that the family is a patriarchal economic system. Their focus is not capitalism in general, but the way men benefit from women's work inside the family.

Key points:

- Men benefit from women's unpaid domestic labour.
- Women's housework, childcare and emotional work are often unpaid and undervalued.
- Even women in paid work may still do most of the domestic labour at home.
- The family can be hierarchical, with husbands/fathers having more power than wives and children.
- This means the family can reproduce patriarchy across generations.

Best exam sentence:

> Delphy and Leonard argue that the family is patriarchal because men benefit from women's unpaid domestic labour and hold more power within the family hierarchy.

## General criticisms of the family

Some criticisms are not only Marxist or Feminist. Sociologists also argue that the family can be criticised because of:

- **Isolation:** the nuclear family can be cut off from wider kinship networks, leaving members with less support.
- **Idealisation:** media, politics and everyday language can present family life as naturally happy and safe, hiding conflict or harm.
- **Loss of traditional functions:** the family may no longer perform all the functions it once did because education, welfare, healthcare and work are now carried out by other institutions.
- **Lack of wider kin contact:** smaller, privatised nuclear families may have less regular contact with extended relatives.
- **Dysfunctional families:** some families may not provide emotional support, protection or effective socialisation.

This links back to Episode 3: Functionalists focus on the functions the family performs, while conflict perspectives ask whether those functions benefit everyone equally.

---

## The dark side of the family

The family can be a place of care and support, but it can also be a place of control, fear or harm.

The dark side includes:

- domestic abuse
- coercive control
- neglect
- financial control
- emotional abuse
- unequal power
- child abuse
- isolation from friends, relatives or services

Handle this content factually and sensitively. The key sociological point is that the family should not be automatically idealised as safe for everyone.

Domestic abuse statistics are difficult to interpret because of the dark figure of crime. Some incidents are not reported due to fear, shame, dependence, lack of trust in authorities or normalisation of abuse.

---

## Difference feminist challenge

Difference feminists argue that some feminist theories overgeneralise women's experiences. Family life is not experienced in the same way by all women.

For example:

- A Black woman may experience the family as a refuge from racism in wider society.
- A working-class woman may experience economic pressure differently from a middle-class woman.
- A lesbian woman, disabled woman or migrant woman may face different family expectations and pressures.

This does not mean feminist criticism is wrong. It means strong evaluation should avoid saying all women experience the family in exactly the same way.

---

## Perspective comparison

- **Functionalists:** the family helps society by socialising children and stabilising adults.
- **Marxists:** the family helps capitalism by reproducing workers, absorbing alienation and encouraging consumption.
- **Feminists:** the family can maintain patriarchy by benefiting men and relying on women's unpaid work.
- **Difference feminists:** family experiences vary by race, class, sexuality, ethnicity and other social factors.

## Likely misconceptions

- MISCONCEPTION: "Zaretsky says the family is bad for capitalism." Correction: Zaretsky says the family is useful to capitalism because it reproduces labour power, encourages consumption and absorbs workers' frustration.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Marxists and feminists criticise the family for the same reason." Correction: Marxists focus on capitalism and class inequality; feminists focus on patriarchy and gender inequality.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Delphy and Leonard only criticise housework." Correction: they criticise the family as a wider patriarchal hierarchy where men benefit from women's labour and power is unequal.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Difference feminism means families are not oppressive." Correction: difference feminism says experiences vary; some families can be oppressive, while some may also provide refuge or support.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Domestic abuse is rare if it is not visible." Correction: abuse may be hidden and underreported because of fear, shame, dependence or coercive control.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Functionalists and conflict theorists are just arguing about whether families are good or bad." Correction: they are asking different questions: Functionalists ask what functions the family performs; conflict theorists ask who benefits and who may be harmed.

## Methods link

Victim surveys, such as the Crime Survey for England and Wales, can help researchers study domestic abuse because they may capture incidents that never reach police statistics.

Strengths:

- Anonymous surveys can encourage more honest reporting.
- They can reveal the dark figure of crime.
- They allow researchers to compare patterns across groups and over time.

Limitations:

- Domestic abuse may still be underreported because of fear, shame, trauma, dependence or coercive control.
- Survey wording affects what respondents recognise as abuse.
- Official categories may miss emotional, financial or coercive forms of control.
- Quantitative statistics show patterns, but interviews may better reveal lived experience and meaning.
