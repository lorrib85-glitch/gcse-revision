## Key concepts / vocabulary

- **AO1:** knowledge and understanding — accurate sociological knowledge, named sociologists, perspectives and specialist concepts.
- **AO2:** application — using knowledge to answer the exact question asked, not a vaguely related question.
- **AO3:** analysis and evaluation — explaining strengths, limits, contrasts, consequences and how far you agree.
- **"How far":** requires a balanced judgement, not a simple yes/no answer.
- **Item/question wording:** the exact words in the exam question that tell you what to focus on.
- **Line of argument:** the overall answer direction that links every paragraph back to the question.
- **Claim:** the main point of a paragraph.
- **Evidence:** named sociologist, concept, study, trend or example used to support the claim.
- **Core exam name:** a researcher students should be able to use confidently in a GCSE answer.
- **Supporting name:** useful evidence if confirmed by teacher/course material, but not essential.
- **Optional enrichment:** helpful for depth, but not required and should not replace core evidence.
- **Challenge:** a counterpoint, limitation or alternative perspective.
- **Judgement:** a clear decision about how far the evidence supports the question.
- **Specialist terminology:** precise sociology vocabulary, such as patriarchy, secularisation, socialisation, cohabitation or stratified diffusion.
- **Balanced answer:** an answer that uses more than one side of the debate and reaches a reasoned conclusion.

---

## The 12-mark answer in plain English

A 12-mark answer is not asking for everything you know.

It is asking you to do three things:

1. **Know it** — accurate sociology: AO1.
2. **Use it** — apply it to this exact question: AO2.
3. **Judge it** — compare, challenge and decide how far: AO3.

Memory trick: **K-U-J** — Know it, Use it, Judge it.

---

## How to decode the question

Before writing, students should do a 30-second decode:

1. **Circle the topic:** family diversity, divorce, conjugal roles, childhood, Functionalism, capitalism, patriarchy, etc.
2. **Underline the command:** usually "Discuss how far sociologists would agree..."
3. **Spot the argument hidden in the wording:** is it saying law is the main reason, families are symmetrical, marriage is still important, or family serves capitalism?
4. **Choose two sides:** one side that supports the statement and one side that challenges it.
5. **Choose named evidence:** two or three secure sociologists used deeply are better than six names listed briefly.

Example:

> Discuss how far sociologists would agree that the main function of the family is to serve capitalism.

Decode:

- Topic: functions of the family / capitalism
- Side 1: Marxists such as Zaretsky agree
- Side 2: Functionalists such as Murdock and Parsons disagree
- Possible evaluation: feminists argue the family serves patriarchy, not just capitalism

## Best structure for a 12-mark answer

### Short introduction

Define the key term and set up the debate.

Example:

> Sociologists disagree about whether the family mainly benefits society, capitalism or men. Marxists argue the family serves capitalism, but Functionalists argue it performs positive functions for society.

### Paragraph 1 — support the statement

Use **claim → evidence → explain → link**.

Example:

> Marxists would agree that the family serves capitalism. Zaretsky argues that the family reproduces labour power because women often provide unpaid domestic labour that keeps workers fed, rested and ready for paid work. This supports capitalism because employers benefit from workers being maintained at home without paying for that work. Therefore, Zaretsky supports the view that one function of the family is to serve capitalism.

### Paragraph 2 — challenge the statement

Use a different perspective or limitation.

Example:

> However, Functionalists would disagree. Murdock argues that the family performs four essential functions: sexual, reproductive, economic and educational. This suggests the family benefits society as a whole, not just capitalism. Parsons also argues that the family provides primary socialisation and stabilisation of adult personalities. This challenges the Marxist view because it presents the family as a source of stability rather than exploitation.

### Paragraph 3 — further evaluation

Add a sharper alternative or qualification.

Example:

> Feminists would also challenge the idea that the family mainly serves capitalism. Delphy and Leonard argue that the family is a patriarchal economic system where men benefit from women’s unpaid labour. This suggests the family may serve men more directly than capitalism. However, Marxist feminists could argue that patriarchy and capitalism work together because unpaid domestic labour benefits both men and employers.

### Conclusion — answer "how far"

Make a supported judgement.

Example:

> Overall, sociologists would agree to some extent that the family serves capitalism because Zaretsky shows how unpaid labour and consumption support the capitalist system. However, this is not the only function of the family. Functionalists see the family as beneficial for society, while feminists argue it also maintains patriarchy. Therefore, the statement is partly convincing, but too narrow on its own.

## Paragraph formula

Use this for each main paragraph:

1. **Point:** one clear answer to the question.
2. **Sociologist / concept:** named evidence or secure concept.
3. **Explain:** how the evidence proves the point.
4. **Challenge / link:** limitation, opposing view or explicit link back to the question.

Fast memory trick: **P-S-E-C** — Point, Sociologist, Explain, Challenge.

## Evidence bank — families and households

Use this as a revision bank. Students do not need every name in every answer. They need the best names for the question.

### Core exam names

These are the safest names to build around.

#### Family functions and Functionalism

- **Murdock:** the nuclear family performs four functions: sexual, reproductive, economic and educational/socialisation.
- **Parsons:** the family performs primary socialisation and stabilisation of adult personalities; instrumental and expressive roles.

#### Family diversity

- **Rapoport and Rapoport:** five types of family diversity — organisational, cultural, social class, life-course and cohort.
- **Oakley:** the conventional family is an ideology that presents one family form as normal.

#### Conjugal roles and equality

- **Bott:** segregated and joint conjugal roles.
- **Willmott and Young:** symmetrical family; family life becoming more home-centred and less segregated; principle of stratified diffusion.
- **Oakley:** criticises symmetry because women still do most domestic labour.
- **Jan Pahl:** money management can reveal power and financial control.

#### Marxist and Feminist criticism

- **Zaretsky:** family supports capitalism through unpaid domestic labour, reproduction of workers, consumption and cushioning alienation.
- **Delphy and Leonard:** family is a patriarchal economic system where men benefit from women’s unpaid labour.

#### Parent-child and wider family relationships

- **Young and Willmott:** parent-child relationships became less authoritarian; Bethnal Green showed strong extended-family ties.

#### Marriage, cohabitation and divorce

- **Giddens:** pure relationship — relationships continue only while both partners find them rewarding.
- **Murray / New Right:** divorce, lone-parent families and welfare dependency weaken the nuclear family.

### Useful supporting names

Use these only where they are confirmed by teacher/course material or where the app labels them as support rather than core.

- **Scott and Clery:** attitudes to gender roles may have changed faster than actual behaviour.
- **Prior and Trinder:** children, especially in some middle-class families, may be involved in decision-making.
- **Scott:** children are not simply dependent; some children contribute work or support.
- **Charles et al.:** wider kinship networks can still matter, especially mothers, daughters and grandparents.

### Optional enrichment

Use these sparingly. They can add nuance, but students should not feel they must memorise them.

- **Gatrell:** fathers may be more involved than previous generations, but this does not prove full equality in housework, childcare or emotional labour.
- **Aries:** childhood can be understood as a social construct that changes over time.

## Question bank and best evidence choices

### Family functions / capitalism

Question style:

> Discuss how far sociologists would agree that the main function of the family is to serve capitalism.

Best evidence:

- Zaretsky agrees: family serves capitalism.
- Murdock / Parsons disagree: family performs functions for society.
- Delphy and Leonard add: family serves patriarchy.

### Symmetrical family / gender equality

Question style:

> Discuss how far sociologists would agree that families in Britain today are symmetrical.

Best evidence:

- Willmott and Young agree: roles are more similar and home-centred.
- Oakley disagrees: equality is overstated.
- Pahl adds secure evidence that money management can still reveal male power.
- Optional supporting evidence: Scott and Clery / Gatrell only if confirmed or clearly labelled as extension.

### Marriage and divorce

Question style:

> Discuss how far sociologists would agree that marriage is still important in Britain today.

Best evidence:

- Functionalists / New Right: marriage and nuclear family stability remain important.
- Giddens: relationships are now based more on satisfaction and choice.
- Feminists: divorce may liberate women from patriarchal relationships.

### Family diversity

Question style:

> Discuss how far sociologists would agree that family diversity exists in Britain today.

Best evidence:

- Rapoport and Rapoport: five types of diversity.
- Oakley: conventional family ideology still shapes what people see as normal.
- New Right / Functionalists may argue the nuclear family remains important.

### Childhood and parent-child relationships

Question style:

> Discuss how far sociologists would agree that childhood and parent-child relationships have improved over time.

Best evidence:

- March of Progress: children have more rights, protection and education.
- Young and Willmott: less authoritarian parent-child relationships.
- Conceptual challenge: childhood remains unequal and some children still contribute work/support.
- Conflict view: class, ethnicity, gender and poverty shape childhood.
- Optional supporting names: Prior and Trinder / Scott / Charles et al. only if confirmed.

## Weak answer vs upgraded answer

### Weak answer

> Zaretsky says the family serves capitalism. Murdock says the family has functions. Feminists disagree. Overall, some sociologists agree and some disagree.

Why this is weak:

- It names sociologists but does not explain their arguments.
- It barely applies to the question.
- It has no developed evaluation.
- The conclusion is vague.

### Upgraded answer

> Zaretsky would agree that the family serves capitalism because he argues that unpaid domestic labour reproduces labour power. For example, families feed, care for and socialise future workers, while adults are emotionally supported after paid work. This benefits capitalism because employers do not have to pay directly for this domestic labour. However, Functionalists such as Murdock would argue that the family performs essential functions for society as a whole, including reproduction and socialisation. This challenges Zaretsky because it suggests the family is not only useful to capitalism but also helps maintain social order.

Why this is stronger:

- It explains the sociologist’s argument.
- It applies directly to the question.
- It includes a counter-perspective.
- It makes a clear comparison.

## Common errors to fix

- **Name-dropping:** naming six sociologists without explaining any of them.
- **One-sided answer:** only agreeing or only disagreeing.
- **No judgement:** ending with "some agree and some disagree" without deciding how far.
- **Generic family essay:** writing everything you know about families without answering the exact question.
- **Everyday language:** saying "families are good" instead of using specialist terms such as socialisation, patriarchy, capitalism, secularisation or family diversity.
- **Fake evaluation:** saying "this is good evidence" without explaining why it strengthens or weakens the argument.
- **Missing comparison:** not showing how perspectives differ.
- **Uncertain name overload:** using optional names as if they are core evidence, which makes the answer harder to remember and less secure.

---

## 12-mark success checklist

Before finishing, check:

- Have I answered the exact wording of the question?
- Have I used at least two secure sociologists or perspectives accurately?
- Have I explained the evidence rather than just naming it?
- Have I included a challenge or counter-perspective?
- Have I used specialist sociology terms?
- Have I made a clear "how far" judgement?
- Have I avoided irrelevant knowledge dumping?
- Have I avoided using optional enrichment names as if they are compulsory?

---

## Likely misconceptions

- MISCONCEPTION: "A longer answer is always better." Correction: A focused answer with accurate knowledge, application and evaluation beats a long answer with weak relevance.
- MISCONCEPTION: "You need to cover every sociologist." Correction: depth beats breadth — two well-developed sociologists with evaluation outperform six names with no explanation.
- MISCONCEPTION: "AO3 just means saying a criticism." Correction: AO3 means evaluating how strong an argument is, comparing perspectives and reaching a supported judgement.
- MISCONCEPTION: "The conclusion can just repeat your introduction." Correction: the conclusion must use "how far" language and make a judgement based on the evidence in the answer.
- MISCONCEPTION: "A 12-marker needs a big introduction." Correction: the introduction should be short. Most marks come from developed paragraphs and judgement.
- MISCONCEPTION: "More named researchers always means a higher mark." Correction: accurate explanation and evaluation of secure evidence is better than a crowded answer full of barely explained names.

## Methods link

This is mainly an exam technique episode. However, methods still matter because strong 12-mark answers often evaluate evidence.

Students should be able to say, for example:

- official statistics show patterns in divorce but not relationship quality
- surveys can collect large-scale data but may rely on self-report
- interviews can reveal lived experience but may not be representative
- victim surveys may reveal the dark figure of crime but still undercount sensitive issues
- historical evidence can show change over time but may be interpreted differently
