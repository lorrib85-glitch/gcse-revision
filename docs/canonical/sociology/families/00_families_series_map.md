# Families and households — Series map

## Series identity

- **Subject:** Sociology
- **Exam board / spec:** AQA GCSE Sociology (8192)
- **Assessment:** Paper 1 (8192/1) — Section A: Families
- **Marks / time:** 50 marks (Section A) / Paper 1 is 1 hour 45 minutes total
- **12-mark question format:** "Discuss how far sociologists would agree that…" — two questions per paper, 12 marks each (AO1: 4, AO2: 4, AO3: 4)
- **Episode count:** 10 episodes
- **Series central hook:** Who gets to decide what a "normal" family is?

---

## Series throughline

The family looks natural. Sociology asks: who benefits when we call one version of family "normal"? Every perspective on the family — functionalist, Marxist, feminist, New Right — reflects a different answer to this question. The module builds from definitions and diversity toward competing theoretical arguments, so that by Episode 10 students can construct a coherent 12-mark argument using named sociologists and evaluation.

**The question every episode must answer:** How does this sociological idea help us understand who the family serves, and at what cost to whom?

---

## Module architecture (shared)

Every episode follows this 7-stage rhythm. Stage mapping for each episode is in its `_Architecture.md` file.

### Stage 1 — Hook & prior recall
**Purpose:** Surface existing knowledge, create curiosity, prime retrieval. A real-life question, dilemma or scenario the student already has an intuition about.
- Typical components: `ChapterHookScreen`, `PriorKnowledgeRecall`, `MisconceptionCheck`

### Stage 2 — Core concept decode
**Purpose:** Precise definition of the episode's key concepts. One concept at a time. No theory yet — just what the words mean.
- Typical components: `ConceptReveal`, `VisualLearning`, `FillInTheBlanksBlock`

### Stage 3 — Sociologist evidence
**Purpose:** Introduce the named researcher(s) for this episode: perspective, key claim, one concrete finding or quote. Students must be able to write "X argues that…" correctly.
- Typical components: `KeyFigureReveal`, `TheoryCompareBlock`, `MatchingTask`

### Stage 4 — Challenge & perspectives
**Purpose:** The opposing view, the limitation, or the competing perspective. Every theory taught here must be challenged. Students should leave knowing the main evaluation point.
- Typical components: `MisconceptionCheck`, `ColSortBlock`, `SwipeSort`, `TheoryCompareBlock`

### Stage 5 — Apply & scenario
**Purpose:** Apply the concepts and theories to a realistic scenario. Card sorts, item questions, decision tasks. Methods link where relevant.
- Typical components: `ColSortBlock`, `InteractiveCollectionExplorer`, `SwipeSort`, `MatchingTask`

### Stage 6 — Exam build
**Purpose:** At least one AQA-style practice moment: 3-mark describe, 4-mark identify-and-explain, or a 12-mark paragraph builder. Feed `FaceTheExaminer` and `GuidedAnswerCoach`.
- Typical components: `FaceTheExaminer`, `GuidedAnswerCoach`, `GuidedExamResponse`, `ExaminerExplainsScreen`

### Stage 7 — Quick fire retrieval
**Purpose:** Five retrieval questions drawing on this episode and at least one earlier episode. Every episode's retrieval feeds the next episode's hook.
- Typical components: `QuickRecallScreen`, `RecoveryQuizPlayer`

## 10-point Sociology Module Completion Test

Every episode, before marking complete, must pass all 10 checks:

- [ ] Students can define every key concept from this episode accurately and in their own words
- [ ] Where named research is included, students can name the relevant sociologist(s), state their perspective, and write one accurate "X argues that…" sentence
- [ ] Where an episode is concept-only, students can accurately apply definitions to examples without forcing named research
- [ ] Where theory is taught, students can state the main evaluation point / limitation
- [ ] Students have applied at least one concept to a scenario or item-style question
- [ ] Students have practised at least one AQA exam question format (3-mark, 4-mark, or 12-mark)
- [ ] Students have retrieved content from at least one earlier episode
- [ ] The episode's core takeaway is explicitly connected to the series throughline (who benefits from the "normal" family?)
- [ ] Where the episode is perspective-led, at least two contrasting perspectives are represented; where it is concept-led, the episode clearly builds foundation knowledge for later perspectives
- [ ] A methods link is present where the episode includes named research

### Shorter question types (from past papers)

- 1-mark: Define a term (e.g. "What term describes a family in which a man holds dominant power?" — Answer: patriarchal family)
- 2-mark: From Item A, examine one strength/weakness of the data
- 3-mark: Describe one example of primary socialisation / describe one family type
- 4-mark: Identify and explain one way conjugal role relationships may be segregated (JUN24, Q4); identify and explain one disadvantage of using a case study (JUN24, Q9)
