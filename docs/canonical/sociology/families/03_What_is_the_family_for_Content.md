## Key concepts / vocabulary

- **Functionalism:** society works as a system where each part, including the family, performs essential functions that help society remain stable.
- **Primary socialisation:** learning society's basic norms and values in early childhood, usually within the family.
- **Agency of socialisation:** a social group or institution responsible for carrying out socialisation, such as the family, school, peer group, religion or media.
- **Stabilisation of adult personalities:** the family provides emotional support and stability for adults. Parsons explains this through the "warm bath" theory.
- **Instrumental role:** the rational, breadwinner and disciplinarian role, typically associated by Parsons with men.
- **Expressive role:** the emotional, caring and domestic role, typically associated by Parsons with women.
- **Universal:** found in every known society.
- **Inevitable:** something that is bound to happen or difficult to avoid.
- **Idealisation:** presenting the family as better, happier or more positive than it may really be.
- **Dysfunctional family:** a family where important functions, such as emotional support or protection, are not being carried out.

## Named evidence

- **George Murdock** (functionalist): argues that the nuclear family performs four key functions:
  - **Sexual function:** the family regulates sexual behaviour through socially approved relationships.
  - **Reproductive function:** the family produces and raises the next generation.
  - **Economic function:** the family provides food, shelter, money and practical support.
  - **Educational/socialisation function:** the family teaches children society's culture, norms and values.
  - Murdock argues that the nuclear family is universal because it exists in every known society. He believes no society has found a better structure for performing these four functions, so the nuclear family is inevitable. Source: *Social Structure*, 1949.
- **Talcott Parsons** (functionalist): argues that the family performs two essential functions: primary socialisation of children and stabilisation of adult personalities.
  - Parsons argues that the family has lost some of its traditional functions to other institutions. For example, schools now provide education, workplaces provide jobs and income, and shops provide goods.
  - However, he argues the family still performs two essential functions: primary socialisation and stabilisation of adult personalities.
  - Parsons also distinguishes between instrumental and expressive roles. Source: 'The social structure of the family', *The Family: Its Functions and Destiny*, 1959.

---

## Evaluation of Functionalism

Critics argue Parsons gives an idealised picture of family life. His view is based mainly on middle-class nuclear families and ignores social class diversity, ethnic diversity and alternatives to the nuclear family such as communes and kibbutzim.

Functionalism can also ignore conflict inside families. Feminists argue that it overlooks domestic violence, child abuse, women's unpaid domestic work and unequal power between men and women.

Another criticism is that Functionalism can be outdated because many women now work outside the home and many families do not follow traditional instrumental and expressive roles.

---

## Critics disagree — preview only

Functionalists see the family as positive because it helps society remain stable. However, critics argue this view is too idealised.

- Marxists argue the family may serve capitalism rather than everyone equally.
- Feminists argue the family may benefit men more than women because women often do unpaid domestic and emotional work.

## Likely misconceptions

- MISCONCEPTION: "Parsons and Murdock say the same thing." Correction: Murdock identifies four functions; Parsons narrows the family down to two essential functions and adds the role distinction.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Functionalists think all families are happy." Correction: Functionalists focus on what the family does for society, but critics argue this idealises family life.
- MISCONCEPTION: "The warm bath is a real bath." Correction: it is a metaphor for emotional support and relief from stress.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Only nuclear families can socialise children." Correction: Functionalists often focus on the nuclear family, but critics argue other family forms can also socialise children.

## Methods link

Murdock's claim that the nuclear family is universal relies on broad comparison across societies. Students should question whether evidence from many societies proves the nuclear family is inevitable, or whether it overlooks family diversity.

Parsons' work is theoretical and has been criticised for presenting an idealised, middle-class picture of family life. This links to validity: does the theory accurately reflect real family experiences?
