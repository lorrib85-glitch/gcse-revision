# Episode 5: Who does the work at home? — Content

## Series: Families and households (AQA 8192, Paper 1 Section A)

## Big question

If both people live there, why is the work still not equal?

---

## Syllabus coverage

- Conjugal roles: domestic roles of married or cohabiting partners
- Conjugal relationships: the relationship between married or cohabiting partners
- Segregated conjugal roles: separate male/female tasks, separate leisure and separate social networks
- Joint/integrated conjugal roles: shared domestic tasks, shared leisure and more joint social life
- Domestic division of labour: the division of housework, childcare, DIY, decision-making, money management and leisure within the home

- Feminist criticism: women often continue to do most domestic labour even when they also work outside the home
- Double shift/triple shift as shorthand for paid work plus unpaid domestic and emotional work

## Key concepts / vocabulary

- **Conjugal roles:** domestic roles within a married or cohabiting partnership.
- **Conjugal relationships:** the relationship between married or cohabiting partners.
- **Domestic division of labour:** the division of household tasks such as cooking, cleaning, laundry, childcare, DIY and gardening between partners.
- **Segregated conjugal roles:** domestic roles that are separated into male and female tasks and are divided unequally.
- **Joint/integrated conjugal roles:** domestic roles that are shared more equally between partners, with more shared leisure and social life.
- **Instrumental role:** the breadwinner and disciplinarian role in the family, associated by Parsons with men.
- **Expressive role:** the caring and nurturing role in the family, associated by Parsons with women.
- **Decision-making:** who has power to make choices in the family, including choices about spending, children and daily routines.
- **Money management:** how household income is controlled, accessed and spent.
- **Childrearing:** bringing up children.
- **Leisure activities:** how partners spend free time, either separately or together.
- **Dual-worker family:** both adult partners are in paid employment.
- **Dual-career family:** both adult partners have careers, not just paid jobs.
- **Male-breadwinner household:** the adult male earns most or all of the household income.
- **Double shift:** women doing paid work and then a second shift of unpaid housework and childcare.
- **Triple shift:** paid work, unpaid domestic labour and emotional labour.

## Segregated and joint roles

### Segregated conjugal roles

In segregated conjugal roles, tasks are clearly separated into male and female work.

- Women are expected to take main responsibility for housework and childcare.
- Men are expected to be breadwinners and carry out tasks such as DIY or discipline.
- Partners may spend leisure time separately and have separate friends or interests.
- This links to Parsons' expressive role for women and instrumental role for men.

### Joint or integrated conjugal roles

In joint or integrated conjugal roles, partners share more domestic tasks and leisure.

- Household tasks are less rigidly divided into male and female jobs.
- Partners may share childcare, cooking, cleaning, decision-making and leisure time.
- Joint roles do not always mean complete equality. One partner may still do more of the routine or boring work.

---

## Conjugal roles in the past

In the early twentieth century, conjugal roles were often segregated. Married women were expected to take the main responsibility for housework and childcare, while husbands were expected to be the main breadwinners.

Social class affected how this worked:

- Many working-class women also had to do paid work or take in work from home, such as laundry, to help the family survive.
- Middle-class women were less likely to be expected to take paid work, but might supervise household employees such as maids or nannies.
- Despite these class differences, families were often male-dominated and men were expected to provide for the family.

## Contemporary issues in conjugal roles

Conjugal roles are not just about chores. Sociologists also study:

- **Decision-making:** who decides how money is spent, where the family lives, or how children are raised.
- **Money management:** whether income is shared equally or controlled by one partner.
- **Dual-worker and dual-career families:** whether both partners working changes the division of domestic labour.
- **Childrearing:** who takes responsibility for everyday childcare, school contact, illness and routines.
- **Leisure:** whether partners spend free time together or separately.

These issues matter because they show whether relationships are becoming more equal or whether traditional gender expectations continue beneath the surface.

## Named evidence

- **Elizabeth Bott** (feminist, 1957): distinguished between segregated and joint conjugal roles. Segregated roles involve separate tasks and separate leisure, while joint roles involve more shared tasks and shared leisure. Source: *Family and Social Network*, 1957.
- **Talcott Parsons** (functionalist): argued that men and women perform different but complementary family roles: the male instrumental role and the female expressive role. This links back to Episode 3.
- **Willmott and Young** (1973): argued that conjugal roles were becoming more equal, with more shared decision-making and more home-centred family life. See Episode 6 for the full symmetrical family argument.
- **Ann Oakley** (feminist): criticised the idea that conjugal roles had become equal. She argued that women still carried most responsibility for domestic labour even when they were also in paid employment. Source: *The Sociology of Housework*, 1974.
- **Jan Pahl** (1989): interviewed 102 married couples in Kent and found that men were still more likely to dominate financial decision-making. Some wives had little access to money, showing that money management can reveal power within families. Source: *Money and Marriage*, 1989.
- **Scott and Clery** (2013): argued that although attitudes to gender roles have changed, women still usually do much of the routine domestic labour, such as laundry. Gender inequalities at home can make equality at work harder to achieve. Source: *British Social Attitudes*, 2013.
- **Caroline Gatrell:** studied heterosexual couples in full-time employment and found that fathers were doing more than their own fathers had done. However, women still reported that men often avoided routine tasks such as ironing, and that sharing parenting should also mean sharing housework.

---

## Perspective comparison

- **Functionalist view:** different roles can be seen as complementary. Parsons argues instrumental and expressive roles help the family function.
- **Feminist view:** different roles often mean unequal power. Feminists argue women are expected to do unpaid domestic labour, childcare and emotional work.
- **Change view:** some sociologists, such as Willmott and Young, argue roles have become more joint, but feminists such as Oakley argue this change is overstated.

## Likely misconceptions

- MISCONCEPTION: "Conjugal roles only means chores." Correction: conjugal roles also include childcare, decision-making, money management and leisure.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Joint conjugal roles mean completely equal roles." Correction: joint roles mean tasks and leisure are shared more than in segregated roles, but this does not always mean full equality.
- MISCONCEPTION: "If both partners work, the housework must be equal." Correction: feminists argue women often still do more domestic labour even in dual-worker families.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Money management is just practical." Correction: sociologists such as Jan Pahl show that control over money can reveal power in relationships.
- MISCONCEPTION: "The double shift and triple shift are the same." Correction: double shift means paid work plus domestic labour; triple shift also includes emotional labour.

## Methods link

Researchers can study conjugal roles using questionnaires, interviews or time-use diaries.

A questionnaire on household labour should operationalise domestic labour clearly by asking about specific tasks such as cooking, cleaning, laundry, childcare, DIY, shopping, school contact, illness care, money decisions and leisure time.

- **Strength:** questionnaires can collect comparable data from many households.
- **Weakness:** people may give socially desirable answers and overstate how equal their relationship is.
- **Interview strength:** interviews can reveal how people feel about fairness, power and money.
- **Interview weakness:** partners may not speak honestly if the topic feels sensitive.
- **Ethics:** questions about money, conflict or unequal power should be handled sensitively.
