# Episode 4: One shape does not fit all — Content

## Series: Families and households (AQA 8192, Paper 1 Section A)

## Big question

Are families becoming more diverse — or were they always diverse?

---

## Key concepts / vocabulary

- **Family diversity:** variation in family forms in contemporary society.
- **Organisational diversity:** families differ in how they are organised, including domestic division of labour, social networks and family structure.
- **Cultural diversity:** families differ because of cultural values, ethnicity, religion and beliefs. These differences can affect gender roles, childrearing, education and work.
- **Social class diversity:** families differ because class affects resources, lifestyles, childrearing and attitudes to gender roles.
- **Life-course diversity:** families differ depending on the stage of life they are at, such as newlyweds without children, families with young children, empty nest families or older couples.
- **Cohort diversity:** families differ depending on the historical period they live through. For example, younger generations may experience divorce or cohabitation differently because attitudes have changed.
- **Migration:** movement of people from one country to another; contributes to cultural diversity in family structure.
- **Census:** a survey taken every 10 years of the population, useful for identifying patterns in households and family forms.
- **Social stigma:** shame or disapproval attached to something by society.
- **Lifestyle:** the way people live, including their leisure, work patterns, beliefs and family arrangements.
- **Childrearing:** bringing up children.
- **Beanpole family:** a multigenerational family with few members in each generation.
- **Multigenerational extended family:** a family household containing more than two generations, such as children, parents and grandparents.

## Additional drivers of diversity

These terms are useful for explaining why family structures change, but they should not dominate this episode.

- **Birth rate:** number of live births per 1,000 of the population per year. Long-term decline can contribute to smaller families and beanpole families.
- **Death rate:** number of deaths per 1,000 of the population per year. Falling death rates contribute to longer life expectancy.
- **Ageing population:** average age of the population is rising due to falling death rates and declining birth rates.
- **Life expectancy:** how long on average a person born in a given year can expect to live.
- **Globalisation:** increasing interconnectedness of the world; affects migration, family structure and family policy.
- **Dependency ratio:** measure of dependants aged 0–14 and over 65 relative to the working-age population aged 15–64.

## Named evidence

- **Rapoport and Rapoport** (1982): five types of family diversity. Source: 'British families in transition' in *Families in Britain*, Routledge, 1982.
  - Rapoport and Rapoport did not carry out their own primary research for this work. They analysed existing secondary sources of research and argued that family life had moved away from one single family norm.
  - They argued that in the past there was one dominant norm of family life, often based on husband, wife and children. They argued that people now have more freedom to choose different family forms that reflect their needs and wishes.
  - Their work predates the legal recognition of same-sex relationships, so same-sex families are a useful later example of increasing family diversity.
- **Census data:** 10% of Bangladeshi and Pakistani households contained multigenerational extended families compared with 2% of White British households in the 2001 Census.
- **Beanpole family emergence:** linked to declining birth rates and an ageing population.

## Rapoport and Rapoport's five types of family diversity

- **Organisational diversity:** families differ in how they are organised, including domestic division of labour, social networks and family structure. Examples include conventional nuclear families, dual-worker families, lone-parent families and reconstituted families.
- **Cultural diversity:** families differ because of cultural values, ethnicity, religion and beliefs. These differences can affect gender roles, childrearing, education and work.
- **Social class diversity:** families differ because class affects resources, lifestyles, childrearing and attitudes to gender roles.
- **Life-course diversity:** families differ depending on the stage of life they are at, such as newlyweds without children, families with young children, empty nest families or older couples.
- **Cohort diversity:** families differ depending on the historical period they live through. For example, younger generations may experience divorce or cohabitation differently because attitudes have changed.

## UK cultural diversity examples

- Migration has contributed to family diversity in Britain, including migration from Ireland, former colonies such as India and Pakistan, and parts of the Caribbean.
- Some African-Caribbean mothers are more likely to continue working after having children, while some Asian family patterns have traditionally placed more emphasis on mothers staying at home, although these patterns are changing among second-generation migrants.
- Census data shows multigenerational extended families are more common in some ethnic groups. In the 2001 Census, 10% of Bangladeshi and Pakistani households contained a multigenerational extended family, compared with 2% of White British households.
- Sociologists warn against overgeneralising about "Asian families" because there are major differences by religion, ethnicity, generation and social class.

---

## Same-sex families and increasing diversity

Same-sex families have become more visible and socially accepted because of changes in social attitudes, legal changes such as civil partnerships and same-sex marriage, and developments in reproductive technology such as IVF and surrogacy.

This matters because it shows that family diversity has continued to increase since Rapoport and Rapoport wrote in the 1980s.

---

## Global family forms

- **Communes:** groups of people who share accommodation, possessions, wealth and property. Some communes are based on shared political or environmental beliefs.
- **Kibbutzim:** communal settlements in Israel based on cooperation and equality. Traditionally, children were raised together in children's houses, although this has changed in many kibbutzim.
- **China's one-child policy:** a government policy introduced in 1979 to control population growth. It shows that family life can be shaped by social policy, culture and economic needs, not just individual choice.

## Likely misconceptions

- MISCONCEPTION: "Family diversity is a new thing." Correction: Rapoport and Rapoport's five types show that diversity existed before recent legal and social changes; cohort diversity captures historical difference.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Cultural diversity just means ethnicity." Correction: cultural diversity includes religion, values and beliefs — not only ethnicity.
- MISCONCEPTION: "The beanpole family is the same as a classic extended family." Correction: beanpole families are multigenerational but have fewer members per generation than a classic extended family.
- MISCONCEPTION: "All families in the same ethnic group are the same." Correction: families also differ by religion, social class, generation, migration history and individual choice.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Family diversity means the conventional family has disappeared." Correction: the conventional family may no longer be the only family form, but it can still be a powerful social ideal.

## Methods link

Rapoport and Rapoport used secondary sources rather than carrying out their own primary research. This is useful because it allowed them to draw on a wider range of evidence, but it also means they depended on the quality, definitions and limits of existing research.

Census statistics are useful for studying family diversity because they are large-scale and can show patterns across the population. However, they are only a snapshot in time, definitions can change, and they may not capture complex arrangements such as living-apart-together relationships or modified extended families.
