## Chapter purpose

This chapter builds on Chapter 4. Chapter 4 introduced the digestive system and the idea that digestion breaks large insoluble molecules into small soluble molecules.

Chapter 5 explains how digestion actually happens: enzymes, bile and the required practicals.

## Hook

Your body does not digest food by crushing it harder and harder.

It uses tiny biological tools called enzymes.

Each enzyme has a very specific shape, like a lock waiting for one matching key. Change the shape, and the whole system stops working.

That is why temperature and pH matter so much.

## Core content

### 1. What enzymes are

An enzyme is a biological catalyst.

A catalyst speeds up a chemical reaction without being used up.

Most enzymes are proteins.

Exam wording:

> Enzymes are biological catalysts that speed up chemical reactions without being used up.

### 2. Lock-and-key model

Enzymes have an active site.

The substrate is the molecule the enzyme acts on.

The substrate fits into the active site because the shapes are complementary.

Then the reaction happens and products are released.

Key terms:

- **enzyme**: biological catalyst
- **active site**: part of the enzyme where the substrate binds
- **substrate**: molecule the enzyme acts on
- **product**: molecule made by the reaction

Exam-safe explanation:

> The substrate fits into the enzyme active site because the shapes are complementary. The enzyme catalyses the reaction and products are released. The enzyme is unchanged and can be used again.

Common trap:

Do not say the enzyme is used up. Enzymes can be reused.

### 3. Temperature and enzymes

At low temperatures:

- particles have less kinetic energy
- fewer successful collisions happen
- enzyme activity is slower

As temperature increases:

- particles move faster
- enzyme and substrate collide more often
- rate of reaction increases

Above the optimum temperature:

- bonds in the enzyme break
- the active site changes shape
- the substrate no longer fits
- the enzyme is denatured
- rate of reaction decreases sharply

Important wording:

Enzymes are **denatured**, not killed.

### 4. pH and enzymes

Different enzymes work best at different pH values.

If pH is too high or too low:

- the active site changes shape
- the substrate no longer fits
- the enzyme is denatured
- rate of reaction decreases

Examples:

- stomach protease works best in acidic conditions
- enzymes from the pancreas and small intestine work best in alkaline conditions

### 5. Digestive enzymes

Students need these three groups:

| Enzyme | Where released | What it digests | Product |
|---|---|---|---|
| Amylase | salivary glands, pancreas, small intestine | starch/carbohydrate | sugars |
| Protease | stomach, pancreas, small intestine | proteins | amino acids |
| Lipase | pancreas, small intestine | lipids/fats | fatty acids and glycerol |

AQA-safe wording:

- carbohydrases break carbohydrates into simple sugars
- amylase breaks starch into sugars
- proteases break proteins into amino acids
- lipases break lipids into fatty acids and glycerol

### 6. Uses of digestion products

The products of digestion are absorbed into the blood and used by the body.

- glucose is used in respiration
- amino acids are used to build proteins
- fatty acids and glycerol are used to build lipids
- some products are used to build new carbohydrates, lipids and proteins

### 7. Bile

Bile is made in the liver and stored in the gall bladder.

Bile has two important roles:

1. It is alkaline, so it neutralises hydrochloric acid from the stomach.
2. It emulsifies fats, breaking large fat droplets into smaller droplets.

Why emulsification matters:

Smaller fat droplets have a larger surface area. This means lipase can break down fats faster.

Exam wording:

> Bile neutralises stomach acid and emulsifies fats, increasing the surface area for lipase.

## Required practical 3: food tests

Students must know qualitative tests for carbohydrates, lipids and proteins.

### Benedict's test for reducing sugars

Method:

1. Add Benedict's solution to the food sample.
2. Heat in a water bath.
3. Positive result: blue turns green/yellow/orange/brick red.

Exam trap:

Benedict's solution must be heated. A colour change towards brick red shows more reducing sugar.

### Iodine test for starch

Method:

1. Add iodine solution to the food sample.
2. Positive result: orange-brown turns blue-black.

### Biuret test for protein

Method:

1. Add Biuret reagent to the food sample.
2. Positive result: blue turns lilac/purple.

### Lipid test

Include the lipid test used by the school. Both are common in GCSE teaching.

#### Sudan III test

1. Add Sudan III stain to the sample.
2. Positive result: a red-stained oil layer separates on top.

#### Emulsion test

1. Add ethanol to the sample and shake.
2. Add water.
3. Positive result: cloudy white emulsion.

Exam trap:

Do not confuse iodine and Biuret. Iodine tests for starch. Biuret tests for protein.

## Required practical 4: effect of pH on amylase

Aim:

Investigate the effect of pH on the rate of reaction of amylase enzyme.

Core idea:

Amylase digests starch. Iodine turns blue-black if starch is present. If starch has been digested, iodine stays orange-brown.

Method summary:

1. Add amylase and buffer solution of a chosen pH to a test tube.
2. Add starch solution.
3. Keep temperature controlled using a water bath.
4. Start a timer.
5. Every 30 seconds, place a drop of the mixture onto iodine on a spotting tile.
6. Record the time when iodine no longer turns blue-black.
7. Repeat with different pH values.

Variables:

- independent variable: pH
- dependent variable: time taken for starch to be digested
- control variables: temperature, concentration and volume of amylase, concentration and volume of starch, total volume, sampling interval

Interpreting results:

- shorter time = faster enzyme activity
- longer time = slower enzyme activity
- optimum pH = pH where starch disappears fastest

Rate calculation:

> rate = 1000 / time taken

This gives a simple comparable rate when time is measured in seconds.

Worked example:

If starch is digested in 50 seconds:

1000 / 50 = 20 arbitrary units

Improving quality:

- repeat each pH and calculate a mean
- identify anomalous results
- keep temperature constant with a water bath
- use the same volumes and concentrations each time

Common exam traps:

- iodine tests for starch; it does not digest starch
- keep temperature constant so pH is the only variable affecting the rate
- use a spotting tile for repeated samples over time
- do not say enzymes die; say denature
