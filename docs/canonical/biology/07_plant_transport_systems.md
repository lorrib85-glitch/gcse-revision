By the end, students should be able to:

## Hook

Plants look still.

They are not.

Water is being pulled up through microscopic pipes. Sugars are being moved from leaves to growing roots, fruits and flowers. Tiny pores are opening and closing to balance gas exchange against water loss.

A plant is a transport system with no heart.

## Prior knowledge retrieval

1. What is osmosis?  
Diffusion of water from a dilute solution to a concentrated solution through a partially permeable membrane.

2. What is active transport?  
Movement against a concentration gradient using energy from respiration.

3. What do chloroplasts do?  
Absorb light energy for photosynthesis.

4. What is the photosynthesis word equation?  
Carbon dioxide + water → glucose + oxygen.

## Core content

### 1. Plant tissues

Students should know these plant tissues:

- epidermal tissue
- palisade mesophyll
- spongy mesophyll
- xylem
- phloem
- meristem tissue

The leaf is a plant organ because it is made of different tissues working together.

### 2. Leaf adaptations

#### Epidermal tissue

- covers and protects the leaf
- transparent so light can pass through to palisade cells

#### Palisade mesophyll

- packed with chloroplasts
- near the top of the leaf to absorb more light
- main site of photosynthesis

#### Spongy mesophyll

- has air spaces
- allows gases to diffuse through the leaf
- helps carbon dioxide reach photosynthesising cells

#### Xylem

- transports water and mineral ions from roots to stem and leaves
- made of hollow tubes
- strengthened by lignin

#### Phloem

- transports dissolved sugars from leaves to the rest of the plant
- movement is called translocation

#### Meristem tissue

- found at growing tips of roots and shoots
- contains cells that can differentiate into different plant cell types

### 3. Stomata and guard cells

Stomata are tiny pores mostly found on the underside of leaves.

They allow:

- carbon dioxide to enter for photosynthesis
- oxygen to leave
- water vapour to leave

Guard cells control the opening and closing of stomata.

Why this matters:

A plant needs stomata open for gas exchange, but open stomata also mean water loss.

### 4. Root hair cells

Root hair cells are adapted for absorption.

Adaptations:

- long hair-like projection gives large surface area
- water enters by osmosis
- mineral ions enter by active transport
- many mitochondria provide energy for active transport

Exam wording:

> Root hair cells absorb water by osmosis and mineral ions by active transport.

### 5. Xylem

Xylem transports water and mineral ions from the roots to the leaves.

Key details:

- movement is one-way: roots to leaves
- xylem vessels are hollow tubes
- walls are strengthened with lignin
- water moves in the transpiration stream

### 6. Transpiration

Transpiration is the loss of water vapour from the leaves, mainly through stomata.

The transpiration stream is the movement of water from roots, through xylem, to the leaves, where water evaporates and diffuses out.

Simple sequence:

1. Water enters roots by osmosis.
2. Water moves up the xylem.
3. Water evaporates from leaf cells.
4. Water vapour diffuses out through stomata.
5. More water is pulled up through the xylem.

### 7. Factors affecting transpiration rate

Transpiration increases when:

#### Temperature increases

- water molecules have more energy
- evaporation happens faster
- diffusion of water vapour out of the leaf is faster

#### Humidity decreases

- air outside the leaf is drier
- concentration gradient for water vapour is steeper
- diffusion out of the leaf is faster

#### Air movement increases

- wind removes water vapour from around the leaf
- maintains a steep concentration gradient
- transpiration increases

#### Light intensity increases

- stomata open for photosynthesis
- more water vapour can diffuse out
- transpiration increases

Common exam trap:

High humidity reduces transpiration because the concentration gradient is smaller.

### 8. Measuring transpiration

Students may be asked about measuring water uptake as an estimate of transpiration rate.

Core idea:

A potometer estimates transpiration by measuring water uptake by a shoot.

Rate calculation:

> rate = distance moved by air bubble ÷ time

or

> rate = volume of water taken up ÷ time

Caution:

Water uptake is only an estimate of transpiration because some water is used in photosynthesis or to support cells.

### 9. Phloem and translocation

Phloem transports dissolved sugars from leaves to the rest of the plant.

This movement is called translocation.

Sugars may move to:

- growing shoots
- roots
- fruits
- seeds
- storage organs

Direction:

Translocation can move both up and down the plant, depending on where sugars are needed.

Do not overteach detailed phloem mechanism for Trilogy.

## Interactive tasks to build

### Task 1: Leaf tissue match

Match:

- epidermis → protection and lets light through
- palisade mesophyll → main photosynthesis tissue
- spongy mesophyll → air spaces for gas diffusion
- xylem → water and mineral ion transport
- phloem → dissolved sugar transport
- guard cells → open and close stomata

### Task 2: Xylem or phloem sort

Xylem:

- transports water
- transports mineral ions
- movement from roots to leaves
- hollow tubes strengthened by lignin

Phloem:

- transports dissolved sugars
- movement called translocation
- can move up or down the plant

### Task 3: Transpiration factor challenge

For each change, decide whether transpiration increases or decreases:

- hotter day → increases
- more humid air → decreases
- stronger wind → increases
- brighter light → increases
- stomata close → decreases

### Task 4: Potometer calculation

Question:

An air bubble moves 24 mm in 6 minutes. Calculate the rate.

Working:

24 ÷ 6 = 4 mm/min

Answer:

4 mm per minute.

### Task 5: Spot the error

Student answer:

> Phloem carries water up the plant.

Correction:

Xylem carries water and mineral ions from roots to leaves. Phloem carries dissolved sugars around the plant by translocation.

## Exam practice

### Question 1 — leaf tissues

Explain how a leaf is adapted for photosynthesis.  
4 marks

Mark points:

- palisade cells contain many chloroplasts
- palisade layer near top of leaf absorbs more light
- transparent epidermis lets light through
- spongy mesophyll has air spaces for gas diffusion
- stomata allow carbon dioxide to enter
- xylem brings water to leaf

### Question 2 — root hair cells

Explain how root hair cells are adapted to absorb water and mineral ions.  
4 marks

Mark points:

- long projection gives large surface area
- water enters by osmosis
- mineral ions enter by active transport
- many mitochondria provide energy for active transport

### Question 3 — transpiration

Explain why transpiration is faster on a hot, windy day.  
4 marks

Mark points:

- higher temperature increases evaporation
- water molecules have more energy
- wind removes water vapour from around the leaf
- maintains steep concentration gradient
- faster diffusion of water vapour out through stomata

### Question 4 — xylem and phloem

Compare the functions of xylem and phloem.  
4 marks

Mark points:

- xylem transports water and mineral ions
- xylem moves substances from roots to leaves
- phloem transports dissolved sugars
- phloem movement is called translocation
- phloem can move substances up and down the plant

### Question 5 — rate calculation

An air bubble in a potometer moves 18 mm in 3 minutes.

Calculate the rate of movement.  
2 marks

Mark points:

- rate = distance ÷ time
- 18 ÷ 3 = 6 mm/min
