# Biology Chapter 8 — Pathogens and disease

AQA Combined Science: Trilogy 8464  
Biology Topic 3: Infection and response

## Chapter purpose

This chapter starts AQA Biology Topic 3: Infection and response.

Chapter 8 introduces pathogens, how they cause disease, how they spread, and the named communicable diseases students must know for AQA Trilogy.

It should come after the Organisation block and before defence, vaccination and treatment.

## Learning goals

By the end, students should be able to:

- define pathogen
- describe bacteria, viruses, fungi and protists as pathogen groups
- explain how pathogens spread
- describe how bacteria and viruses make people ill
- recall key details for measles, HIV, tobacco mosaic virus, salmonella, gonorrhoea, rose black spot and malaria
- explain how to reduce the spread of communicable disease
- compare communicable and non-communicable disease

## Hook

Disease is not just “being ill”.

Sometimes the cause is alive. Sometimes it is not quite alive. Sometimes it hijacks your cells. Sometimes it poisons you with toxins.

AQA wants students to know the enemy before they learn how the body fights back.

## Prior knowledge retrieval

1. What is a cell membrane?  
A structure that controls what enters and leaves a cell.

2. What is a bacterial cell?  
A prokaryotic cell with no nucleus, a single DNA loop and sometimes plasmids.

3. What is the difference between communicable and non-communicable disease?  
Communicable diseases can spread between organisms; non-communicable diseases cannot.

## Core content

### 1. Pathogens

A pathogen is a microorganism that causes disease.

Pathogens may be:

- bacteria
- viruses
- fungi
- protists

Pathogens can infect plants or animals.

### 2. How pathogens spread

Pathogens can spread by:

- direct contact
- water
- air
- body fluids
- vectors

A vector is an organism that carries and transmits a pathogen, without necessarily getting the disease itself.

Example:

Mosquitoes are vectors for the protist that causes malaria.

### 3. How bacteria cause disease

Bacteria can reproduce rapidly inside the body.

They may produce toxins that damage tissues and make a person feel ill.

Exam wording:

> Bacteria can multiply rapidly and produce toxins that damage cells and tissues.

### 4. How viruses cause disease

Viruses live and reproduce inside cells.

They replicate inside host cells, causing cell damage.

Exam wording:

> Viruses reproduce inside cells, damaging or destroying the host cells.

Common trap:

Do not say antibiotics kill viruses. Antibiotics treat bacterial infections, not viral infections.

## Named diseases students must know

### 5. Measles

Type of pathogen:

- virus

Spread:

- droplets from sneezes and coughs

Symptoms:

- fever
- red skin rash

Prevention:

- vaccination

Extra detail:

Measles can be serious and may lead to complications.

### 6. HIV and AIDS

Type of pathogen:

- virus

Spread:

- sexual contact
- exchange of body fluids, such as blood

How it affects the body:

- HIV initially causes a flu-like illness
- if untreated, it attacks immune cells
- late-stage HIV infection can lead to AIDS
- AIDS means the immune system is badly damaged and cannot deal with other infections or cancers effectively

Control:

- antiretroviral drugs can control HIV replication

### 7. Tobacco mosaic virus

Type of pathogen:

- virus

Affects:

- plants

Symptoms/effects:

- mosaic pattern of discolouration on leaves
- reduced chlorophyll
- reduced photosynthesis
- reduced plant growth

### 8. Salmonella

Type of pathogen:

- bacterium

Spread:

- food poisoning from contaminated food

Symptoms:

- fever
- abdominal cramps
- vomiting
- diarrhoea

Cause of symptoms:

- bacteria and toxins irritate/damage the digestive system

Prevention:

- good hygiene
- cooking food thoroughly
- vaccination of poultry in the UK helps reduce salmonella spread

### 9. Gonorrhoea

Type of pathogen:

- bacterium

Spread:

- sexual contact

Symptoms:

- thick yellow or green discharge from vagina or penis
- pain when urinating

Treatment:

- antibiotics

Problem:

- antibiotic-resistant strains have appeared

Prevention:

- condoms
- screening and treating infected people

### 10. Rose black spot

Type of pathogen:

- fungus

Affects:

- plants, especially roses

Symptoms/effects:

- purple or black spots on leaves
- leaves turn yellow and drop early
- less photosynthesis
- reduced growth

Spread:

- water
- wind

Treatment/control:

- fungicides
- removing and destroying infected leaves

### 11. Malaria

Type of pathogen:

- protist

Spread:

- mosquitoes act as vectors

Symptoms:

- recurrent fever
- can be fatal

Control:

- mosquito nets
- insecticides
- preventing mosquitoes from breeding

## Reducing the spread of disease

Ways to reduce spread:

- hygiene, such as handwashing and food hygiene
- destroying infected organisms or tissues
- isolation of infected individuals
- vaccination
- controlling vectors

Examples:

- mosquito nets reduce malaria transmission
- condoms reduce spread of gonorrhoea and HIV
- vaccination reduces measles spread
- cooking food thoroughly reduces salmonella risk
- removing infected leaves reduces rose black spot spread

## Communicable vs non-communicable disease

Communicable disease:

- caused by pathogens
- can spread between organisms
- examples: measles, salmonella, malaria

Non-communicable disease:

- not caused by pathogens spreading between organisms
- not passed from person to person
- examples: coronary heart disease, cancer, Type 2 diabetes

Common exam trap:

Some diseases can interact. For example, HIV damages the immune system, which increases the risk of other infections and some cancers.

## Interactive tasks to build

### Task 1: Pathogen type sort

Sort each disease by pathogen type:

Virus:

- measles
- HIV
- tobacco mosaic virus

Bacterium:

- salmonella
- gonorrhoea

Fungus:

- rose black spot

Protist:

- malaria

### Task 2: Spread route match

Match:

- measles → droplets from coughs/sneezes
- HIV → body fluids/sexual contact/blood
- salmonella → contaminated food
- gonorrhoea → sexual contact
- rose black spot → wind/water
- malaria → mosquito vector

### Task 3: Symptom/effect match

Match:

- measles → red rash and fever
- HIV → damages immune system over time
- TMV → mosaic leaf discolouration and reduced photosynthesis
- salmonella → fever, cramps, vomiting and diarrhoea
- gonorrhoea → discharge and pain when urinating
- rose black spot → black/purple leaf spots and leaf drop
- malaria → recurrent fever

### Task 4: Prevention challenge

For each situation, choose the best prevention:

- malaria in a high-risk area → mosquito nets / insecticides / vector control
- salmonella risk in food → cook thoroughly and use hygiene
- gonorrhoea → condoms and screening
- measles outbreak → vaccination and isolation
- rose black spot → remove infected leaves / fungicide

### Task 5: Spot the error

Student answer:

> Malaria is caused by mosquitoes.

Correction:

Malaria is caused by a protist. Mosquitoes are vectors that transmit the protist between people.

## Exam practice

### Question 1 — pathogens

Define pathogen and name four types of pathogen.  
5 marks

Mark points:

- microorganism that causes disease
- bacteria
- viruses
- fungi
- protists

### Question 2 — viruses and bacteria

Compare how bacteria and viruses cause disease.  
4 marks

Mark points:

- bacteria reproduce rapidly
- bacteria may produce toxins
- viruses reproduce inside host cells
- viruses damage/destroy cells

### Question 3 — malaria

Explain how malaria is spread and describe two ways its spread can be reduced.  
4 marks

Mark points:

- caused by protist
- spread by mosquito vectors
- mosquito nets / insecticides
- prevent mosquitoes breeding / remove standing water

### Question 4 — tobacco mosaic virus

Explain why tobacco mosaic virus can reduce plant growth.  
3 marks

Mark points:

- causes discolouration/mosaic pattern on leaves
- less chlorophyll / less light absorbed
- reduced photosynthesis
- less glucose for growth

### Question 5 — gonorrhoea

Gonorrhoea used to be treated easily with antibiotics. Explain why it has become harder to treat.  
3 marks

Mark points:

- some strains have developed antibiotic resistance
- resistant bacteria survive antibiotic treatment
- resistant bacteria reproduce and spread

An organism that carries and transmits a pathogen.
