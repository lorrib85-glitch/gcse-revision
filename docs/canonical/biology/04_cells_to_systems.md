# Biology Chapter 4 — Cells to systems

AQA Combined Science: Trilogy 8464  
Biology Topic 2: Organisation

## Chapter purpose

This chapter starts AQA Biology Topic 2: Organisation. It bridges the student from tiny cells into the larger body systems that keep organisms alive.

It should come after the three Cell Biology chapters:

1. Building blocks
2. Seeing cells and making new cells
3. Moving in and out of cells

## Learning goals

By the end, students should be able to:

- put biological organisation in the correct order
- define cell, tissue, organ, organ system and organism
- explain why multicellular organisms need organisation
- describe the digestive system as an organ system
- identify the main organs in the digestive system
- explain that digestion breaks large insoluble molecules into small soluble molecules
- explain why digested molecules must be soluble before absorption into the blood

## Hook

A single cell can stay alive on its own if it is tiny enough.

A human cannot.

Your body is too big for every cell to grab what it needs directly from the outside world. So cells team up, specialise and build systems.

That is what Organisation is: cells working together so a large organism can survive.

## Prior knowledge retrieval

1. What controls what enters and leaves a cell?  
   **Cell membrane**

2. What process moves oxygen from high to low concentration?  
   **Diffusion**

3. Why do large organisms need exchange surfaces and transport systems?  
   They have a smaller surface area to volume ratio, so diffusion alone is not enough.

## Core content

### 1. The order of organisation

Students must know the hierarchy:

> cell → tissue → organ → organ system → organism

Definitions:

- **Cell:** the basic unit of life.
- **Tissue:** a group of similar cells with a similar structure and function.
- **Organ:** a group of different tissues working together to perform a specific function.
- **Organ system:** a group of organs working together to perform a larger function.
- **Organism:** a living thing made of organ systems.

Example chain:

> muscle cell → muscle tissue → stomach → digestive system → human

Plant example:

> palisade cell → palisade mesophyll tissue → leaf → shoot system → plant

### 2. Why organisation matters

Cells in large multicellular organisms cannot all directly access oxygen, glucose, minerals or water.

Specialised cells group into tissues and organs so the organism can:

- exchange substances efficiently
- transport substances around the body
- digest food
- remove waste
- coordinate responses

Simple explanation:

A body is not a bag of random cells. It is more like a city: cells are workers, tissues are teams, organs are departments, organ systems are whole services.

### 3. The digestive system as an organ system

The digestive system is an organ system because several organs work together to digest and absorb food.

Main organs to know:

- mouth
- oesophagus
- stomach
- liver
- gall bladder
- pancreas
- small intestine
- large intestine
- anus

Students do not need every tiny detail of the gut, but they should understand the journey:

1. Food enters the mouth.
2. Food moves down the oesophagus.
3. The stomach begins digestion and mixes food with acid and enzymes.
4. The pancreas releases digestive enzymes.
5. The liver makes bile.
6. The gall bladder stores bile.
7. The small intestine is where most digestion and absorption happen.
8. The large intestine absorbs water.
9. Waste leaves through the anus.

### 4. What digestion actually means

Digestion is the breakdown of large insoluble molecules into small soluble molecules.

Why this matters:

- large molecules are too big to pass into the blood
- insoluble molecules cannot dissolve and be carried in plasma
- small soluble molecules can be absorbed through the wall of the small intestine into the bloodstream

Exam wording:

> Digestion breaks large insoluble molecules into small soluble molecules so they can be absorbed into the blood.

### 5. Absorption in the small intestine

The small intestine absorbs digested molecules into the blood.

Link back to Chapter 3:

The small intestine is adapted for exchange because it has:

- villi to increase surface area
- thin walls for a short diffusion distance
- a good blood supply to maintain the concentration gradient

Examples of absorbed molecules:

- glucose from carbohydrate digestion
- amino acids from protein digestion
- fatty acids and glycerol from lipid digestion

### Task 4: Spot the error

Student answer:

> Digestion means food is turned into energy.

Correction:

Digestion means large insoluble molecules are broken down into small soluble molecules. Glucose can later be used in respiration to transfer energy.
