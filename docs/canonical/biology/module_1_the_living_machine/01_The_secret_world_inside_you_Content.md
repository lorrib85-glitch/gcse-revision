## 2. Core argument

**Central claim:**

DRAFT (for user confirmation): Every living thing — from a skin cell to a bacterium — is made of cells, but not all cells are the same. The differences between animal, plant and bacterial cells reveal three billion years of evolution: prokaryotes came first, eukaryotes added a nucleus and specialist compartments, and plants added a second layer of machinery for photosynthesis. Understanding these differences is the foundation for understanding everything else in GCSE Biology.

**Evidence for the claim:**
- Bacterial cells (prokaryotes) have no nucleus — DNA floats freely as a circular chromosome in the cytoplasm. This contrasts with animal and plant cells where DNA is enclosed in a membrane-bound nucleus.
- Plant cells have chloroplasts and cell walls — structures animal cells lack entirely — because plants make their own food via photosynthesis and need rigid support.
- All three cell types share: cell membrane, cytoplasm, ribosomes — the irreducible minimum for life.
- Mitochondria appear in both animal and plant cells (but not bacteria) because aerobic respiration evolved after the prokaryote-to-eukaryote transition.
- Size difference: bacterial cells ~1–10 µm; animal/plant cells ~10–100 µm.

**Series throughline connection:**
This chapter opens Module 1 (The living machine). It establishes the cellular unit from which all later biology follows: diffusion (Ch 7–9) requires a cell membrane; mitosis (Ch 5) requires a nucleus; specialised cells (Ch 4) require the organelles introduced here; stem cells (Ch 6) require understanding of what differentiation means. Every later chapter in the 40-chapter spine assumes this chapter's knowledge.

## 3. Specification requirements

### Animal cells
- **Nucleus** — contains genetic material (DNA); controls the activities of the cell
- **Cell membrane** — controls the movement of substances into and out of the cell; thin, flexible bilayer
- **Cytoplasm** — gel-like fluid where chemical reactions take place
- **Mitochondria** — site of aerobic respiration; release energy for the cell; have a double membrane (outer + inner folded cristae)
- **Ribosomes** — site of protein synthesis; very small, not membrane-bound

### Plant cells (all animal cell structures PLUS)
- **Cell wall** — provides rigid support and structure; made of cellulose; found outside the cell membrane
- **Permanent vacuole** — contains cell sap (dilute sugar/salt solution); maintains cell turgor and shape
- **Chloroplasts** — site of photosynthesis; contain chlorophyll (green pigment); absorb light energy; only in cells that photosynthesise (NOT in root cells)

### Bacterial cells (prokaryotic)
- **Cell wall** — present, but NOT made of cellulose (made of peptidoglycan / murein)
- **Cell membrane** — controls movement in and out
- **Cytoplasm** — site of reactions
- **Ribosomes** — present (but smaller than eukaryotic ribosomes — 70S vs 80S)
- **Plasmid** — small, circular loop of DNA separate from the chromosomal DNA; may carry antibiotic resistance genes
- **Chromosomal DNA** — single circular chromosome, NOT enclosed in a membrane-bound nucleus; floats in cytoplasm
- **Flagellum (optional)** — whip-like tail for movement; not present in all bacterial cells
- **NO** nucleus (no nuclear membrane), **NO** mitochondria, **NO** chloroplasts

### Eukaryotic vs prokaryotic
- **Eukaryotic:** animal cells, plant cells, fungal cells; have a true membrane-bound nucleus; larger (~10–100 µm)
- **Prokaryotic:** bacterial cells; no membrane-bound nucleus; DNA is in the cytoplasm; smaller (~1–10 µm; typical ~2 µm)
- Key distinction: the presence or absence of a membrane-bound nucleus is THE defining difference

### Organelle functions (exam-precise phrasing required)
- Nucleus → "controls the activities of the cell" (not "contains DNA" alone — that only scores 1 if function not given)
- Mitochondria → "where aerobic respiration occurs / releases energy for the cell" (NOT "makes energy" — energy is released, not made)
- Ribosomes → "where protein synthesis occurs" (not just "makes proteins" — must say synthesis/site of)
- Cell membrane → "controls what moves in and out of the cell" or "partially permeable membrane controlling movement"
- Chloroplasts → "where photosynthesis occurs" or "contain chlorophyll and absorb light energy for photosynthesis"
- Cell wall (plant) → "made of cellulose; provides rigid support to the cell"
- Vacuole → "contains cell sap; maintains turgor pressure"

## 4. Content reference pack

### Chronology / scale

- Prokaryotes evolved ~3.5 billion years ago (first life on Earth)
- Eukaryotes evolved ~1.8 billion years ago (endosymbiotic theory: mitochondria/chloroplasts were once free-living bacteria)
- Typical bacterial cell size: 1–10 µm (average ~2 µm)
- Typical animal cell size: 10–100 µm (e.g. human red blood cell ~8 µm)
- Typical plant cell size: 10–100 µm (leaf palisade cell ~70–80 µm tall)
- 1 mm = 1000 µm; 1 µm = 1000 nm

### Key terms & definitions

- **Organelle** — a specialised sub-unit within a cell that has a specific function (analogous to organ in a body)
- **Eukaryotic cell** — a cell with a true membrane-bound nucleus (animals, plants, fungi)
- **Prokaryotic cell** — a cell without a membrane-bound nucleus; DNA is in the cytoplasm (bacteria)
- **Nucleus** — membrane-bound compartment containing chromosomal DNA; controls cell activities
- **Cell membrane** — partially permeable lipid bilayer controlling what enters and leaves the cell
- **Cytoplasm** — gel-like aqueous solution inside the cell where metabolic reactions occur
- **Mitochondrion (pl. mitochondria)** — double-membrane organelle; site of aerobic respiration
- **Ribosome** — tiny organelle (no membrane); site of protein synthesis
- **Cell wall** — rigid structure outside the cell membrane (cellulose in plants, peptidoglycan in bacteria)
- **Chloroplast** — double-membrane organelle containing chlorophyll; site of photosynthesis
- **Vacuole** — membrane-bound sac; in plants contains cell sap and maintains turgidity
- **Plasmid** — small, circular DNA molecule in bacteria; separate from chromosomal DNA
- **Flagellum** — whip-like appendage used for bacterial locomotion
- **Chromosomal DNA** — the main genetic material of a cell (in bacteria = single circular molecule in cytoplasm)

### Causes & effects / mechanism chains

- Plant cell has chloroplasts → can photosynthesise → produces glucose and oxygen from CO₂ + water + light
- Root hair cell has no chloroplasts → underground → no light → chloroplasts would be useless
- Bacterial cell has no nuclear membrane → prokaryote → DNA can be transcribed and translated simultaneously (no mRNA export needed)
- Mitochondria present in animal + plant cells → both carry out aerobic respiration → both need energy for cellular processes
- Cell wall (plant) → rigidity → enables turgor pressure from vacuole to work → supports plant without skeleton
- Plasmid in bacteria → can carry resistance genes → shared between bacteria (conjugation) → rapid spread of antibiotic resistance

- For cell membrane: "controls movement of substances into/out of cell" or "partially permeable"

- For vacuole: "contains cell sap" or "maintains turgor"

**High-value misconceptions (suitable for MisconceptionCheck):**
- "All plant cells contain chloroplasts" → FALSE: root cells, storage cells (e.g. in potato), guard cells at night do not always have chloroplasts; only photosynthetic cells do
- "Mitochondria make energy" → FALSE: they release energy (from glucose via aerobic respiration); energy cannot be created
- "Bacterial cells have no cell wall" → FALSE: they have a cell wall made of peptidoglycan (not cellulose)
- "The nucleus contains all the DNA in the cell" → TRUE for eukaryotes (mostly), but mitochondria and chloroplasts also contain small amounts of their own DNA (endosymbiotic theory — GCSE level: nucleus contains the chromosomal DNA that controls the cell)
- "Animal cells have no vacuole" → MOSTLY TRUE for permanent vacuole; animal cells may have temporary vacuoles (phagocytosis etc.) but these differ from the plant permanent vacuole
- "Eukaryotic and prokaryotic refer to the presence/absence of a nucleus" → CORRECT: the membrane-bound nucleus is THE defining difference

**SpotTheError candidates:**
- "Plant cells are different from animal cells because they have a nucleus and mitochondria." (Error: animal cells also have these — should name structures unique to plant cells)
- "Bacterial cells are smaller than animal cells and have a nucleus in their cytoplasm." (Error: bacterial cells have NO nucleus / no membrane-bound nucleus — DNA is in the cytoplasm but not enclosed)
- "Mitochondria make ATP energy for the cell to use." (Error: mitochondria RELEASE energy / ATP is the carrier but "make energy" is incorrect phrasing; better: "mitochondria are the site of aerobic respiration which releases energy")
