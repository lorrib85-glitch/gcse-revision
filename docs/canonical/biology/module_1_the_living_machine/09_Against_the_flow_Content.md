## 2. Core argument

**Central claim:**

DRAFT (for user confirmation): Sometimes biology cannot afford to wait for diffusion. When the concentration of a vital substance is already higher inside the cell than outside — or when the gradient is simply not steep enough — cells use energy to actively pull molecules in against the flow. Active transport is the proof that living systems can push uphill, and it explains how root hairs absorb minerals from nutrient-poor soil, and how your gut absorbs the last traces of glucose even after a meal.

**Evidence for the claim:**
- Mineral ions (nitrates, potassium) in soil water are at LOWER concentration than inside root hair cells — diffusion would move them OUT, not in; active transport moves them IN against the gradient
- Glucose in the small intestine is absorbed even when blood glucose > gut glucose — only active transport can explain this (diffusion would move glucose in the wrong direction)
- Both processes require carrier proteins in the membrane AND ATP from aerobic respiration in mitochondria
- Cells performing active transport have more mitochondria than comparable cells (e.g. root hair cells, gut epithelial cells — higher mitochondrial density than resting cells)
- Cyanide (which blocks ATP production) → active transport stops; diffusion continues unaffected

## 3. Specification requirements

### Active transport — definition

- **Definition:** Active transport is the movement of particles from a region of **lower concentration** to a region of **higher concentration** (against the concentration gradient) using **energy** from **respiration**
- It requires:
  1. **Energy (ATP)** from aerobic respiration (provided by mitochondria)
  2. **Carrier proteins** in the cell membrane (specific to each substance being transported)
- Active transport can move substances UPHILL (against the gradient) — diffusion and osmosis cannot

### Key differences — active transport vs diffusion

| Feature | Diffusion | Osmosis | Active transport |
|---------|-----------|---------|-----------------|
| Direction | High → Low (down gradient) | High → Low water potential | Low → High (against gradient) |
| Energy required? | No (passive) | No (passive) | Yes (from respiration/ATP) |
| Carrier proteins? | Not always needed | Not needed | Yes — required |
| What moves? | Any dissolved particle or gas | Water molecules only | Specific ions/molecules (e.g. glucose, mineral ions) |
| Can move against gradient? | No | No | Yes |

### Mineral ion uptake in root hair cells

- **Problem:** Mineral ions (e.g. nitrate ions, potassium ions) are present in the soil water at **low concentration**
- Inside the root hair cell, the mineral ion concentration is already **higher** than in the soil solution
- Therefore, diffusion would move ions OUT of the root, not in
- **Solution:** active transport pumps mineral ions INTO the root hair cells against the concentration gradient
- This requires: carrier proteins in the root hair cell membrane + ATP from mitochondria
- This is why root hair cells have **many mitochondria** (to provide the energy for active transport)
- AQA note: mineral ions absorbed include nitrate (for amino acid/protein synthesis), potassium (for water potential regulation) and others

### Glucose absorption in the small intestine

- After digestion, glucose concentration in the small intestine lumen is initially HIGH → glucose diffuses into blood (blood glucose < gut glucose)
- As digestion continues and glucose is absorbed, the concentration in the gut becomes LOW while blood glucose may remain higher
- At this point, diffusion would reverse — but active transport allows glucose to CONTINUE being absorbed from the gut into the blood, even against the gradient
- Gut epithelial cells have many mitochondria and carrier proteins → active transport of glucose maintains complete absorption
- This ensures maximum energy extraction from food — important for survival

### Energy from respiration

- ATP (adenosine triphosphate) is the energy currency of the cell
- ATP is produced by aerobic respiration in the mitochondria
- Word equation for aerobic respiration: glucose + oxygen → carbon dioxide + water (energy released)
- ATP provides the energy to "pump" carrier proteins in the cell membrane, moving specific substances against their concentration gradient
- If respiration is blocked (e.g. low oxygen, poison like cyanide), active transport stops; diffusion continues
- This is evidence that active transport requires metabolic energy, confirming it is an active, not passive, process

## 4. Content reference pack

### Key terms & definitions

- **Active transport** — movement of particles from lower to higher concentration (against gradient) using energy from respiration; requires carrier proteins
- **Concentration gradient** — the difference in concentration of a substance between two areas; in active transport, particles move AGAINST this gradient (from low to high)
- **Carrier protein** — membrane protein that binds to a specific substance and moves it across the membrane using energy; required for active transport
- **ATP (adenosine triphosphate)** — the immediate energy currency of the cell; released by aerobic respiration; used to power active transport (and other energy-requiring processes)
- **Mitochondria** — organelles where aerobic respiration occurs; provide ATP for active transport; cells performing active transport have more mitochondria
- **Mineral ions** — inorganic ions needed by plants for growth (e.g. nitrate → amino acids and proteins; potassium → osmotic regulation); absorbed by root hair cells via active transport
- **Nitrate ions** — absorbed from soil by root hair cells via active transport; used to make amino acids and proteins in the plant
- **Gut epithelial cells** — cells lining the small intestine; absorb glucose and amino acids; have many mitochondria for active transport

### Causes & effects / mechanism chains

- Low mineral ion concentration in soil < high concentration inside root cell → diffusion would move ions out, not in → active transport required → carrier proteins + ATP → ions pumped in against gradient
- Root hair cells have many mitochondria → aerobic respiration → lots of ATP → powers active transport of mineral ions
- Glucose absorbed from gut by diffusion when [gut] > [blood] → as [gut] falls below [blood] → diffusion reverses or stops → active transport continues absorbing glucose → maximum absorption
- Gut epithelial cells have many mitochondria → ATP → active transport of glucose and amino acids
- Block aerobic respiration (e.g. cyanide, low O₂) → no ATP produced → active transport stops → mineral ions not absorbed → diffusion continues (unaffected by energy block)
- High temperature: faster aerobic respiration up to optimum → more ATP → faster active transport (also: carrier proteins are enzymes — denature above optimum)

### Three-way comparison table (exam-critical)

| Feature | Diffusion | Osmosis | Active transport |
|---------|-----------|---------|-----------------|
| Direction of movement | High → Low | High → Low water potential | Low → High (against gradient) |
| Energy needed? | No | No | Yes (ATP from respiration) |
| Concentration gradient direction | With | With (water potential) | Against |
| Membrane required? | Not always (in solution); yes through cells | Yes (partially permeable) | Yes |
| Carrier proteins? | Not usually | No | Yes (required) |
| Examples | O₂ into blood; CO₂ out of cells | Water into root hair cells | Mineral ions into roots; glucose from gut into blood |
| Rate affected by energy poisons? | No | No | Yes |

### Exam angles

**Common question types:**
- "Explain what is meant by active transport" — 2 marks
- "Explain why active transport is needed for mineral ion uptake in plants" — 3 marks (concentration in soil < in root cells; cannot be absorbed by diffusion; requires active transport using energy from respiration)
- "Explain why root hair cells have many mitochondria" — 2 marks (provide energy/ATP from aerobic respiration; powers active transport of mineral ions)
- "Compare active transport and diffusion" — 3–4 marks (direction; energy; gradient; carrier proteins)
- "Explain how glucose is absorbed in the small intestine" — 3 marks (diffusion when [gut] > [blood]; then active transport when gradient is insufficient or reversed)

**Mark scheme keywords:**
- "Against concentration gradient" or "from low to high concentration" — essential for definition
- "Using energy from respiration" — must say respiration (not just "energy"); ATP is acceptable
- "Carrier proteins" — must mention for full marks in some questions
- Root hair cells / mitochondria: "aerobic respiration provides energy/ATP" + "for active transport of mineral ions"
- Glucose absorption: "by diffusion when concentration in gut is higher" + "by active transport when concentration is low/lower than blood"

**High-value misconceptions (MisconceptionCheck):**
- "Active transport is just a faster version of diffusion" → FALSE: active transport moves in the OPPOSITE direction to diffusion (against the gradient); it is a fundamentally different mechanism
- "Active transport doesn't need a membrane" → FALSE: carrier proteins are embedded in the cell membrane; the membrane is essential
- "All substances can be actively transported" → FALSE: active transport is specific — each carrier protein is specific to one or a few substances
- "Active transport uses the same energy as muscle contraction" → MISLEADING but useful: both use ATP from aerobic respiration; however, active transport is not "muscular" — it uses molecular pumps, not muscle fibres

**SpotTheError candidates:**
- "Active transport is the movement of particles from a high concentration to a low concentration. Unlike diffusion, it requires energy." (error: direction is wrong — active transport moves from LOW to HIGH concentration; the energy part is correct)
- "Root hair cells have many chloroplasts to provide energy for active transport of mineral ions." (two errors: root hair cells have NO chloroplasts (underground); energy for active transport comes from MITOCHONDRIA via aerobic respiration, not from photosynthesis)
