## 2. Core argument

**Central claim:**

DRAFT (for user confirmation): Osmosis is a special case of diffusion — but because it involves water crossing a membrane, it drives some of the most important processes in biology: plant support, cell turgor, kidney function, and why you dehydrate when you drink sea water. The potato practical makes osmosis measurable, and percentage change in mass is the calculation that separates students who understand the method from those who merely followed the steps.

**Evidence for the claim:**
- A potato cylinder in pure water gains mass (water enters by osmosis — concentration of solutes inside potato cell > pure water → water potential gradient drives water in)
- A potato cylinder in concentrated salt solution loses mass (water leaves the cell — concentration outside > inside → water potential drives water out)
- At one specific concentration, mass doesn't change — that's the isotonic point (solute concentration of solution = solute concentration inside potato cells)
- Percentage change in mass = the dependent variable — avoids bias from different initial masses
- Turgor pressure in plants depends on osmosis — without it, plants wilt (cells flaccid or even plasmolysed)

## 3. Specification requirements

### Osmosis — definition

- **Definition:** Osmosis is the movement of **water molecules** from a region of **higher water potential** (more dilute solution) to a region of **lower water potential** (more concentrated solution) through a **partially permeable membrane**
- Key features:
  - Only WATER molecules move by osmosis (not dissolved particles)
  - Requires a partially permeable membrane (one that allows water but not (large) solutes through)
  - Moves from dilute → concentrated (from higher water potential to lower water potential)
  - Passive process (no energy required)
- Net movement stops when water potential is equal on both sides (osmotic equilibrium)

### Osmosis in animal cells

- **In dilute solution:** cells take in water → swell → may burst (lysis/cytolysis) in extreme cases
  - Human red blood cells in pure water → take in water → burst (haemolysis)
- **In concentrated solution:** cells lose water → shrink / crenate (crenation of red blood cells)
- **In isotonic solution:** no net movement of water; cells maintain normal size and shape

### Osmosis in plant cells

- **In dilute solution (more water outside):** water enters cell by osmosis → vacuole swells → cytoplasm pushes against cell wall → cell wall resists → cell becomes **turgid** (firm, swollen)
  - Turgor pressure: the pressure exerted by the cell contents against the cell wall; supports plant cells and soft plant structures
- **In concentrated solution (less water outside):** water leaves cell by osmosis → vacuole shrinks → cytoplasm pulls away from cell wall → cell becomes **flaccid** (soft, limp)
  - Flaccid cells → plant wilts
- **In very concentrated solution:** cytoplasm and membrane pull away from the cell wall → **plasmolysis**
  - The cell membrane fully separates from the cell wall; the cell is said to be **plasmolysed**; can be reversed if returned to water quickly
- **Turgid cells provide structural support** to non-woody plants (e.g. herbaceous plants, leaves, young stems)

### The osmosis required practical (Required Practical 2)

**Aim:** Investigate the effect of a range of sucrose concentrations on the mass of potato tissue

**Method:**
1. Prepare a range of sucrose solutions (e.g. 0.0 M, 0.2 M, 0.4 M, 0.6 M, 0.8 M, 1.0 M) using serial dilution or pre-made solutions
2. Cut potato cylinders of the same length and diameter using a cork borer (or cut with a scalpel to the same length); dry gently with filter paper
3. Weigh each cylinder/group of cylinders accurately using a balance; record initial mass
4. Place cylinders into the appropriate sucrose solution in test tubes/beakers; ensure all cylinders are fully submerged
5. Leave for a set time period (typically 20–30 minutes; or longer for more stable results)
6. Remove cylinders, dry gently (same technique for all) and reweigh; record final mass
7. Calculate percentage change in mass for each concentration

**Percentage change in mass formula:**
% change = ((final mass − initial mass) / initial mass) × 100

- Positive value = mass increased = water entered the tissue (solution was MORE dilute than the tissue)
- Negative value = mass decreased = water left the tissue (solution was MORE concentrated than the tissue)
- Zero = no change = isotonic point (solution concentration = tissue concentration)

**Variables:**
- **Independent variable:** sucrose concentration (the thing you change)
- **Dependent variable:** percentage change in mass (the thing you measure)
- **Control variables:** same species/type of potato; same size/mass cylinders; same length of time; same temperature; same surface area; same volume of solution; same drying technique

**Why percentage change (not absolute change)?**
- Different cylinders may have slightly different initial masses → percentage change removes this bias
- Allows fair comparison across all concentrations

**Expected results:**
- 0.0 M (pure water): mass increases (turgid)
- Increasing sucrose concentration: less mass increase, then no change, then decreasing mass
- At the isotonic point (~0.3–0.4 M for potato): no change in mass (solution = potato cell concentration)
- High concentrations (0.8 M, 1.0 M): significant mass loss (plasmolysis at extreme)

**Graph:** Percentage change in mass (y-axis) vs sucrose concentration (x-axis); line of best fit crosses x-axis at the isotonic point; smooth curve (not dot-to-dot)

**Evaluating the method:**
- Repeat each concentration and take a mean — reduces effect of anomalies
- Dry cylinders the same way — inconsistent drying is a major source of error
- Same time in solution — longer time gives more extreme results; must be consistent
- Same temperature — osmosis rate changes with temperature; water bath or controlled room temperature
- Anomalous result: identify from graph; do not include in line of best fit; suggest explanation (e.g. cylinder dried differently; weighing error)

## 4. Content reference pack

### Key terms & definitions

- **Osmosis** — movement of water molecules from higher water potential (dilute) to lower water potential (concentrated) through a partially permeable membrane; passive
- **Water potential** — the tendency of water to move from one place to another; pure water has the highest water potential; adding solutes lowers water potential; water moves from high to low water potential
- **Partially permeable membrane** — a membrane that allows water molecules through but not (large) solute molecules; cell membrane functions as a partially permeable membrane
- **Turgid** — a plant cell that has taken in water by osmosis; vacuole swollen; cytoplasm pushes against cell wall; cell firm and hard; provides support
- **Flaccid** — a plant cell that has lost water by osmosis; vacuole shrunken; cytoplasm pulled in; cell soft and limp
- **Plasmolysis** — when a plant cell loses so much water that the cell membrane pulls away from the cell wall; cell contents shrink; cell appears wrinkled under microscope
- **Turgor pressure** — pressure exerted by cell contents against cell wall when cell is turgid; supports non-woody plant structures
- **Isotonic** — the concentration at which there is no net movement of water; solution has the same water potential as the cell contents
- **Hypotonic solution** — a solution that has a higher water potential (more dilute) than the cell; cell gains water by osmosis
- **Hypertonic solution** — a solution that has a lower water potential (more concentrated) than the cell; cell loses water by osmosis
- **Percentage change in mass** — ((final mass − initial mass) / initial mass) × 100; used to compare osmosis results across cylinders of different initial masses
- **Serial dilution** — a method of preparing a range of concentrations by progressively diluting a stock solution (e.g. adding 1 M solution + water in steps)
- **Cork borer** — cylindrical cutting tool used to cut uniform potato cylinders of the same diameter
- **Anomalous result** — a data point that does not fit the general trend; should not be included in the mean or line of best fit; should be investigated

### Causes & effects / mechanism chains

- Pure water outside cell → water potential outside > inside → water molecules move into cell by osmosis → vacuole swells → cell becomes turgid → cell wall resists expansion → turgor pressure → plant supported
- Concentrated sucrose outside cell → water potential outside < inside → water molecules move out of cell → vacuole shrinks → cell becomes flaccid → plant wilts
- Very concentrated solution → cell loses large amount of water → membrane pulls from cell wall → plasmolysis
- Potato cylinder in 0.0 M: mass increases (turgid)
- Potato cylinder in 0.4 M (approx isotonic for potato): mass unchanged
- Potato cylinder in 1.0 M: mass decreases significantly (flaccid/incipient plasmolysis)
- Inconsistent drying of cylinders → surface water included or excluded → affects mass reading → source of error
- Percentage change removes initial mass variation → fair comparison across concentrations

### Worked calculation examples

**Example 1:**
- Initial mass: 5.0 g; Final mass: 5.8 g
- % change = ((5.8 − 5.0) / 5.0) × 100 = (0.8 / 5.0) × 100 = **+16%**
- Interpretation: solution was more dilute than potato cells; water entered; potato gained mass

**Example 2:**
- Initial mass: 5.0 g; Final mass: 4.2 g
- % change = ((4.2 − 5.0) / 5.0) × 100 = (−0.8 / 5.0) × 100 = **−16%**
- Interpretation: solution was more concentrated than potato cells; water left; potato lost mass

### Exam angles

**Common question types:**
- "Define osmosis" — 2 marks (must include: water; higher to lower water potential / dilute to concentrated; partially permeable membrane; passive)
- "Describe how to investigate the effect of sucrose concentration on potato mass" — 4–6 marks (method steps + variables)
- "Calculate the percentage change in mass" — 2–3 marks
- "Explain why percentage change in mass is used rather than the actual change in mass" — 2 marks
- "Explain what happens to a plant cell when it is placed in a concentrated sugar solution" — 3 marks (water leaves by osmosis; vacuole shrinks; cell becomes flaccid; extreme → plasmolysis)
- "Suggest why using percentage change in mass is more appropriate than using the change in mass" — 2 marks (removes variation due to different initial masses; allows fair comparison)

**Mark scheme keywords:**
- Definition: "water molecules" (not just "water" — molecules is the precision word), "higher water potential / more dilute" to "lower water potential / more concentrated", "partially permeable membrane", "passive/no energy"
- Method: "same length/size cylinders", "weigh before and after", "same concentration of solutions", "same time", "dry cylinders before weighing", "calculate percentage change"
- % change: must show formula, substitution with values, correct answer with sign (+ or −)
- Turgid/flaccid: "water enters/leaves by osmosis", "vacuole swells/shrinks", "cell wall resists/doesn't resist", "turgid/flaccid"

**High-value misconceptions (MisconceptionCheck):**
- "Osmosis is the movement of dissolved particles through a membrane" → FALSE: osmosis is specifically the movement of WATER molecules; the dissolved particles do not move through the partially permeable membrane
- "Osmosis requires energy" → FALSE: osmosis is passive (like diffusion); water moves down its water potential gradient
- "A turgid plant cell will burst" → FALSE: the cell wall prevents bursting; only animal cells (which have no cell wall) can burst in a hypotonic solution
- "The potato loses mass in concentrated solution because the sucrose molecules are absorbed" → FALSE: sucrose molecules are too large to cross the partially permeable membrane; the potato loses mass because WATER leaves by osmosis

**SpotTheError candidates:**
- "Osmosis is the diffusion of sucrose from a dilute solution to a concentrated solution through a partially permeable membrane." (two errors: osmosis is the movement of WATER, not sucrose; the direction is correct only if rephrased for water — but the student has described the sucrose moving)
- "When a plant cell is placed in a concentrated salt solution, it takes in water by osmosis and becomes turgid, which supports the plant." (error: concentrated solution → water LEAVES the cell → cell becomes FLACCID, not turgid; the reasoning is backwards)
