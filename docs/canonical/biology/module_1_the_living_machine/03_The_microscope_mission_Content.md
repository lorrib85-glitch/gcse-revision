## 2. Core argument

**Central claim:**

DRAFT (for user confirmation): Making a microscope slide is a scientific method in itself — every step has a reason. Staining, coverslip angle, focus order — these are not rituals but solutions to real problems. Knowing the method means knowing the science behind the method, which is exactly what AQA examiners test.

**Evidence for the claim:**
- Iodine stain makes starch and cell structures visible — without it, transparent plant cells appear featureless
- Applying the coverslip at an angle removes air bubbles — air bubbles distort the image and create false shapes
- Starting with the lowest-power objective prevents lens crash and gives a wide field of view for locating cells
- Drawing must use pencil and continuous lines — pen is banned from scientific biological drawing; shading replaces with stippling (dots) only where needed
- Scale bars or stated magnification must appear on every biological drawing
- Calculating magnification from a drawing: M = Image size ÷ Actual size (same formula as Ch 2)

## 3. Specification requirements

### Preparing a microscope slide

**General method for both plant and animal cells:**
1. Place a small sample (thin section or cells in liquid) on a clean glass slide
2. Add a drop of stain (iodine for plant cells / starch; methylene blue for animal cells such as cheek cells)
3. Lower a coverslip at 45° to the slide surface, then gently lower it down — this prevents air bubbles
4. Place the prepared slide on the microscope stage and secure with stage clips

**Stains:**
- **Iodine solution** — stains starch blue-black; also stains cell walls and nuclei yellow-brown; used for plant cells and onion cells
- **Methylene blue** — stains nuclei and other cell structures blue; used for animal cells (e.g. cheek cells)
- Why stain? Cells are often transparent — stains increase contrast so organelles and cell boundaries are visible

**Coverslip technique:**
- Always lower at 45° (not flat) to prevent trapping air bubbles
- Air bubbles appear as dark-ringed circles under the microscope — they ruin the image

### Using the microscope

1. Start with the **lowest power (shortest) objective lens** — gives a wide field of view, easy to locate cells, prevents lens-slide collision
2. Coarse focus knob first (large knob) to get approximate focus
3. Fine focus knob (small knob) to achieve sharp image
4. Move to higher power objective if more detail is needed
5. Re-focus with fine focus only after changing objective (coarse focus risks crashing the lens into the slide)

**Objective lens convention:**
- ×4 (scanning/low), ×10 (medium), ×40 (high) — typical school microscope
- Eyepiece lens is usually ×10
- Total magnification = objective lens × eyepiece lens (e.g. ×10 objective × ×10 eyepiece = ×100 total)

### Drawing cells (biological drawing rules)

- Use a **sharp pencil** — never pen; never felt-tip; no sketching
- **Clear, continuous, unbroken lines** — no fuzzy edges, no sketching/hatching
- **No shading** — biological drawings are outlines only; if shading is needed, use stippling (dots), not shading
- **Label lines** must be ruled (straight) lines using a ruler; they must not cross each other
- Labels must be on the right side if possible; must not have arrow heads on label lines (some specifications vary — AQA accepts ruled lines without arrowheads)
- Title the drawing: what the organism/cell type is, at what magnification it was observed
- Add a **scale bar** or state the **magnification** on or below the drawing
- The drawing should represent what you actually see, not what you know is there (do not draw structures not visible)

### Calculating magnification from a drawing

- Measure the image size of the drawn cell/structure (in mm, using a ruler)
- Convert to same units as the actual size
- Apply: M = I / A
- Example: drawn cell = 40 mm; actual cell = 0.05 mm → M = 40 / 0.05 = ×800

### Variables and controls (practical skills)

- **Independent variable:** the thing being changed (e.g. which cell type is being observed; which objective lens magnification is used)
- **Dependent variable:** the thing being measured/observed (e.g. the image seen; the size of the cell in the image)
- **Control variables:** same slide preparation method, same stain concentration, same light level, same eyepiece
- **Valid results:** all other variables kept constant; method repeated

### Evaluating the method

Common evaluation points:
- Air bubbles are a source of error — controlled by correct coverslip technique
- Too much stain obscures fine detail — only a small drop needed
- Over-staining or under-staining — stain concentration should be consistent
- Difficulty finding cells at high power — always locate at low power first, then increase
- Bias in drawing — only draw what is seen, not what is expected

## 4. Content reference pack

### Chronology / sequence of the practical

1. Prepare sample (cut thin section of onion skin / collect cheek cells by swabbing inside cheek)
2. Place sample on slide
3. Add one drop of stain
4. Lower coverslip at 45°
5. Secure slide on stage
6. Select lowest objective lens
7. Use coarse focus, then fine focus
8. Find and centre a suitable cell
9. Sketch/draw with pencil
10. Label with ruler
11. State magnification or add scale bar
12. Calculate magnification if required

### Key terms & definitions

- **Slide** — flat glass rectangle on which a specimen is placed for viewing
- **Coverslip** — thin glass square placed over the specimen; protects the lens and keeps the specimen in place
- **Stain** — coloured dye added to increase contrast (make structures visible); iodine (plant) or methylene blue (animal)
- **Objective lens** — the lens closest to the specimen; comes in different magnifications (×4, ×10, ×40)
- **Eyepiece lens** — the lens the viewer looks through; usually ×10
- **Total magnification** — eyepiece lens magnification × objective lens magnification
- **Coarse focus** — large focus knob; gives approximate focus; used only with low-power objective
- **Fine focus** — small focus knob; gives precise, sharp focus; used with all objectives
- **Biological drawing** — a scientific pencil diagram of a specimen; follows strict rules (no shading, ruled labels, stated magnification)
- **Field of view** — the circular area visible through the eyepiece at a given magnification; decreases as magnification increases
- **Stippling** — dots used instead of shading in biological drawings; shows density without blocking detail

### Causes & effects / mechanism chains

- Iodine stain → stains cell wall and nucleus → visible contrast against pale cytoplasm → student can identify and label structures
- Coverslip at 45° → air excluded as coverslip lowers → fewer bubbles → cleaner image
- Start at low power → wide field of view → easy to locate cells → then increase magnification for detail
- Coarse focus at high power → risk of lens crashing into slide → use fine focus only at high power
- Drawing only what is seen → honest record of observation → valid scientific method
- Measuring image size + knowing actual size → apply M = I/A → determines magnification of drawing

### Exam angles

**Common question types:**
- "Describe how you would use a light microscope to observe and draw a plant cell" — 3–5 marks (method steps in order)
- "Explain why iodine is used when preparing a plant cell slide" — 1–2 marks
- "Suggest why the student started with the low-power objective" — 1–2 marks
- "The student drew a cell 45 mm long. The actual cell is 0.09 mm. Calculate the magnification of the drawing." — 2 marks
- "Suggest one improvement to the method described" — 1–2 marks (evaluate a given method)
- "Identify two control variables in this experiment" — 1–2 marks

**Mark scheme keywords:**
- Method: "place on slide" / "add stain" / "lower coverslip at 45° to prevent air bubbles" / "use low-power objective first" / "coarse then fine focus"
- Stain purpose: "to make cell structures visible" / "increase contrast" / "stains nucleus/cell wall"
- Drawing: "pencil" / "continuous lines" / "no shading" / "label lines ruled" / "magnification stated"
- Evaluation: "control variables kept the same" / "repeat" / "reduce air bubbles" / "use fresh stain"

**High-value misconceptions (MisconceptionCheck):**
- "You should use the highest-power objective lens first to see the most detail" → FALSE: always start with lowest power to locate cells; high power has too small a field of view
- "You should shade your biological drawing to show the 3D shape" → FALSE: biological drawings use outlines only; shading is replaced by stippling (dots) if needed
- "A drop of stain makes no difference — cells are already visible" → FALSE: unstained cells are mostly transparent; stain is essential for contrast
- "The coverslip can be placed flat onto the slide" → FALSE: this traps air bubbles; must be lowered at 45°
