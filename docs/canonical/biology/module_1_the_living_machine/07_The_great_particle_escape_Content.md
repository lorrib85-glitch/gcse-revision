## 2. Core argument

**Central claim:**

DRAFT (for user confirmation): Diffusion is the most fundamental transport mechanism in biology — it costs nothing, requires no machinery, and moves substances exactly where they are most needed. But it only works across short distances, and it cannot move things uphill against a concentration gradient. That limitation explains why every large organism on Earth has evolved specialised exchange surfaces — from alveoli to villi to root hairs — all optimised by the same engineering principle: maximise surface area, minimise thickness.

**Evidence for the claim:**
- Oxygen enters the blood in the lungs by diffusion — no energy required, moves from high O₂ (alveolus) to low O₂ (blood)
- CO₂ leaves cells by diffusion — produced by respiration, concentration builds up → diffuses out
- Alveoli: 300 million in each lung → total surface area ~70 m² (same as a tennis court) → maximises rate of gas exchange
- Villi of small intestine: finger-like projections increasing surface area ~600× — absorb digested food by diffusion
- Root hair cells: long extensions increase surface area for water and mineral absorption
- SA:V ratio: as a cell grows larger, surface area grows by square, volume grows by cube → ratio decreases → diffusion cannot meet the cell's needs → multicellular organisms need specialised systems

**Series throughline connection:**
Chapter 7 opens the three-chapter transport series (Ch 7 diffusion → Ch 8 osmosis → Ch 9 active transport). It directly applies the cell membrane concept from Ch 1 and the exchange surface concepts underpin Module 2 (Ch 16 — alveoli; Ch 12 — small intestine). SA:V ratio is one of the most frequently examined calculations across the whole biology course.

## 3. Specification requirements

### Diffusion — definition

- **Definition:** Diffusion is the movement of particles from an area of **higher concentration** to an area of **lower concentration** (down the concentration gradient) by **random particle movement**
- Diffusion is a **passive process** — it does NOT require energy
- Net movement continues until equilibrium is reached (equal concentrations on both sides)
- Can occur in liquids and gases
- Occurs across all cell membranes (the membrane must be permeable to the substance)

### Factors affecting rate of diffusion

1. **Concentration gradient:** steeper gradient (greater difference in concentration) → faster rate of diffusion
2. **Temperature:** higher temperature → particles have more kinetic energy → move faster → faster diffusion
3. **Surface area:** larger surface area → more particles can cross simultaneously → faster rate
4. **Diffusion distance (thickness):** shorter distance (thinner membrane/surface) → faster rate
5. **Size of molecule:** smaller molecules diffuse faster than larger ones

### Exchange surfaces — features

Large multicellular organisms need specialised exchange surfaces because:
- Their cells are far from the exchange surface → diffusion alone too slow
- SA:V ratio decreases as organism size increases → diffusion cannot supply all cells fast enough

**Features of an efficient exchange surface:**
- **Large surface area** — maximises contact between environment and organism
- **Thin** (short diffusion distance) — minimises the path particles must travel
- **Well-supplied with blood** (in animals) — maintains steep concentration gradient by constantly removing/delivering particles
- **Moist** (for gas exchange) — gases dissolve in moisture before diffusing

### Examples of exchange surfaces (AQA required)

**Alveoli (lungs — gas exchange):**
- ~300 million alveoli in each lung
- Total surface area ~70 m² (comparable to a tennis court)
- Alveolar walls are one cell thick (and capillary walls are one cell thick) → very short diffusion distance (~0.1–0.5 µm)
- Extensive capillary network → blood flow constantly removes O₂ and brings CO₂ → maintains steep gradient
- Moist inner surface → gases dissolve and diffuse

**Villi (small intestine — nutrient absorption):**
- Finger-like projections lining the small intestine
- Each villus is covered in microvilli (brush border) → increases surface area further (~600×)
- Thin epithelial cells → short diffusion distance
- Rich capillary supply (and lacteals for fat absorption) → removes absorbed nutrients → maintains gradient
- Glucose and amino acids enter by diffusion (and some by active transport — Ch 9)

**Root hair cells (roots — water and mineral absorption):**
- Long thin extension increases surface area (already covered in Ch 4)
- Water absorption by osmosis (Ch 8), mineral ions by active transport (Ch 9)

### Surface area to volume ratio (SA:V)

- **Why it matters:** As organisms increase in size, their volume increases faster than their surface area → SA:V ratio decreases → the surface is inadequate to supply the whole volume by diffusion alone
- **Formula:** SA:V = total surface area ÷ total volume
- **Cube example:**
  - 1 cm cube: SA = 6 cm²; V = 1 cm³; SA:V = 6:1
  - 2 cm cube: SA = 24 cm²; V = 8 cm³; SA:V = 3:1
  - 4 cm cube: SA = 96 cm²; V = 64 cm³; SA:V = 1.5:1
  - As size doubles, SA:V ratio halves — the large cube has a much worse ratio
- **Single-celled organisms:** very high SA:V → diffusion alone is sufficient → no need for specialised exchange systems
- **Large multicellular organisms:** very low SA:V → need specialised exchange surfaces AND transport systems (blood, xylem/phloem) to carry substances around

## 4. Content reference pack

### Key terms & definitions

- **Diffusion** — movement of particles from high to low concentration (down concentration gradient) by random movement; passive; no energy required
- **Concentration gradient** — the difference in concentration of a substance between two areas; drives diffusion
- **Passive process** — a process that does not require energy from the cell (contrast with active transport, Ch 9)
- **Equilibrium** — state where particles are equally distributed and there is no net movement
- **Exchange surface** — a surface adapted for efficient transfer of substances between an organism and its environment (or between compartments)
- **Surface area to volume ratio (SA:V ratio)** — the ratio of an object's surface area to its volume; decreases as size increases; determines whether diffusion can meet the organism's needs
- **Alveolus (pl. alveoli)** — tiny air sac in the lung; site of gas exchange (O₂ into blood, CO₂ out)
- **Villus (pl. villi)** — finger-like projection of the small intestine lining; increases surface area for nutrient absorption
- **Microvilli** — microscopic projections on each villus cell; further increase surface area (also called brush border)
- **Capillary** — smallest blood vessel; one cell thick; surrounds alveoli and villi; maintains concentration gradient

### Causes & effects / mechanism chains

- Respiration in cells → CO₂ produced → concentration in cells > in blood → CO₂ diffuses out of cells into blood → carried to lungs → CO₂ concentration in blood > in alveolus → CO₂ diffuses into alveolus → exhaled
- Breathing maintains high O₂ in alveolus → O₂ concentration in alveolus > in blood → O₂ diffuses into blood → carried to cells → O₂ concentration in blood > in cells → O₂ diffuses into cells
- Larger organism → more cells → lower SA:V → diffusion too slow → natural selection favoured specialised exchange surfaces → alveoli/villi/root hairs evolved
- Higher temperature → more kinetic energy → faster random movement of particles → faster diffusion
- Steeper concentration gradient → greater net movement per unit time → faster net diffusion
- Thin surface → shorter diffusion path → faster diffusion

### Exam angles

**Common question types:**
- "Define diffusion" — 2 marks (most common; must include: high to low concentration; passive/no energy)
- "Explain why the alveoli are well-adapted for gas exchange" — 4–6 marks (large SA, thin wall, rich blood supply, moist, maintain gradient)
- "Calculate the SA:V ratio of a 3 cm cube" — 2 marks
- "Explain why large organisms need specialised exchange systems" — 2–3 marks (SA:V decreases; diffusion too slow)
- "State two factors that increase the rate of diffusion" — 2 marks (any two of: steeper gradient, higher temp, larger SA, shorter distance)

**Mark scheme keywords:**
- Definition: "higher concentration to lower concentration" (not just "concentration gradient"); "passive" / "no energy required"; "random movement of particles"
- Alveoli: "large surface area", "thin walls" / "one cell thick", "good blood supply" / "maintains concentration gradient", "moist surface"
- SA:V: must show working for calculation marks; state the answer as a ratio
- Factors: "steeper concentration gradient", "higher temperature", "larger surface area", "shorter/thinner/shorter diffusion distance"

**High-value misconceptions (MisconceptionCheck):**
- "Diffusion requires energy" → FALSE: diffusion is passive; it uses the random kinetic energy particles already have (not cellular energy/ATP)
- "Diffusion only happens in living organisms" → FALSE: diffusion occurs in any medium wherever there is a concentration gradient (in non-living systems too)
- "All substances can diffuse through a cell membrane" → FALSE: cell membranes are selectively (partially) permeable; only small non-polar molecules (e.g. O₂, CO₂) diffuse freely; large or polar molecules need special transport
- "Diffusion is a one-way process" → FALSE: particles move in both directions; there is NET movement from high to low, but individual particles move randomly in all directions

**SpotTheError candidates:**
- "Diffusion is the movement of particles from a low concentration to a high concentration using energy." (two errors: direction is wrong; diffusion is passive, no energy)
- "The alveoli are adapted for gas exchange because they have a very large volume, which gives them more space to hold gases." (error: it is large SURFACE AREA, not large volume, that matters; high SA:V ratio, not volume itself)
