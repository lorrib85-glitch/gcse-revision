## 2. Core argument

**Central claim:**

DRAFT (for user confirmation): Specialised cells are the result of the same basic cell design being modified — sometimes radically — to perform one job with maximum efficiency. Every structural adaptation has a functional explanation, and that explanation is always the same question: what does this cell need to do, and how does its structure help it do that faster, further or more reliably?

**Evidence for the claim:**
- Sperm cell: tail (flagellum), acrosome, densely packed mitochondria — each feature maps directly to one function (movement, egg penetration, energy supply)
- Root hair cell: extended surface area, thin cell wall, no chloroplasts — every feature is a tradeoff for absorbing water and minerals underground
- Xylem: dead at maturity (no cytoplasm), lignified walls — stripped down to a hollow pipe; the cell sacrifices its life to become better at water transport
- Neurone: axon up to 1m long, myelin sheath, branching dendrites — geometry shaped entirely by the need to carry electrical signals quickly over distance
- All six cells share the same original blueprint from Ch 1 — the adaptations are modifications, not replacements

**Series throughline connection:**
Chapter 4 is the application of Chapter 1. Every structure introduced in Ch 1 (mitochondria, nucleus, cell membrane, chloroplasts, cell wall) is now seen through the lens of "why does this cell have MORE or FEWER of these?" ## 3. Specification requirements

### Sperm cell

- **Function:** swim to and fertilise an egg cell
- **Adaptations:**
  - Long tail (flagellum) → allows the cell to swim through fluid to reach the egg
  - Many mitochondria (concentrated in the mid-piece / between head and tail) → provide energy (ATP from aerobic respiration) for tail movement
  - Acrosome (cap at the front of the head) → contains enzymes that digest through the zona pellucida (egg coat) to allow fertilisation
  - Haploid nucleus (23 chromosomes) → so that when sperm and egg fuse, the zygote has the correct diploid number (46 chromosomes)
  - Streamlined head shape → reduces drag when swimming

### Nerve cell (motor neurone)

- **Function:** carry electrical impulses from the CNS (brain/spinal cord) to effectors (muscles/glands)
- **Adaptations:**
  - Long axon (can be up to 1 m) → carries impulses over long distances without the need for many synapses
  - Myelin sheath (fatty insulating layer around the axon) → speeds up nerve impulse transmission (impulse "jumps" between nodes of Ranvier — saltatory conduction)
  - Many dendrites (branching extensions) → receive signals from many other neurones simultaneously
  - Synaptic knobs at the end of the axon → allow chemical neurotransmitters to be released to the next cell

### Muscle cell

- **Function:** contract to produce movement
- **Adaptations:**
  - Contains myofibrils (strands of protein — actin and myosin) → enable the cell to contract and relax
  - Many mitochondria → provide energy (ATP) for sustained contraction
  - Can store glycogen → fuel reserve that can be broken down to glucose for respiration during exercise

### Root hair cell

- **Function:** absorb water and mineral ions from the soil
- **Adaptations:**
  - Long, thin extension (root hair) → greatly increases surface area for absorption of water by osmosis and mineral ions by active transport
  - Thin cell wall → short diffusion/osmosis distance; water enters quickly
  - No chloroplasts → underground, no light reaches root cells, so photosynthesis is impossible; chloroplasts would be wasted
  - Large permanent vacuole → maintains a low water potential inside the cell; keeps osmotic gradient for water uptake
  - Many mitochondria → provide energy (ATP) for active transport of mineral ions against the concentration gradient

### Xylem cell

- **Function:** transport water and dissolved mineral ions from roots to leaves (unidirectional, upward)
- **Adaptations:**
  - Dead at maturity (no cytoplasm, no cell contents) → forms a hollow, unobstructed tube for water flow
  - Thick, lignified cell walls (lignin deposited in spiral/ring/ladder patterns) → provide structural support; prevent xylem from collapsing under tension; waterproof (prevents water leaking out)
  - No end walls (end walls break down as cell matures) → continuous column of water from root to leaf
  - Pits in cell wall → allow lateral movement of water between adjacent xylem vessels

### Phloem cell

- **Function:** transport dissolved sugars (sucrose) and other organic molecules from leaves to all parts of the plant (bidirectional)
- **Adaptations:**
  - Sieve plates (perforated end walls between sieve tube elements) → allow flow of sap between cells
  - Living cells (unlike xylem) but with little cytoplasm → minimises resistance to flow
  - Companion cells adjacent to each sieve tube element → have many mitochondria to provide energy for active loading/unloading of sucrose; maintain the living sieve tube cells (which have no nucleus of their own)

## 4. Content reference pack

### Key terms & definitions

- **Specialised cell** — a cell that has developed specific structural features to perform one particular function efficiently (also called a differentiated cell)
- **Differentiation** — the process by which a cell becomes specialised; a stem cell develops into a specialised cell by switching specific genes on or off
- **Adaptation** — a structural feature of a cell that makes it better suited to its function
- **Acrosome** — enzyme-containing cap on the head of a sperm cell; enables penetration of the egg's outer layer
- **Axon** — long projection of a nerve cell that carries electrical impulses away from the cell body
- **Myelin sheath** — fatty (lipid-rich) insulating layer around the axon of some neurones; speeds up impulse transmission
- **Flagellum** — whip-like tail on a sperm cell (or bacterium); used for locomotion
- **Dendrites** — branching extensions of a nerve cell that receive signals from other neurones
- **Myofibril** — contractile protein strand within a muscle cell (contains actin and myosin)
- **Root hair** — long, thin extension of a root hair cell that dramatically increases surface area for absorption
- **Xylem** — plant vascular tissue; dead, hollow, lignified cells that transport water and minerals upward
- **Phloem** — plant vascular tissue; living cells with sieve plates that transport dissolved sugars in both directions
- **Lignin** — tough waterproof polymer deposited in xylem cell walls; provides structural support
- **Sieve plate** — perforated end wall of a phloem sieve tube element; allows sap to pass through
- **Companion cell** — phloem cell adjacent to sieve tube element; provides energy for active transport of sugars

### Causes & effects / mechanism chains

- Root hair cell has long extension → increased surface area → more contact with soil water → faster absorption by osmosis
- Root hair cell has no chloroplasts → underground, no light → photosynthesis impossible → chloroplasts would serve no function
- Xylem cell dies at maturity → cytoplasm removed → hollow tube → unobstructed water flow from root to leaf
- Xylem cell wall lignified → waterproof → prevents water escaping sideways → maintains column for upward flow
- Sperm cell has many mitochondria → lots of aerobic respiration → lots of ATP → sustained tail movement → reaches egg
- Sperm cell has acrosome → contains digestive enzymes → breaks down zona pellucida → allows nucleus to enter egg
- Nerve cell has myelin sheath → insulates axon → impulse jumps between gaps (nodes of Ranvier) → faster transmission
- Muscle cell contains myofibrils → actin and myosin filaments slide past each other → cell shortens → muscle contracts

### Exam angles

**Common question types:**
- "Give two adaptations of a sperm cell and explain how each helps it carry out its function" — 4 marks
- "Explain why root hair cells do not contain chloroplasts" — 2 marks
- "Describe one way a xylem cell is adapted to transport water" — 2 marks
- "Explain the role of companion cells in the phloem" — 2 marks
- "Compare the structure of xylem and phloem" — 3–4 marks

**Mark scheme keywords:**
- Sperm: "tail/flagellum for movement", "many mitochondria to provide energy for movement", "acrosome contains enzymes to digest outer layer of egg"
- Root hair: "large surface area to absorb water by osmosis", "no chloroplasts because underground / no light / cannot photosynthesise"
- Xylem: "dead/no cytoplasm so hollow tube", "lignified walls for support", "no end walls so water can flow freely"
- Phloem: "sieve plates allow movement of sap", "companion cells provide energy for active transport"

**High-value misconceptions (MisconceptionCheck):**
- "Xylem cells are alive and pump water up the plant" → FALSE: xylem cells are dead; water moves passively by transpiration pull
- "Root hair cells have chloroplasts to make energy for absorbing minerals" → FALSE: they use mitochondria (respiration) for active transport; no chloroplasts because underground
- "All nerve cells have a myelin sheath" → FALSE: some neurones are unmyelinated; myelin is found on neurones where speed matters most
- "Sperm cells have more mitochondria than usual because they make more ATP" → PARTIALLY CORRECT but imprecise: mitochondria release energy via respiration (they do not make it); phrasing matters

**SpotTheError candidates:**
- "Root hair cells are adapted for absorption by having chloroplasts to make energy and a large surface area." (error: root hair cells have NO chloroplasts — they use mitochondria for respiration to power active transport)
- "Xylem cells are living cells that use energy to pump water up from the roots." (two errors: xylem cells are DEAD; water movement in xylem is PASSIVE — driven by transpiration pull, not active pumping)
