## 2. Core argument

**Central claim:**

DRAFT (for user confirmation): Your body replaces billions of cells every day — skin, gut lining, red blood cells — using a copying process so precise that the daughter cells are genetically identical to the original. Mitosis is the body's copy machine. Understanding it means understanding both how growth works and how cancer begins when the machine breaks down.

**Evidence for the claim:**
- Human body replaces ~3.8 million red blood cells every second
- Mitosis produces two daughter cells genetically identical to the parent — same 46 chromosomes
- The cell cycle ensures DNA is replicated before division so each daughter gets a full set
- Cancer is uncontrolled mitosis — the copy machine loses its off switch (cell cycle control breaks down)
- Mitosis is also used for asexual reproduction (some animals, most plants via cuttings) — producing clones

**Series throughline connection:**
Chapter 5 is the mechanism behind growth — the outcome of Chapters 1–4 (cells → specialised cells) requires cell division to produce them. It also provides the foundation for Chapter 6 (stem cells differentiate after mitosis) and Chapters 31–37 (inheritance — meiosis contrasts with mitosis; chromosomes and DNA introduced here). The link between uncontrolled mitosis and cancer connects to Chapter 18 (cancer, tumours, risk factors).

## 3. Specification requirements

### Chromosomes and DNA

- Chromosomes are found in the **nucleus**
- Chromosomes are made of **DNA**
- DNA carries genetic information in **genes** — a gene is a section of DNA that codes for a specific protein
- Humans have **46 chromosomes** arranged in **23 pairs** (diploid = 2n = 46)
- Each chromosome carries hundreds to thousands of genes

### The cell cycle

The cell cycle is the ordered sequence of events a cell goes through from its formation to division into two daughter cells.

Three stages:

1. **Interphase** (the longest stage — cells spend most of their life here):
   - **G1 phase (first gap / growth):** cell grows; proteins are synthesised; organelles increase in number
   - **S phase (synthesis):** DNA is replicated — each chromosome is duplicated to form two identical chromatids joined at the centromere
   - **G2 phase (second gap / growth):** further growth and protein synthesis; cell prepares for division; mitochondria divide

2. **Mitosis** (nuclear division — see below)

3. **Cytokinesis** (cytoplasmic division):
   - The cytoplasm and cell membrane divide to form two separate cells
   - In animal cells: cell membrane pinches inward
   - In plant cells: a new cell wall (cell plate) forms between the two daughter nuclei

### Mitosis (the division stage of the cell cycle)

Produces **2 daughter cells** that are genetically identical to each other and to the parent cell.

Four stages (students should know the sequence and key events):

1. **Prophase:**
   - Chromosomes condense and become visible (short, fat)
   - Nuclear envelope breaks down
   - Spindle fibres begin to form from centrioles (animal cells) or from the cell's periphery

2. **Metaphase:**
   - Chromosomes line up along the equator (middle) of the cell
   - Spindle fibres attach to the centromere of each chromosome

3. **Anaphase:**
   - Sister chromatids are pulled apart to opposite poles of the cell
   - Chromatids separate — one copy of each chromosome moves to each pole

4. **Telophase:**
   - New nuclear envelopes form around each set of chromosomes at the poles
   - Chromosomes decondense
   - Cytokinesis begins

**AQA GCSE level requirement:** Students are NOT expected to name all four stages (prophase, metaphase, anaphase, telophase) — they should know the sequence of events: chromosomes condense → line up at equator → pulled to opposite poles → nuclear envelopes reform → cell divides. Naming all four stages is helpful but labelling diagrams is the key skill.

### Outcomes of mitosis

- **2 daughter cells** produced
- Each daughter cell has the **same number of chromosomes** as the parent (diploid; in humans, 46)
- Daughter cells are **genetically identical** to each other and to the parent
- Mitosis is used for: **growth**, **repair**, **replacement of cells**, **asexual reproduction** (in some organisms)

### Cancer link

- Normally, the cell cycle is tightly controlled — cells only divide when needed
- Cancer = when cell cycle control breaks down → cells divide uncontrollably → tumour forms
- Caused by mutations in genes that control the cell cycle (Ch 18 covers this in detail)

### Key terms

- **Chromosome:** thread-like structure of DNA in the nucleus; humans have 46 (23 pairs)
- **DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid):** molecule that carries genetic information; made of two strands twisted into a double helix
- **Gene:** section of DNA that codes for a specific protein
- **Diploid:** cell containing two full sets of chromosomes (2n); human diploid cells have 46 chromosomes
- **Haploid:** cell containing one set of chromosomes (n); human gametes (sperm and egg) have 23 chromosomes (covered in Ch 32)
- **Cell cycle:** the ordered sequence of events from cell formation to division into two daughter cells
- **Interphase:** the non-dividing phase of the cell cycle; includes DNA replication and cell growth
- **Mitosis:** type of cell division producing two genetically identical daughter cells (diploid → diploid)
- **Cytokinesis:** division of the cytoplasm following mitosis
- **Chromatid:** one of the two identical copies of a replicated chromosome, joined at the centromere
- **Centromere:** the region where two sister chromatids are joined
- **Spindle fibres:** protein fibres that pull chromatids to opposite poles during mitosis
- **Tumour:** mass of cells formed by uncontrolled division; benign (localised) or malignant (cancer — invasive/spreading)

## 4. Content reference pack

### Chronology / timeline

- 1830s: Mathias Schleiden and Theodor Schwann — all living things are made of cells (cell theory)
- 1855: Rudolf Virchow — "Omnis cellula e cellula" (all cells arise from pre-existing cells) — foundational for mitosis concept
- 1882: Walther Flemming — first described and named mitosis (from the Greek "mitos" — thread); observed chromosomes moving in dividing cells
- 1953: Watson and Crick — DNA double helix structure described; explained how DNA replication is possible
- Modern oncology: cell cycle control genes (tumour suppressors like p53; proto-oncogenes like Ras) — mutations cause cancer

### Key people / figures

- **Walther Flemming (1843–1905):** German biologist; discovered chromosomes and described mitosis in 1882 using aniline dyes; named the process after "mitos" (thread) for the thread-like chromosomes he observed
- **Rudolf Virchow (1821–1902):** German physician; established that all cells come from pre-existing cells — the cellular basis of disease and growth

### Causes & effects / mechanism chains

- Interphase: DNA replication → each chromosome duplicated into 2 identical chromatids → cell now has 92 chromatids (46 pairs)
- Mitosis: chromatids separate → one copy to each pole → nuclear envelope reforms → 2 nuclei each with 46 chromosomes
- Cytokinesis: cytoplasm divides → 2 daughter cells each with 46 chromosomes → genetically identical to parent and each other
- Gene mutation in cell cycle control → loss of growth control → uncontrolled mitosis → tumour → if invasive = cancer
- Asexual reproduction via mitosis → offspring genetically identical to parent → clone

### Human body cell replacement rates (context, not examinable):
- Red blood cells: replaced every ~120 days; ~3.8 million per second
- Gut lining cells: replaced every ~2–4 days (some sources say every 3–5 days)
- Skin cells: replaced every ~2–4 weeks
- Liver cells: replaced approximately every 300–500 days
- Most neurones: not replaced after early development (relevant to Ch 27)

### Exam angles

**Common question types:**
- "Describe the stages of mitosis" — 3–4 marks; sequence of events, not necessarily names
- "Explain why cells produced by mitosis are genetically identical" — 2 marks (DNA replicated in interphase; same chromosomes to each daughter)
- "State the number of chromosomes in a human body cell" — 1 mark (46)
- "Give one reason why mitosis is important in the human body" — 1 mark (growth / repair / replacement)
- "Explain how cancer is related to the cell cycle" — 2 marks (uncontrolled division; cell cycle control breaks down)

**Mark scheme keywords:**
- "DNA replication in interphase" — essential context for why daughters are identical
- "Chromosomes line up at the equator"
- "Chromatids pulled to opposite poles" (not "chromosomes split" — chromatids separate)
- "Two genetically identical daughter cells"
- "46 chromosomes" (human; not "23 pairs" alone — must be able to say total number)
- Cancer: "uncontrolled cell division" or "cell cycle control breaks down"

**High-value misconceptions (MisconceptionCheck):**
- "Mitosis produces 4 cells" → FALSE: mitosis produces 2 daughter cells (meiosis produces 4 — commonly confused; important distinction)
- "Mitosis produces genetically different cells" → FALSE: both daughters are genetically IDENTICAL to the parent
- "DNA is copied during mitosis" → FALSE: DNA is replicated during INTERPHASE (S phase) before mitosis begins
- "Cell division and mitosis are the same thing" → IMPRECISE: mitosis = nuclear division; cell division = mitosis + cytokinesis; they are closely linked but technically distinct
- "Human gametes (sperm and eggs) are produced by mitosis" → FALSE: gametes are produced by MEIOSIS (Ch 32); mitosis produces body (somatic) cells

**SpotTheError candidates:**
- "During mitosis, DNA is copied as the chromosomes line up at the equator." (error: DNA is replicated during interphase, not during mitosis)
- "Mitosis produces four genetically different daughter cells, each with 23 chromosomes." (three errors: mitosis produces 2 cells, they are identical, and human mitosis daughter cells have 46 chromosomes — the student is confusing with meiosis)
