Biology Topic 4: Bioenergetics

## Chapter purpose

This chapter starts AQA Biology Topic 4: Bioenergetics.

It should come after Infection and response in the full AQA order, but the concept also links back to plant cells and plant transport.

This chapter replaces the earlier misplaced `sci_bio_w1` idea as the correct Bioenergetics photosynthesis chapter.

## Learning goals

By the end, students should be able to:

- describe photosynthesis as an endothermic reaction
- write the photosynthesis word equation
- write the balanced symbol equation where required
- explain the role of chlorophyll and chloroplasts
- describe how plants use glucose
- explain limiting factors of photosynthesis
- interpret graphs showing limiting factors
- describe the required practical for light intensity and photosynthesis
- explain inverse-square style light-distance patterns without overcomplicating them

## Hook

Plants do not eat soil.

They build themselves from carbon dioxide and water, using light energy.

A tree trunk, a strawberry, a blade of grass: all built using glucose made by photosynthesis.

It sounds calm. It is actually one of the most important chemical reactions on Earth.

## Core content

### 1. What photosynthesis is

Photosynthesis is an endothermic reaction.

This means it transfers energy from the environment.

In photosynthesis, light energy is transferred to chemical energy stored in glucose.

AQA-safe wording:

> Photosynthesis transfers light energy into chemical energy stored in glucose.

Exam trap:

Do not say plants “create energy”. Energy is transferred, not created.

### 2. Word equation

The word equation is:

> carbon dioxide + water → glucose + oxygen

Light energy is required.

Chlorophyll absorbs light energy.

Photosynthesis happens in chloroplasts.

### 3. Symbol equation

Students should recognise the balanced symbol equation:

> 6CO2 + 6H2O → C6H12O6 + 6O2

Do not let the equation become the whole lesson. Students need to understand what enters, what leaves and why glucose matters.

### 4. Where the reactants come from

Carbon dioxide:

- diffuses into the leaf through stomata
- moves through air spaces in the spongy mesophyll

Water:

- absorbed by root hair cells by osmosis
- transported to leaves in xylem

Light energy:

- absorbed by chlorophyll in chloroplasts

### 5. Products of photosynthesis

Glucose:

- used by the plant
- stored or converted into other useful substances

Oxygen:

- released as a by-product
- diffuses out of the leaf through stomata

### 6. Uses of glucose

Use the memory trick **SCARF**:

- **S**tarch for storage
- **C**ellulose for cell walls
- **A**mino acids for proteins, using nitrate ions from soil
- **R**espiration to transfer energy
- **F**ats and oils for storage, especially in seeds

AQA-safe explanation:

Plants use glucose for respiration, convert it to insoluble starch for storage, use it to make cellulose cell walls, combine it with nitrate ions to make amino acids, and convert it into lipids for storage.

Exam trap:

Plants still respire. Photosynthesis makes glucose; respiration transfers energy from glucose.

### 7. Limiting factors

A limiting factor is something that limits the rate of photosynthesis.

AQA limiting factors:

- light intensity
- carbon dioxide concentration
- temperature

If one factor is in short supply, increasing another factor may not increase the rate.

### 8. Light intensity

As light intensity increases, the rate of photosynthesis increases at first.

Eventually the rate levels off because another factor becomes limiting, such as carbon dioxide concentration or temperature.

### 9. Carbon dioxide concentration

As carbon dioxide concentration increases, the rate of photosynthesis increases at first.

Eventually the rate levels off because another factor becomes limiting, such as light intensity or temperature.

### 10. Temperature

As temperature increases, the rate of photosynthesis increases at first because enzymes work faster.

Above the optimum temperature, enzymes involved in photosynthesis denature.

The active site changes shape, so the rate decreases.

### 11. Required practical: light intensity and photosynthesis

Aim:

Investigate the effect of light intensity on the rate of photosynthesis.

Common method using pondweed:

1. Place pondweed in water with sodium hydrogencarbonate solution to provide carbon dioxide.
2. Place a lamp at a measured distance from the pondweed.
3. Allow time for the pondweed to adjust.
4. Count the number of bubbles produced in a fixed time.
5. Move the lamp to different distances.
6. Repeat and calculate a mean.
7. Control temperature, carbon dioxide concentration, pondweed length/species and time.

What bubbles show:

The bubbles are oxygen released during photosynthesis.

Rate can be estimated by:

> number of bubbles / time

or more accurately by measuring volume of oxygen produced per unit time.

### 12. Inverse-square light idea

If distance from the lamp increases, light intensity decreases.

For Higher students, light intensity can be calculated using:

> light intensity = 1 / distance squared

Example:

At 20 cm:

1 / 20² = 1 / 400 = 0.0025 arbitrary units

Keep this as a calculation skill, not a physics lecture.

### 13. Graph interpretation

Students should be able to explain graph shapes.

If the graph rises then levels off:

- the factor on the x-axis is limiting at first
- then another factor becomes limiting

If temperature graph rises then falls:

- enzymes work faster up to the optimum
- above optimum, enzymes denature

## Interactive tasks to build

### Task 1: Equation builder

Build:

> carbon dioxide + water → glucose + oxygen

Distractors:

- nitrogen
- starch
- energy
- chlorophyll

Feedback:

Light energy is needed, but it is not a substance in the equation.

### Task 2: SCARF glucose uses

Match:

- starch → storage
- cellulose → cell walls
- amino acids → proteins
- respiration → energy transfer
- fats and oils → storage in seeds

### Task 3: Limiting factor graph judgement

Prompt:

A graph of photosynthesis rate against light intensity rises, then levels off. Explain why.

Mark points:

- light intensity is limiting at first
- increasing light increases rate
- graph levels off because another factor becomes limiting
- carbon dioxide or temperature may now limit the rate

### Task 4: Practical method order

Put in order:

- place pondweed in sodium hydrogencarbonate solution
- place lamp at measured distance
- allow pondweed to adjust
- count bubbles for fixed time
- repeat at different distances
- repeat readings and calculate mean

### Task 5: Spot the error

Student answer:

> Plants create energy during photosynthesis.

Correction:

Photosynthesis transfers light energy into chemical energy stored in glucose. Energy is not created.

### Task 6: Required practical variables

Sort:

Independent variable:

- distance of lamp / light intensity

Dependent variable:

- number of bubbles per minute / oxygen produced per minute

Control variables:

- temperature
- carbon dioxide concentration
- length/species of pondweed
- time counted

## Exam practice

### Question 1 — equation

Write the word equation for photosynthesis.  
2 marks

Answer:

carbon dioxide + water → glucose + oxygen

### Question 2 — glucose uses

Describe three ways a plant uses glucose made in photosynthesis.  
3 marks

Mark points:

- used in respiration
- converted to starch for storage
- used to make cellulose cell walls
- combined with nitrate ions to make amino acids/proteins
- converted to fats/oils for storage

### Question 3 — limiting factors

A graph shows that increasing light intensity increases the rate of photosynthesis at first, but then the rate levels off.

Explain why.  
4 marks

Mark points:

- light intensity is limiting at first
- more light increases rate
- graph levels off because light is no longer limiting
- another factor such as carbon dioxide concentration or temperature becomes limiting

### Question 4 — temperature

Explain why photosynthesis decreases above the optimum temperature.  
3 marks

Mark points:

- enzymes involved in photosynthesis denature
- active site changes shape
- substrates no longer fit / fewer enzyme-substrate complexes form

### Question 5 — required practical

Describe how to investigate the effect of light intensity on the rate of photosynthesis.  
6 marks

Mark points:

- use pondweed in sodium hydrogencarbonate solution
- place lamp at measured distance
- count bubbles / measure oxygen in a fixed time
- change distance or light intensity
- control temperature/carbon dioxide/pondweed length/time
- repeat and calculate mean

### Question 6 — inverse square calculation

Calculate the light intensity at 10 cm using:

light intensity = 1 / distance²

2 marks

Mark points:

- 1 / 10²
- 1 / 100 = 0.01 arbitrary units
