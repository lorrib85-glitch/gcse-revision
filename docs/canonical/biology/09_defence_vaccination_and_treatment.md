# Biology Chapter 9 — Defence, vaccination and treatment

AQA Combined Science: Trilogy 8464  
Biology Topic 3: Infection and response

## Chapter purpose

This chapter follows Chapter 8: Pathogens and disease. Students already know the main pathogen groups and named diseases. Chapter 9 explains how the body defends itself and how medicine helps prevent and treat disease.

## Learning goals

By the end, students should be able to:

- describe non-specific human defence systems
- explain how white blood cells defend the body
- describe phagocytosis, antibody production and antitoxin production
- explain how vaccination protects individuals and populations
- explain why antibiotics work on bacteria but not viruses
- explain antibiotic resistance and why it is a problem
- describe how new drugs are discovered and tested
- explain the stages of preclinical and clinical testing
- evaluate drug testing data and risk

## Hook

Your body is not helpless.

Before a pathogen even reaches your blood, it has to get past skin, mucus, stomach acid and tiny hairs in your airways.

If it breaks through, white blood cells become the defence team: some swallow invaders, some make antibodies, and some neutralise toxins.

Vaccines are the training mission. Antibiotics are the targeted weapon. Drug trials are how we check the weapon is safe.

## Prior knowledge retrieval

1. What is a pathogen?  
A microorganism that causes disease.

2. What type of pathogen causes salmonella and gonorrhoea?  
Bacteria.

3. What type of pathogen causes measles and HIV?  
Viruses.

4. Why do antibiotics not treat viruses?  
Viruses reproduce inside body cells, while antibiotics target bacteria.

## Core content

### 1. Non-specific defence systems

The body has several first-line defences.

#### Skin

- acts as a physical barrier
- produces antimicrobial secretions
- platelets help form scabs to seal cuts

#### Nose

- has hairs and mucus to trap particles and pathogens

#### Trachea and bronchi

- secrete mucus to trap pathogens
- have cilia that waft mucus up to the throat to be swallowed

#### Stomach

- produces hydrochloric acid
- acid kills many pathogens swallowed in food or mucus

Exam wording:

> The body has non-specific defences that stop pathogens entering or surviving long enough to cause disease.

### 2. White blood cells

White blood cells defend the body in three main ways:

1. phagocytosis
2. antibody production
3. antitoxin production

### 3. Phagocytosis

Phagocytosis means white blood cells engulf and digest pathogens.

Simple sequence:

1. White blood cell detects pathogen.
2. White blood cell moves around it.
3. Pathogen is engulfed.
4. Enzymes digest the pathogen.

Exam wording:

> Some white blood cells engulf and digest pathogens by phagocytosis.

### 4. Antibodies

Some white blood cells produce antibodies.

Antibodies are specific to the antigens on a pathogen.

Key ideas:

- antigens are molecules on the pathogen surface
- one type of antibody fits one antigen shape
- antibodies help destroy the pathogen
- after infection, some white blood cells remain as memory cells
- memory cells allow a faster response if the same pathogen returns

### 5. Antitoxins

Some white blood cells produce antitoxins.

Antitoxins neutralise toxins released by pathogens.

Exam trap:

Antibodies target pathogens/antigens. Antitoxins neutralise toxins.

### 6. Vaccination

A vaccine contains a small quantity of dead or inactive pathogen, or parts of a pathogen.

This stimulates white blood cells to produce antibodies.

Memory cells remain in the body.

If the real pathogen enters later:

- memory cells recognise it quickly
- antibodies are produced faster and in larger amounts
- the pathogen is destroyed before it causes serious illness

Exam wording:

> Vaccination stimulates antibody production and memory cell formation, giving a faster secondary immune response.

### 7. Herd immunity

If a large proportion of the population is vaccinated, the pathogen is less likely to spread.

This helps protect people who cannot be vaccinated or who are more vulnerable.

Use careful wording:

Vaccination reduces spread; it does not guarantee no one will ever get the disease.

### 8. Antibiotics and painkillers

#### Antibiotics

Antibiotics kill bacteria or stop them growing.

They do not kill viruses.

Example:

- penicillin is an antibiotic

#### Painkillers

Painkillers treat symptoms such as pain or fever.

They do not kill pathogens.

Exam trap:

Painkillers make you feel better. They do not cure the infection.

### 9. Antibiotic resistance

Antibiotic resistance happens when bacteria evolve so that an antibiotic no longer kills them.

Sequence:

1. A mutation produces a resistant bacterium.
2. Antibiotic kills non-resistant bacteria.
3. Resistant bacteria survive.
4. Resistant bacteria reproduce.
5. The resistant strain spreads.

Why it is a problem:

- infections become harder to treat
- doctors may need stronger or different antibiotics
- some infections could become untreatable

Reducing resistance:

- avoid unnecessary antibiotic use
- complete prescribed courses
- restrict antibiotic use in farming and medicine
- develop new antibiotics

### 10. Drug discovery

Many useful drugs were originally found in plants or microorganisms.

Examples:

- digitalis from foxgloves, used for heart conditions
- aspirin from willow
- penicillin from Penicillium mould

Modern drug discovery often involves designing and testing new compounds.

### 11. Drug testing

New drugs are tested for:

- toxicity — is it harmful?
- efficacy — does it work?
- dose — what amount is safe and effective?

### 12. Preclinical testing

Before testing on humans, drugs are tested:

- in the laboratory using cells and tissues
- on live animals

Purpose:

- check toxicity
- check whether the drug works
- find a safe starting dose

### 13. Clinical trials

Clinical trials test drugs on humans.

Stages:

1. Healthy volunteers: very low doses to check safety.
2. Patients: test whether the drug works and find the best dose.
3. Larger trials: compare effectiveness and monitor side effects.

Key features:

- placebo: treatment with no active drug
- double-blind trial: neither doctor nor patient knows who has the real drug
- peer review: other scientists check the research before publication

Why double-blind?

It reduces bias from doctors or patients expecting a result.

### Question 2 — vaccination

5 marks

Mark points:

- vaccine contains dead/inactive pathogen or antigens
- white blood cells produce antibodies
- memory cells form
- on later infection, response is faster
- antibodies produced quickly destroy pathogen before serious illness

### Question 3 — antibiotics

Explain why antibiotics can treat salmonella but not measles.  
3 marks

Mark points:

- salmonella is caused by bacteria
- measles is caused by a virus
- antibiotics kill bacteria or stop bacterial growth, but do not kill viruses

### Question 4 — resistance

Explain how antibiotic-resistant bacteria can become more common.  
5 marks

Mark points:

- mutation produces resistant bacterium
- antibiotic kills non-resistant bacteria
- resistant bacteria survive
- resistant bacteria reproduce
- resistant strain spreads / frequency increases

### Question 5 — drug testing

A new drug is tested using a double-blind clinical trial with a placebo group.

Explain why a placebo and double-blind method are used.  
4 marks

Mark points:

- placebo provides a comparison/control
- shows whether the drug has an effect beyond expectation
- double-blind means doctor and patient do not know who has the real drug
- reduces bias
