# Chapter 18: Risk, damage and runaway cells — Content

## Series: Biology Module 2 — Body systems under pressure (AQA GCSE Combined Science Trilogy 8464, Biology Paper 1)

## Big question

When does a risk factor become a cause — and what happens when cells stop following the rules?

---

## Syllabus coverage

- Health as a state of physical and mental wellbeing, not just absence of disease
- Disease as a cause of ill health
- Communicable disease: spread between organisms, usually caused by pathogens
- Non-communicable disease: not spread between organisms
- Risk factors for non-communicable disease: diet, smoking, alcohol, exercise, obesity, stress and life situation

- Smoking and alcohol effects on unborn babies
- Carcinogens, including ionising radiation, as cancer risk factors

- Disease interactions: immune system defects, viruses and cancer, immune reactions/allergies, physical illness and mental health

- Benign and malignant tumours

## Key concepts / vocabulary

- **Health:** state of physical and mental wellbeing, not just absence of disease
- **Disease:** condition that affects the normal functioning of the body
- **Communicable disease:** disease that can spread between organisms, often caused by pathogens
- **Non-communicable disease:** disease that cannot be passed from one organism to another
- **Risk factor:** factor linked to an increased chance of developing a disease
- **Correlation:** two variables are linked or change together
- **Causation:** one factor directly contributes to causing another
- **Causal mechanism:** biological explanation showing how a factor could cause a disease
- **Cancer:** disease caused by changes in cells that lead to uncontrolled growth and division
- **Tumour:** mass of abnormal cells
- **Benign tumour:** abnormal growth contained in one area, usually within a membrane, that does not invade other tissues
- **Malignant tumour:** cancerous tumour that invades nearby tissues and can spread around the body
- **Secondary tumour:** tumour formed when malignant cells spread and grow elsewhere
- **Carcinogen:** substance or agent that increases cancer risk, such as tobacco smoke or ionising radiation

## Core explanation

Health is not just “not being ill”.

AQA defines health as physical and mental wellbeing. Disease, stress, diet, life situation and lifestyle can all affect health.

Diseases can be communicable or non-communicable.

| Type | Meaning | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Communicable | can spread between organisms | measles, salmonella, malaria |
| Non-communicable | cannot spread between organisms | coronary heart disease, cancer, Type 2 diabetes |

### Risk factors

A risk factor increases the chance of disease.

It does not guarantee disease.

Important examples:

- poor diet, smoking and lack of exercise are risk factors for cardiovascular disease
- obesity is a risk factor for Type 2 diabetes
- alcohol can affect the liver and brain
- smoking is a risk factor for lung disease and lung cancer
- smoking and alcohol can affect unborn babies
- carcinogens, including ionising radiation, increase cancer risk

Exam sentence:

> A risk factor increases the probability of developing a disease, but does not mean the disease will definitely develop.

### Correlation vs causation

A correlation means two factors are linked in data.

Causation means one factor directly contributes to causing another.

A graph can show correlation, but a graph alone does not always prove causation.

To argue causation, scientists need stronger evidence, such as:

- controlled studies
- repeated evidence from different groups
- a biological mechanism explaining how the factor causes disease
- consideration of other variables

Good exam wording:

> The data show a correlation between smoking and lung cancer, but this alone does not prove causation because other factors may be involved.

### Disease interactions

Different diseases and health factors can interact.

Examples AQA may test:

- defects in the immune system increase risk of infectious diseases
- viruses can trigger some cancers
- immune reactions can trigger allergies
- severe physical illness can lead to depression or other mental illness

### Cancer

Cancer is caused by changes in cells that make them divide uncontrollably.

This can form a tumour.

### Benign tumours

Benign tumours:

- are abnormal cell growths
- are usually contained in one area
- are often surrounded by a membrane
- do not invade nearby tissues
- do not spread to other parts of the body

They are less dangerous than malignant tumours, but they can still cause problems if they press on organs or important tissues.

### Malignant tumours

Malignant tumours are cancers.

They:

- grow and invade nearby tissues
- can spread through the blood or lymph
- can form secondary tumours in other parts of the body

Exam sentence:

> Malignant tumours invade surrounding tissues and can spread in the blood to form secondary tumours.

## Likely misconceptions

- MISCONCEPTION: "A risk factor means you will definitely get the disease." Correction: risk factors increase probability, not certainty.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Correlation proves causation." Correction: correlation shows a link but does not prove one factor caused the other.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Non-communicable means not serious." Correction: non-communicable diseases such as cancer and CHD can be life-threatening.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Benign tumours are always harmless." Correction: benign tumours do not spread, but they can still cause damage by pressing on organs.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Malignant means untreatable or fatal." Correction: malignant means cancerous/invasive and able to spread; treatment can be successful, especially if detected early.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Cancer is always caused by lifestyle." Correction: cancer risk can be affected by lifestyle, genetics, viruses and radiation.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Mental and physical health are separate." Correction: physical illness can affect mental health and mental health can affect physical wellbeing.

## Suggested active learning checks

### Risk factor or disease?

Sort:

Risk factors:

- smoking
- obesity
- high alcohol intake
- ionising radiation
- lack of exercise

Diseases:

- lung cancer
- coronary heart disease
- Type 2 diabetes
- liver disease

### Correlation judgement

Prompt:

A graph shows that people who drink more alcohol have higher rates of liver disease. What can and cannot be concluded?

Good answer:

The graph shows a correlation. It suggests alcohol may be a risk factor, but the graph alone does not prove causation because other factors may be involved.

### Benign or malignant?

Match:

- contained in one area → benign
- invades surrounding tissue → malignant
- does not spread → benign
- can form secondary tumours → malignant
