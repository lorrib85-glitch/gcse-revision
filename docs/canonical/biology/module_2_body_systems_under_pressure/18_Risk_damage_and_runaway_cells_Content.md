# Chapter 18: Risk, damage and runaway cells — Content

## Series: Biology Module 2 — Body systems under pressure (AQA GCSE Combined Science Trilogy 8464, Biology Paper 1)

---

## Big question

When does a risk factor become a cause — and what happens when cells stop following the rules?

---

## Syllabus coverage

- Definitions of health: physical, mental and social wellbeing — not merely the absence of disease
- Disease and illness: communicable (infectious) and non-communicable disease
- Lifestyle risk factors for non-communicable disease: poor diet, lack of exercise, obesity, alcohol consumption, smoking
- Correlation and causation: understanding the difference; risk factors increase the chance of disease but do not guarantee it
- Cancer: tumours arising from uncontrolled cell division
- Benign tumours: grow slowly, contained, do not spread (e.g. lipoma)
- Malignant tumours: grow rapidly, invade surrounding tissue, can spread to other parts of the body via the blood or lymph (metastasis)
- Risk factors for cancer: ionising radiation (UV, X-rays, nuclear radiation), smoking (carcinogens), viral infections (e.g. HPV linked to cervical cancer), genetics (inherited mutations)
- The relationship between different diseases: pathogens can trigger immune responses that increase cancer risk; mental illness can arise from physical health conditions

---

## Key concepts / vocabulary

- **Health:** a state of physical, mental and social wellbeing, not just the absence of disease
- **Communicable disease:** a disease that can spread from one organism to another (infectious disease, caused by pathogens)
- **Non-communicable disease:** a disease that cannot be passed from person to person (e.g. cancer, heart disease, type 2 diabetes)
- **Risk factor:** a factor associated with an increased chance of developing a disease; does not guarantee the disease will develop
- **Correlation:** a statistical association between two variables; does not prove one causes the other
- **Causation:** a relationship in which one factor directly brings about another; stronger than correlation
- **Tumour:** a mass of abnormal cells resulting from uncontrolled cell division
- **Benign tumour:** a tumour that grows slowly, remains localised, and does not spread to other tissues
- **Malignant tumour:** a tumour that grows rapidly, invades surrounding tissue, and may spread to other parts of the body (metastasis)
- **Metastasis:** the spread of cancer cells from a primary tumour to other parts of the body via the bloodstream or lymphatic system
- **Carcinogen:** a substance or agent that causes cancer (e.g. tobacco smoke, UV radiation)
- **Uncontrolled cell division:** cell division that is not regulated by the normal cellular controls; the cause of tumour formation

## Likely misconceptions

- MISCONCEPTION: "A risk factor causes the disease." Correction: a risk factor increases the probability of developing a disease but does not guarantee it. Smoking is a risk factor for lung cancer, but not all smokers develop cancer, and some non-smokers do.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Correlation proves causation." Correction: correlation shows that two variables tend to change together, but does not prove one causes the other. AQA requires students to distinguish between these carefully.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Benign tumours are safe and don't need treatment." Correction: benign tumours can cause problems if they press on vital structures (e.g. benign brain tumours). However, they are less dangerous than malignant tumours because they do not spread.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Only smokers get cancer." Correction: cancer has multiple risk factors including radiation, viral infection and genetic mutations. Lifestyle factors increase risk but are not the only cause.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Malignant means fatal." Correction: malignant means the tumour is invasive and can spread. Early detection and treatment of malignant tumours can be successful. Malignant does not automatically mean the patient will die.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Mental health and physical health are separate." Correction: the AQA definition of health includes mental and social wellbeing. Physical illness can affect mental health, and mental illness can affect physical health — they are not independent.
