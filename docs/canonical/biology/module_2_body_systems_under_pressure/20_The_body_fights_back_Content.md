# Chapter 20: The body fights back — Content

## Series: Biology Module 2 — Body systems under pressure (AQA GCSE Combined Science Trilogy 8464, Biology Paper 1)

---

## Big question

How does your body know the difference between self and invader — and what happens when the defence fails?

---

## Syllabus coverage

- Non-specific physical and chemical barriers: skin (physical barrier, waterproof), mucus (traps pathogens), cilia (sweep mucus away), stomach acid (kills pathogens in food/water), tears (contain lysozyme)
- White blood cells — two main types: phagocytes (engulf and digest pathogens — phagocytosis) and lymphocytes (produce antibodies and antitoxins)
- Phagocytosis: the engulfing and digestion of pathogens by phagocytes
- Antibodies: proteins produced by lymphocytes; complementary to specific antigens on pathogens; cause agglutination; trigger destruction of pathogens; memory cells provide long-term immunity
- Antitoxins: proteins produced by lymphocytes that neutralise toxins produced by bacteria
- Vaccination: introducing a small amount of antigen (dead/weakened pathogen or antigen fragment) to stimulate antibody production without causing disease; memory cells formed; provides future rapid response
- Herd immunity: when a large proportion of the population is vaccinated, pathogens cannot spread easily even to unvaccinated individuals
- Antibiotics: chemicals that kill or inhibit bacteria; cannot work against viruses; antibiotic resistance is an increasing problem due to natural selection
- Painkillers and other medicines: relieve symptoms but do not kill pathogens
- Drug development: discovery → preclinical testing (cells, tissues, animals) → clinical trials (volunteers, double-blind, placebo) → peer review → prescribing

---

## Key concepts / vocabulary

- **Pathogen:** a microorganism that causes infectious disease
- **Phagocyte:** a white blood cell that engulfs and digests pathogens (phagocytosis); non-specific (attacks all pathogens)
- **Phagocytosis:** the process by which phagocytes engulf, enclose and digest pathogens
- **Lymphocyte:** a white blood cell that produces antibodies (in response to antigens) or antitoxins; provides specific immunity
- **Antibody:** a protein produced by lymphocytes that is complementary to a specific antigen; triggers destruction of the pathogen; unique to each pathogen
- **Antigen:** a protein marker on the surface of a pathogen that is recognised as foreign by the immune system
- **Antitoxin:** a protein produced by lymphocytes that neutralises bacterial toxins
- **Memory cell:** a long-lived lymphocyte produced during an infection or vaccination; allows a rapid immune response on re-exposure to the same pathogen
- **Vaccination:** the introduction of antigens (in a safe form) to stimulate the immune system to produce antibodies and memory cells, providing future protection
- **Herd immunity:** protection of unvaccinated individuals when a sufficiently high proportion of the population is immune, reducing pathogen spread
- **Antibiotic:** a drug that kills or inhibits the growth of bacteria; ineffective against viruses
- **Antibiotic resistance:** the ability of a bacterial strain to survive exposure to an antibiotic; arises through natural selection; a growing global health threat
- **Placebo:** an inactive treatment used in clinical trials to allow comparison with the drug being tested
- **Double-blind trial:** a clinical trial in which neither the patient nor the doctor knows whether the patient is receiving the drug or the placebo, eliminating bias
- **Preclinical testing:** testing of a drug on cells, tissues and animals before human trials
- **Clinical trial:** testing of a drug on human volunteers to assess safety, dosage and efficacy

## Likely misconceptions

- MISCONCEPTION: "Antibiotics kill all pathogens." Correction: antibiotics only kill or inhibit bacteria. They have no effect on viruses. Taking antibiotics for a viral infection is ineffective and contributes to antibiotic resistance.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Vaccination injects a disease into the body." Correction: vaccines contain dead, weakened or fragmented pathogens — or just their antigens — not the active disease-causing form. They cannot give you the disease they protect against (in standard vaccines).
- MISCONCEPTION: "Once vaccinated, you are protected forever." Correction: immunity from some vaccines fades over time and booster doses may be needed. Some diseases (like flu) mutate rapidly, requiring new vaccines each year.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Antibodies are the same as antibiotics." Correction: antibodies are proteins produced by the body's own lymphocytes to fight specific pathogens. Antibiotics are man-made drugs that kill bacteria. These are completely different.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Phagocytes produce antibodies." Correction: antibodies are produced by lymphocytes, not phagocytes. Phagocytes engulf and digest pathogens (phagocytosis) — they do not produce antibodies.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Painkillers kill the pathogens causing illness." Correction: painkillers relieve symptoms (e.g. reduce fever or pain) but do not kill pathogens. Only antibiotics (for bacteria) or antiviral drugs address the pathogen itself.
- MISCONCEPTION: "The placebo group in a trial receives a harmful substance." Correction: a placebo is an inactive, harmless substance (e.g. a sugar pill). It has no biological effect and is used to separate the true drug effect from psychological effects (the placebo effect).
