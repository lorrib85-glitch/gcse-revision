## Series: Biology Module 2 — Body systems under pressure (AQA GCSE Combined Science Trilogy 8464, Biology Paper 1)

## Big question

How does your body know the difference between self and invader — and how do vaccines and medicines help?

---

- Non-specific human defence systems: skin, nose, trachea/bronchi and stomach acid
- Skin as a physical barrier and producer of antimicrobial secretions; platelets form scabs over cuts
- Nose hairs and mucus trapping particles and pathogens
- Trachea and bronchi secreting mucus; cilia waft mucus to the throat
- Stomach hydrochloric acid killing many swallowed pathogens
- White blood cells: phagocytosis, antibody production and antitoxin production
- Antigens and antibody specificity
- Memory cells and the secondary immune response
- Vaccination: dead/inactive pathogen or antigens stimulate antibodies and memory cells
- Herd immunity as reduced spread when many people are immune
- Antibiotics killing bacteria or inhibiting bacterial growth; no effect on viruses

- Preclinical testing using cells, tissues and animals
- Clinical trials using healthy volunteers and patients

## Key concepts / vocabulary

- **Non-specific defence:** defence that acts against many pathogens, not one specific pathogen
- **Skin:** physical barrier that stops pathogens entering; can produce antimicrobial secretions
- **Scab:** clot formed by platelets that seals a wound and reduces pathogen entry
- **Mucus:** sticky substance that traps pathogens
- **Cilia:** tiny hair-like structures that move mucus out of the airways
- **White blood cell:** immune cell that helps defend the body against pathogens
- **Phagocytosis:** process where a white blood cell engulfs and digests a pathogen
- **Antigen:** molecule on pathogen surface recognised by the immune system
- **Antibody:** protein made by white blood cells that binds to a specific antigen
- **Antitoxin:** substance produced by white blood cells that neutralises toxins
- **Memory cell:** immune cell that remains after infection or vaccination and enables a faster response later
- **Vaccination:** introduction of dead/inactive pathogen or antigens to stimulate antibody production and memory cells
- **Antibiotic:** medicine that kills bacteria or stops bacterial growth
- **Painkiller:** medicine that relieves symptoms such as pain or fever, but does not kill pathogens
- **Antibiotic resistance:** when bacteria evolve so an antibiotic no longer kills them effectively
- **Toxicity:** whether a drug is harmful
- **Efficacy:** whether a drug works
- **Dose:** amount of drug given
- **Placebo:** treatment with no active drug
- **Double-blind trial:** trial where neither doctor nor patient knows who has the real drug or placebo
- **Peer review:** other scientists check research before it is published

## Core explanation

The body defends itself in layers.

First, it tries to stop pathogens getting in. If pathogens get through, white blood cells attack them. Vaccines train the immune system before the real pathogen arrives. Medicines can help treat infection or symptoms.

### Non-specific defences

| Defence | How it helps |
|---|---|
| Skin | physical barrier; produces antimicrobial secretions |
| Platelets/scabs | seal cuts and reduce pathogen entry |
| Nose hairs and mucus | trap particles and pathogens |
| Trachea and bronchi | produce mucus to trap pathogens |
| Cilia | waft mucus to the throat to be swallowed |
| Stomach acid | kills many pathogens swallowed in food or mucus |

### White blood cells

White blood cells defend the body in three main ways:

1. phagocytosis
2. antibody production
3. antitoxin production

### Phagocytosis

Phagocytosis means a white blood cell engulfs and digests a pathogen.

Sequence:

1. White blood cell detects pathogen.
2. White blood cell surrounds pathogen.
3. Pathogen is engulfed.
4. Enzymes digest the pathogen.

### Antibodies

Some white blood cells produce antibodies.

Antibodies are specific. This means one antibody shape binds to one antigen shape on a pathogen.

After the first infection, memory cells remain.

If the same pathogen enters again:

- memory cells recognise it quickly
- antibodies are produced faster
- antibodies are produced in larger amounts
- the pathogen is destroyed before serious illness develops

### Antitoxins

Some white blood cells produce antitoxins.

Antitoxins neutralise toxins released by pathogens.

Exam trap:

Antibodies bind to antigens on pathogens. Antitoxins neutralise toxins.

### Vaccination

A vaccine contains dead or inactive pathogens, or parts of pathogens such as antigens.

Vaccination protects by:

1. introducing safe antigens
2. stimulating white blood cells to produce antibodies
3. causing memory cells to form
4. allowing a faster antibody response if the real pathogen appears later

Exam sentence:

> Vaccination stimulates antibody production and memory cell formation, so a future infection produces a faster immune response.

### Herd immunity

If a high proportion of a population is vaccinated, the pathogen is less likely to spread.

This helps protect people who cannot be vaccinated.

Use careful wording:

Vaccination reduces spread and risk. It does not guarantee no infections.

### Antibiotics and painkillers

Antibiotics:

- kill bacteria or stop them growing
- do not kill viruses
- may be used for bacterial diseases such as gonorrhoea

Painkillers:

- relieve symptoms such as pain or fever
- do not kill pathogens
- do not cure infection

### Antibiotic resistance

Antibiotic resistance happens when bacteria evolve so an antibiotic no longer kills them.

Sequence:

1. A random mutation produces a resistant bacterium.
2. Antibiotic kills non-resistant bacteria.
3. Resistant bacterium survives.
4. Resistant bacterium reproduces.
5. Resistant strain becomes more common.

Reducing resistance:

- avoid unnecessary antibiotic use
- complete prescribed courses
- use antibiotics carefully in medicine and farming
- develop new antibiotics

### Drug discovery and testing

New drugs are tested for:

- toxicity: is it harmful?
- efficacy: does it work?
- dose: what amount is safe and effective?

Preclinical testing happens before human trials:

- cells and tissues in the laboratory
- live animals

Clinical trials happen in humans:

1. Healthy volunteers are given low doses to check safety.
2. Patients are given the drug to test whether it works.
3. Larger trials compare the drug with a placebo or existing treatment.

Double-blind trials reduce bias because neither doctor nor patient knows who has the real drug.

Peer review means other scientists check the research before publication.

## Likely misconceptions

- MISCONCEPTION: "Antibiotics kill all pathogens." Correction: antibiotics target bacteria, not viruses.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Painkillers cure infection." Correction: painkillers reduce symptoms but do not kill pathogens.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Vaccination gives you the active disease." Correction: vaccines contain dead/inactive pathogens or antigens that stimulate the immune response safely.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Phagocytes produce antibodies." Correction: phagocytes engulf and digest pathogens. Other white blood cells produce antibodies.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Antibodies and antibiotics are the same thing." Correction: antibodies are proteins made by the immune system; antibiotics are medicines that kill bacteria.
- MISCONCEPTION: "The person becomes resistant to antibiotics." Correction: bacteria become resistant, not the person.
- MISCONCEPTION: "A placebo is a harmful fake drug." Correction: a placebo has no active drug and is used as a comparison.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Double-blind means the drug is tested twice." Correction: double-blind means neither doctor nor patient knows who has the real drug.

### Question 1

Describe three ways the body prevents pathogens entering.  
3 marks

Mark points:

- skin acts as a barrier
- mucus traps pathogens
- cilia move mucus to throat
- stomach acid kills pathogens
- scabs seal cuts

### Question 2

Explain how vaccination can protect someone from future infection.  
5 marks

Mark points:

- vaccine contains dead/inactive pathogen or antigens
- white blood cells produce antibodies
- memory cells form
- on later infection, response is faster
- antibodies are produced quickly and destroy pathogen before serious illness

### Question 3

Explain why antibiotics can treat gonorrhoea but not measles.  
3 marks

Mark points:

- gonorrhoea is caused by bacteria
- measles is caused by a virus
- antibiotics kill bacteria or stop bacterial growth, but do not kill viruses

### Question 4

Explain how antibiotic-resistant bacteria can become more common.  
5 marks

Mark points:

- mutation produces resistant bacteria
- antibiotic kills non-resistant bacteria
- resistant bacteria survive
- resistant bacteria reproduce
- resistant strain increases/spreads

### Question 5

A new drug is tested using a placebo in a double-blind trial.

Explain why both are used.  
4 marks

Mark points:

- placebo provides comparison/control
- shows whether drug has effect beyond expectation/placebo effect
- double-blind means doctor and patient do not know treatment group
- reduces bias
