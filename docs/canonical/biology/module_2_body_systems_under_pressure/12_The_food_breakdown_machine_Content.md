## Big question

How does a piece of food become small enough to enter your bloodstream — and what goes wrong when the system breaks down?

## Key concepts / vocabulary

- **Digestion:** the breakdown of large insoluble food molecules into small soluble molecules that can be absorbed into the bloodstream
- **Mechanical digestion:** physical breakdown of food into smaller pieces, for example chewing or churning
- **Chemical digestion:** chemical breakdown of large molecules into smaller molecules by enzymes
- **Enzyme:** a biological catalyst that speeds up a chemical reaction without being used up
- **Active site:** the region of an enzyme molecule where the substrate binds; its shape is complementary to the substrate
- **Substrate:** the molecule an enzyme acts on
- **Product:** the molecule or molecules produced by the enzyme reaction
- **Lock-and-key model:** a model explaining enzyme specificity — the substrate fits into the enzyme's active site because their shapes are complementary
- **Carbohydrase:** an enzyme that breaks down carbohydrates into simple sugars
- **Amylase:** a carbohydrase that breaks down starch into sugars
- **Protease:** an enzyme that breaks down proteins into amino acids
- **Lipase:** an enzyme that breaks down lipids into fatty acids and glycerol
- **Bile:** a fluid produced by the liver, stored in the gall bladder and released into the small intestine; it neutralises stomach acid and emulsifies fats
- **Emulsification:** the physical breaking down of large fat droplets into smaller droplets, increasing the surface area for lipase to work on
- **Villi:** finger-like projections lining the small intestine; increase surface area for absorption
- **Absorption:** the movement of small, soluble digested molecules from the small intestine into the bloodstream

## Core explanation

Food molecules such as starch, proteins and lipids are often too large and insoluble to pass through the wall of the small intestine into the blood.

Digestion breaks these large insoluble molecules into small soluble molecules.

The digestive system is an organ system because several organs work together to digest food and absorb the products.

### The digestive route

| Organ | Main role |
|---|---|
| Mouth | chewing starts mechanical digestion; saliva contains amylase |
| Oesophagus | moves food from mouth to stomach by muscular contractions |
| Stomach | churns food; acid kills many bacteria and provides acidic conditions; protease begins protein digestion |
| Pancreas | produces amylase, protease and lipase and releases them into the small intestine |
| Liver | produces bile |
| Gall bladder | stores bile before release into the small intestine |
| Small intestine | completes digestion and absorbs soluble products into the blood |
| Large intestine | absorbs water from undigested food |

### Enzyme action

Enzymes are biological catalysts.

They speed up reactions without being used up.

In digestion, enzymes break large molecules into smaller molecules.

Lock-and-key sequence:

1. The substrate fits into the enzyme active site.
2. The active site and substrate have complementary shapes.
3. An enzyme-substrate complex forms.
4. The reaction happens.
5. Products are released.
6. The enzyme is unchanged and can be reused.

### Digestive enzymes

| Enzyme group | Specific example | Where produced | What it digests | Products |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Carbohydrase | Amylase | salivary glands, pancreas, small intestine | starch/carbohydrates | simple sugars |
| Protease | e.g. stomach protease | stomach, pancreas, small intestine | proteins | amino acids |
| Lipase | lipase | pancreas, small intestine | lipids/fats | fatty acids and glycerol |

Exam note:

AQA expects students to know the enzyme groups, what they digest and their products. Do not over-focus on named enzymes beyond amylase.

### Bile

Bile is not an enzyme.

It helps lipid digestion in two ways:

1. It neutralises hydrochloric acid from the stomach, creating alkaline conditions in the small intestine.
2. It emulsifies fats, breaking large fat droplets into smaller droplets.

Emulsification increases the surface area of fat droplets, so lipase can work faster.

AQA-safe exam sentence:

> Bile neutralises stomach acid and emulsifies fats, increasing the surface area for lipase to break lipids into fatty acids and glycerol.

### Absorption in the small intestine

The small intestine is adapted for absorption.

Adaptations:

- villi provide a large surface area
- thin surface gives a short diffusion distance
- good blood supply carries absorbed molecules away and maintains concentration gradients

Absorbed products include:

- simple sugars such as glucose
- amino acids
- fatty acids and glycerol

### What the products are used for

- glucose is used in respiration
- amino acids are used to build proteins
- fatty acids and glycerol are used to build lipids
- some absorbed molecules are used for growth and repair

## Likely misconceptions

- MISCONCEPTION: "Enzymes are living things that get used up in reactions." Correction: enzymes are biological catalysts — proteins that speed up reactions without being used up. They can be reused unless denatured.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Bile is an enzyme that digests fat." Correction: bile is not an enzyme. It does not chemically break down fat molecules. It emulsifies fat and neutralises acid.
- MISCONCEPTION: "All digestion takes place in the stomach." Correction: most digestion and absorption happen in the small intestine. The stomach mainly churns food and begins protein digestion.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Lipids are broken down into sugars." Correction: lipids are broken down into fatty acids and glycerol. Carbohydrates are broken down into simple sugars.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Amylase works in the stomach." Correction: amylase works in the mouth and small intestine. The stomach is too acidic for amylase.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Any enzyme can break down any substrate." Correction: enzymes are specific because each enzyme's active site is complementary to a particular substrate.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Acid digests all food." Correction: stomach acid helps conditions for stomach protease and kills many bacteria, but enzymes carry out chemical digestion.
- MISCONCEPTION: "The large intestine absorbs digested food." Correction: most soluble digested food molecules are absorbed in the small intestine. The large intestine mainly absorbs water.

4 marks

Mark points:

- enzyme has an active site
- substrate has complementary shape to active site
- substrate binds to active site / enzyme-substrate complex forms
- products are released and enzyme is unchanged/reused

### Question 5

Explain how the small intestine is adapted for absorption.  
4 marks

Mark points:

- villi increase surface area
- thin surface / short diffusion distance
- good blood supply
- blood carries absorbed molecules away, maintaining concentration gradients

### Question 6

A student writes: "The stomach is where all food is digested and absorbed." Explain what is wrong with this statement.  
4 marks

Mark points:

- not all digestion happens in the stomach
- most digestion happens in the small intestine
- absorption of soluble products happens in the small intestine
- stomach mainly churns food and begins protein digestion / contains acid and protease
