# Chapter 12: The food breakdown machine — Content

## Series: Biology Module 2 — Body systems under pressure (AQA GCSE Combined Science Trilogy 8464, Biology Paper 1)

---

## Big question

How does a piece of food become small enough to enter your bloodstream — and what goes wrong when the system breaks down?

---

## Syllabus coverage

- The digestive system organs: mouth, oesophagus, stomach, small intestine, large intestine, pancreas, liver, gall bladder
- Enzymes in digestion: carbohydrases (including amylase), proteases, lipases
- Substrates and products: starch → simple sugars; proteins → amino acids; lipids → fatty acids + glycerol
- Where enzymes are produced: salivary glands, stomach, pancreas, small intestine
- The role of bile: produced in the liver, stored in the gall bladder, released into the small intestine; emulsifies fats (increases surface area); neutralises stomach acid
- The lock-and-key model: active site, substrate, enzyme-substrate complex, complementary shape
- Absorption in the small intestine: villi, microvilli, large surface area, good blood supply

---

## Key concepts / vocabulary

- **Digestion:** the breakdown of large insoluble food molecules into small soluble molecules that can be absorbed into the bloodstream
- **Enzyme:** a biological catalyst that speeds up a chemical reaction without being used up; its active site is complementary in shape to its substrate
- **Carbohydrase:** an enzyme that breaks down carbohydrates; amylase breaks down starch into maltose/sugars in the mouth and small intestine
- **Protease:** an enzyme that breaks down proteins into amino acids; produced in the stomach (pepsin) and pancreas
- **Lipase:** an enzyme that breaks down lipids (fats) into fatty acids and glycerol; produced in the pancreas and small intestine
- **Bile:** a green fluid produced by the liver, stored in the gall bladder, released into the small intestine; emulsifies fats and neutralises acid
- **Emulsification:** the breaking down of large fat droplets into smaller droplets, increasing the surface area for lipase to work on
- **Lock-and-key model:** a model explaining enzyme specificity — the substrate fits exactly into the enzyme's active site, like a key into a lock
- **Active site:** the region of an enzyme molecule where the substrate binds; its shape is complementary to the substrate
- **Villi:** finger-like projections lining the small intestine; increase surface area for absorption
- **Absorption:** the movement of small, soluble digested molecules from the small intestine into the bloodstream

## Likely misconceptions

- MISCONCEPTION: "Enzymes are living things that get used up in reactions." Correction: enzymes are biological catalysts — proteins that speed up reactions without being used up. They can be reused unless denatured.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Bile is an enzyme that digests fat." Correction: bile is not an enzyme — it does not chemically break down fat molecules. It emulsifies fat (breaks large droplets into smaller ones) to increase surface area for lipase.
- MISCONCEPTION: "All digestion takes place in the stomach." Correction: most digestion and all absorption takes place in the small intestine. The stomach mainly digests protein (pepsin) and churns food.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Lipids are broken down into sugars." Correction: lipids are broken down into fatty acids and glycerol. Carbohydrates are broken down into simple sugars.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Amylase works in the stomach." Correction: amylase works in the mouth and small intestine. The stomach is too acidic — amylase has an optimum pH around 7 and is denatured by stomach acid.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Any enzyme can break down any substrate." Correction: enzymes are specific — each enzyme's active site is complementary to only one type of substrate (lock-and-key model).
