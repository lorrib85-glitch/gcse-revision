# Chapter 16: The oxygen swap — Content

## Series: Biology Module 2 — Body systems under pressure (AQA GCSE Combined Science Trilogy 8464, Biology Paper 1)

---

## Big question

How does every breath you take move oxygen into your blood — and what makes the lungs so good at this job?

---

## Syllabus coverage

- The respiratory system: trachea, bronchi, bronchioles, alveoli, diaphragm, intercostal muscles, ribs
- Ventilation (breathing): inhalation — diaphragm contracts and flattens; intercostal muscles contract; ribcage moves up and out; volume increases; pressure decreases; air flows in. Exhalation — diaphragm relaxes and domes upward; intercostal muscles relax; ribcage moves down and in; volume decreases; pressure increases; air flows out
- Gas exchange at the alveoli: oxygen diffuses from alveoli into the blood; carbon dioxide diffuses from blood into the alveoli; by diffusion down concentration gradients
- Adaptations of the alveoli: large surface area (millions of alveoli); thin walls (one cell thick); moist lining (gases dissolve); rich blood supply (maintains concentration gradient)
- Link to diffusion: gas exchange depends on concentration gradients; continuous blood flow removes oxygen and delivers CO₂, maintaining the gradient

---

## Key concepts / vocabulary

- **Trachea:** the windpipe; a tube reinforced with rings of cartilage that carries air to the lungs
- **Bronchi (sing. bronchus):** two tubes branching from the trachea, one to each lung
- **Bronchioles:** smaller tubes branching from the bronchi inside the lungs
- **Alveoli (sing. alveolus):** tiny air sacs at the end of the bronchioles where gas exchange takes place
- **Diaphragm:** a dome-shaped muscle below the lungs that contracts during inhalation
- **Intercostal muscles:** muscles between the ribs that move the ribcage during breathing
- **Ventilation:** the process of breathing — moving air into and out of the lungs
- **Gas exchange:** the movement of oxygen from alveoli into the blood and carbon dioxide from the blood into the alveoli, by diffusion
- **Concentration gradient:** a difference in concentration between two regions; substances diffuse from high to low concentration
- **Diffusion:** the net movement of particles from a region of high concentration to a region of low concentration; requires no energy
- **Surface area to volume ratio:** larger surface area relative to volume means more efficient exchange; alveoli maximise this
- **Inhalation:** breathing in; diaphragm contracts; ribcage rises; lung volume increases; pressure falls; air flows in
- **Exhalation:** breathing out; diaphragm relaxes; ribcage falls; lung volume decreases; pressure rises; air flows out

## Likely misconceptions

- MISCONCEPTION: "We breathe in oxygen and breathe out only carbon dioxide." Correction: exhaled air still contains oxygen (approximately 16%) — we do not use all the oxygen we inhale. Exhaled air has more CO₂ and less O₂ than inhaled air, but is not simply carbon dioxide.
- MISCONCEPTION: "The lungs push air in during inhalation." Correction: the lungs do not actively push or pull — they are passive. The diaphragm and intercostal muscles change the volume of the chest cavity, which changes the pressure, causing air to flow in or out passively.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Gas exchange uses active transport." Correction: gas exchange in the alveoli occurs by diffusion — it is a passive process, down concentration gradients, requiring no energy input.
- MISCONCEPTION: "All four alveolar adaptations are equally important." Correction: the large surface area and the rich blood supply (maintaining the concentration gradient) are the most commonly examined. The thin walls and moist lining are also required but students often forget the blood supply.
- MISCONCEPTION: "The diaphragm pushes air out during exhalation." Correction: the diaphragm relaxes (returns to its domed shape) during exhalation. It does not actively push air out — it simply stops contracting.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Breathing and respiration are the same thing." Correction: breathing (ventilation) is the physical movement of air. Respiration is the chemical process of releasing energy from glucose in cells. These are different processes that students frequently confuse.
