# Chapter 16: The oxygen swap — Content

## Series: Biology Module 2 — Body systems under pressure (AQA GCSE Combined Science Trilogy 8464, Biology Paper 1)

---

## Big question

How does every breath move oxygen into your blood — and why are the lungs so good at exchange?

---

## Syllabus coverage

- The respiratory system: trachea, bronchi, bronchioles, alveoli, ribs, intercostal muscles and diaphragm
- Ventilation as the movement of air into and out of the lungs
- Inhalation: diaphragm contracts and flattens; intercostal muscles contract; ribcage moves up and out; thorax volume increases; pressure decreases; air flows in
- Exhalation: diaphragm relaxes and domes upward; intercostal muscles relax; ribcage moves down and in; thorax volume decreases; pressure increases; air flows out
- Gas exchange at the alveoli: oxygen diffuses from alveoli into blood; carbon dioxide diffuses from blood into alveoli
- Diffusion down concentration gradients
- Adaptations of alveoli: large surface area, thin walls, moist lining, rich blood supply and ventilation maintaining gradients
- Link between gas exchange, blood circulation and respiration in cells
- Difference between breathing/ventilation and respiration

## Key concepts / vocabulary

- **Trachea:** the windpipe; carries air from the mouth/nose towards the lungs and is supported by cartilage
- **Bronchi:** two tubes branching from the trachea, one to each lung
- **Bronchioles:** smaller tubes branching from the bronchi inside the lungs
- **Alveoli:** tiny air sacs where gas exchange takes place
- **Diaphragm:** sheet of muscle below the lungs that contracts and flattens during inhalation
- **Intercostal muscles:** muscles between the ribs that move the ribcage during breathing
- **Ventilation:** moving air into and out of the lungs
- **Gas exchange:** movement of oxygen and carbon dioxide between alveoli and blood
- **Diffusion:** net movement of particles from a region of higher concentration to a region of lower concentration
- **Concentration gradient:** difference in concentration between two areas
- **Inhalation:** breathing in
- **Exhalation:** breathing out
- **Thorax:** chest cavity containing the lungs and heart
- **Respiration:** chemical process in cells that transfers energy from glucose

## Core explanation

Breathing gets air into the lungs.

Gas exchange gets oxygen into the blood and carbon dioxide out of the blood.

Respiration happens inside cells and uses oxygen and glucose to transfer energy.

These three ideas connect, but they are not the same thing.

### Respiratory system route

Air moves through:

1. mouth/nose
2. trachea
3. bronchi
4. bronchioles
5. alveoli

The alveoli are the exchange surface where oxygen enters the blood and carbon dioxide leaves the blood.

### Inhalation

During inhalation:

1. diaphragm contracts and flattens
2. intercostal muscles contract
3. ribcage moves up and out
4. thorax volume increases
5. pressure inside the thorax decreases
6. air flows into the lungs

Exam sentence:

> During inhalation, the diaphragm contracts and the ribcage moves up and out, increasing thorax volume. Pressure decreases, so air moves into the lungs.

### Exhalation

During exhalation:

1. diaphragm relaxes and returns to a dome shape
2. intercostal muscles relax
3. ribcage moves down and in
4. thorax volume decreases
5. pressure inside the thorax increases
6. air flows out of the lungs

Exam sentence:

> During exhalation, the diaphragm relaxes and the ribcage moves down and in, decreasing thorax volume. Pressure increases, so air moves out of the lungs.

### Gas exchange at the alveoli

Oxygen concentration is higher in the alveoli than in the blood arriving at the lungs.

So oxygen diffuses:

> alveoli → blood

Carbon dioxide concentration is higher in the blood than in the alveoli.

So carbon dioxide diffuses:

> blood → alveoli

Both gases move by diffusion down concentration gradients. No energy is needed.

### Why alveoli are adapted for gas exchange

| Adaptation | Why it helps |
|---|---|
| Millions of alveoli | large surface area for diffusion |
| Alveolar walls one cell thick | short diffusion distance |
| Capillary walls one cell thick | short diffusion distance into/out of blood |
| Moist lining | gases dissolve before diffusing |
| Rich blood supply | maintains concentration gradients by carrying oxygen away and bringing carbon dioxide |
| Ventilation | refreshes air in alveoli, maintaining oxygen and carbon dioxide gradients |

AQA exam trap:

Do not just list adaptations. Link each one to why it increases the rate of diffusion.

### Link to blood circulation

The pulmonary artery brings deoxygenated blood from the heart to the lungs.

The pulmonary vein carries oxygenated blood from the lungs back to the heart.

This links the lung topic back to Chapter 15.

### Breathing vs respiration

Breathing/ventilation:

- physical movement of air into and out of the lungs

Respiration:

- chemical reaction in cells
- transfers energy from glucose
- uses oxygen in aerobic respiration

Exam trap:

Do not write “we breathe to make energy”. Better: we breathe to bring oxygen into the body, which cells use in aerobic respiration to transfer energy from glucose.

## Likely misconceptions

- MISCONCEPTION: "We breathe in oxygen and breathe out only carbon dioxide." Correction: inhaled and exhaled air are mixtures. Exhaled air still contains oxygen, but less oxygen and more carbon dioxide than inhaled air.
- MISCONCEPTION: "The lungs actively pull air in." Correction: air moves because diaphragm/intercostal muscle movement changes thorax volume and pressure.
- MISCONCEPTION: "The diaphragm relaxes during inhalation." Correction: the diaphragm contracts and flattens during inhalation.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Pressure increases during inhalation." Correction: volume increases, so pressure decreases and air flows in.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Gas exchange uses active transport." Correction: oxygen and carbon dioxide move by diffusion down concentration gradients.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Alveoli have thick walls to stop them bursting." Correction: alveolar walls are thin, giving a short diffusion distance.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Breathing and respiration are the same thing." Correction: breathing is ventilation; respiration is a chemical process in cells.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Blood supply is not an alveolar adaptation." Correction: rich blood supply maintains steep concentration gradients, making diffusion faster.

Sort each statement:

Inhalation:

- diaphragm contracts
- ribcage moves up and out
- thorax volume increases
- pressure decreases
- air moves in

Exhalation:

- diaphragm relaxes
- ribcage moves down and in
- thorax volume decreases
- pressure increases
- air moves out

### Gas exchange arrows

Complete the arrows:

- oxygen: alveoli → blood
- carbon dioxide: blood → alveoli

### Adaptation link

Match:

- large surface area → more diffusion can happen at once
- thin walls → short diffusion distance
- moist lining → gases dissolve
- rich blood supply → maintains concentration gradient
- ventilation → refreshes alveolar air

### Spot the error

Student answer:

> During inhalation, the diaphragm relaxes, pressure increases and air is pushed into the lungs.

Correction:

During inhalation, the diaphragm contracts and flattens. Thorax volume increases, pressure decreases and air flows into the lungs.

### Question 2

Explain how alveoli are adapted for efficient gas exchange.  
4 marks

Mark points:

- large surface area from many alveoli
- thin walls / one cell thick gives short diffusion distance
- moist lining so gases dissolve
- good blood supply maintains concentration gradients
- ventilation maintains concentration gradients

Explain the direction of oxygen and carbon dioxide movement at the alveoli.  
4 marks

Mark points:

- oxygen diffuses from alveoli into blood
- oxygen moves down concentration gradient
- carbon dioxide diffuses from blood into alveoli
- carbon dioxide moves down concentration gradient

A student says, "Breathing and respiration are the same thing." Explain why this is incorrect.  
3 marks

Mark points:

- breathing/ventilation is movement of air into and out of lungs
- respiration is a chemical process in cells
- respiration transfers energy from glucose

### Question 5

Why does a rich blood supply improve gas exchange in the alveoli?  
2 marks

Mark points:

- carries oxygen away / brings carbon dioxide to alveoli
- maintains steep concentration gradients
