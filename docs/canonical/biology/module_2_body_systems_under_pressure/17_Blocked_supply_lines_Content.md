## Big question

What happens when the heart's own blood supply gets blocked — and how do doctors reduce the risk or fix the problem?

---

## Syllabus coverage

- Coronary heart disease: fatty material builds up in coronary arteries, narrowing the lumen
- Reduced blood flow to the heart muscle means less oxygen for aerobic respiration
- Link between reduced oxygen supply, chest pain and heart attack
- Treatments for cardiovascular disease: stents, statins, replacement valves, heart transplants and artificial hearts
- Benefits and risks of stents
- Benefits and risks of statins
- Faulty heart valves: valves may leak or become stiff and not open fully
- Biological and mechanical replacement valves
- Heart transplants: donor shortage, rejection risk and immunosuppressant drugs
- Artificial hearts as temporary support while waiting for a transplant or to allow the heart to rest
- Risk factors that increase the chance of coronary heart disease
- Evaluation language: advantages, disadvantages and balanced judgement

## Key concepts / vocabulary

- **Coronary arteries:** arteries that supply the heart muscle with oxygenated blood
- **Coronary heart disease:** condition where fatty material builds up in coronary arteries, narrowing them and reducing blood flow to the heart muscle
- **Lumen:** the space inside a blood vessel where blood flows
- **Fatty material/plaque:** deposit that narrows an artery and restricts blood flow
- **Stent:** metal mesh tube inserted into an artery to keep it open
- **Statin:** drug that lowers blood cholesterol and slows the build-up of fatty material in arteries
- **Valve:** structure that prevents backflow of blood
- **Faulty valve:** valve that does not open fully or leaks, reducing blood flow efficiency
- **Biological valve:** replacement valve made from animal or human tissue
- **Mechanical valve:** artificial valve made from materials such as metal or plastic
- **Heart transplant:** replacement of a failing heart with a donor heart
- **Artificial heart:** mechanical device that pumps blood, often used temporarily while waiting for a transplant
- **Immunosuppressant drug:** drug that reduces immune response to lower the risk of transplant rejection
- **Risk factor:** something linked to an increased chance of disease

## Core explanation

The heart pumps blood around the body, but the heart muscle also needs its own blood supply.

Coronary arteries supply the heart muscle with oxygenated blood.

If fatty material builds up inside these arteries, the lumen becomes narrower. Less blood reaches the heart muscle, so less oxygen is delivered.

Without enough oxygen, heart muscle cells cannot respire aerobically enough. This can cause chest pain and may lead to a heart attack.

### Coronary heart disease sequence

1. Fatty material builds up in coronary arteries.
2. The artery lumen narrows.
3. Blood flow to heart muscle is reduced.
4. Less oxygen reaches heart muscle cells.
5. Less aerobic respiration happens.
6. Heart muscle may be damaged or die.

Exam sentence:

> In coronary heart disease, fatty material narrows the coronary arteries, reducing blood flow and oxygen supply to the heart muscle.

### Risk factors

Risk factors linked to coronary heart disease include:

- smoking
- high blood cholesterol
- diet high in saturated fat
- obesity
- lack of exercise
- high blood pressure
- genetics/family history

Risk factors increase probability. They do not guarantee that a person will get the disease.

### Stents

A stent is a metal mesh tube inserted into a narrowed artery.

How it helps:

- holds the artery open
- restores blood flow
- increases oxygen supply to the heart muscle
- reduces risk of heart attack

Advantages:

- effective quickly
- less invasive than major surgery
- long-lasting

Disadvantages:

- surgery/procedure has risks
- risk of infection
- risk of blood clots
- artery can narrow again
- patient may still need drugs such as statins

### Statins

Statins are drugs that lower blood cholesterol.

How they help:

- reduce cholesterol in blood
- slow build-up of fatty material in arteries
- reduce risk of cardiovascular disease

Advantages:

- reduce risk of CHD and stroke
- can be used long-term
- do not require surgery

Disadvantages:

- must be taken regularly over a long time
- can have side effects
- effect is not immediate
- do not remove existing plaques directly

Exam trap:

Statins reduce risk and slow progression. They do not physically hold an artery open like a stent.

### Faulty valves

Heart valves prevent backflow of blood.

A valve may become faulty because:

- it does not open fully
- it leaks and allows backflow

This makes the heart work less efficiently.

Replacement valves may be biological or mechanical.

| Valve type | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|---|---|---|
| Biological valve | less need for lifelong blood-thinning medication; works more like natural tissue | may wear out and need replacing |
| Mechanical valve | very durable / lasts a long time | may require blood-thinning medication; risk of clots |

### Heart transplants

A heart transplant may be used when heart failure is severe.

Advantages:

- can restore heart function
- may greatly improve quality of life

Disadvantages:

- shortage of donor hearts
- major surgery risks
- immune system may reject donor heart
- immunosuppressant drugs are needed
- immunosuppressants increase risk of infection

### Artificial hearts

Artificial hearts are mechanical devices used to pump blood.

They may be used:

- while waiting for a donor heart
- to allow the heart to rest

Advantages:

- can keep a patient alive while waiting for a transplant
- less risk of immune rejection than donor tissue

Disadvantages:

- risk of blood clots
- risk of infection
- mechanical parts may fail
- patient may need to stay in hospital or be carefully monitored

### How to evaluate treatments

A strong evaluation answer compares benefits and drawbacks, then makes a judgement.

Example judgement:

A stent may be better for a narrowed coronary artery because it restores blood flow quickly, but statins may still be needed long term to reduce cholesterol and lower future risk.

## Likely misconceptions

- MISCONCEPTION: "Statins cure coronary heart disease." Correction: statins lower cholesterol and reduce risk, but they do not directly remove existing plaques or hold arteries open.
- MISCONCEPTION: "A stent is a drug." Correction: a stent is a metal mesh tube that physically keeps an artery open.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Stents dissolve fatty deposits." Correction: stents hold the narrowed artery open; they do not dissolve plaques.
- MISCONCEPTION: "A heart transplant is always the best treatment." Correction: transplants have major risks, donor shortage and rejection risk.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Artificial hearts are usually permanent cures." Correction: artificial hearts are often temporary support while waiting for a transplant or while the heart rests.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Only smokers get coronary heart disease." Correction: CHD has several risk factors including diet, cholesterol, obesity, lack of exercise, high blood pressure and genetics.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Mechanical valves are always better because they last longer." Correction: they may require blood-thinning medication and have clotting risks.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Biological valves are always better because they are natural." Correction: they may wear out and need replacing.

## Suggested active learning checks

### CHD sequence

Put in order:

- fatty material builds up
- coronary artery narrows
- blood flow reduces
- less oxygen reaches heart muscle
- less aerobic respiration
- heart muscle may be damaged

### Treatment match

Match:

- stent → mesh tube keeps artery open
- statin → lowers blood cholesterol
- biological valve → replacement valve from animal/human tissue
- mechanical valve → durable artificial valve
- heart transplant → donor heart replaces failing heart
- artificial heart → mechanical pump used as support

### Advantage or drawback?

Sort:

Stents:

- restores blood flow quickly
- risk of blood clots
- procedure risk

Statins:

- no surgery needed
- must be taken long term
- possible side effects

Heart transplant:

- can restore function
- donor shortage
- rejection risk

### Spot the error

Student answer:

> Statins are metal tubes that keep coronary arteries open.

Correction:

Stents are metal mesh tubes that keep arteries open. Statins are drugs that lower blood cholesterol.

## Exam-style questions

### Question 1

Explain how coronary heart disease can lead to a heart attack.  
4 marks

Mark points:

- fatty material builds up in coronary arteries
- arteries narrow / lumen reduces
- blood flow to heart muscle decreases
- less oxygen reaches heart muscle
- less aerobic respiration / heart muscle cells may die

### Question 2

Explain how a stent treats coronary heart disease.  
3 marks

Mark points:

- stent is inserted into narrowed coronary artery
- keeps artery open
- restores blood flow / oxygen supply to heart muscle

### Question 3

Evaluate the use of statins and stents for treating cardiovascular disease.  
6 marks

High-mark answer should include:

- statins lower blood cholesterol
- statins reduce risk / slow build-up of fatty material
- statins are long-term and may have side effects
- stents physically keep narrowed arteries open
- stents restore blood flow quickly
- stent procedure has risks such as infection or blood clots
- balanced judgement comparing situations where each may be useful

### Question 4

Compare biological and mechanical replacement heart valves.  
4 marks

Mark points:

- biological valves come from animal/human tissue
- biological valves may not require lifelong blood-thinning drugs
- biological valves may wear out
- mechanical valves are durable/long lasting
- mechanical valves may require blood-thinning medication / increase clotting risk

### Question 5

Explain why heart transplant patients need immunosuppressant drugs.  
2 marks

Mark points:

- donor heart may be recognised as foreign
- immunosuppressants reduce immune response / reduce rejection risk

### Question 6

Give two disadvantages of using an artificial heart.  
2 marks

Mark points:

- blood clots
- infection
- mechanical failure
- careful monitoring / hospital stay
