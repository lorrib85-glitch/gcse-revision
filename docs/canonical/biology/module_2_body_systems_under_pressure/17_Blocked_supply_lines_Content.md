# Chapter 17: Blocked supply lines — Content

## Series: Biology Module 2 — Body systems under pressure (AQA GCSE Combined Science Trilogy 8464, Biology Paper 1)

---

## Big question

What happens when the heart's own blood supply gets blocked — and how do doctors solve the problem?

---

## Syllabus coverage

- Coronary heart disease (CHD): fatty deposits (atherosclerosis/plaques) narrowing the coronary arteries; reduced blood flow to the heart muscle
- Risk factors for CHD: high-fat diet, smoking, lack of exercise, high blood pressure, obesity, genetics
- Treatments for CHD: stents (wires mesh inserted to keep arteries open); statins (drugs that lower blood cholesterol levels); bypass surgery (using a blood vessel from elsewhere to route around the blockage)
- Faulty heart valves: valves may become stiff or leaky; replacement with biological valves (from humans or animals) or mechanical valves
- Heart transplants: replacing a failing heart; limited donor availability; immune rejection risk; need for immunosuppressant drugs
- Artificial hearts: used as a temporary measure while waiting for a transplant; mechanical pump; avoids immune rejection but has mechanical failure risk
- Lungs: the effect of smoking — carcinogens, tar causing lung cancer; chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD); links to cardiovascular risk

---

## Key concepts / vocabulary

- **Coronary heart disease (CHD):** a condition in which the coronary arteries are narrowed by fatty deposits, reducing blood flow to the heart muscle
- **Atherosclerosis:** the build-up of fatty plaques inside artery walls, reducing the lumen and making arteries less elastic
- **Fatty deposit (plaque):** a build-up of fat, cholesterol and other substances inside an artery wall
- **Stent:** a mesh tube inserted into a narrowed artery to keep it open and restore blood flow
- **Statin:** a drug that lowers LDL cholesterol levels in the blood, slowing the build-up of fatty deposits
- **Bypass surgery:** a surgical procedure in which a section of healthy blood vessel is used to route blood around a blocked coronary artery
- **Heart valve:** a flap in the heart that prevents backflow of blood; may become faulty through disease or age
- **Biological valve:** a replacement heart valve made from animal or human tissue; feels more natural but may deteriorate over time
- **Mechanical valve:** an artificial replacement heart valve made from metal or plastic; durable but requires lifelong blood-thinning medication
- **Heart transplant:** replacement of a failing heart with a donor heart; requires immunosuppressant drugs to prevent rejection
- **Artificial heart:** a mechanical device that takes over the pumping function of a failing heart; used as a bridge to transplant

## Likely misconceptions

- MISCONCEPTION: "Statins cure coronary heart disease." Correction: statins reduce the risk of CHD worsening by lowering cholesterol, but they do not remove existing plaques or cure the disease. They are a long-term management drug.
- MISCONCEPTION: "A heart transplant is always the best treatment." Correction: heart transplants carry major risks (immune rejection, limited donors, need for lifelong immunosuppression). Stents or bypass surgery are often preferred where the heart can still function.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Stents are permanent and need no follow-up." Correction: stents can become blocked again (restenosis) over time. Patients often also take statins and antiplatelet drugs alongside stenting.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Only smokers get coronary heart disease." Correction: CHD has multiple risk factors — diet, genetics, inactivity, obesity and high blood pressure all contribute. Non-smokers can develop CHD.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Artificial hearts are permanent solutions." Correction: current artificial hearts are mechanical devices used as a bridge while patients wait for a donor heart, not as permanent replacements.
- MISCONCEPTION: "All fats are bad for the heart." Correction: at GCSE, the focus is on saturated fats and cholesterol raising LDL levels, which is linked to plaque formation. The relationship is more nuanced, but for the exam, high saturated fat intake is the dietary risk factor.
