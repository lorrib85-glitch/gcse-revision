# Chapter 11: From tiny cells to full-body systems — Content

## Series: Biology Module 2 — Body systems under pressure (AQA GCSE Combined Science Trilogy 8464, Biology Paper 1)

---

## Big question

How does a single cell become a whole working body — and why does organisation at every level matter?

---

## Syllabus coverage

- Cells as the basic unit of life
- Tissues: groups of similar cells working together to perform a specific function
- Organs: groups of different tissues working together to perform a specific function
- Organ systems: groups of organs working together to perform a specific function
- Organisms: the complete living thing
- The stomach as an example organ: muscular tissue (churning), glandular tissue (producing enzymes and acid), epithelial tissue (covering the surface)
- The leaf as an example plant organ: mesophyll tissue (photosynthesis), xylem and phloem tissue (transport), epidermal tissue (covering)
- The hierarchy: cell → tissue → organ → organ system → organism

---

## Key concepts / vocabulary

- **Cell:** the basic structural and functional unit of life
- **Tissue:** a group of similar cells working together to carry out a specific function (e.g. muscle tissue, glandular tissue)
- **Organ:** a structure made of different tissues working together to perform a specific function (e.g. stomach, heart, leaf)
- **Organ system:** a group of organs that work together to perform a specific function (e.g. digestive system, circulatory system)
- **Organism:** a complete living thing made up of one or more cells
- **Muscular tissue:** tissue that contracts to produce movement; found in the stomach wall
- **Glandular tissue:** tissue that secretes substances such as enzymes and hormones; found in the stomach
- **Epithelial tissue:** tissue that covers the surface of an organ; found lining the stomach
- **Mesophyll tissue:** tissue in leaves where photosynthesis takes place
- **Xylem:** tissue that transports water and mineral ions up the plant from roots to leaves
- **Phloem:** tissue that transports dissolved sugars from leaves to the rest of the plant
- **Epidermal tissue:** the outer covering of a plant organ; reduces water loss

## Likely misconceptions

- MISCONCEPTION: "All cells in an organ are identical." Correction: an organ contains different types of tissue, each made of different cell types suited to different jobs — for example the stomach contains muscular, glandular and epithelial tissue.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Tissues are just collections of cells with no structure." Correction: cells in a tissue are organised to work together for a specific function — it is not random grouping.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Organs work independently." Correction: organs work within organ systems, and organ systems are interdependent — for example the digestive system depends on the circulatory system to distribute nutrients after absorption.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Only animals have organs." Correction: plants also have organs (roots, stems, leaves, flowers) made of plant tissues such as xylem, phloem, mesophyll and epidermis.
- MISCONCEPTION: "The heart is a muscle." Correction: the heart is an organ made of cardiac muscle tissue, connective tissue, epithelial tissue and others — it is not a single tissue type.
