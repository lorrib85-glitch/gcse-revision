## Big question

Why do some conditions destroy enzymes — and how does the examiner catch students who don't know the difference between slow and denatured?

---

- Required practical: effect of pH on rate of amylase activity using starch, iodine solution and a spotting tile
- Variables in the enzyme investigation: independent, dependent and control variables
- Timing colour change and interpreting shorter time as faster rate

- Presenting results as a graph and identifying optimum pH
- Evaluating the method: repeats, means, anomalies and improvements

## Key concepts / vocabulary

- **Enzyme:** a biological catalyst; a protein with an active site complementary in shape to its substrate
- **Active site:** the region of the enzyme where the substrate binds
- **Substrate:** the molecule an enzyme acts on
- **Optimum temperature:** the temperature at which an enzyme works at its fastest rate; for most human enzymes around 37°C
- **Optimum pH:** the pH at which an enzyme works at its fastest rate; varies between enzymes
- **Denaturation:** change in the shape of the active site so the substrate can no longer bind
- **Rate of reaction:** how fast a reaction happens
- **Iodine solution:** turns blue-black if starch is present and stays orange-brown if starch has been digested
- **Spotting tile:** tile with wells used to test small samples from the reaction mixture over time
- **Buffer solution:** solution used to keep pH constant
- **Independent variable:** the factor deliberately changed; pH in this practical
- **Dependent variable:** the factor measured; time taken for starch to be digested
- **Control variable:** a factor kept constant; temperature, volumes and concentrations of starch/amylase
- **Anomalous result:** a result that does not fit the pattern and may need repeating or excluding with justification

## Core explanation

Enzymes work because their active site has the right shape for a specific substrate.

Anything that changes the active site's shape can reduce or stop enzyme activity.

### Temperature and enzyme activity

At low temperatures:

- particles have less kinetic energy
- enzyme and substrate collide less often
- reaction rate is slower
- the enzyme is not denatured

As temperature increases:

- particles gain kinetic energy
- collisions happen more often
- more enzyme-substrate complexes form
- rate increases up to the optimum temperature

Above the optimum temperature:

- bonds in the enzyme structure break
- active site changes shape
- substrate no longer fits
- enzyme is denatured
- rate decreases sharply

Exam trap:

Low temperature makes enzymes slower. High temperature above the optimum can denature enzymes. Slow does not automatically mean denatured.

### pH and enzyme activity

Each enzyme has an optimum pH.

If pH is too high or too low for that enzyme:

- active site changes shape
- substrate fits less well or cannot bind
- rate decreases
- extreme pH can denature the enzyme

Examples:

- amylase works best around neutral pH
- stomach protease works best in acidic conditions

### Required practical: effect of pH on amylase

Aim:

Investigate the effect of pH on the rate of reaction of amylase breaking down starch.

Core idea:

- amylase breaks down starch into sugars
- iodine turns blue-black when starch is present
- iodine stays orange-brown when starch has been digested
- the faster the iodine stops turning blue-black, the faster the amylase reaction

### Practical method

1. Put iodine solution into the wells of a spotting tile.
2. Add amylase solution and buffer solution of a chosen pH to a test tube.
3. Add starch solution to the test tube and start the timer.
4. Keep the tube at a constant temperature using a water bath.
5. Every 30 seconds, place a drop of the reaction mixture onto iodine on the spotting tile.
6. Record the time when the iodine no longer turns blue-black and stays orange-brown.
7. Repeat with different pH buffer solutions.
8. Repeat each pH and calculate a mean.

### Variables

| Variable type | Variable |
|---|---|
| Independent variable | pH of buffer solution |
| Dependent variable | time taken for starch to be digested |
| Control variables | temperature, amylase concentration, starch concentration, volumes, sampling interval |

### Rate calculation

Shorter time means faster rate.

A common GCSE calculation is:

> rate = 1000 / time taken

Example:

If starch disappears after 50 seconds:

1000 / 50 = 20 arbitrary units

### Results and graph interpretation

A graph of rate against pH should peak at the optimum pH.

If a graph shows time taken rather than rate:

- the shortest time indicates the fastest rate
- the pH with the shortest time is the optimum pH

Common trap:

Students often think the highest time means the best enzyme activity. It is the opposite if the y-axis is time taken.

### Improving the method

To improve reliability:

- repeat each pH
- calculate a mean
- identify anomalous results
- use clean equipment
- keep temperature constant with a water bath
- use the same volumes and concentrations each time

To improve precision:

- sample more frequently than every 30 seconds
- use a colorimeter if available to measure colour change more objectively
- use smaller pH intervals near the apparent optimum

## Likely misconceptions

- MISCONCEPTION: "An enzyme that is slowed down has been denatured." Correction: denaturation means the active site has changed shape so the substrate can no longer bind. At low temperatures, enzymes are usually just slower.
- MISCONCEPTION: "All enzymes have an optimum temperature of 37°C." Correction: many human enzymes work best around 37°C, but enzymes from other organisms may have different optima.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Increasing temperature always increases enzyme activity." Correction: above the optimum, enzyme activity decreases as the active site changes shape.
- MISCONCEPTION: "The enzyme is destroyed by acid in the stomach." Correction: some stomach enzymes work best in acidic conditions. Whether acid denatures an enzyme depends on that enzyme's optimum pH.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Lower pH always means more enzyme activity." Correction: activity depends on the specific enzyme and its optimum pH.
- MISCONCEPTION: "A longer time means faster enzyme activity." Correction: in this practical, a shorter time means faster starch breakdown. Rate can be calculated as 1000 / time.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Iodine digests starch." Correction: iodine tests whether starch is present. Amylase digests starch.
- MISCONCEPTION: "The reaction is finished when iodine turns blue-black." Correction: blue-black means starch is still present. The reaction endpoint is when iodine stays orange-brown.

## Suggested active learning checks

### Slow or denatured?

Sort:

- cold temperature → slower, not denatured
- high temperature above optimum → denatured
- pH close to optimum → fast rate
- extreme pH away from optimum → active site changes shape

### Practical order

Put in order:

- add iodine to spotting tile
- add amylase and buffer to test tube
- add starch and start timer
- keep in water bath
- test drops on iodine every 30 seconds
- record time when iodine stays orange-brown
- repeat with different pH values

### Graph judgement

Question:

A pH graph shows the shortest digestion time at pH 7. What is the optimum pH?

Answer:

pH 7, because the shortest time means the fastest rate.

### Spot the error

Student answer:

> The iodine digested the starch, so the solution stopped turning blue-black.

Correction:

Amylase digested the starch. Iodine only tests whether starch is still present.

## Exam-style questions

### Question 1

Explain why enzyme activity decreases above the optimum temperature.  
3 marks

Mark points:

- active site changes shape
- substrate no longer fits / fewer enzyme-substrate complexes form
- enzyme is denatured

### Question 2

Describe how to investigate the effect of pH on amylase activity.  
6 marks

Mark points:

- use amylase, starch and buffer solutions of different pH
- keep temperature constant using a water bath
- add iodine to spotting tile
- start timer when starch is added
- test samples with iodine at regular intervals
- record time when iodine stays orange-brown
- repeat and calculate mean

### Question 3

Identify the independent, dependent and one control variable in the amylase pH practical.  
3 marks

Mark points:

- independent: pH
- dependent: time taken for starch to be digested
- control: temperature / volumes / concentrations / sampling interval

### Question 4

A student records that starch disappears after 80 seconds.

Calculate the rate using rate = 1000 / time.  
2 marks

Mark points:

- 1000 / 80
- 12.5 arbitrary units

### Question 5

Explain why using a water bath improves the investigation.  
2 marks

Mark points:

- keeps temperature constant
- ensures pH is the only variable affecting rate / makes test fair

### Question 6

A student says: "The enzyme was slower at 20°C because it was denatured." Explain why this is unlikely.  
2 marks

Mark points:

- low temperature usually reduces kinetic energy/collision frequency
- denaturation is caused by high temperature or extreme pH changing the active site shape
