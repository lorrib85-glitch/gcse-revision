# Chapter 14: The enzyme danger zone — Content

## Series: Biology Module 2 — Body systems under pressure (AQA GCSE Combined Science Trilogy 8464, Biology Paper 1)

---

## Big question

Why do some conditions destroy enzymes — and how does the examiner catch students who don't know the difference between slow and dead?

---

## Syllabus coverage

- Effect of temperature on enzyme activity: rate increases with temperature up to the optimum; above the optimum, the enzyme denatures
- Effect of pH on enzyme activity: each enzyme has an optimum pH; activity decreases above and below the optimum; extreme pH denatures the enzyme
- Denaturation: the irreversible change in the shape of an enzyme's active site so the substrate can no longer bind
- Optimum temperature and optimum pH
- Required practical: effect of pH on the rate of amylase activity (using iodine spotting tile; timing colour change from blue-black to orange/yellow)
- Variables in the enzyme investigation: independent (pH), dependent (time for starch to be digested), control (temperature, enzyme concentration, starch concentration, volume)
- Presenting results as a graph and drawing conclusions
- Evaluating the method: suggesting improvements to increase reliability

---

## Key concepts / vocabulary

- **Enzyme:** a biological catalyst; a protein with an active site complementary in shape to its substrate
- **Active site:** the region of the enzyme where the substrate binds; its shape is determined by the enzyme's three-dimensional structure
- **Optimum temperature:** the temperature at which an enzyme works at its fastest rate; for most human enzymes ~37°C
- **Optimum pH:** the pH at which an enzyme works at its fastest rate; varies by enzyme (e.g. amylase ~pH 7; pepsin ~pH 2)
- **Denaturation:** the irreversible change in the shape of an enzyme's active site caused by high temperature or extreme pH; the substrate can no longer bind
- **Rate of reaction:** how fast a reaction proceeds; in enzyme investigations often measured as the time taken for a reaction to complete (shorter time = faster rate)
- **Iodine spotting tile:** a tile with wells used to test samples from an amylase reaction at regular intervals; a colour change from blue-black to orange/yellow indicates starch has been digested
- **Amylase:** an enzyme that breaks down starch into sugars; optimum pH approximately 7; produced in the salivary glands and pancreas
- **Independent variable:** the factor deliberately changed (pH in this practical)
- **Dependent variable:** the factor measured (time for starch to be digested)
- **Control variable:** a factor kept constant to ensure a fair test (e.g. temperature, enzyme and starch concentration)

## Required practicals

**This chapter IS the required practical.** AQA specification: investigate the effect of pH on the rate of reaction of amylase on starch using iodine solution and a spotting tile.

Key practical skills:
- Setting up buffer solutions of different pH
- Using a spotting tile and iodine to track starch breakdown
- Controlling temperature using a water bath
- Recording time for colour change at each pH
- Plotting results and identifying optimum pH from a graph
- Evaluating the method and identifying improvements

## Likely misconceptions

- MISCONCEPTION: "An enzyme that is slowed down has been denatured." Correction: denaturation is specific — it means the active site has permanently changed shape and the enzyme cannot function. At lower temperatures (below optimum), the enzyme is just slower — it can still work. Denaturation is irreversible.
- MISCONCEPTION: "All enzymes have an optimum temperature of 37°C." Correction: 37°C is the optimum for most human body enzymes. Enzymes in other organisms may have different optima (e.g. enzymes in bacteria from hot springs work best at 70°C+).
- MISCONCEPTION: "Increasing temperature always increases enzyme activity." Correction: above the optimum, activity decreases rapidly as the enzyme denatures. The relationship is not linear — it peaks then drops sharply.
- MISCONCEPTION: "The enzyme is destroyed by acid in the stomach." Correction: only enzymes with the wrong optimum pH are denatured by stomach acid. Pepsin (protease in the stomach) has an optimum pH of ~2 — it functions well in acid.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Lower pH always means more enzyme activity." Correction: activity depends on the specific enzyme. Some enzymes (like pepsin) work best at low pH; others (like amylase) work best near neutral pH.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Rate of reaction = how much product is made." Correction: at GCSE, rate is often measured as time taken for a reaction to complete — so a shorter time means a faster rate. Make sure students can invert the relationship when reading graphs.
