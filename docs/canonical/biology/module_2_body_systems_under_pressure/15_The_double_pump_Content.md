## Key concepts / vocabulary

- **Atrium (plural atria):** upper chamber of the heart that receives blood
- **Ventricle:** lower chamber of the heart that pumps blood out of the heart
- **Valve:** structure that prevents backflow of blood
- **Aorta:** large artery carrying oxygenated blood from the left ventricle to the body
- **Vena cava:** large vein carrying deoxygenated blood from the body to the right atrium
- **Pulmonary artery:** carries deoxygenated blood from the right ventricle to the lungs
- **Pulmonary vein:** carries oxygenated blood from the lungs to the left atrium
- **Coronary arteries:** blood vessels that supply the heart muscle with oxygenated blood
- **Double circulatory system:** blood passes through the heart twice for each complete circuit of the body
- **Pulmonary circulation:** heart → lungs → heart
- **Systemic circulation:** heart → body → heart
- **Natural pacemaker:** group of cells in the right atrium that controls the resting heart rate
- **Artificial pacemaker:** electrical device used to correct irregularities in heart rhythm
- **Artery:** blood vessel that carries blood away from the heart under high pressure
- **Vein:** blood vessel that carries blood towards the heart under lower pressure
- **Capillary:** tiny blood vessel with walls one cell thick, allowing exchange of substances with tissues
- **Rate of blood flow:** volume of blood moved per unit time

## Core explanation

The heart is an organ that pumps blood around the body.

Humans have a double circulatory system. This means blood passes through the heart twice during one complete circuit:

1. heart → lungs → heart
2. heart → body → heart

This matters because blood can be sent to the lungs to pick up oxygen, then returned to the heart and pumped around the body at high pressure.

### Heart route

The route of blood is:

1. Deoxygenated blood returns from the body in the vena cava.
2. It enters the right atrium.
3. It moves into the right ventricle.
4. The right ventricle pumps it through the pulmonary artery to the lungs.
5. In the lungs, blood gains oxygen and loses carbon dioxide.
6. Oxygenated blood returns in the pulmonary vein.
7. It enters the left atrium.
8. It moves into the left ventricle.
9. The left ventricle pumps it through the aorta to the body.

Memory check:

- right side → lungs
- left side → body

### Why the left ventricle is thicker

The left ventricle has a thicker muscular wall than the right ventricle.

This is because the left ventricle pumps blood all around the body, so it must generate higher pressure.

The right ventricle only pumps blood to the lungs, which are close to the heart and would be damaged by too much pressure.

### Valves

Valves prevent backflow of blood.

AQA does not require students to memorise all valve names, but students should know that valves make sure blood flows in the correct direction.

Valves are found:

- between heart chambers
- at the exits from the ventricles
- in veins

### Coronary arteries

The heart muscle needs oxygen and glucose for aerobic respiration.

Coronary arteries supply the heart muscle with oxygenated blood.

This idea matters for the next heart-disease chapter: if coronary arteries narrow, the heart muscle receives less oxygen.

### Natural and artificial pacemakers

The natural resting heart rate is controlled by a group of cells in the right atrium that act as a pacemaker.

The pacemaker sends electrical impulses that cause the heart muscle to contract.

An artificial pacemaker can be used if the heart rhythm is irregular.

### Blood vessel structure and function

| Vessel | Direction | Pressure | Structure | Function |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Artery | away from heart | high | thick muscular and elastic walls, small lumen | carries blood away from heart |
| Vein | towards heart | low | thinner walls, larger lumen, valves | carries blood back to heart |
| Capillary | between arteries and veins | low/controlled | walls one cell thick | exchange of substances with tissues |

Important exam precision:

- arteries carry blood away from the heart
- veins carry blood towards the heart
- arteries do not always carry oxygenated blood
- veins do not always carry deoxygenated blood

The exceptions are:

- pulmonary artery: deoxygenated blood from heart to lungs
- pulmonary vein: oxygenated blood from lungs to heart

### Capillaries and exchange

Capillaries are adapted for exchange because:

- walls are one cell thick, giving a short diffusion distance
- they form networks close to body cells
- they allow oxygen and glucose to diffuse from blood into cells
- they allow carbon dioxide and waste products to diffuse from cells into blood

### Blood flow calculation

AQA may ask students to calculate rate of blood flow.

Formula:

> rate of blood flow = volume of blood / time

Worked example:

A heart pumps 4900 cm3 of blood in 70 seconds.

4900 / 70 = 70 cm3/s

Answer: **70 cm3 per second**

## Likely misconceptions

- MISCONCEPTION: "The left side of the heart pumps blood to the lungs." Correction: the right side pumps deoxygenated blood to the lungs. The left side pumps oxygenated blood to the body.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Arteries always carry oxygenated blood." Correction: arteries carry blood away from the heart. The pulmonary artery carries deoxygenated blood.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Veins always carry deoxygenated blood." Correction: veins carry blood towards the heart. The pulmonary vein carries oxygenated blood.
- MISCONCEPTION: "The aorta comes from the right ventricle." Correction: the aorta leaves the left ventricle and carries oxygenated blood to the body.
- MISCONCEPTION: "The pulmonary vein goes from the heart to the lungs." Correction: the pulmonary vein carries blood from the lungs to the heart.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Capillaries have thick walls because they are important." Correction: capillaries have walls one cell thick for a short diffusion distance.
- MISCONCEPTION: "The heart is an artery or vein." Correction: the heart is an organ. Arteries and veins are blood vessels connected to it.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Valves push blood forward." Correction: valves prevent backflow; the heart muscle contraction provides the force.

### Vessel comparison

Sort features:

Artery:

- away from heart
- thick muscular and elastic walls
- high pressure
- small lumen

Vein:

- towards heart
- valves
- lower pressure
- larger lumen

Capillary:

- walls one cell thick
- exchange of substances
- close to body cells

### Spot the error

Student answer:

> The pulmonary artery carries oxygenated blood from the lungs to the heart.

Correction:

The pulmonary artery carries deoxygenated blood from the right ventricle to the lungs. The pulmonary vein carries oxygenated blood from the lungs to the left atrium.

### Question 1

Describe the route of blood from the vena cava to the aorta.  
5 marks

Mark points:

- vena cava to right atrium
- right atrium to right ventricle
- pulmonary artery to lungs
- pulmonary vein to left atrium
- left atrium to left ventricle
- aorta to body

### Question 2

Explain why the left ventricle has a thicker muscular wall than the right ventricle.  
2 marks

Mark points:

- left ventricle pumps blood around the whole body
- must generate higher pressure / pumps further than right ventricle

### Question 3

Compare arteries and veins.  
4 marks

Mark points:

- arteries carry blood away from heart; veins carry blood towards heart
- arteries have thicker muscular/elastic walls
- arteries carry blood at higher pressure
- veins have valves / larger lumen / lower pressure

### Question 4

Explain how capillaries are adapted for exchange.  
3 marks

Mark points:

- walls one cell thick
- short diffusion distance
- networks close to cells / large surface area for exchange

### Question 5

A heart pumps 4200 cm3 of blood in 60 seconds.

Calculate the rate of blood flow.  
2 marks

Mark points:

- rate = volume / time
- 4200 / 60 = 70 cm3/s

### Question 6

Why is it incorrect to say that all arteries carry oxygenated blood?  
2 marks

Mark points:

- arteries are defined as carrying blood away from the heart
- pulmonary artery carries deoxygenated blood to the lungs
