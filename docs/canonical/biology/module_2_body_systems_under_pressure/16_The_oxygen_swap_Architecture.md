## 2. Six-part structure mapping

### Part 1 — Situation + prediction

- Hook: "Every breath moves oxygen from outside your body into your blood — across a barrier thinner than a piece of tissue paper. How?" Cinematic — a single breath, zooming into the alveolus.
- Prediction: "What do you think happens to your ribcage when you breathe in — does it move up and out, or down and in?"
- You will learn: how ventilation works; the structure of the respiratory system; how alveoli are adapted for gas exchange.

**Components:** `CinematicRevealMoment`, `ChapterHookScreen`

---

### Part 2 — Investigate the evidence

- Visual walkthrough of the respiratory system: trachea → bronchi → bronchioles → alveoli, with labels at each level
- Animated or visual sequence showing inhalation (diaphragm contracts, ribs rise, volume up, pressure down, air in) and exhalation (diaphragm relaxes, ribs fall, volume down, pressure up, air out)

**Components:** `VisualLearning`, `ExplainReveal`

---

### Part 3 — Discover the science

- ExplainReveal: gas exchange at the alveolus — oxygen diffuses from alveolus into capillary blood (high to low concentration); CO₂ diffuses from blood into alveolus (high to low concentration); continuous blood flow maintains gradient
- TheoryCompareBlock or ConceptReveal: the four alveolar adaptations — large surface area (millions of alveoli), thin walls (one cell thick), moist lining (gases dissolve), rich blood supply (maintains gradient); explain why each adaptation matters
- Link to Module 1 diffusion: gas exchange is passive diffusion — no energy needed

**Components:** `ExplainReveal`, `ConceptReveal`, `TheoryCompareBlock`

---

### Part 4 — Check precision

- MisconceptionCheck: "We breathe in oxygen and breathe out only carbon dioxide." → false (exhaled air still contains ~16% oxygen)
- MisconceptionCheck: "Gas exchange in the alveoli uses active transport." → false (diffusion — passive, down concentration gradients)
- SpotTheError: "During inhalation, the diaphragm relaxes and moves upward, increasing the pressure inside the chest." (Two errors: the diaphragm contracts and flattens during inhalation; pressure decreases, not increases)

**Components:** `MisconceptionCheck` × 2, `SpotTheError`

## 4. Retrieval points

- Inhalation: diaphragm contracts (flattens), intercostal muscles contract, ribcage rises and expands, volume increases, pressure decreases, air flows in
- Exhalation: diaphragm relaxes (domes), ribcage falls, volume decreases, pressure increases, air flows out
- Gas exchange: O₂ diffuses alveolus → blood; CO₂ diffuses blood → alveolus; both by diffusion down concentration gradients
- Four alveolar adaptations: large surface area, thin walls (one cell thick), moist lining, rich blood supply (maintains gradient)
- Breathing ≠ respiration: breathing is mechanical (ventilation); respiration is a chemical process releasing energy from glucose

---

## 5. Exam skill focus

- 4-mark explain: explain how the alveoli are adapted for gas exchange (each adaptation + why)
- 2-mark explain: explain how inhalation occurs (diaphragm + ribcage + pressure + direction)
- 1-mark describe: describe the direction of oxygen movement during gas exchange
- Data interpretation: identify diffusion direction from a concentration graph

---

## 6. Build notes

The most examined element in this chapter is the four alveolar adaptations — each must be linked explicitly to how it helps gas exchange, not merely listed. Ventilation mechanics (diaphragm contracts = inhales) are frequently confused with exhalation. The distinction between breathing (ventilation) and respiration (cellular energy release) must be made explicit early. The structure-to-function chain for each alveolar adaptation should be taught as a complete sentence, not isolated bullet points.
