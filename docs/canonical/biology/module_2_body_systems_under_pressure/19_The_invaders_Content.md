# Chapter 19: The invaders — Content

## Series: Biology Module 2 — Body systems under pressure (AQA GCSE Combined Science Trilogy 8464, Biology Paper 1)

---

## Big question

How do microscopic invaders make you ill — and how do they get in without you noticing?

---

## Syllabus coverage

- Pathogens: microorganisms that cause infectious disease; four types — bacteria, viruses, fungi, protists
- How pathogens cause disease: bacteria produce toxins; viruses replicate inside cells and damage them; fungi and protists cause damage by invading and consuming host tissue
- Transmission routes: direct contact; contaminated water or food; droplets in air (sneezing, coughing); vectors (organisms that carry disease without being affected themselves)
- Named viral diseases: measles (rash, fever; transmitted via droplets; MMR vaccine); HIV/AIDS (damage to immune system; transmitted via body fluids; no cure; antiretroviral drugs); tobacco mosaic virus (mottling of leaves in plants; disrupts photosynthesis)
- Named bacterial diseases: salmonella (food poisoning; transmitted via undercooked food or contaminated water; toxins cause vomiting and diarrhoea); gonorrhoea (sexually transmitted; treated with antibiotics; increasing antibiotic resistance)
- Named fungal disease: rose black spot (black/purple spots on rose leaves; spread by wind and water; treated with fungicides or by removing affected leaves)
- Named protist disease: malaria (caused by Plasmodium; transmitted via the bite of female Anopheles mosquito; recurring fever; treated with antimalarial drugs; prevention via insecticide-treated bed nets and draining standing water)

---

## Key concepts / vocabulary

- **Pathogen:** a microorganism that causes infectious disease
- **Bacteria:** single-celled prokaryotic organisms; can cause disease by producing toxins
- **Virus:** non-cellular pathogen; replicates by hijacking the host cell's machinery; damages cells in the process
- **Fungi:** eukaryotic organisms; some cause disease by invading and breaking down host tissue (e.g. rose black spot, athlete's foot)
- **Protist:** single-celled eukaryotic organism; some are parasitic (e.g. Plasmodium causing malaria)
- **Toxin:** a poisonous substance produced by bacteria that damages host cells or tissues
- **Vector:** an organism that carries a pathogen from one host to another without being affected itself (e.g. Anopheles mosquito for malaria)
- **Transmission:** the spread of a pathogen from one host to another
- **Droplet infection:** transmission of pathogens via tiny water droplets released by sneezing, coughing or breathing
- **Measles:** viral disease causing fever and a red skin rash; highly contagious; prevented by MMR vaccine
- **HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus):** a virus that attacks the immune system; transmitted via body fluids; leads to AIDS if untreated; managed with antiretroviral drugs
- **Tobacco mosaic virus (TMV):** a plant virus causing mottled, discoloured leaves; disrupts photosynthesis; no cure
- **Salmonella:** a bacterial pathogen causing food poisoning; produces toxins causing vomiting and diarrhoea
- **Gonorrhoea:** a sexually transmitted bacterial infection; treatable with antibiotics; increasing resistance is a concern
- **Malaria:** a disease caused by the protist Plasmodium, transmitted by female Anopheles mosquitoes; causes recurring fever
- **Rose black spot:** a fungal disease of rose plants causing black/purple leaf spots

## Likely misconceptions

- MISCONCEPTION: "All bacteria cause disease." Correction: most bacteria are harmless or beneficial (e.g. gut bacteria aid digestion; bacteria are used in food production). Only a small number are pathogenic.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Viruses are alive." Correction: viruses are not considered living organisms — they have no cells, no metabolism of their own, and can only reproduce inside a host cell. This is why antibiotics do not work against viruses.
- MISCONCEPTION: "All mosquitoes transmit malaria." Correction: only the bite of the female Anopheles mosquito transmits Plasmodium. Male mosquitoes feed on plant nectar, not blood.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Rose black spot is a viral disease." Correction: rose black spot is caused by a fungus (Diplocarpon rosae), not a virus.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Antibiotics can treat all infectious diseases." Correction: antibiotics only work against bacterial infections. They have no effect on viral, fungal or protist diseases.
- MISCONCEPTION: "HIV and AIDS are the same thing." Correction: HIV is the virus that attacks the immune system. AIDS is the late stage of HIV infection when the immune system is so damaged it can no longer fight infections. HIV infection does not immediately mean AIDS.
