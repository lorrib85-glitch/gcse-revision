# Chapter 19: The invaders — Content

## Series: Biology Module 2 — Body systems under pressure (AQA GCSE Combined Science Trilogy 8464, Biology Paper 1)

---

## Big question

How do microscopic invaders make you ill — and how do they spread without you noticing?

---

## Syllabus coverage

- Pathogens as microorganisms that cause communicable disease
- Four pathogen groups: bacteria, viruses, fungi and protists
- How bacteria make people ill: reproduce rapidly and produce toxins
- How viruses make people ill: reproduce inside cells and damage/destroy host cells
- How pathogens spread: direct contact, water, air, body fluids and vectors
- Reducing spread: hygiene, destroying infected organisms/tissues, isolation, vaccination and controlling vectors
- Named viral diseases: measles, HIV/AIDS and tobacco mosaic virus
- Named bacterial diseases: salmonella and gonorrhoea
- Named fungal disease: rose black spot
- Named protist disease: malaria
- Antibiotics treat bacterial disease, not viral disease
- Antibiotic resistance in gonorrhoea as an increasing problem

---

## Key concepts / vocabulary

- **Pathogen:** microorganism that causes disease
- **Communicable disease:** disease that can spread between organisms
- **Bacteria:** single-celled prokaryotes; some cause disease by producing toxins
- **Virus:** pathogen that reproduces inside host cells and damages them
- **Fungus:** eukaryotic organism; some fungi cause plant or animal disease
- **Protist:** single-celled eukaryotic organism; some are parasites
- **Toxin:** poisonous substance produced by some bacteria
- **Vector:** organism that carries and transmits a pathogen without necessarily getting the disease itself
- **Transmission:** spread of a pathogen from one host to another
- **Droplet infection:** spread through tiny droplets from coughs or sneezes
- **Body fluids:** blood, semen or vaginal fluids; important in HIV transmission
- **Antibiotic resistance:** when bacteria evolve so antibiotics no longer kill them effectively

---

## Core explanation

A pathogen is a microorganism that causes disease.

The four pathogen groups are:

| Pathogen type | How it can cause disease | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Bacteria | reproduce rapidly and may produce toxins | salmonella, gonorrhoea |
| Viruses | reproduce inside cells and damage/destroy them | measles, HIV, TMV |
| Fungi | infect tissues and damage cells | rose black spot |
| Protists | some are parasites that damage hosts | malaria |

### How pathogens spread

Pathogens may spread by:

- air, such as droplets from coughs and sneezes
- water or contaminated food
- direct contact
- sexual contact/body fluids
- vectors, such as mosquitoes

Ways to reduce spread:

- hygiene, including handwashing and food hygiene
- isolation of infected individuals
- vaccination
- destroying infected plant material
- controlling vectors, such as mosquitoes

## Named diseases

### Measles

Pathogen type:

- virus

Transmission:

- droplets from coughs and sneezes

Symptoms:

- fever
- red skin rash

Prevention:

- vaccination, including MMR

Exam note:

Measles can be serious and may cause complications.

### HIV and AIDS

Pathogen type:

- virus

Transmission:

- sexual contact
- exchange of body fluids, such as blood

Effects:

- initial flu-like illness
- HIV attacks immune cells
- if untreated, late-stage HIV can lead to AIDS
- AIDS means the immune system is badly damaged and cannot deal with other infections or cancers effectively

Treatment/control:

- antiretroviral drugs help control HIV replication

### Tobacco mosaic virus

Pathogen type:

- virus

Affects:

- plants

Effects:

- mosaic pattern of discolouration on leaves
- reduced chlorophyll
- reduced photosynthesis
- reduced growth

### Salmonella

Pathogen type:

- bacterium

Transmission:

- contaminated food

Symptoms:

- fever
- stomach cramps
- vomiting
- diarrhoea

Cause of symptoms:

- bacteria and toxins affect the digestive system

Prevention:

- cook food thoroughly
- good food hygiene
- vaccination of poultry helps reduce spread in the UK

### Gonorrhoea

Pathogen type:

- bacterium

Transmission:

- sexual contact

Symptoms:

- thick yellow or green discharge from vagina or penis
- pain when urinating

Treatment/control:

- antibiotics
- condoms reduce spread
- screening and treating infected people

Problem:

- antibiotic-resistant strains have appeared, making treatment harder

### Rose black spot

Pathogen type:

- fungus

Affects:

- rose plants

Symptoms/effects:

- purple or black spots on leaves
- leaves turn yellow and drop early
- less photosynthesis
- reduced growth

Transmission:

- wind
- water

Treatment/control:

- fungicides
- removing and destroying infected leaves

### Malaria

Pathogen type:

- protist

Transmission:

- mosquito vector

Key precision:

Malaria is caused by a protist. Mosquitoes do not cause malaria; they transmit the protist.

Symptoms:

- recurrent fever
- can be fatal

Prevention/control:

- mosquito nets
- insecticides
- preventing mosquitoes from breeding, such as removing standing water

## Likely misconceptions

- MISCONCEPTION: "All bacteria cause disease." Correction: many bacteria are harmless or useful; only some are pathogenic.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Viruses are killed by antibiotics." Correction: antibiotics work against bacteria, not viruses.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Malaria is caused by mosquitoes." Correction: malaria is caused by a protist; mosquitoes are vectors.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Rose black spot is viral." Correction: rose black spot is caused by a fungus.
- MISCONCEPTION: "HIV and AIDS are the same thing." Correction: HIV is the virus; AIDS is the late stage when the immune system is severely damaged.
- MISCONCEPTION: "TMV affects humans who smoke tobacco." Correction: tobacco mosaic virus is a plant disease.
- MISCONCEPTION: "Antibiotic resistance happens in the human body because the person becomes resistant." Correction: bacteria become resistant, not the patient.

## Suggested active learning checks

### Disease sort

Virus:

- measles
- HIV
- tobacco mosaic virus

Bacterium:

- salmonella
- gonorrhoea

Fungus:

- rose black spot

Protist:

- malaria

### Transmission match

- measles → droplets
- HIV → body fluids/sexual contact
- salmonella → contaminated food
- gonorrhoea → sexual contact
- rose black spot → wind/water
- malaria → mosquito vector

### Spot the error

Student answer:

> Malaria is caused by mosquitoes, so antibiotics can treat it.

Correction:

Malaria is caused by a protist and transmitted by mosquito vectors. Antibiotics treat bacterial infections, not protist diseases.

## Exam-style questions

### Question 1

Define pathogen and name four types of pathogen.  
5 marks

Mark points:

- microorganism that causes disease
- bacteria
- viruses
- fungi
- protists

### Question 2

Compare how bacteria and viruses can cause disease.  
4 marks

Mark points:

- bacteria reproduce rapidly
- bacteria may produce toxins
- viruses reproduce inside host cells
- viruses damage/destroy host cells

### Question 3

Explain how malaria is transmitted and give two ways transmission can be reduced.  
4 marks

Mark points:

- malaria is caused by a protist
- mosquitoes act as vectors
- mosquito nets/insecticides
- prevent mosquitoes breeding / remove standing water

### Question 4

Explain why tobacco mosaic virus can reduce plant growth.  
3 marks

Mark points:

- causes discolouration/mosaic pattern on leaves
- less chlorophyll / less light absorbed
- reduced photosynthesis / less glucose for growth

### Question 5

Explain why gonorrhoea has become harder to treat.  
3 marks

Mark points:

- some bacteria have developed antibiotic resistance
- resistant bacteria survive treatment
- resistant bacteria reproduce and spread
