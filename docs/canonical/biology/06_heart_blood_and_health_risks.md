## Chapter purpose

By the end, students should be able to:

- explain how the heart pumps blood to the lungs and body

- explain coronary heart disease

- explain health, disease and risk factors

- interpret risk-factor data with care

## Hook

Your heart is not just a pump.

It is two pumps working side by side: one sends blood to the lungs, the other sends blood to the body.

If the pipes narrow, the whole system is in danger.

That is why AQA links the heart to lifestyle, risk factors and disease.

## Core content

### 1. Double circulatory system

Humans have a double circulatory system.

This means blood passes through the heart twice for each complete circuit:

1. Heart to lungs to heart.
2. Heart to body to heart.

Why this matters:

- blood goes to the lungs to pick up oxygen
- blood then returns to the heart
- the heart pumps oxygenated blood around the body at high pressure

Exam wording:

> The right ventricle pumps deoxygenated blood to the lungs. The left ventricle pumps oxygenated blood around the body.

### 2. Heart structure

Students should know:

- right atrium
- right ventricle
- left atrium
- left ventricle
- aorta
- vena cava
- pulmonary artery
- pulmonary vein
- coronary arteries

Important vessel roles:

- **Vena cava:** brings deoxygenated blood from the body to the heart.
- **Pulmonary artery:** carries deoxygenated blood from the heart to the lungs.
- **Pulmonary vein:** carries oxygenated blood from the lungs to the heart.
- **Aorta:** carries oxygenated blood from the heart to the body.
- **Coronary arteries:** supply the heart muscle with oxygenated blood.

AQA note:

Students do not need to know the names of the heart valves, but they should understand that valves prevent backflow.

### 3. Natural and artificial pacemakers

The natural resting heart rate is controlled by a group of cells in the right atrium that act as a pacemaker.

Artificial pacemakers are electrical devices used to correct irregularities in heart rhythm.

### 4. Blood vessels

| Vessel | Structure | Function |
|---|---|---|
| Arteries | thick muscular walls, small lumen | carry blood away from the heart under high pressure |
| Veins | thinner walls, large lumen, valves | carry blood back to the heart under lower pressure |
| Capillaries | walls one cell thick | allow exchange of substances with cells |

Exam traps:

- arteries carry blood away from the heart, not always oxygenated blood
- veins carry blood towards the heart, not always deoxygenated blood
- pulmonary artery and pulmonary vein are the exceptions students often mix up

### 5. Blood components

Blood is a tissue made of plasma with cells and fragments suspended in it.

| Component | Function |
|---|---|
| Plasma | transports dissolved substances such as glucose, amino acids, carbon dioxide, urea and hormones |
| Red blood cells | transport oxygen using haemoglobin |
| White blood cells | defend against pathogens |
| Platelets | help blood clot |

Red blood cell adaptations:

- biconcave shape gives large surface area
- contains haemoglobin to bind oxygen
- no nucleus, giving more space for haemoglobin

### 6. Coronary heart disease

Coronary heart disease happens when fatty material builds up inside the coronary arteries.

This narrows the arteries and reduces blood flow to the heart muscle.

The heart muscle receives less oxygen, so it cannot respire properly.

This can lead to chest pain or a heart attack.

### 7. Treatments for cardiovascular disease

Students should be able to evaluate advantages and disadvantages.

#### Stents

- keep narrowed coronary arteries open
- restore blood flow
- reduce risk of heart attack
- surgery has risks such as infection or blood clots

#### Statins

- lower blood cholesterol
- slow down fatty material build-up
- reduce risk of cardiovascular disease
- must be taken long-term
- can have side effects

#### Faulty valves

Faulty valves may not open properly or may leak.

They can be replaced with:

- biological valves
- mechanical valves

#### Heart transplant / artificial heart

- donor heart or heart-lung transplant may be used in heart failure
- artificial hearts may keep a patient alive while waiting for transplant or allow the heart to rest
- transplants risk rejection and require immunosuppressant drugs

### 8. Health and disease

Health is a state of physical and mental well-being.

Disease can be:

- communicable: spread between organisms
- non-communicable: not spread between organisms

Other factors affecting health:

- diet
- stress
- life situation

Different diseases can interact:

- immune system defects increase risk of infectious disease
- viruses can trigger some cancers
- immune reactions can trigger allergies
- severe physical illness can lead to mental illness such as depression

### 9. Lifestyle risk factors

A risk factor is something linked to an increased chance of disease.

Examples:

- diet, smoking and exercise affect cardiovascular disease
- obesity is a risk factor for Type 2 diabetes
- alcohol affects the liver and brain
- smoking increases risk of lung disease and lung cancer
- smoking and alcohol affect unborn babies
- carcinogens, including ionising radiation, increase cancer risk

Important exam distinction:

- correlation means two factors are linked
- causal mechanism means one factor has been proven to contribute to the disease

### 10. Cancer

Cancer is caused by changes in cells that lead to uncontrolled growth and division.

Benign tumours:

- growth of abnormal cells
- contained in one area
- usually within a membrane
- do not invade other tissues

Malignant tumours:

- cancers
- invade neighbouring tissues
- can spread through the blood
- can form secondary tumours

Prompt:

## Exam practice

### Question 1 — double circulation

Explain why humans have a double circulatory system.  
4 marks

Mark points:

- blood passes through heart twice per circuit
- right side pumps blood to lungs
- left side pumps blood to body
- oxygenated blood can be pumped around body at high pressure

### Question 2 — coronary heart disease

Explain how coronary heart disease can lead to a heart attack.  
4 marks

Mark points:

- fatty material builds up in coronary arteries
- arteries narrow
- reduced blood flow to heart muscle
- less oxygen reaches heart muscle
- less aerobic respiration
- heart muscle cells may die

### Question 3 — treatments

Evaluate the use of statins and stents for treating cardiovascular disease.  
6 marks

High-mark answer should include:

- statins lower cholesterol and slow fatty build-up
- statins are long-term drugs and may have side effects
- stents physically keep arteries open
- stents restore blood flow quickly
- stent surgery has risks such as infection or blood clots
- balanced judgement

### Question 4 — blood

Describe the function of plasma, red blood cells, white blood cells and platelets.  
4 marks

Mark points:

- plasma transports dissolved substances
- red blood cells transport oxygen
- white blood cells defend against pathogens
- platelets help blood clot
