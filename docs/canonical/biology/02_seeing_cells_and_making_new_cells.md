# Biology Chapter 2 — Seeing cells and making new cells

AQA Combined Science: Trilogy 8464  
Biology Topic 1: Cell biology

## Chapter purpose

This chapter follows **Building blocks**. Chapter 1 teaches what animal, plant and bacterial cells contain. Chapter 2 teaches how scientists see cells, measure cells, and explain how cells divide to make new cells.

Do not include photosynthesis here. Photosynthesis belongs in Bioenergetics.

## Student-facing hook

Your body is not built cell by cell once. It is constantly replacing, repairing and rebuilding itself.

A cut heals. Hair grows. Skin renews. An embryo becomes a baby.

That only works because cells can copy themselves accurately.

But first, scientists had to learn how to see them.

## Learning goals

By the end, students should be able to:

- explain why microscopes changed our understanding of cells
- compare light microscopes and electron microscopes
- calculate magnification, image size and real size
- convert between mm, µm and nm
- describe what chromosomes are
- describe the cell cycle and mitosis in AQA-safe language
- explain why mitosis matters for growth and repair
- explain what stem cells are and evaluate their uses and risks

## Core content

### 1. Microscopes reveal cells

Simple explanation:

Cells are too small to study properly with the naked eye, so scientists use microscopes.

A **light microscope** uses light and lenses. It can show whole cells and some larger structures, such as the nucleus and chloroplasts.

An **electron microscope** has much higher magnification and resolution. It can show much finer detail, including smaller organelles such as ribosomes.

Key distinction:

- **Magnification** = how many times bigger the image is than the real object.
- **Resolution** = how clearly two close points can be seen as separate.

Exam wording:

> Electron microscopes have higher magnification and higher resolution than light microscopes, so they allow scientists to see smaller sub-cellular structures in more detail.

### 2. Required practical: using a light microscope

Students must know the idea of the required practical:

**Required practical activity 1:** use a light microscope to observe, draw and label a selection of plant and animal cells. A magnification scale must be included.

Method summary:

1. Place the slide on the microscope stage.
2. Start with the lowest power objective lens.
3. Use the coarse focus knob to bring the image roughly into focus.
4. Use the fine focus knob to sharpen the image.
5. Increase magnification if needed.
6. Draw cells using clear, unshaded lines.
7. Add labels and include magnification or a scale.

Common exam traps:

- Do not start on the highest magnification.
- Do not use sketchy shaded drawings.
- Label visible structures only.
- Use a sharp pencil and clear lines.

### 3. Magnification calculations

Formula:

> magnification = image size ÷ real size

Rearranged:

> image size = magnification × real size

> real size = image size ÷ magnification

Memory trick:

**I AM**

Image size = Actual size × Magnification

Use a triangle if the app has one:

Image size at the top.  
Actual size and Magnification at the bottom.

### 4. Units and scale

Students need to move between units.

Core conversions:

- 1 mm = 1000 µm
- 1 µm = 1000 nm
- 1 mm = 1,000,000 nm

Exam reminder:

Always convert both measurements into the same unit before calculating magnification.

Worked example:

A cell is 50 µm wide. Its image is 25 mm wide. Calculate the magnification.

Step 1: convert 25 mm to µm.  
25 mm = 25,000 µm

Step 2: magnification = image size ÷ real size  
25,000 ÷ 50 = 500

Answer: **×500**

### 5. Chromosomes

A chromosome is a long molecule of DNA found in the nucleus.

Each chromosome carries many genes.

In body cells, chromosomes are normally found in pairs.

Use simple wording:

> DNA is the instruction code. Chromosomes are packages of DNA. Genes are sections of DNA that code for characteristics or proteins.

### 6. The cell cycle and mitosis

Cells divide in a series of stages called the **cell cycle**.

AQA-safe stages:

1. The cell grows and increases the number of sub-cellular structures, such as ribosomes and mitochondria.
2. The DNA replicates to make two copies of each chromosome.
3. In mitosis, one set of chromosomes is pulled to each end of the cell and the nucleus divides.
4. The cytoplasm and cell membrane divide to form two genetically identical daughter cells.

Students do not need named phases of mitosis.

Key outcome:

> Mitosis produces two genetically identical cells with the same number of chromosomes as the parent cell.

Why it matters:

- growth
- repair
- replacement of damaged cells
- asexual reproduction in some organisms

Common mistake:

Wrong: mitosis produces four different cells.  
Correct: mitosis produces two genetically identical cells.

### 7. Stem cells

A **stem cell** is an undifferentiated cell that can divide and differentiate into specialised cells.

Types required for Trilogy:

#### Embryonic stem cells

- Found in early embryos.
- Can differentiate into most types of human cell.
- Potential for treating conditions such as diabetes and paralysis.
- Ethical concerns because embryos are destroyed.

#### Adult stem cells

- Found in some adult tissues, such as bone marrow.
- Can form fewer types of cell than embryonic stem cells.
- Bone marrow stem cells can form blood cells.

#### Plant meristem stem cells

- Found in growing tips of roots and shoots.
- Can differentiate throughout the life of the plant.
- Used to clone plants quickly and economically.
- Can preserve rare species or produce many crop plants with useful features.

### 8. Therapeutic cloning

Simple explanation:

In therapeutic cloning, an embryo is produced with the same genes as the patient. Stem cells from this embryo are less likely to be rejected by the patient’s body.

Benefits:

- could replace damaged cells
- could treat conditions such as diabetes or paralysis
- cells are genetically matched to the patient, reducing rejection

Risks/concerns:

- possible transfer of viral infection
- stem cells may grow uncontrollably
- ethical or religious objections to embryo use

## Exam practice

### Question 1 — microscopy calculation

A student sees a cell under a microscope. The image is 30 mm wide. The real cell is 60 µm wide.

Calculate the magnification.  
**3 marks**

Mark scheme:

- converts 30 mm to 30,000 µm
- uses magnification = image size ÷ real size
- 30,000 ÷ 60 = ×500

### Question 2 — mitosis

Describe the main stages of the cell cycle that lead to two new cells being formed.  
**4 marks**

Mark scheme:

- cell grows
- increases number of sub-cellular structures
- DNA replicates
- chromosomes are separated / nucleus divides
- cytoplasm and cell membrane divide
- two genetically identical daughter cells form

### Question 3 — stem cells

Evaluate the use of embryonic stem cells in medical treatments.  
**6 marks**

High-mark answer should include:

- embryonic stem cells can differentiate into most cell types
- could replace damaged cells/tissues
- could treat conditions such as diabetes/paralysis
- ethical concerns because embryos are destroyed
- risk of infection or uncontrolled cell growth
- balanced judgement
