## Big question

Why do −3 × −2 = +6 and not −6, and how do examiners use this rule to trap students every single paper?

## Key concepts and vocabulary

- **Negative number:** any number less than zero; written with a − sign
- **Absolute value / magnitude:** the size of a number ignoring sign; |−5| = 5
- **Number line:** negative numbers extend to the left of zero; −10 is further left (smaller) than −2
- **Difference:** for temperature, difference = larger value − smaller value (always positive)
- **Directed number:** a number with a sign indicating direction (positive = right/up, negative = left/down)
- **Double negative:** subtracting a negative is equivalent to adding a positive: a − (−b) = a + b
- **Debt/credit:** overdraft = negative balance; credit = positive balance

## Required methods

### Ordering negatives
1. Draw a number line mentally (or on paper)
2. Numbers further left = smaller; −10 < −2 < 0 < 5
3. For decimals: −0.3 is to the right of −0.35, so −0.3 > −0.35

### Adding and subtracting with negatives
- **Adding a negative:** a + (−b) = a − b (move left on number line)
- **Subtracting a negative:** a − (−b) = a + b (double negative = addition; move right)
- **Useful check:** temperature change — if temperature goes from −3°C to 5°C, rise = 5 − (−3) = 5 + 3 = 8°C

### Multiplying and dividing with negatives
**Sign rules (must memorise):**
- Positive × Positive = Positive (+)
- Positive × Negative = Negative (−)
- Negative × Positive = Negative (−)
- Negative × Negative = Positive (+)
- Same rule applies to division
- **Memory hook:** "Same signs → positive; different signs → negative"

**Examples:**
- (−3) × 4 = −12
- (−6) × (−2) = +12
- (−15) ÷ 3 = −5
- (−8) ÷ (−4) = +2

### Squaring a negative
- (−4)² = (−4) × (−4) = +16 (NOT −16)
- −4² = −(4²) = −16 (brackets matter — the square applies to 4, not to −4)

## Likely misconceptions (from mark schemes and research)

- **MISCONCEPTION:** "(−3) × 3 = 6" (ignoring the negative sign or applying wrong sign rule). Correction: negative × positive = negative; answer is −9.
- **MISCONCEPTION:** "(−8)² = −64." Correction: (−8)² = (−8) × (−8) = +64. Squaring a negative always gives a positive. Contrast: −8² = −(8²) = −64 (no brackets).
- **MISCONCEPTION:** "−3 > −1 because 3 > 1." Correction: on a number line, −3 is further left than −1; −3 < −1.
- **MISCONCEPTION:** "5 − (−3) = 2" (treating double negative as subtraction). Correction: 5 − (−3) = 5 + 3 = 8.
- **MISCONCEPTION:** Confusing temperature difference with temperature change direction. E.g. "If temperature rises from −5°C to 2°C, the rise is 2 − (−5) = 7°C" — students often write 2 + 5 = 7 correctly but forget to set up the subtraction for rise/fall questions with negatives.
- **MISCONCEPTION:** "(−2) × (−3) = −6." Correction: negative × negative = positive; answer is +6. This is the classic trap.

## Exam angles (from AQA past papers)

**How N1/N2 with negatives appears on Paper 1 (non-calculator):**

1. **Ordering negatives (1 mark):** "Write these temperatures in order from coldest to warmest: −8°C, 3°C, −1°C, 5°C" — standard; students must not write −8 as largest
2. **Calculation with negatives (1–2 marks):** "Work out (−3) × (−4)" or "Work out 8 − (−5)" — routine sign rule application
3. **Negative in context (2–3 marks):** "A company's profit over 3 months is −£2400, −£1800, +£950. Find the total." — addition with negatives in finance context
4. **Squaring negative (1 mark):** "Work out (−7)²" — appears regularly; answer should be +49
5. **Temperature change (2 marks):** "At midnight it is −6°C. By noon it is 11°C. What is the rise in temperature?" — requires 11 − (−6) = 17
6. **Coordinates with negatives (embedded later):** plotting points in quadrants 2, 3, 4 — prerequisite understanding built here

**AQA wording patterns:**
- "Work out" with negative values in brackets — student must apply sign rules
- Temperatures are the most common N1 context for negatives on non-calculator paper
- Mark scheme: negative products/quotients often have SC (special case) for students who get magnitude right but sign wrong
