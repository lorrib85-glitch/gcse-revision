## Big question

Why does 60 ÷ 2 + 4 = 34 and not 10 — and how does misreading this cost students multiple marks every paper?

## AQA syllabus coverage

**Spec codes: N3, N6, N7**

- Order of operations: brackets first, then indices (powers/roots), then division/multiplication (left to right), then addition/subtraction (left to right) — BIDMAS/BODMAS
- Brackets as a tool to override default order
- Powers in calculations: recognise and apply before addition/subtraction
- Roots in calculations: square roots and cube roots (non-calculator: only perfect squares/cubes)
- Reciprocals in calculations: 1/x
- Calculator entry errors: failing to bracket expressions entered into a calculator
- Estimating before using a calculator: round to 1 s.f. to check reasonableness
- Recognise when a calculator answer cannot be right (order of magnitude check)

## Key concepts and vocabulary

- **BIDMAS / BODMAS:** mnemonic for order of operations:
  - **B** — Brackets (evaluate what's inside first)
  - **I/O** — Indices / Order (powers and roots)
  - **D** — Division
  - **M** — Multiplication
  - **A** — Addition
  - **S** — Subtraction
  - D and M are equal priority — work left to right. A and S are equal priority — work left to right.
- **Index (plural: indices):** the power to which a base is raised; e.g. in 5³, the index is 3
- **Square:** multiply a number by itself; 6² = 6 × 6 = 36
- **Cube:** multiply a number by itself three times; 4³ = 4 × 4 × 4 = 64
- **Square root (√):** the inverse of squaring; √49 = 7 (must know up to √225 = 15)
- **Cube root (∛):** the inverse of cubing; ∛8 = 2, ∛27 = 3, ∛125 = 5, ∛1000 = 10
- **Reciprocal:** the reciprocal of x is 1/x; the reciprocal of 4 is 0.25
- **Estimate:** a rough calculation using rounded values (usually to 1 significant figure) to check whether a calculator answer is plausible
- **Order of magnitude:** the approximate size of a number (e.g. hundreds, thousands); useful for spotting calculator errors

## Required methods

### Evaluating an expression with BIDMAS
1. Evaluate everything inside **B**rackets first (working innermost outward)
2. Apply **I**ndices (powers and roots)
3. Apply **D**ivision and **M**ultiplication, left to right
4. Apply **A**ddition and **S**ubtraction, left to right

**Worked example:** 3 + 4 × 2² − (6 ÷ 3)
- Brackets: (6 ÷ 3) = 2 → 3 + 4 × 2² − 2
- Indices: 2² = 4 → 3 + 4 × 4 − 2
- Multiplication: 4 × 4 = 16 → 3 + 16 − 2
- Addition then subtraction: 3 + 16 = 19; 19 − 2 = 17

### Calculator entry errors
- Danger: typing 3 + 4 × 2² without brackets gives the same answer (calculator applies BIDMAS)
- Danger: typing (3+4) × 2² then forgetting to square — calculator processes left to right
- Rule: use brackets in your calculator entry to match the mathematical expression exactly
- Check: (numerator) ÷ (denominator) — must bracket both if using ÷ button

### Estimation before calculating
1. Round each number to 1 significant figure
2. Perform the simplified calculation mentally
3. Compare to calculator result — if very different, check bracket entry

**Example:** (4.83 × 21.7) ÷ 9.2 ≈ (5 × 20) ÷ 10 = 100 ÷ 10 = 10; calculator answer should be around 10

### Perfect squares and cubes (non-calculator — must know)
- Squares: 1, 4, 9, 16, 25, 36, 49, 64, 81, 100, 121, 144, 169, 196, 225 (up to 15²)
- Cubes: 1, 8, 27, 64, 125, 216, 343, 512, 729, 1000

## Likely misconceptions (from mark schemes and research)

- **MISCONCEPTION:** "60 ÷ 2 + 4 = 10" (treating 2 + 4 = 6 then 60 ÷ 6). Correction: division before addition; 60 ÷ 2 = 30, then 30 + 4 = 34.
- **MISCONCEPTION:** "3 + 4 × 5 = 35" (adding first). Correction: multiplication before addition; 4 × 5 = 20, then 3 + 20 = 23.
- **MISCONCEPTION:** Multiplication takes priority over division. Correction: D and M have equal priority — work left to right. 12 ÷ 4 × 3 = 3 × 3 = 9, NOT 12 ÷ 12 = 1.
- **MISCONCEPTION:** "√(16 + 9) = √16 + √9 = 4 + 3 = 7." Correction: evaluate inside brackets first; √(16 + 9) = √25 = 5.
- **MISCONCEPTION:** "(−3)² = −9." Correction: (−3)² = 9 (square of a negative is positive). See Chapter 3.
- **MISCONCEPTION:** Entering 8 ÷ 2(2+2) into a calculator without checking bracket intent — a common viral maths problem; the AQA answer is 16 (left to right after brackets resolved), but students need to bracket according to the intended expression.
- **MISCONCEPTION:** Not checking calculator answers for reasonableness — accepts answers that are off by a factor of 10.

## Exam angles (from AQA past papers)

**How N3/N6/N7 appears on Paper 1 (non-calculator) and Papers 2/3 (calculator):**

1. **Evaluate with BIDMAS (1–2 marks):** "Work out 5 + 3 × 2²" — tests that student applies indices before multiplication before addition; answer 17
2. **Insert brackets to make a statement true (2 marks):** "Place brackets in this expression to make it equal 20: 5 + 3 × 4 − 1" — students must try positions; (5+3) × (4−1) = 8 × 3 = 24 (no); 5 + 3 × (4−1) = 5 + 9 = 14 (no); etc. This is an AO3 question.
3. **Estimation check (2 marks):** "A student says 4.79 × 20.3 ÷ 9.76 ≈ 10. Is this reasonable? Show your estimate." — AO2 mark for communicating the estimate; answer with working (5 × 20 ÷ 10 = 10), B1 for correct rounded values, B1 for conclusion
4. **Non-calculator power (1 mark):** "Work out √144" or "Work out ∛27" — requires recall of perfect squares/cubes
5. **Calculator entry (2 marks, Paper 2/3):** "Use your calculator to work out (5.8 + 3.1)² ÷ (4.7 − 2.1). Write down all the digits on your display then round to 3 s.f." — tests that student brackets numerator and denominator correctly
6. **Indices law warm-up:** "Simplify 2³ × 2⁴" — tests index law (covered more in Ch7 but often paired with BIDMAS in AQA questions); answer 2⁷ = 128

**AQA wording patterns:**
- "Work out, showing your method" — written calculation expected
- "Show that [expression] = [value]" — must show all steps; answer alone = 0
- "Use your calculator to work out" — bracket entry requirement implied
- "Give your answer correct to [n] significant figures" — follows from calculator use

**Mark scheme notes:**
- M1 for correct method (e.g. correct bracket resolution), A1 for answer
- Estimation questions: B1 for each reasonable rounded value; B1 for conclusion
- ft: if student makes one bracket error but applies BIDMAS correctly to their expression, method mark may be awarded
