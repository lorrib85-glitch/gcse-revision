## Big question

Why does dividing by 1/2 give you twice as much — and how does "flip and multiply" save your marks when the examiner asks for an exact answer?

## Key concepts and vocabulary

- **Reciprocal of a fraction:** flip numerator and denominator; reciprocal of 3/4 is 4/3; reciprocal of a/b is b/a
- **Common denominator:** required for addition and subtraction; use LCM of denominators
- **Cancelling:** simplifying by dividing numerator and denominator (or cross-dividing before multiplying) to keep numbers smaller
- **Exact answer:** leaving a result as a fraction rather than converting to a decimal (e.g. 7/8, not 0.875)
- **Adding fractions:** must share a common denominator; only numerators are added
- **Multiplying fractions:** no common denominator needed; numerator × numerator; denominator × denominator
- **Dividing fractions:** invert the second fraction and multiply; a/b ÷ c/d = a/b × d/c
- **Mixed number in calculations:** must convert to improper fraction first — critical before multiplying or dividing

## Required methods

### Adding and subtracting fractions
1. Find a common denominator (LCM of the two denominators from Ch6)
2. Convert each fraction to the common denominator (multiply numerator and denominator)
3. Add or subtract numerators only — do NOT add/subtract denominators
4. Simplify result if possible

**Example:** 3/4 + 5/6 → common denominator 12: 9/12 + 10/12 = 19/12 = 1 and 7/12

**Example:** 11/18 − 1/3 → common denominator 18: 11/18 − 6/18 = 5/18

### Multiplying fractions
1. Multiply numerators together
2. Multiply denominators together
3. Simplify (can simplify by cross-cancelling before multiplying to keep numbers small)

**Example:** 2/3 × 5/7 = 10/21

**Example with cancelling:** 4/9 × 3/8 = (4×3)/(9×8); cancel: 4÷4=1, 8÷4=2; 3÷3=1, 9÷3=3 → 1/6

### Dividing fractions
1. **Keep** the first fraction
2. **Change** ÷ to ×
3. **Flip** the second fraction (take its reciprocal)
4. Multiply as above; simplify

**Example:** 3/4 ÷ 3/8 = 3/4 × 8/3 = 24/12 = 2

**Example with mixed number:** 2⅓ ÷ 4/5
1. Convert: 2⅓ = 7/3
2. Divide: 7/3 ÷ 4/5 = 7/3 × 5/4 = 35/12 = 2 and 11/12

### Mixed numbers in calculations
- ALWAYS convert to improper fraction before multiplying or dividing
- For addition/subtraction of mixed numbers: can work with whole parts and fraction parts separately, then combine — OR convert to improper fractions

## Likely misconceptions (from mark schemes and research)

- **MISCONCEPTION:** "Dividing fractions: divide numerators and divide denominators." E.g. 3/4 ÷ 3/8 → student writes 1/2 by dividing 3÷3=1 and 4÷8=½ → gets 1/(½) = 2 (accidentally correct here but wrong method for non-cancellable cases). Correct method: flip and multiply. The systematic "Keep, Change, Flip" is more reliable.
- **MISCONCEPTION:** "Adding fractions: add the denominators too." E.g. 1/3 + 1/4 = 2/7. Correction: find common denominator first; 4/12 + 3/12 = 7/12.
- **MISCONCEPTION:** "Mixed number division: divide without converting to improper fraction first." E.g. 2⅓ ÷ 4/5 → student works with 2 and 1/3 separately. Correction: convert 2⅓ to 7/3 first — this is explicitly tested in AQA past papers.
- **MISCONCEPTION:** Inverting the wrong fraction when dividing. E.g. a/b ÷ c/d → student flips a/b instead of c/d. Correction: flip the second fraction (the divisor), not the first.
- **MISCONCEPTION:** Not simplifying the final answer. AQA requires "Give your answer as a fraction in its simplest form" — an unsimplified answer loses the A mark.
- **MISCONCEPTION:** "Exact answer" means decimal. Correction: when AQA says "exact answer", they mean a fraction (not rounded decimal). 35/12 or 2 and 11/12 is the exact answer; 2.9167... is not.

## Exam angles (from AQA past papers)

**How N2/N8 fraction calculations appear in Paper 1 (non-calculator):**

1. **Adding fractions (2 marks):** "Work out 11/18 − 1/3. Give your answer as a fraction in its simplest form." — common denominator 18: 11/18 − 6/18 = 5/18 (already simplest); M1 for common denominator method; A1 for 5/18
2. **Dividing mixed numbers (4 marks):** "Work out 2⅓ ÷ 4/5." — convert: 7/3 ÷ 4/5; flip and multiply: 7/3 × 5/4 = 35/12 = 2 and 11/12; M1 for converting to improper; M1 for flipping and multiplying; A1 for 35/12; B1 for converting to mixed number
3. **Fraction multiplication in context (2–3 marks):** "Sam eats 3/4 of a pizza. He shares 2/3 of what he has with Beth. What fraction of the whole pizza does Beth eat?" — 2/3 × 3/4 = 6/12 = 1/2
4. **Combined fraction operations (3–4 marks):** multi-step problem requiring addition then comparison; may involve ratio (R8) requiring conversion to a fraction first
5. **"Leave as an exact value" (1 mark):** follow-on instruction after a fraction calculation; exact = fraction form required

**AQA wording patterns:**
- "Work out" + fraction calculation
- "Give your answer as a fraction in its simplest form"
- "Give your answer as a mixed number where appropriate"
- "Leave your answer as an exact value" — means fraction, not decimal

**Mark scheme patterns:**
- M1 for converting mixed number to improper fraction (7/3 for 2⅓)
- M1 for inverting second fraction and multiplying
- A1 for correct improper fraction answer
- B1 (independent) for converting to correct mixed number
- "oe fraction" — equivalent unsimplified fraction accepted at intermediate steps; simplest form required for final A mark

## Maths in the wild — context bank

- **Recipes:** multiplying fractions ("use 3/4 of the 2/3 cup of butter") — fraction × fraction
- **Time/distance:** how many 3/4-hour sessions fit into 3⅓ hours? → 3⅓ ÷ 3/4
- **Sharing:** two people each eat 1/3 of a cake; what fraction is left? → 1 − 2/3 = 1/3
- **Construction:** material lengths in fractions of a metre
- **Probability:** P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B) when independent — fraction multiplication (links to Module 4)
