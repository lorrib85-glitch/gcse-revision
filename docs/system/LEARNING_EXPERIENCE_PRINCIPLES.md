# Learning Experience Principles

RISE should not feel like studying. It should feel like becoming fascinated by something.

This document exists for the moments when more than one implementation would be technically correct. When that happens, choose the path that creates curiosity, emotional engagement, and a memorable insight — while respecting the learner's intelligence. The goal is never to maximise what's on screen. It's to maximise what's still in the learner's head a year later.

### Curiosity over explanation

Open with a question or a tension before the answer. A learner wanting to know what happens next is already paying attention; a learner being told what happens next is waiting for it to end.

### Respect the learner over entertaining the learner

Assume curiosity and intelligence. Never patronise, over-explain, or manufacture excitement through gimmicks. The experience should feel quietly confident, premium and trustworthy — not like it's trying too hard to be fun.

### Discovery over exposition

Let the learner arrive at the idea themselves wherever possible, even if it takes one extra step. The same fact lands differently depending on whether it was worked out or handed over — and only one of those makes someone feel clever.

### Stories over abstractions

A person, a decision, a consequence — these are how people actually remember things. Where the content allows it, root abstract ideas in someone's choice and what it cost them.

### Memorable moments over information density

Every chapter should contain at least one genuine "I didn't know that" — something surprising, counter-intuitive, or dramatic enough to repeat to a friend. A page that leaves one fact lodged permanently is worth more than a page that teaches five competently.

### Investigation over instruction

Where a concept can be set up as something to figure out — predict, compare, sort, diagnose — choose that over simply being told. The destination is the same; how the learner gets there changes whether they own it.

### Decisions over descriptions

Asking "what would you do?" engages differently than describing what was done. Where the content allows it, turn description into a choice the learner has to make first.

### Understanding over memorisation

A learner who understands why something is true can reconstruct the fact under exam pressure. A learner who memorised the fact alone cannot reconstruct the reasoning. Always build toward the why.

### Confidence over completion

Finishing a screen is not the same as feeling capable. A learner who leaves feeling "I can do this" has gained more than one who simply reached the end — design for that feeling, not just the finish line.

### Clarity over simplification

Strip away friction, not intellectual depth. Make difficult ideas easier to grasp through sequencing, analogy and interaction — not by removing the nuance that makes them true.

### Genuine interaction over decorative interaction

Every tap, drag, or choice should change what the learner knows or believes. If removing an interaction would make no difference to understanding, it shouldn't be there.

### One unforgettable insight over ten forgettable facts

When deciding what to cut, protect the moment that will stick — even if it means covering less ground. Breadth that isn't retained isn't breadth; it's noise.

### Emotional significance over visual spectacle

A striking image earns its place by making the learner feel something about what it shows, not by being striking. Stakes, consequence and human cost do more for memory than polish.

### Quality of attention over quantity of content

A student who remembers one thing for ten years has learned more than one who skims ten things they'll forget by tomorrow. When in doubt, slow down.

---

When faced with two technically correct designs, choose the one that a learner is most likely to remember a year later.
